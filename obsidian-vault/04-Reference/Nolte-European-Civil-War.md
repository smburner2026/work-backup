## About the Work

Nolte's controversial thesis: that the National Socialist and Bolshevik movements must be understood within the framework of a **European civil war** — a radical ideological conflict that spanned national boundaries from 1917 to 1945. The work argues for a causal-existential connection between the Gulag and Auschwitz, positioning Nazism as a reaction to the existential threat of Bolshevism.

## Key Arguments

- **Civil war framework:** The Weimar Republic was not a stable democracy destroyed by Nazis, but a civil-war zone where both communist and fascist parties viewed each other as existential enemies
- **Chronological priority:** The Bolshevik Revolution (1917) preceded and enabled the fascist reaction — the "genetic primacy" of the Soviet model
- **Asymmetric comparison:** Not equating systems, but insisting that a causal-existential relationship existed
- **Historians' Dispute (*Historikerstreit*):** Nolte's 1986 FAZ article triggered Germany's most famous post-war intellectual controversy
