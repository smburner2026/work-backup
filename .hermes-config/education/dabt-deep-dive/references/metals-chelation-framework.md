# Metals & Chelation: Core Framework

## Principle 1: Metals Are Indestructible Elements

Unlike carbon-based toxicants, metals cannot be metabolized to less toxic forms. There are only three removal strategies:
1. **Enhance excretion** — chelators work here
2. **Sequester into inert forms** — metallothionein, inclusion bodies (lead–protein complexes in kidney)
3. **Stop further exposure**

This is why chelation therapy exists — it's the only active exit strategy.

## Principle 2: Entry/Exit Is Via Transporters, Not Diffusion

Metals are charged ions. They enter cells through **promiscuous ion channels and transporters**:
- Cd²⁺ rides DMT1/ZIP channels (mimics Zn²⁺)
- Tl⁺ mimics K⁺
- Arsenate mimics phosphate
- Methylmercury rides amino acid transporters (binds cysteine, looks like methionine)
- Cr(VI) enters via sulfate/phosphate transporters
- As(III) enters via AQP7/9 (aquaglyceroporins)

The body has no dedicated excretion pumps for nonessential toxic metals. Clearance is slow. Bioaccumulation is the default.

## Principle 3: Four Mechanisms of Metal Toxicity

**(a) Molecular mimicry** — metal replaces essential element at its site of action
**(b) ROS via redox cycling** — transition metals (Fe, Cu, Cr, Mn) catalyze Fenton/Haber-Weiss → hydroxyl radicals
**(c) Enzyme inhibition via sulfhydryl (–SH) binding** — As, Hg, Pb are classic –SH poisons
**(d) Aberrant gene expression** — Ni → HIF-1; As → altered DNA methylation

## HSAB (Hard-Soft Acid-Base) Framework

- **Lewis acid**: electron pair acceptor (metal cation)
- **Lewis base**: electron pair donor (ligand atom)
- **Hard acid** (Ca²⁺, Fe³⁺, Cr³⁺): small, high charge, not polarizable
- **Soft acid** (Hg²⁺, As³⁺, Cu⁺): large, low charge density, polarizable
- **Hard base** (carboxylate –COO⁻, H₂O): holds electrons tightly
- **Soft base** (thiol –SH, sulfide): holds electrons loosely, polarizable

**Rule: hard likes hard, soft likes soft.**

## Thiol (–SH) Chemistry

- Sulfur analog of alcohol's –OH
- Cysteine provides the main biological thiol
- Metals like As, Hg, Pb attack cysteine –SH in enzyme active sites = **sulfhydryl poisoning**
- Chelators with –SH groups (BAL, DMSA) outcompete biological cysteines for soft metals

## Chelator Matrix

| Chelator | Lewis base type | Route | Target metals | Key limitation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **BAL** (dimercaprol) | Soft (–SH ×2) | IM only | As, Hg, Au, Pb | Toxic, painful |
| **DMSA** (succimer) | Soft (–SH ×2) | Oral | **Pb** (first-line peds), As, Hg | Crosses BBB poorly |
| **EDTA** (CaNa₂EDTA) | Hard (–COO⁻, –NH₂) | IV | **Pb**, Cd | Not for Hg (mismatch); nephrotoxic |
| **Penicillamine** | Intermediate (–SH, –NH₂) | Oral | Cu (Wilson's), Pb | Autoimmune reactions |
| **DMPS** (unithiol) | Soft (–SH ×2) | IV/Oral | Hg, As, Pb | Not FDA-approved in US |
| **Deferoxamine** | Hard (hydroxamate O) | IV/IM/SC | **Fe** | Not for others |

## Critical Exception: Chromium

- **Cr(VI)** is the toxic form — enters cells easily via sulfate transporters
- **Cr(III)** is the nontoxic form — hard Lewis acid, forms octahedral complexes, kinetically trapped inside cells
- Toxicity occurs *during intracellular reduction* of Cr(VI) → Cr(III) (free radicals, DNA adducts)
- **No chelator works** — Cr(III) is already trapped. Treatment: **ascorbic acid** (accelerates extracellular reduction of Cr(VI) before cell entry)

## HSAB Limitations (When Theory ≠ Clinical Practice)

1. **Kinetic access** — can the chelator reach the metal? (brain lead, intracellular Cr(III))
2. **Timing** — is the metal still in circulation or already sequestered?
3. **Chelator toxicity** — BAL causes nephrotoxicity; EDTA chelates Ca²⁺

## Key Regulatory Threshold: Blood Lead

| Context | Threshold | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **OSHA — adult occupational maintenance** | 40 µg/dL | Maintain below |
| **OSHA — adult occupational removal** | ≥50 µg/dL | Remove from exposure |
| **CDC — pediatric level of concern** (former) | 10 µg/dL | Public health intervention |
| **CDC — pediatric reference level** (current) | 5 µg/dL | 97.5th percentile; no safe level |
| **Chelation therapy initiation** | >60 µg/dL (adults) | DMSA (peds) or EDTA/CaNa₂EDTA |

**Common trap**: confusing pediatric CDC concern levels (5–10 µg/dL) with adult OSHA occupational standards (40/50 µg/dL).

## Comparative Metals Framework (Six-Axis Model)

Validated in the 2026-05-19 As/Cd/Cr deepdive. When consolidating multiple metals, compare along exactly six axes in fixed order:

### 1. Valence / Chemical Form
| Metal | Toxic form | Less-toxic / essential form | Why it matters |
|-------|-----------|----------------------------|----------------|
| **As** | As³⁺ (arsenite) | As⁵⁺ (arsenate) | As³⁺ binds SH groups → enzyme inhibition. As⁵⁺ mimics phosphate → arsenolysis. Both toxic, different mechanisms. |
| **Cd** | Free Cd²⁺ | Cd-MT (metallothionein-bound) | Free Cd²⁺ causes oxidative stress. Cd-MT sequesters it until lysosomal degradation releases free Cd²⁺. |
| **Cr** | Cr(VI) (hexavalent) | Cr(III) (trivalent) | Cr(VI) enters cells via sulfate transporters → intracellular reduction to Cr(III) → DNA damage. Cr(III) is essential nutrient but can't enter cells. Cr(VI) is a Trojan horse. |

### 2. Half-Life / Accumulation Pattern
| Metal | Half-life | Toxicological consequence |
|-------|-----------|--------------------------|
| **As** | Hours (blood), days-weeks (tissue) | Event-driven acute or subacute toxicity. Mees lines at 6-8 weeks (nail growth delay). |
| **Cd** | >26 years | Cumulative poison. Lifetime body burden accumulates. No "resetting" — every exposure adds. |
| **Cr** | Cr(VI): days. Cr(III): weeks. | Peak-exposure-driven (occupational inhalation). Not cumulative like Cd. |

### 3. Target Organs (Route-Dependent)
| Metal | Lung | Kidney | Skin | Other |
|-------|------|--------|------|-------|
| **As** | Lung cancer (IARC 1) | — | Hyperkeratosis, Bowen disease, SCC (pathognomonic) | Bladder cancer, liver angiosarcoma, Torsade de pointes, peripheral neuropathy |
| **Cd** | Emphysema, lung cancer (IARC 1) | Proximal tubular damage (β₂-MG, NAG) — classical target | — | Bone (Itai-Itai → osteomalacia) |
| **Cr** | Lung cancer (bronchial + nasal), nasal septum perforation | — | Chrome ulcers ("chrome holes") | — |

**Exam pattern:** Skin finding → As. Renal tubular proteinuria → Cd. Nasal perforation / lung cancer in a welder → Cr(VI).

### 4. Biomarkers
| Metal | Primary matrix | What is measured | Critical nuance |
|-------|---------------|-----------------|----------------|
| **As** | Urine | Speciated: inorganic As + MMA + DMA | **Must speciate** to exclude arsenobetaine (dietary seafood). Blood useless except acute. |
| **Cd** | Urine + Blood | Urinary Cd (body burden), β₂-MG (effect) | β₂-MG = **tubular damage**, not exposure. Blood Cd = recent. Urine Cd = lifetime. |
| **Cr** | Urine | Total chromium | **Cannot distinguish Cr(VI) from Cr(III)**. Speciation required. Total Cr measurement alone is uninformative for carcinogenic risk assessment. |

### 5. Chelator Availability
| Metal | Effective chelator? | Chelator | Why yes / why no |
|-------|--------------------|----------|-----------------|
| **As** | YES | BAL (dimercaprol) or DMSA | As-SH bond is reversible. BAL competes for the same SH groups. |
| **Cd** | NO | None | Cd-MT Kd ~10⁻¹⁵ — strongest metal-MT bond. Chelators can't displace it and may redistribute Cd to kidney, worsening damage. |
| **Cr(VI)** | NO | Supportive care only | Cr(VI) rapidly enters cells and reduces to Cr(III) intracellularly. By the time chelation could act, the damage is DNA-level and inaccessible. |

### 6. Carcinogenic Mechanism
| Metal | Mechanism | IARC class | Key distinction |
|-------|-----------|------------|----------------|
| **As** | Genotoxic + epigenetic (oxidative stress, inhibits DNA repair via zinc finger interference, aberrant DNA methylation) | Group 1 | Multi-site: skin, lung, bladder, liver. Promiscuous because it doesn't form direct DNA adducts easily — epigenetic effects give broad tissue range. |
| **Cd** | Indirect genotoxic (oxidative stress → 8-oxodG, displaces Zn in DNA repair proteins, suppresses apoptosis of damaged cells) | Group 1 | Primarily lung + prostate. Weak direct mutagen; chronic inflammation drives proliferation. |
| **Cr(VI)** | Direct genotoxic (intracellular reduction → Cr(III) + ROS → DNA adducts, strand breaks, cross-links) | Group 1 | Clean dose-response. Classic direct DNA-damaging agent. Lung only (inhalation route). |

### One-Sentence Architecture
- **Arsenic poisons enzymes**
- **Cadmium overstays its welcome**
- **Chromium smuggles its toxic form past the cell membrane**

## Exam Traps (From the 446-Question Database)

1. **"Elevated urinary chromium proves Cr(VI) exposure"** → WRONG. Total Cr cannot distinguish Cr(VI) from Cr(III). Speciation required.
2. **"Elevated urinary arsenic proves acute poisoning"** → WRONG. Need speciation to exclude arsenobetaine (dietary seafood).
3. **"Cadmium chelation is treatment"** → WRONG. Cd has no effective chelator. Chelation may worsen renal damage.
4. **"BAL is oral chelator"** → WRONG. BAL is IM only. DMSA is oral.
5. **"Pediatric blood lead concern level = 40 µg/dL"** → WRONG. 5-10 µg/dL pediatric vs 40-50 µg/dL adult occupational.
6. **"All Group 1 metal carcinogens are direct genotoxic"** → WRONG. As is epigenetic/genotoxic mix, Cd is indirect genotoxic. Only Cr(VI) is direct genotoxic.
7. **"Elevated urinary Cd proves acute exposure"** → WRONG. Urinary Cd reflects lifetime body burden, not recent exposure. Blood Cd is the recent-exposure marker.
8. **"Arsenic biomethylation is purely detoxification"** → WRONG. The intermediate MMA³⁺ is more toxic than inorganic As³⁺. Methylation reduces tissue retention but transiently produces a more reactive species.
