## Domain I — Conduct of Toxicological Studies (36%)

### A. Design (11%)
1. Design scientifically valid studies to answer questions or address defined hypotheses
   - Elements of a scientific hypothesis
   - Elements of a scientifically valid hypothesis-driven research or regulatory guideline study, including selection of statistical analyses
   - Experimental and control groups
   - Considerations: dose/concentration, duration, life-stage, endpoints, route, alternative in vitro and ecotoxicological studies
   - Principles of the 3Rs (Reduce/Refine/Replace)
   - Selection of appropriate species

2. Comply with applicable regulations and guidelines (GLP, OECD, USEPA, ICH, USFDA, EMA)

3. Identify characteristics of test agent needed for study (chemical identity, physical properties)
   - Analytical methods (HPLC, MS, ELISA)
   - Formulation, stability, homogeneity
   - Solubility, vehicles, excipients
   - Impurities per ICH Q3A(R2)

4. Identify and apply new testing methods fit for purpose
   - Validation standards
   - Alternative methods (dermal sensitization, cell-based assays, omics)
   - Limitations and advantages (specificity, sensitivity)

### B. Execute (9%)
1. Ensure compliance with GLPs, SOPs, regulatory guidances
2. Characterize toxicological effects in vivo (models, endpoints)
3. Characterize toxicological effects in vitro (models, endpoints)
4. Characterize toxicological effects in silico (QSAR, read-across, PBPK, AI/ML)
5. Characterize toxicological effects in field/clinical settings (ecotox, human trials, epidemiology)

### C. Interpret (16%)
1. Analyze test results using informatics, statistics, and modeling
2. Identify systemic/local effects, target organs, dose response, thresholds, reversibility
3. Prepare research reports fit for purpose (elements, audience tailoring, narrative/tabular/graphical)
4. Interpret and integrate study results with other data into cogent narrative
   - Systematic literature review principles (risk of bias, search strategies)
   - Relevance for human or animal safety

## Domain II — Mechanistic Toxicology (13%)

1. Develop hypotheses to investigate mechanisms
   - Steps in mechanistic hypothesis development
   - Methods to test (in vitro/in vivo)
   - Molecular/signaling pathways in chemical-specific toxicities
   - Physiology and organ function
   - Genetic variant phenotypes (KOs, SNPs)
   - Omics technologies

2. Assess role of species differences in mechanism
   - ADME, PK/TK, PK/PD, parent vs metabolite
   - Species-specific toxicokinetics/toxicodynamics
   - Species differences in anatomy/physiology (respiratory tract, dermal, oral)

3. Identify susceptibility factors (genetic polymorphisms, life-stage, gender, disease states)

4. Assess MOA/AOP for relevance to humans/target species
   - AOP concept
   - Fundamental molecular mechanisms
   - Receptor/protein-level toxicant interactions

5. Distinguish direct/indirect, primary/secondary, on-target/off-target
   - Direct targeted interaction
   - Secondary/indirect effect (e.g., delayed ossification due to maternal toxicity)
   - Downstream effects in individuals and populations

6. Translate results across biological levels (sub-cellular → populations)
   - Methods to translate across organization levels
   - Species differences (anatomical, physiological, biochemical, pharmacological)
   - Analytical/computational tools
   - Omics → organism-level effects

7. Apply mechanistic results to outcomes, prevention, risk clarification
   - MOA of chemicals and regulation (endocrine disruptors, PBTs, nanoparticles)
   - Mechanistic data informing AOPs
   - Mixtures toxicity (synergism, potentiation, additive, antagonistic)
   - Cellular/biochemical/molecular mechanisms for risk interpretation

8. Apply disease and genetically-engineered models
   - Alternative animal disease models
   - Strengths and limitations vs human conditions

## Domain III — Risk Assessment (38%)

### A. Hazard Identification (12%)
1. Characterize, describe, interpret effects, target organs, endpoints
   - Major endpoints: acute lethality, repeat dose, repro/developmental, genotoxicity, carcinogenicity, sensitization, immunotoxicity, phototoxicity
   - Ecotoxicological studies
   - Toxicity by different routes
   - Relevant agents: pesticides, metals, solvents, radiation, nanomaterials, air pollution, natural toxins
   - Organ system toxic responses (blood, bone, immune, GI, liver, kidney, respiratory, nervous, eye, heart, skin, reproductive, endocrine)
   - Adverse vs non-adverse/adaptive effects
   - False negative/positive concepts
   - Chemical-specific biomarkers of effect

2. Apply in silico, in vitro, in vivo systems for safety evaluation
   - Application of data from various methods (SAR, in vitro, animal bioassays, epidemiology)
   - Relevant target organisms/species

3. Identify exposure routes as appropriate surrogates/alternatives
   - SAR/QSAR in risk assessment context
   - Oral study types (gavage vs dietary/drinking water)
   - Inhalation study types (aerosol vs vapor vs IT instillation)
   - Metabolic differences between routes
   - Anatomical differences across species

4. Assess impact of anticipated vs unanticipated effects
   - Target and off-target effects at organism, species, population, environmental levels

### B. Exposure Assessment (8%)
1. Select appropriate exposure endpoints
   - Measures/units of exposure (dose, airborne concentration, plasma level)
   - Biomarkers of exposure
   - Reactive metabolites, toxicophores

2. Assess internal exposure
   - Routes of exposure and effects on cells/tissues/organisms/environment
   - ADME, kinetics, storage
   - Constituent selection (parent vs metabolite, free vs total)

3. Assess exposure in general public, occupational, populations, environment
   - Technologies (biomonitoring, sentinel species, spatial models)
   - Default values (body weight, surface area, inhalation rates)

4. Assess environmental fate and transport
   - Persistence, biotransformation, bioavailability, bioaccumulation, biomagnification
   - Exposure patterns (conceptual site model)
   - Fate/transport processes

### C. Dose Response Assessment (9%)
1. Characterize dose-response relationships
   - Types of curves: lethality, threshold, linear, non-linear, non-monotonic, hormesis
   - Frequency and cumulative curves
   - Parameters: slope, plateau, midpoint
   - ED50, EC50, LD50, LC50
   - BMD methodology (general principles, statistical methods, modeling)
   - PBPK outputs in dose-response
   - Dosimetric adjustments (HED/HEC)
   - Extrapolations and uncertainty: UFs, CSAFs, DDEFs

2. Analyze data to determine safety margin and FIH dose
   - FIH criteria per regulatory guidance

3. Analyze data to determine safe dose, reference dose, POD, BMD
   - Threshold/non-threshold curves
   - Endpoint integration (organ weights, clinical chemistry, histopathology)
   - Essential nutrient dynamics, hormesis
   - Therapeutic Index calculation
   - PODs: LOAEL, BMD, NOAEL, HNSTD, MABEL, STD10 + UFs
   - Safety thresholds: TTC, Cramer Class, DNELs, DMELs, ADI, OELs
   - Allometric scaling including defaults

### D. Risk Characterization & Management (9%)
1. Integrate evidence with exposure to characterize health risks
   - Metrics: MOE, MOS, HQ, HI, probability estimates
   - Aggregate and cumulative risk
   - Mixtures: dose-addition, TEFs

2. Perform weight of evidence analyses
   - WoE criteria (Bradford Hill)
   - Systematic review (Cochrane)

3. Assess health risks and management options
   - Modifying factors (engineering, economic, social, political)
   - Risk perception and communication
   - Precautionary principle
   - Health-protective limits for vulnerable populations (immunocompromised, pregnant, elderly, asthmatic)

4. Evaluate alternatives to reduce risk
   - Mitigation (antidotes, remediation, engineering controls, PPE)
   - Cleanup goals, emission limits
   - Health-based guidance values (PALs, AEGLs)

## Domain IV — Applied Toxicology (13%)

1. Characterize effects of ecotoxicological concern
   - Individual vs population responses
   - Indirect effects in ecological communities

2. Respond to public health issues, including emerging concerns
   - Risk communication, risk perception, mitigation
   - Major events: Bhopal, radium dial painters, cytokine storm, Minamata, thalidomide, DDT
   - Emerging risks: climate change, infrastructure, food scarcity, infectious disease

3. Assess public health impact from environmental toxicants
   - Ecosystem services (clean water, air)
   - Zoonotic disease prevalence

4. Investigate health outcomes using measured/modeled exposures
   - Exposure reconstruction in epidemiological context

5. Use population-based biomonitoring for temporal trends
   - NHANES and national databases
   - Cross-sectional vs longitudinal epidemiology
   - Community public health investigations

6. Assess and address occupational health concerns
   - Workplace exposure assessment and monitoring
   - Occupational exposure limits (OELs, TLVs, BEIs)
   - Industrial hygiene hierarchy of controls

7. Apply forensic toxicology principles
   - Postmortem redistribution, specimen selection
   - Interpretation of postmortem concentrations

## Exam Logistics

- **Questions:** 160 (140 scored + 20 pretest), 4 hours
- **Format:** MCQ, computer-based at Pearson VUE
- **Scoring:** Single composite pass/fail; scores not released
- **Blueprint basis:** 2022 Practice Analysis (Int J Toxicol 2023, PMC10493040)
- **Passing standard:** Modified Angoff procedure
