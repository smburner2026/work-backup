Module 1: Fundamentals of Immunotoxicology
(Tied directly to your uploaded resources: ICH S8 “Immunotoxicity Studies for Human Pharmaceuticals” (S8_Guideline_0.pdf), OECD TG 407/408/443, Casarett & Doull’s Toxicology 8th ed., FDA Redbook, and ABT 2026 Candidate Handbook context for DABT exam prep)

Basic Summary (3–5 sentences)
Immunotoxicology examines how xenobiotics (drugs, chemicals, environmental agents) adversely affect immune structure or function, leading to four core outcomes: immunosuppression (↑ infection/cancer risk), immunostimulation, hypersensitivity (Type I–IV allergies), and autoimmunity. Regulatory testing follows a tiered, weight-of-evidence (WoE) strategy: standard toxicity studies (STS) such as OECD TG 407 (28-day) and TG 408 (90-day) screen via hematology (WBC/differentials), lymphoid organ weights (thymus, spleen), bone-marrow cellularity, and histopathology of immune organs (lymph nodes, Peyer’s patches); if signals emerge (or the drug targets the immune system), functional assays like T-cell-dependent antibody response (TDAR) are triggered. This integrates with reproductive (TG 443, ICH S5(R3)) and carcinogenicity (TG 451/453, ICH S1B(R1)) guidelines and directly supports DABT exam emphasis on risk assessment and 3Rs (Replacement, Reduction, Refinement).

In-Depth Explanation (8 paragraphs – building layered understanding)
Immune System Primer (why it matters for toxicology): The immune system comprises innate (rapid, non-specific: macrophages, NK cells, complement) and adaptive (specific, memory: T/B lymphocytes) arms. Key organs include bone marrow (hematopoiesis), thymus (T-cell maturation), spleen/lymph nodes (antigen presentation/activation), and mucosal sites. Xenobiotics can disrupt at any level—e.g., direct cytotoxicity to rapidly dividing lymphocytes or indirect via stress hormones—making immunotoxicology a cross-cutting discipline.
Four Categories of Immunotoxicity (DABT high-yield framework): (a) Immunosuppression (most common regulatory concern); (b) Immunostimulation (cytokine release, autoimmunity risk); (c) Hypersensitivity (linked to your uploaded TG 497 Defined Approaches on Skin Sensitisation); (d) Autoimmunity (molecular mimicry or bystander activation). Casarett & Doull (Ch. ~12) stresses that suppression is often the most sensitive endpoint in repeat-dose studies.
Screening in Standard Toxicity Studies (STS – OECD TG 407/408): Your uploaded TG 407 (28-day oral rodent) and TG 408 (90-day) mandate immune endpoints: absolute/relative thymus & spleen weights, histopathology (cortical/medullary thymus ratio, splenic lymphoid depletion, lymph-node architecture), hematology (lymphocyte counts), and clinical signs (infections). These are GLP-compliant, cost-effective first-tier screens. No functional testing is required unless WoE flags concern.
ICH S8 Weight-of-Evidence Decision Tree (pharma-specific, your uploaded S8_Guideline_0.pdf): If STS show changes (e.g., ↓ thymus weight >20%, lymphoid atrophy) OR the molecule is intended to modulate immunity OR the target population is immunocompromised (infants, elderly), proceed to additional studies. Factors weighed: dose-response, reversibility, exposure margin, pharmacology. This avoids unnecessary animal use (3Rs alignment).
Functional Assays (Tier 2): Gold-standard TDAR (sheep red blood cell or keyhole limpet hemocyanin immunization → IgM/IgG ELISA) is most sensitive for humoral immunity. Others: NK-cell cytotoxicity (⁵¹Cr-release or flow-based), CTL activity, host-resistance models (influenza, Listeria), immunophenotyping (flow cytometry: CD4/CD8, B-cell markers). ICH S8 Appendix details methods and positive controls (e.g., cyclophosphamide).
Developmental Immunotoxicology (DIT) Intersection: Critical window (gestation/lactation). Your uploaded TG 443 (Extended One-Generation Reproductive Toxicity Study) and ICH S5(R3) require DIT endpoints (e.g., TDAR in F1 pups, lymphoid histopathology). Infants are uniquely vulnerable due to immature thymus and Th2 bias.
Mechanisms & Human Relevance: Direct (alkylating agents cross-link DNA in dividing lymphocytes), receptor-mediated (AhR agonists like TCDD cause thymic involution), or epigenetic. Parallelogram approach (animal in vivo/in vitro ↔ human cells) bridges to risk assessment. FDA Redbook and EPA Cancer Guidelines (your uploaded EPA_cancer_guidelines_2005.pdf) incorporate immunotox into hazard ID and mode-of-action analysis.
Regulatory & Risk-Assessment Context: Ties into chronic/carcinogenicity (TG 451/452/453 – immune suppression can promote tumors), genotox (ICH S2(R1)), and anticancer pharma (ICH S9 – abbreviated immunotox if mechanism known). DABT exam loves this synthesis: how does a positive TDAR trigger labeling, exposure limits, or clinical monitoring?

Module 1.1: Basic Overview of the Immune System – Components & Mechanisms
(Building directly on your uploaded Casarett & Doull’s Toxicology 8th ed., ICH S8, OECD TG 407/408/443, and ABT 2026 Candidate Handbook – this is the foundational “why” behind every immunotoxicity screen you’ll see on the DABT exam)

Basic Summary (3–5 sentences)
The immune system is the body’s defense network that distinguishes self from non-self, protecting against pathogens, damaged cells, and foreign substances. It has two interconnected arms: innate immunity (rapid, non-specific, present from birth) and adaptive immunity (slow, antigen-specific, with memory). Key components include physical barriers, immune cells (phagocytes, lymphocytes), lymphoid organs, and soluble mediators (cytokines, antibodies, complement). In toxicology, these elements are screened in OECD TG 407/408 repeat-dose studies because xenobiotics often target rapidly dividing lymphocytes or disrupt cytokine balance, leading to the four immunotoxic outcomes (immunosuppression, hypersensitivity, autoimmunity, immunostimulation) detailed in ICH S8.

In-Depth Explanation (7 paragraphs – layered for durable retention)
Core Purpose & Self/Non-Self Discrimination: Every cell in the body expresses “self” markers (MHC class I on nucleated cells; MHC class II on antigen-presenting cells). The immune system uses pattern-recognition receptors (PRRs) to detect pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) or damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs). Failure of self-tolerance leads to autoimmunity; excessive response to harmless antigens produces hypersensitivity (Casarett & Doull Ch. 12 emphasizes this as the root of immunotoxic risk).
Innate Immunity – First Line of Defense (0–96 hours): Comprises anatomical barriers (skin, mucosa, cilia), chemical barriers (lysozyme, gastric acid, defensins), and cellular components. Neutrophils and macrophages phagocytose invaders; natural killer (NK) cells kill virus-infected or tumor cells via perforin/granzyme. Complement proteins tag pathogens for destruction (C3b opsonization) and trigger inflammation via C5a. This arm is always “on” and is the most common early target in 28-day OECD TG 407 studies (e.g., neutrophil counts, macrophage histopathology).
Adaptive Immunity – Specific & Memory (begins ~4–7 days): Driven by lymphocytes. B cells produce antibodies (IgM first, then class-switched IgG/IgA/IgE). T cells include helper (CD4+ – orchestrate via cytokines), cytotoxic (CD8+ – kill via perforin), and regulatory (Treg – prevent autoimmunity). Antigen presentation by dendritic cells/MHC links innate to adaptive. Memory B and T cells enable faster secondary responses – the reason vaccines work and the reason chronic immunosuppression (e.g., by drugs screened in TG 408) increases cancer/infection risk.
Key Immune Organs (primary & secondary – critical in GLP histopathology):
Primary: Bone marrow (hematopoiesis of all lineages; B-cell maturation) and thymus (T-cell education & negative selection).
Secondary: Spleen (blood filtration, antibody production), lymph nodes (lymph filtration), mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue (MALT – Peyer’s patches in gut).
OECD TG 407/408 and TG 443 mandate weighing and detailed histopathology of thymus, spleen, lymph nodes, and bone-marrow cellularity exactly because these are the most sensitive sites for xenobiotic damage.
Soluble Mediators – The Chemical Language: Cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α, IFNs) drive inflammation and cell communication. Chemokines direct leukocyte migration. Antibodies neutralize pathogens or opsonize them. Complement cascade amplifies innate responses. Disruption of cytokine balance (e.g., cytokine storm or suppression) is a major immunotoxic mechanism flagged in ICH S8 weight-of-evidence decisions.
Integration & Regulation: Innate and adaptive arms talk constantly (e.g., macrophages present antigen → activate T cells → help B cells). Regulatory mechanisms (Tregs, inhibitory checkpoints like CTLA-4/PD-1) prevent over-reaction. In developmental immunotoxicology (TG 443 & ICH S5(R3)), the fetal/neonatal immune system is Th2-biased and has an immature thymus – making it uniquely vulnerable to xenobiotics.
Why This Matters for DABT & Regulatory Toxicology: Every repeat-dose study you interpret on the exam starts with these components. A ↓ thymus weight or lymphoid depletion in TG 408 triggers ICH S8 Tier 2 functional testing (TDAR, NK assay). Understanding the normal system lets you spot deviations that predict human risk (infections, cancer, allergy) and justify risk-assessment decisions.

Real-World Example (Cyclophosphamide – classic positive control across your uploaded resources):
Cyclophosphamide (alkylating agent) enters cells and cross-links DNA, preferentially killing rapidly proliferating lymphocytes. In a standard OECD TG 408 90-day rat study, you see profound thymic cortical atrophy (histopathology), spleen weight ↓30–50%, circulating lymphocyte counts ↓60%, and near-total loss of TDAR response when animals are immunized with sheep red blood cells (IgM titer <10% of control). Mechanistically, it blocks both T- and B-cell proliferation in the adaptive arm while sparing some innate cells. This mirrors exactly what ICH S8 uses as the benchmark for immunosuppression: increased infection risk in cancer patients (Pneumocystis pneumonia) and secondary malignancies – the precise human hazard the animal data predict. Module 1.2: T Cells vs. B Cells – Clear Differentiation & Mastering Immune Cell Types for Immunotoxicology
(Building directly on your uploaded Casarett & Doull’s Toxicology 8th ed. (immunotoxicology fundamentals), ICH S8 weight-of-evidence approach, and OECD TG 407/408/443 lymphoid histopathology endpoints – this is the “aha!” moment that makes every repeat-dose study and TDAR result click for the DABT exam)

Basic Summary (3–5 sentences)
T cells and B cells are the two major lymphocyte lineages of adaptive immunity, both arising from common bone-marrow hematopoietic stem cells, but they diverge dramatically in maturation site, receptors, and function. B cells mature entirely in the bone marrow (hence “B”), express surface immunoglobulin (B-cell receptor), and ultimately differentiate into plasma cells that secrete antibodies—driving humoral (fluid-phase) immunity. T cells leave the bone marrow as immature precursors, mature in the thymus (hence “T”), express T-cell receptor (TCR) that recognizes antigen only when presented on MHC molecules, and orchestrate cell-mediated immunity via helper (CD4+), cytotoxic (CD8+), and regulatory (Treg) subsets. In immunotoxicology (ICH S8 & OECD TG 407/408), these differences matter because xenobiotics often hit one lineage harder (e.g., T-cell depletion in thymus cortex), and the gold-standard TDAR assay actually tests both (B-cell antibody output requires CD4+ T-cell help).

In-Depth Explanation (7 paragraphs + comparison table – designed for deliberate memorization)
Shared Origin but Immediate Branch Point: Both start in bone marrow from common lymphoid progenitors. B cells complete maturation locally (rearranging Ig genes via RAG enzymes). T cells must migrate to the thymus for positive/negative selection—ensuring they recognize self-MHC but not self-antigens. This thymic step is why thymus weight and cortical/medullary ratio in OECD TG 408 are ultra-sensitive immunotox screens (Casarett & Doull highlights this as the first red flag for many immunosuppressants).
Receptors & Antigen Recognition – The Core Mechanic Difference: B cells use membrane-bound antibody (BCR = IgM/IgD) that directly binds soluble antigen—no MHC needed. T cells use TCR that only sees peptide fragments presented on MHC (Class II for CD4 helpers, Class I for CD8 killers). This MHC restriction is why T cells are the “conductors” and B cells the “antibody factories.”
Functional Outcomes & Memory: B cells → plasma cells secrete IgM (first response), then class-switch to IgG/IgA/IgE (with T-help). Memory B cells give rapid high-affinity recall. T cells: CD4+ helpers release cytokines (IL-2, IL-4, IFN-γ) to activate everything else; CD8+ kill infected/tumor cells; Tregs suppress over-reaction. Adaptive memory is the hallmark—and the reason chronic immunosuppression (flagged in TG 407/443) raises lifelong infection/cancer risk.
Surface Markers Used in Immunophenotyping (ICH S8 Tier 2): Flow cytometry in additional studies distinguishes them instantly: B cells = CD19/CD20/CD45R (B220 in rodents); T cells = CD3 (all), plus CD4 or CD8. OECD TG 443 extended one-gen studies now routinely include these in F1 pups for developmental immunotox (DIT).
Why This Differentiation Trips Students Up in Immunotox: Many xenobiotics are lineage-specific. Cyclosporine hits T cells hard (calcineurin inhibitor → no IL-2 → no helper function). Cyclophosphamide (alkylator) kills both but prefers rapidly dividing cells (both lineages affected). Understanding this lets you predict TDAR results: if only B cells are hit, TDAR fails; if only T helpers are hit, TDAR also fails (because B cells need T-cell cytokines).
Integration in Standard Toxicity Studies (your OECD uploads): TG 407/408 histopathology shows: thymus cortex = T-cell rich (depletion = T-cell toxicity); spleen periarteriolar sheath = T zone, follicles = B zone. Bone-marrow cellularity affects both lineages. These are your first-tier screens—zero functional testing unless signals appear (ICH S8 decision tree).
DABT Exam Relevance & Synthesis: Expect questions like “Which assay requires both T- and B-cell cooperation?” (Answer: TDAR). Or “Why is thymic atrophy more sensitive than spleen changes?” (T-cell maturation is a bottleneck). Mastering this table below will make every future module (hypersensitivity, DIT, host-resistance models) effortless.

Module 1.3: What Do B-Cell Antibodies Actually Do? – Effector Functions Explained
(Direct tie-in to your uploaded Casarett & Doull’s Toxicology 8th ed. (Ch. 12 immunology section), ICH S8 “Immunotoxicity Studies” (TDAR as the gold-standard functional assay), and OECD TG 407/408/443 histopathology + antibody endpoints – this locks in why antibody suppression is a critical immunotoxic flag for the DABT exam)

Basic Summary (3–5 sentences)
B-cell antibodies (immunoglobulins: IgM, IgG, IgA, IgE, IgD) are Y-shaped glycoproteins secreted by plasma cells (terminally differentiated B cells) after T-cell help; they do NOT recognize antigen on MHC like T cells do—they bind native antigen directly in solution or on cell surfaces. Their five main effector functions are: (1) neutralization (block pathogen entry), (2) opsonization (tag for phagocytosis), (3) complement activation (lysis + inflammation), (4) antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC – recruit NK cells/macrophages to kill), and (5) agglutination/precipitation (clump antigens for easier clearance). In immunotoxicology, the T-cell-dependent antibody response (TDAR) assay (ICH S8 Tier 2, triggered by OECD TG 407/408 lymphoid depletion) measures IgM/IgG titers exactly because loss of these functions predicts real-world infection risk. Xenobiotics that hit B cells or T-helper signaling (e.g., cyclophosphamide) collapse these functions, which is why antibody endpoints are mandatory in developmental immunotoxicity (TG 443).

In-Depth Explanation (7 paragraphs – building the “why this matters” synthesis)
Antibody Structure Drives Function: Each antibody has two Fab arms (antigen-binding) and one Fc tail (effector region). The Fab binds epitope with exquisite specificity; the Fc recruits the rest of the immune system. Class switching (IgM → IgG etc., driven by CD4+ T-helper cytokines) changes the Fc tail and therefore the downstream functions—exactly why TDAR measures both early IgM and later IgG.
Neutralization – The First Line Blocker: Antibodies coat viruses/bacteria/toxins, physically blocking attachment to host cells (e.g., spike protein on SARS-CoV-2). No cell killing needed—just steric hindrance. This is why vaccine-induced antibodies protect without inflammation.
Opsonization – “Eat Me” Tag: Fc region binds Fc receptors on macrophages/neutrophils, marking the pathogen for phagocytosis. Complement fragments (C3b) do the same. This amplifies innate clearance—super important in repeat-dose studies where macrophage function can also be screened (OECD TG 408).
Complement Activation – The Cascade Explosion: Classical pathway starts when IgM or IgG binds antigen → C1q binds Fc → full membrane-attack complex (MAC) punches holes in pathogen membranes (lysis). Also releases C5a (chemotaxis) and C3a (mast-cell degranulation). Casarett & Doull notes this is why low antibody titers = increased bacterial sepsis risk.
ADCC – Killer Recruitment: NK cells and macrophages have Fcγ receptors that grab the antibody-coated target → perforin/granzyme release → target cell death. This is how therapeutic monoclonal antibodies (e.g., rituximab anti-CD20) kill cancer cells and why B-cell suppression is a big deal in oncology immunotox.
Agglutination & Precipitation – Clumping for Cleanup: Multivalent IgM cross-links antigens into large complexes that are easily filtered by spleen/liver or phagocytosed. Precipitation works for soluble toxins. All five functions are T-dependent in the TDAR (sheep RBC or KLH immunization), which is why ICH S8 uses it as the most sensitive functional readout.
DABT & Regulatory Synthesis: OECD TG 407/408 first-tier screens (lymphoid depletion, ↓ spleen weight) trigger ICH S8 TDAR. If titers drop >50–70% with dose-response, you have immunosuppression → risk assessment includes infection models, labeling, and clinical monitoring. This integrates with carcinogenicity (TG 451/453: suppressed surveillance = tumors) and developmental studies (TG 443: fetal/neonatal antibody deficits).

Real-World Example (Cyclophosphamide – classic positive control across your uploaded ICH S8, OECD TG 408, and Casarett & Doull):
In a 28-day OECD TG 407 rat study (10–50 mg/kg), cyclophosphamide alkylates DNA in dividing B-cell precursors and plasma cells. Result: near-zero IgM/IgG response when immunized with sheep red blood cells (TDAR collapse). Mechanistically, neutralization fails → virus spreads unchecked; opsonization/complement fail → bacteria proliferate; ADCC fails → infected cells survive. Humans on cyclophosphamide (chemo or autoimmune therapy) show the exact mirror: recurrent sinopulmonary infections, poor vaccine responses, and secondary bacterial sepsis. This is why ICH S8 flags it as a benchmark immunosuppressant and why antibody functions are the downstream “so what” of B-cell toxicity. Recovery after dosing stops (reversibility) is also scored.

Module 1.4: Deep Dive on T Cells – Subsets, Activation, Functions & Immunotoxicology Relevance
(Building straight from your uploaded Casarett & Doull’s Toxicology 8th ed. (Ch. 12 lymphocyte biology), ICH S8 “Immunotoxicity Studies” (T-cell help required for TDAR), OECD TG 407/408 (thymus cortical depletion as ultra-sensitive screen), and TG 443 (developmental T-cell windows) – this is the “conductor” module that makes every repeat-dose study interpretation click for the DABT exam)

Basic Summary (3–5 sentences)
T cells are the master orchestrators of adaptive immunity: they originate in bone marrow, mature in the thymus (positive/negative selection on self-MHC), and exit expressing a unique T-cell receptor (TCR) that recognizes antigen only when presented on MHC molecules by antigen-presenting cells. The three main subsets are CD4+ helper T cells (orchestrate via cytokines), CD8+ cytotoxic T cells (direct killing), and regulatory T cells (Treg – maintain tolerance). In immunotoxicology, T cells are the most sensitive lineage in standard OECD TG 407/408 studies because the thymus cortex is packed with rapidly dividing immature T cells; any depletion here (weight loss, histopathology) triggers ICH S8 Tier 2 functional testing (TDAR, CTL assay) since CD4+ help is required for B-cell antibody production and effective cell-mediated immunity.

In-Depth Explanation (8 paragraphs – layered for synthesis & retention)
Maturation & Thymic Education (the bottleneck): Immature T precursors enter the thymus as double-negative (CD4–CD8–), become double-positive, then single-positive (CD4+ or CD8+). Positive selection ensures TCR recognizes self-MHC; negative selection deletes self-reactive clones. This is why OECD TG 407/408 thymus cortical atrophy is the earliest immunotox flag—many xenobiotics kill these proliferating thymocytes before they ever reach the periphery (Casarett & Doull classic mechanism).
TCR & MHC Restriction – The “Lock & Key” Mechanic: Unlike B cells, TCR never sees free antigen. It binds peptide fragments loaded on MHC class II (CD4+ cells) or class I (CD8+ cells). Co-receptors (CD4 or CD8) stabilize the interaction. This MHC restriction explains why T cells are the “conductors” – they only activate when the right cell presents the right antigen.
CD4+ Helper Subsets – The Cytokine Directors:
Th1: IFN-γ, IL-2 → macrophage activation, cell-mediated immunity.
Th2: IL-4, IL-5, IL-13 → B-cell class switching, IgE, eosinophils (allergy).
Th17: IL-17 → neutrophil recruitment (autoimmunity).
Tfh: IL-21 → germinal center B-cell help (high-affinity antibodies).
These subsets drive the entire downstream response; loss of any = cascade failure.
CD8+ Cytotoxic T Cells – The Executioners: Recognize MHC-I on infected/tumor cells → release perforin (pores) + granzymes (induce apoptosis). Essential for viral clearance and tumor surveillance—exactly why chronic T-cell suppression (ICH S8 positive TDAR) predicts increased cancer/infection risk in carcinogenicity studies (TG 451/453).
Regulatory T Cells (Treg) – The Brakes: FoxP3+ CD4+ cells suppress over-reaction via IL-10, TGF-β, and contact inhibition. Failure of Treg function → autoimmunity (a separate ICH S8 concern). Many drugs inadvertently tip this balance.
Activation Cascade (Signal 1-2-3): Signal 1 = TCR-MHC, Signal 2 = co-stimulation (CD28-B7), Signal 3 = cytokines. Without all three → anergy or apoptosis. This is why calcineurin inhibitors (cyclosporine) or mTOR inhibitors (rapamycin) are potent T-cell suppressants and positive controls in ICH S8.

Basic Summary (3–5 sentences – simple version)
Hypersensitivity means the immune system over-reacts or reacts wrongly to something that is normally harmless (like pollen, nickel, or a drug). This over-reaction causes tissue damage and is one of the four big problems immunotoxicologists watch for (the other three are immunosuppression = too weak, immunostimulation = too strong in a bad way, and autoimmunity = attacking self). Doctors and regulators divide hypersensitivity into four types (I, II, III, IV) based on how fast it happens and which part of the adaptive immune system (the T cells or B cells we learned about) is driving it. OECD Test Guideline 497 gives modern, animal-light ways to test whether a chemical can cause skin allergy (skin sensitisation) by combining several lab tests instead of using many animals. In everyday repeat-dose toxicity studies (your OECD TG 407 and 408), if the pathologist sees extra inflammation or certain cell changes, it may be a clue that hypersensitivity testing with TG 497 is needed.

In-Depth Explanation (explained slowly, step by step)
What “Hypersensitivity” really means in plain language
After the first contact with a chemical (called sensitisation), the immune system “remembers” it. On the next contact (elicitation), the immune system attacks too strongly and damages normal tissue. This is the opposite of immunosuppression (which we saw with cyclophosphamide making the immune system too quiet). In Casarett & Doull and ICH S8, hypersensitivity is listed as one of the four main unwanted immune effects that regulators must evaluate for drugs and chemicals.
Type I – Immediate (fast) hypersensitivity – the “allergy” most people know
This happens within minutes. B cells (with help from a certain kind of T cell called Th2) make a special antibody called IgE. IgE sticks to mast cells (like alarm bells in the skin, lungs, gut). When the allergen comes back, it cross-links the IgE → mast cells explode and release histamine and other chemicals. Result: itching, hives, swelling, asthma, or even life-threatening anaphylaxis (e.g., peanut allergy or penicillin reaction). This uses the antibody functions we learned (especially the part where antibodies trigger other cells).
Type II – Antibody attacks cells
Antibodies (IgG or IgM) bind directly to something on the surface of a person’s own cells (often because a drug has stuck to the cell and made it look foreign). The antibodies then call in complement (the punch-hole system) or NK cells to destroy those cells. Example: some drugs cause red-blood-cell destruction leading to anemia. This again uses the antibody effector functions (complement activation and ADCC) we reviewed in Module 1.3.
Type III – Immune complexes get stuck
Antibodies and the foreign substance form clumps (immune complexes) that float in the blood and get trapped in small blood vessels, kidneys, or joints. Complement is activated, neutrophils rush in, and inflammation occurs. This can look like serum sickness (fever, rash, joint pain) after certain drugs or repeated injections of foreign proteins.
Type IV – Delayed (slow) hypersensitivity – the one that matters most for skin
This takes 24–72 hours to appear and does not use antibodies at all. It is driven entirely by T cells (the conductors we studied in Module 1.4).
First exposure (sensitisation): the chemical (often a small molecule called a hapten) binds to skin proteins. Skin cells send danger signals. Dendritic cells (the immune system’s messengers) carry the information to the lymph node and “train” T cells to remember it.
Second exposure (elicitation): memory T cells arrive, release chemical signals (cytokines), and call in macrophages. The result is red, itchy, swollen skin – classic allergic contact dermatitis (e.g., poison ivy, nickel in jewelry, or certain preservatives in cosmetics).
This is the main type tested by OECD TG 497.
OECD TG 497 – Modern way to test for skin allergy without many animals
Your uploaded TG497.pdf (2025 version) explains “Defined Approaches” (DA). Instead of putting the chemical on many mouse ears (old LLNA method), scientists now combine 3–4 quick lab tests that measure the early steps of the allergy process:
Does the chemical bind to proteins? (DPRA test)
Does it turn on stress signals in skin cells? (KeratinoSens test)
Does it activate dendritic-like cells? (h-CLAT test)
If 2 out of 3 tests are positive, the chemical is classified as a skin sensitiser. This follows an “Adverse Outcome Pathway” (AOP) – a chain of events from chemical contact all the way to the T-cell reaction we learned about. The guideline supports 3Rs (replace, reduce, refine animal use) and gives information for GHS classification (how to label the product).
How this connects back to Module 1 and repeat-dose studies
In your OECD TG 407 (28-day) or TG 408 (90-day) studies, if the pathologist sees extra eosinophils (a cell linked to Type I), mast-cell changes, or unexpected skin/lymph-node inflammation, it can be a signal of hypersensitivity. That may trigger TG 497 testing. ICH S8 (your uploaded guideline) says: look at all the data together (weight-of-evidence) before deciding if more tests are needed. Type IV uses the exact T-cell mechanisms (MHC presentation, CD4+ and CD8+ T cells) we already mastered. Types I–III use the B-cell antibody functions. Everything is connected.

These alarm messages leak out and tell the nearby immune cells: “Hey, pay attention — there is a problem in the skin that needs fixing.”
The alarm does two important jobs:

It wakes up the dendritic cells (the messenger cells that live in the skin).
It makes the dendritic cells pick up the hapten-protein combination and carry it to the lymph node.
In the lymph node, the dendritic cells “show” the hapten to naïve T cells and train them to become memory T cells that remember this specific chemical. (This is the same T-cell training we talked about in Module 1.4.)

Step 2: Second contact – Elicitation (the actual reaction)

The same hapten touches the skin again.
The memory T cells (now trained) rush to the spot.
They release their own signaling chemicals (cytokines).
These chemicals call in macrophages (the clean-up cells) and cause redness, swelling, and itching.
The reaction appears slowly — usually 24–72 hours later — which is why it is called “delayed.”
That is the whole Type IV process: skin cells sound the alarm → T cells get trained → on re-exposure the trained T cells cause the skin reaction.

Nickel in cheap earrings or belt buckles.

Basic Summary (3–5 sentences)
Immunosuppression means the immune system is weakened, so the body has less ability to fight infections or control cancer. It is one of the four major immunotoxic outcomes (along with hypersensitivity, immunostimulation, and autoimmunity) that regulators watch for in ICH S8. In standard repeat-dose studies like OECD TG 407 (28-day) and TG 408 (90-day), scientists look for simple clues such as lower thymus or spleen weight, fewer lymphocytes in blood, or lymphoid tissue shrinking under the microscope. If these clues appear, or if the drug is meant to affect the immune system, ICH S8 tells them to do extra functional tests like the TDAR assay (T-cell-dependent antibody response), which measures how well T cells help B cells make antibodies after vaccination. This tiered approach (screen first, test function only when needed) protects people while using fewer animals.

In-Depth Explanation (7 paragraphs – simple wording)
What immunosuppression really means in plain language
The immune system is like a security team. When it is suppressed, the team is smaller or slower, so germs or cancer cells can get through more easily. This can happen with many medicines (e.g., chemotherapy, transplant drugs) or chemicals. In toxicology we care because even small weakening can lead to more infections or tumors in real life. Casarett & Doull Ch. 12 calls this the most frequent immunotoxic effect seen in animal studies.
Why standard OECD TG 407 and TG 408 studies catch it first
These are the 28-day and 90-day repeat-dose studies you have uploaded. They are the first “screen” because they are done anyway for general safety. Scientists look at simple, cheap clues:
Thymus weight (especially the cortex where T cells mature) going down.
Spleen or lymph node shrinking.
Fewer white blood cells (lymphocytes) in blood counts.
Under the microscope, lymphoid tissue looks empty or atrophied.
These changes often appear before the animal gets obviously sick, so they are sensitive early warnings.
Why the thymus is the most sensitive organ
The thymus is full of rapidly dividing young T cells. Many drugs or chemicals kill these fast-growing cells first (remember cyclophosphamide from earlier modules). That is why a drop in thymus weight or cortical thinning in TG 407/408 is often the first red flag for immunosuppression. The spleen and lymph nodes can also shrink, but the thymus usually reacts earliest.
ICH S8 Weight-of-Evidence Decision Tree – the “what next?” rule
ICH S8 (your uploaded S8_Guideline_0.pdf) does not require extra tests on every chemical. Instead it uses a decision tree:
Look at all the data from TG 407/408 (weights, blood counts, histopathology, clinical signs).
Ask: Is there a signal? Is the drug designed to affect immunity? Is the patient population already vulnerable (babies, elderly)?
If yes → do additional functional studies.
This is called “weight-of-evidence” because it weighs everything together instead of relying on one test.
The TDAR Assay – the most important functional test
TDAR stands for T-cell-dependent antibody response.
Scientists give the animal a harmless vaccine (e.g., sheep red blood cells or KLH protein).
They measure how much antibody (IgM first, then IgG) the animal makes.
A good response needs both T-helper cells (to give signals) and B cells (to make the antibodies).
If the response is weak or absent, it proves functional immunosuppression even if the animal looks normal.
This test is very sensitive and is the gold standard when TG 407/408 shows signals.
Link to developmental studies (TG 443) and the four outcomes
Babies and fetuses have an even more sensitive immune system. OECD TG 443 (extended one-generation study) includes TDAR in the young animals because immunosuppression during development can have lifelong effects. Remember the four outcomes: immunosuppression is the “too weak” side, while hypersensitivity (Module 2) is the “over-reaction” side.

Module 3: Immunosuppression Mechanisms in Standard Toxicity Studies (OECD TG 407/408), the TDAR Assay, and ICH S8 Weight-of-Evidence Decision Tree

Basic Summary (3–5 sentences)
Immunosuppression happens when a xenobiotic (drug or chemical) weakens the immune system so it cannot fight infections or cancer cells as well as it should. The main mechanisms are: (1) direct killing of rapidly dividing lymphocytes (especially in the thymus), (2) blocking signals between immune cells (cytokine inhibition), (3) interfering with cell maturation or activation, and (4) stressing the whole system indirectly (e.g., through the stress hormone pathway). In OECD TG 407 (28-day) and TG 408 (90-day) studies, these mechanisms often show up first as lower thymus/spleen weight, fewer lymphocytes in blood, or empty-looking lymphoid tissue under the microscope. If these clues appear, ICH S8 triggers the TDAR assay (T-cell-dependent antibody response) to check real function. This tiered approach is the standard way to find immunosuppression early and decide human risk.

In-Depth Explanation (7 paragraphs – simple wording)
Direct killing of lymphocytes (most common mechanism)
Many chemicals and drugs damage the DNA or kill cells that are dividing quickly. The thymus cortex is packed with young, rapidly dividing T cells, so it is the first place to shrink. Bone marrow and spleen can also be affected. Cyclophosphamide (your classic example) is the textbook case — it cross-links DNA so the cells cannot copy themselves and die. In TG 407/408 studies you see a smaller thymus with a thin cortex under the microscope. This is direct cytotoxicity.
Blocking signals between immune cells (cytokine or receptor inhibition)
Immune cells talk to each other with chemical messages called cytokines. Some drugs stop these messages. For example, cyclosporine blocks the signal that tells T cells to make IL-2 (the growth factor they need). Without IL-2, T-helper cells cannot help B cells make antibodies, so the TDAR response collapses. This is a functional block rather than outright cell killing.
Interfering with cell maturation or selection
In the thymus, young T cells must go through positive and negative selection to learn what is “self” and what is dangerous. Some chemicals (like dioxins via the AhR receptor) disrupt this education process, so fewer good T cells leave the thymus. This leads to a smaller thymus and poor immune responses later. Developmental studies (TG 443) are especially sensitive to this because the fetal thymus is still forming.
Indirect stress-related immunosuppression
Severe stress from the chemical raises cortisol-like hormones in the animal. These hormones tell the thymus and spleen to shrink and send lymphocytes into hiding. This is why you sometimes see lymphoid depletion even when the chemical does not directly touch immune cells. It is usually reversible once the stress stops.
Effects on different immune cell types
T cells (especially helpers and those in the thymus) are often hit hardest.
B cells can be affected if their maturation or help from T cells is blocked.
Innate cells (macrophages, NK cells) are usually more resistant but can be suppressed at high doses.
This is why TDAR (which needs both T and B cooperation) is such a sensitive test — it catches problems that single-cell counts might miss.
How these mechanisms appear in your OECD studies
In TG 407 and TG 408 you look for: lower thymus/spleen weight, fewer lymphocytes in blood, empty-looking cortex in thymus, smaller white pulp in spleen, or reduced cellularity in bone marrow. These are the screening clues. If they are present (or the drug is designed to affect immunity), ICH S8 says move to functional testing like TDAR.
DABT Exam & Regulatory Synthesis
The exam loves questions that ask you to link mechanism → histopathology finding → ICH S8 decision → TDAR result → human risk. For example: a drug that kills dividing thymocytes will show thymic atrophy in TG 408 and a weak TDAR; this predicts higher infection risk in patients. Understanding the four mechanisms helps you predict what will be seen in studies and why regulators care.

Simple Explanation of Lymphoid Tissue and Its Role in Immune Cells
Lymphoid tissue is the “school and factory” system where immune cells are made, trained, and activated. It is divided into two groups:

Primary lymphoid tissue (the factories):
Bone marrow → makes all immune cells and finishes B-cell training.
Thymus → finishes T-cell training (this is the key organ we will focus on).
Secondary lymphoid tissue (the schools):
Spleen, lymph nodes, and Peyer’s patches in the gut.
Here, the finished T and B cells meet real threats (antigens) and get activated to fight.
In immunosuppression, the primary tissue (especially the thymus) is often damaged first, so fewer good immune cells are produced. This shows up in TG 407/408 as smaller thymus or spleen weight and empty-looking tissue under the microscope.

The Thymus and Positive/Negative Selection (the “quality-control” steps)
The thymus is a small organ in the chest that acts like a strict training camp for young T cells. Immature T cells arrive from the bone marrow and must pass two checks before they are allowed to leave and protect the body:

Positive selection (the “can you recognize self-MHC?” test)
Every T cell has a receptor (TCR) on its surface.
The thymus checks: “Can this TCR at least weakly recognize the body’s own MHC molecules (the ID tags on all cells)?”
If yes → the T cell survives and matures.
If no → it dies (because it would be useless in the body).
This happens mostly in the outer part of the thymus (the cortex).
Result: only useful T cells move forward.
Negative selection (the “are you dangerous to self?” test)
Now the surviving T cells are checked again: “Does your TCR strongly recognize the body’s own normal proteins (self-antigens)?”
If yes (it would attack the body) → the T cell is deleted (killed).
If no → the T cell is allowed to leave the thymus as a mature, safe T cell.
This happens in the inner part of the thymus (the medulla).

Basic Summary (3–5 sentences)
The TDAR assay (T-cell-dependent antibody response) is the most sensitive functional test for immunosuppression. It works by giving the animal a harmless vaccine and measuring how well T-helper cells help B cells make antibodies (IgM first, then IgG). A weak response proves the immune system is not working properly even if the animal looks normal. ICH S8 uses a weight-of-evidence decision tree that starts with the simple screening findings from OECD TG 407 and TG 408 (thymus/spleen weights, blood counts, histopathology) and decides whether extra tests like TDAR are needed. This tiered system protects people while using fewer animals and directly supports risk assessment for the “immunosuppression” outcome.

In-Depth Explanation (7 paragraphs – simple wording)
Why TDAR is the gold-standard functional test
Screening in TG 407/408 only shows that something might be wrong (e.g., small thymus). TDAR proves whether the immune system can actually do its job. It is called “T-cell-dependent” because it needs both T-helper cells (to give signals) and B cells (to make antibodies) working together — exactly the cooperation we learned in Module 1.
How the TDAR assay actually works (step by step)
On about day 21–28 of a repeat-dose study, the animal is injected with a harmless antigen (usually sheep red blood cells or KLH protein).
Five to seven days later, blood is taken and the amount of specific antibody (IgM first, then class-switched IgG) is measured by a simple lab test.
A strong response = lots of antibody.
A weak or absent response = immunosuppression.
This is done at the end of TG 407 or TG 408 when screening clues are present.
Why TDAR is so sensitive
It catches problems that blood counts or organ weights might miss. Even if the thymus is only mildly smaller, the T-helper cells may not be able to give the right signals to B cells. Because it tests the full chain (T-cell help + B-cell antibody production), it is more reliable than looking at one cell type alone.
What the results mean in real studies
Normal TDAR = immune function is okay.
Dose-related drop in antibody levels = clear immunosuppression.
Scientists also check reversibility (does the response come back after dosing stops?) because reversibility is important for human risk assessment.
The full ICH S8 Weight-of-Evidence Decision Tree (your uploaded S8_Guideline_0.pdf)
ICH S8 does not require TDAR on every chemical. It uses a simple flow chart:
Step 1: Look at all TG 407/408 data (thymus/spleen weights, lymphocyte counts, histopathology, clinical signs).
Step 2: Ask three questions: Is there a signal? Is the drug designed to affect immunity? Is the patient population already vulnerable?
Step 3: If any answer is yes → do additional functional studies (TDAR is usually the first choice).
Step 4: Weigh everything together (dose, reversibility, exposure margin) to decide human risk.
Link to developmental immunotoxicity (TG 443)
The same decision tree and TDAR are used in TG 443 for young animals because the developing immune system is even more sensitive. A weak TDAR in pups can mean lifelong increased infection risk.

Basic Summary (3–5 sentences)
Autoimmunity is when the immune system loses its “self-tolerance” brakes and starts attacking the body’s own normal, unchanged cells and tissues as if they were foreign. It is the fourth major immunotoxic outcome (along with immunosuppression, hypersensitivity, and immunostimulation) that regulators watch for in ICH S8. Unlike Type II hypersensitivity (where a hapten temporarily makes cells look guilty), autoimmunity continues even after the chemical is gone because the normal tolerance controls (especially Treg cells) have been broken. In standard OECD TG 407/408 studies it can appear as unexpected inflammation in organs (thyroid, joints, kidneys) or autoantibodies in blood. Developmental studies (TG 443) are extra sensitive because the fetal immune system is still learning self-tolerance.

In-Depth Explanation (7 paragraphs – simple wording)
What “self-tolerance” normally does
The immune system has built-in brakes so it does not attack the body’s own cells. These brakes are mainly the regulatory T cells (Treg) we learned about in Module 1.4 and the negative selection step in the thymus factory. When everything works, T and B cells ignore normal self-proteins and only attack real threats.
How autoimmunity starts (the loss of brakes)
Something breaks those brakes. The immune system then treats normal self-cells as enemies. Antibodies or T cells begin attacking the body’s own tissues. This is different from all four hypersensitivity types because no hapten or foreign substance is needed after the initial trigger.
Common ways chemicals/drugs can trigger autoimmunity
Molecular mimicry: the drug looks a little like a normal body protein, so the immune system gets confused and attacks the real protein too.
Bystander activation: the drug causes some tissue damage or inflammation, which accidentally wakes up the immune system and breaks tolerance.
Direct interference with Treg cells or negative selection in the thymus, so the brakes stop working.
These are more complex and nuanced than the direct killing or signal-blocking mechanisms we saw in immunosuppression.
How autoimmunity shows up in OECD TG 407/408 studies
You may see unexpected inflammation in organs (e.g., thyroiditis, glomerulonephritis in kidneys, or joint swelling) even though no obvious infection or hypersensitivity is present. Blood tests might show autoantibodies (antibodies against self-proteins). These findings are not as common as immunosuppression signals, but when they appear they are taken very seriously because they may not go away after dosing stops.
Link to developmental studies (TG 443)
The fetal and newborn immune system is still learning self-tolerance. A chemical that interferes with thymus selection or Treg development in the fetus can cause lifelong autoimmunity. That is why TG 443 includes extra checks for lymphoid organs and immune function in the young animals.
ICH S8 and the four immunotoxic outcomes
ICH S8 lists autoimmunity as one of the four things to evaluate in the weight-of-evidence decision tree. If screening studies show signs of self-attack (inflammation without obvious cause), scientists may do extra tests such as autoantibody panels or special histopathology to confirm it.
DABT Exam Relevance & Synthesis
The exam often asks you to distinguish autoimmunity from Type II hypersensitivity (temporary vs permanent attack) and from immunosuppression (too weak vs self-attack). You need to know the mechanisms (molecular mimicry, bystander activation, Treg failure) and why it is harder to predict and reverse than simple immunosuppression. This ties all four outcomes together: immunosuppression = too weak, hypersensitivity = over-reaction to foreign, autoimmunity = over-reaction to self, immunostimulation = too strong activation.

Basic Summary (3–5 sentences)
Lupus (Systemic Lupus Erythematosus or SLE) is a classic example of autoimmunity where the immune system attacks the body’s own normal cells and tissues because the self-tolerance brakes (especially Treg cells) have failed. In drug-induced lupus, a chemical or drug triggers the loss of tolerance through molecular mimicry or bystander activation, leading to autoantibodies and immune-complex damage that can persist even after the drug is stopped. This differs from Type II hypersensitivity (temporary attack on hapten-coated cells) and from immunosuppression (too weak). In-Depth Explanation (7 paragraphs – simple wording)
What lupus actually is in plain language
Lupus is the immune system treating the body’s own normal proteins (especially in the nucleus of cells) as enemies. B cells make autoantibodies (antibodies against self), and these autoantibodies form clumps with the self-proteins. The clumps get stuck in tissues and cause inflammation, exactly like the immune-complex mechanism we saw in Type III but aimed at self instead of foreign.
The two main kinds of lupus
Idiopathic (spontaneous) lupus: the brakes fail for unknown reasons.
Drug-induced lupus: a chemical or drug is the trigger. This is the type toxicologists care about most because it shows how a xenobiotic can cause autoimmunity. Common drugs that have caused it in the past include procainamide, hydralazine, and some older anti-seizure medicines.
How a drug can trigger lupus (the nuanced mechanisms)
Molecular mimicry: the drug or its metabolite looks a little like a normal body protein, so the immune system gets confused and starts attacking the real protein.
Bystander activation: the drug causes some cell stress or inflammation, which accidentally wakes up the immune system and breaks the normal tolerance controls (Treg cells or negative selection in the thymus factory).
These are more complex than the direct killing we see in immunosuppression or the temporary hapten glue in Type II.
What happens inside the body once tolerance is lost
Autoantibodies (especially anti-nuclear antibodies and anti-dsDNA) are produced. These bind to self-proteins and form immune complexes. The complexes deposit in kidneys (glomerulonephritis), skin (butterfly rash), joints (arthritis), or blood vessels. Complement is activated and neutrophils rush in, causing ongoing inflammation. Unlike Type II, the attack continues even after the drug is stopped because the brakes are permanently damaged.
How lupus shows up in your OECD toxicity studies
In TG 407 (28-day) or TG 408 (90-day) you might see unexpected kidney inflammation (glomerulonephritis with immune-complex deposits), skin changes, or swollen joints. Blood tests may show positive autoantibodies or low complement levels. In TG 443 developmental studies it is even more important because the fetal thymus is still learning self-tolerance, so early exposure can cause lifelong lupus-like disease in the offspring.
Link to ICH S8 and the four immunotoxic outcomes
ICH S8 lists autoimmunity as a separate outcome that must be considered in the weight-of-evidence decision tree. If screening studies show unexplained inflammation or autoantibody signals, scientists may do extra tests (autoantibody panels, special kidney histopathology) even if the usual immunosuppression screens are normal.

The exam often tests whether you can distinguish drug-induced lupus (autoimmunity) from Type II hypersensitivity (temporary) or immunosuppression (weak response). This example ties together all four outcomes: immunosuppression = too weak, hypersensitivity = over-reaction to foreign, autoimmunity = over-reaction to self, immunostimulation = too strong activation.

Basic Summary (3–5 sentences)
Immunostimulation is when the immune system is turned on too strongly or in the wrong way, causing harmful over-activation such as excessive inflammation, cytokine release, or unwanted antibody production. It is the fourth major immunotoxic outcome (along with immunosuppression = too weak, hypersensitivity = over-reaction to foreign, and autoimmunity = over-reaction to self). In standard OECD TG 407 (28-day) and TG 408 (90-day) studies it often shows up as the opposite of immunosuppression: bigger thymus or spleen weights, extra lymphoid tissue (hyperplasia), higher white blood cell counts, or signs of cytokine storm. ICH S8 includes immunostimulation in the same weight-of-evidence decision tree as the other three outcomes, and it can sometimes overlap with hypersensitivity or lead to autoimmunity. Developmental studies (TG 443) are especially important because an over-active immune system in a fetus or pup can cause lasting problems.

In-Depth Explanation (7 paragraphs – simple wording)
What immunostimulation really means in plain language
The immune system is like a security team that suddenly gets too excited and starts firing at everything — even things that are not real threats. This can cause fever, swelling, tissue damage, or a “cytokine storm” (too many signaling chemicals flooding the body). It is the opposite of immunosuppression, where the team is too quiet.
Main ways chemicals or drugs cause immunostimulation
Direct stimulation of immune cells (some drugs act like switches that turn cells on).
Massive release of cytokines (the chemical messages) from many cells at once.
Acting like an adjuvant (something that boosts immune responses, like in vaccines but in a harmful way).
Interfering with the normal “off” switches (Treg brakes we learned about).
How it shows up in your OECD TG 407 and TG 408 studies
The opposite clues from immunosuppression: bigger thymus or spleen (hyperplasia = extra cells), more lymphocytes in blood, extra white pulp in the spleen under the microscope, or signs of widespread inflammation. These are the screening signals that tell scientists “the immune system is being pushed too hard.”
Cytokine storm – the most dramatic form
Certain drugs (especially some monoclonal antibodies) can make many immune cells release cytokines all at once. This causes high fever, low blood pressure, and organ damage. It is a severe type of immunostimulation and is why some new drugs need special safety testing before they are given to people.
Link to the other three outcomes
Immunostimulation can sometimes look like hypersensitivity (especially Type I or III) or even trigger autoimmunity if the over-activation breaks self-tolerance. That is why ICH S8 looks at the whole picture together in the decision tree — one study finding can point to more than one problem.
Special concern in developmental studies (TG 443)
A fetus or newborn has an immune system that is still learning balance. Over-stimulation at the wrong time can cause lifelong allergies, autoimmunity, or other immune problems. TG 443 therefore checks for extra lymphoid hyperplasia or cytokine changes in the young animals.

Basic Summary (3–5 sentences)
Hematotoxicology is the study of how xenobiotics damage blood cells or the organs that make them (bone marrow, spleen). The main problems are anemia (low red cells), leukopenia (low white cells), thrombocytopenia (low platelets), and bone-marrow suppression. These effects often appear in OECD TG 407/408 studies as changes in complete blood count (CBC), lower organ weights, or abnormal histopathology of bone marrow and spleen. Like immunotoxicity, it is evaluated using a tiered approach: first screen with routine hematology, then (if needed) bone-marrow smears or functional tests. Understanding hematotoxicity is critical for the DABT because it links directly to the immunosuppression mechanisms we just covered and to real-world clinical risks (bleeding, infection, fatigue).

In-Depth Explanation (7 paragraphs)
Why blood and bone marrow are so vulnerable
Bone marrow is the ultimate factory for all blood cells. It contains rapidly dividing stem cells that produce red cells, white cells, and platelets every day. Anything that damages DNA, blocks cell division, or stresses the marrow shows up quickly in blood counts. This is why hematotoxicity is often one of the earliest and most sensitive findings in repeat-dose studies.
The three major types of blood toxicity
Anemia → too few red blood cells or hemoglobin (can be from marrow suppression or direct red-cell destruction).
Leukopenia / neutropenia → too few white blood cells (especially neutrophils), increasing infection risk.
Thrombocytopenia → too few platelets, increasing bleeding risk.
These can occur alone or together (pancytopenia = all three).
How xenobiotics cause these effects
Direct marrow toxicity (most common): drugs like cyclophosphamide kill dividing stem cells or erythroid precursors.
Peripheral destruction: some chemicals destroy mature red cells or platelets in the blood (immune-mediated or direct lysis).
Indirect effects: stress hormones or nutritional interference can also suppress marrow output.
What you see in OECD TG 407 and TG 408
Routine hematology (CBC) is part of every study: lower hemoglobin, lower WBC count, lower platelets, or abnormal cell morphology. Histopathology shows hypocellular bone marrow or changes in spleen red/white pulp. These are the first-tier screening clues, just like the lymphoid changes we saw in immunotoxicity.
When extra testing is needed
If CBC or histopathology shows clear changes, scientists may do bone-marrow smears (to count cell lines), reticulocyte count (young red cells), or flow cytometry to see which lineage is hit. This is similar to the ICH S8 decision tree we used for immunosuppression.
Link back to immunotoxicology
Bone marrow damage often causes both hematotoxicity and immunosuppression at the same time (fewer red cells + fewer lymphocytes). That is why the two topics are taught together on the DABT. The factory model still applies: damage the marrow factory and both blood and immune deployment suffer.
DABT Exam Relevance
Expect questions on interpreting CBC changes, linking marrow findings to specific mechanisms (e.g., “megaloblastic anemia from folate antagonism”), and distinguishing direct marrow toxicity from peripheral destruction.
