Section 20 of Beyond Good and Evil (Part One: On the Prejudices of Philosophers) reads as follows in the standard Walter Kaufmann translation:

“That the separate philosophical ideas are not anything arbitrary or randomly growing, but grow up in connection and relationship with each other, that, however suddenly and arbitrarily they seem to appear in the history of thought, they nevertheless belong just as much to a system as the collective members of the fauna of a Continent—is betrayed in the end by the circumstance: that they always grow up in the closest association with each other, under the same atmosphere and on the same soil. He is always the expression of the prevailing ‘prejudices’ of his time, and cannot free himself from them.”

Plain-English Breakdown
Nietzsche is making a genealogical-historical claim about how philosophy actually works:

Ideas are not free-floating or accidental.
A new philosophical concept doesn’t just pop into existence like a random mutation. It emerges organically from a whole cluster of earlier ideas, assumptions, and cultural conditions — exactly the way animal species on a continent evolve together in an ecosystem. You can’t understand one without the others.
They share the same “atmosphere and soil.”
“Atmosphere” = the intellectual climate, dominant values, religious background, political situation, scientific knowledge, and language of the era.
“Soil” = the deeper cultural and psychological ground from which those ideas grow.
Every philosopher breathes the same air and is rooted in the same ground as his contemporaries.
The proper study of the “history of ideas” reveals this determinism.
When you trace ideas carefully, you see they form systems, not isolated flashes of genius. The historian’s job is to expose how tightly each thinker is bound to his moment.
The philosopher is never “free.”
No matter how loudly he claims to speak from pure reason or timeless truth, he remains a child of his time. He cannot leap out of his historical skin. What he presents as eternal verities are almost always the prejudices (unexamined assumptions, dogmas, blind spots) of his age dressed up in philosophical language.
What Nietzsche Is Really Doing Here
This short paragraph is a methodological bombshell in the middle of Part One. Nietzsche has already spent the previous sections dismantling the pretensions of philosophers (their faith in opposites, their dogmatism, their “will to truth,” their misuse of language, their misunderstanding of the will). Now he delivers the historical punchline:

Philosophy has never been free thought. It has always been situated thought.

This is Nietzsche’s early version of what later thinkers (Foucault, Derrida, the Frankfurt School, etc.) would call historicism or situated knowledge. He is saying:

There is no “view from nowhere.”
Every philosopher is wearing the invisible spectacles of his century.
The “great systems” of Plato, Descartes, Kant, Hegel, etc., are not monuments to pure reason but symptoms of the prejudices that dominated their epochs.
Why This Matters in Beyond Good and Evil
Part One’s whole project is to expose the prejudices of philosophers so that a new, more honest kind of philosopher (the “free spirit” Nietzsche is trying to create) can emerge. Section 20 is crucial because it shows why those prejudices are so stubborn: they are not personal failings but historical necessities. You cannot simply “think your way out” of your time; you can only become more conscious of the forces shaping you.

This prepares the ground for Nietzsche’s later concepts:

Perspectivism (there is no absolute truth, only interpretations from particular standpoints)
Genealogy (tracing ideas back to the power relations and psychological needs that produced them)
The will to power as the deeper drive behind all philosophical systems

Every academic discipline today is shaped by its “atmosphere” (neoliberal universities, identity politics, climate anxiety, AI hype, etc.).

Bottom line: Nietzsche is telling us that philosophy is never above history; it is always inside it. The honest philosopher’s first task is therefore not to discover eternal truths, but to diagnose the prejudices of his own age — including his own. That is the only way any genuine intellectual freedom becomes possible.

Instead of:

“It wills this”
or
“Something in me is willing this”

The “I” is not the cause of the willing. It is a grammatical habit — language forces us to put a subject in front of every verb. Nietzsche calls the “I” a synthetic concept, a useful fiction we’ve come to believe is a real thing.

This is the deepest part of §19 (the long section you’re referring to). Nietzsche says willing is never a simple, unified act. It always contains two simultaneous processes that we normally confuse:

A. The Affect of Commanding
When you “will” something strongly, you feel:

A sense of superiority and power (“I am free — this must happen”)
Focused attention
The inner certainty that you will be obeyed
This is the commanding part. It feels like the “I” is sovereign.

B. The Affect of Obeying
At the same time, other parts of you must submit and carry out the command:

Muscular tension
The actual execution of the action
All the “under-drives” or “under-souls” that do the real work
Nietzsche’s key insight:

“In all willing it is absolutely a question of commanding and obeying…”

We are both the commander and the obey-er at the same moment. The body (and the psyche) is a social structure — a little commonwealth made up of many drives, some of which command and others that obey.

4. Why We Get Confused (The Melding You Mentioned)
This is the psychological trick Nietzsche wants to expose:

Because the commanding part feels so powerful and superior, we identify with it. We say “I did this” and take full credit. We ignore or forget that the obeying parts had to be forced or persuaded to go along.

The result is the illusion we call “free will”:

We feel the commanding affect (“I am free!”)
We feel the successful execution (“It happened!”)
We mash them together and conclude: “My sovereign I caused this action freely.”
Nietzsche says this is a double error:

We treat the “I” as a real substance instead of a grammatical fiction.
We treat the feeling of willing as proof that the will is free and unified, when in reality it is a complex of commanding + obeying that only feels unified.

Imagine you decide to go for a run even though you’re tired.

The commanding part says: “We’re going. Now.”

1. Kant’s “Proud Table of Categories”

In his Critique of Pure Reason (1781), Kant claimed to have discovered the 12 fundamental categories of the human understanding — things like causality, substance, unity, plurality, necessity, etc. These were supposedly the a priori structures that the mind imposes on all experience.

Kant presented this as a monumental, almost heroic achievement — the “Copernican revolution” in philosophy. He was very proud of it.

Nietzsche finds this childish and pretentious. He sees Kant as a dry, pedantic professor who thought he had uncovered the deepest secrets of the mind, when in reality he was just building an elaborate intellectual scaffolding that later philosophers would play with like toys.

2. The “Adolescent Movement” in German Philosophy

After Kant, German philosophy exploded into German Idealism (Fichte, Schelling, Hegel, and their followers). These thinkers took Kant’s categories and turned them into vast, systematic metaphysical constructions.

Nietzsche calls this phase adolescent because:

It was full of self-importance and grandiosity.
Young German philosophers felt they were participating in something world-historical and profound.
They used incredibly heavy, technical language and built enormous systems that claimed to explain everything.
In Nietzsche’s eyes, it was all a bit ridiculous — like teenagers discovering big words and feeling very clever.
He says it’s amusing to watch how seriously they took themselves.

3. The Contrast with German Romanticism

Before the Kantian critical turn really took over, there was the German Romantic movement (roughly 1790s–1810s) — the era of Novalis, Friedrich Schlegel, Tieck, Wackenroder, and the young Schelling.

This period was:

Artistic, passionate, intuitive, and mystical.
Focused on feeling, genius, nature, the infinite, irony, and the power of art.
Anti-systematic and anti-academic in spirit.
Nietzsche has a certain sympathy for this romantic spirit (he sees it as more vital and life-affirming than what came after). But he says that Kant’s critical philosophy killed it.

Once Kant’s followers turned philosophy into a technical, scholastic, category-based system, the romantic fire went out. German thought became dry, heavy, professorial, and ultimately decadent.

What Nietzsche Means When He Says “It Ends”
He means that the romantic era of German philosophy came to an end with the rise of Kantian criticism and German Idealism. The creative, artistic, passionate spirit was replaced by a new kind of academic dogmatism — one that pretended to be “critical” but was actually just as rigid and life-denying as the old metaphysics it replaced.

In Nietzsche’s view:

Romanticism had life in it.
Kantianism and its aftermath had only concepts — and very dry ones at that.

Grammar: Subject-predicate language makes us believe in stable substances and agents.

1. Atomism: “Unlife” in Both Science and Philosophy
Nietzsche attacks atomism in two forms:

Scientific atomism (the idea in physics that reality is made of indivisible, solid little particles called atoms).
Psychological/philosophical atomism (the idea that the soul or the “I” is a simple, indivisible substance — the “soul-atom”).
He calls both versions life-denying (“unlife”).

Why he hates scientific atomism (§12)
In §12 Nietzsche writes that modern physics still clings to the old atomistic prejudice:

“As for the materialistic atomism… it is one of the best-refuted theories there are… But it is not for that reason that we must get rid of it. We must still reckon with the fact that it has a long and honorable history behind it… and that it continues to live on in hidden ways.”

He argues that atomism is a simplification that makes reality seem more stable and mechanical than it actually is. Reality is not made of little solid building blocks. It is a dynamic play of forces, relations, and tensions. Treating it as made of atoms turns living, changing processes into something dead and static.

This is what he means by “unlife”: it drains the world of its vitality, its will to power, and its constant becoming. It replaces life with a machine.

Why he also attacks the “atomism of the soul” (§17)
In §17 he extends the same critique to psychology and metaphysics:

“The belief in the ‘soul’ as a substance, as an ‘entity,’ as something that ‘thinks,’ ‘wills,’ ‘feels’… is a prejudice of the ‘grammatical’ sort.”

Just as physics wrongly assumes indivisible atoms, philosophers wrongly assume an indivisible “I” or soul-substance. Both are life-denying simplifications. They turn the rich, conflicting, multiple nature of human drives into a single, static thing.

In short: Atomism (whether physical or psychological) is “unlife” because it replaces the dynamic, power-driven, plural reality of life with something static, indivisible, and mechanical. Nietzsche wants us to think in terms of forces, drives, and relations instead of solid little units.

2. Nietzsche’s (Temporary) Praise for Plato (§14)
You’re right — this is one of the more surprising moments in Part One.

In §14, right after attacking modern physics for worshipping the senses, Nietzsche writes:

“One should recall, on the other hand, the ages when the opposite was true: when the ‘eyes’ and ‘fingers’ were the most feared things, and when one wanted to get away from ‘the senses’ and from every ‘empirical’ interpretation, as quickly as possible, in order to reach ‘the true world,’ the world of the ‘idea’ or of the ‘spirit’ or of ‘God.’”

He is saying that Plato (and the Platonic tradition) had a nobler instinct than modern empirical scientists. Plato was suspicious of the senses and wanted to transcend them toward a higher, more spiritual world. This showed a greater spiritual rank and a deeper dissatisfaction with mere appearance.

By contrast, modern science (and the plebeian taste of the modern age) worships the senses and treats what can be seen, touched, and measured as ultimate reality. Nietzsche calls this a decline in taste and spirit.

The Irony (Which Nietzsche Knows Perfectly Well)
Of course, Nietzsche spends much of the rest of the book (and especially §§2–4 and later parts) attacking Plato for creating the very dualism of “true world” vs. “apparent world” that he here seems to admire.

Nietzsche is making a historical and psychological point, not endorsing Plato’s metaphysics:

Plato’s instinct to go beyond the senses was nobler than the modern scientific instinct to worship them.
But Plato’s solution (the world of Ideas) was still a metaphysical error — just a higher-quality error than the crude materialism of modern science.
In other words: Plato was wrong, but he was wrong in a grand and aristocratic way. Modern science is wrong in a petty, plebeian, and life-denying way.

This fits Nietzsche’s broader theme: he often respects the rank and will to power behind an idea more than the idea itself.

Both points reinforce his larger attack on all previous ways of thinking (whether idealist or materialist) as still trapped in prejudices.

Summary of Costin Alamariu’s argument in Selective Breeding and the Birth of Philosophy

Alamariu’s central thesis is that philosophy originates in an aristocratic awareness of the distinction between nomos (convention, custom, positive law, tradition) and phusis (nature, heredity, biological stock, breeding). This is the opening section of Part Five (“On the Natural History of Morals”). Nietzsche uses it to set the agenda for the entire part by criticizing the current state of moral philosophy and clarifying what a genuine “science of morals” would require.

Main points of the section

Section 209 – Historical Context on Frederick the Great

In Section 209, Nietzsche distinguishes between two forms of skepticism. He criticizes a lighter, more ironic, and somewhat superficial skepticism (which he associates with French intellectual culture), and contrasts it with what he calls a more masculine and more German form of skepticism.

The key passage reads:

“There is a more manly and more German form of skepticism… This is the skepticism of the bold soldier who has seen much, who has been in many lands, who has grown tired of superficial things, who no longer believes in ‘truth’ or in ‘virtue’ or in ‘happiness’ or in ‘God’ — but who has nevertheless retained a certain cheerfulness and even a certain audacity in his attitude toward life.”

Nietzsche explicitly links this stronger, harder form of skepticism to Frederick the Great (Frederick II of Prussia).

Historical Context: Frederick the Great (1712–1786)
Frederick II, known as Frederick the Great, was King of Prussia from 1740 until his death. He is one of the most significant figures in Prussian and German history for several reasons that are directly relevant to Nietzsche’s use of him in this section:

Military realism and power politics: Frederick was a highly successful and aggressive military leader. He waged multiple wars (notably the War of the Austrian Succession and the Seven Years’ War) to expand Prussian territory and power. He ruled with cold strategic calculation rather than moral or religious idealism. This gave him a reputation for hard realism and a lack of sentimental illusions about human nature or politics.
Enlightenment influence combined with hardness: Frederick was deeply influenced by French Enlightenment thought (he corresponded extensively with Voltaire and wrote philosophical works himself). However, unlike many Enlightenment thinkers, he did not combine this intellectual refinement with moral optimism or humanitarian sentiment. He remained a pragmatic, often cynical ruler who viewed religion and morality largely as tools of statecraft rather than as binding truths.
Personal skepticism: Frederick was notably irreligious and skeptical toward traditional Christian doctrine. He did not believe in divine providence in any serious way and treated religious institutions primarily as instruments for maintaining social order. This combination — intellectual sophistication + practical ruthlessness + religious skepticism — made him, in Nietzsche’s eyes, a model of a stronger, more “masculine” form of doubt.
Cultural contrast: In the 19th century, many German intellectuals looked back on Frederick as a symbol of a harder, more aristocratic, and more commanding Prussian spirit, in contrast to what Nietzsche and others saw as the softer, more moralistic, and more “herd-like” tendencies of later German culture (especially after the Napoleonic Wars and the rise of romantic nationalism).
How Nietzsche Uses Frederick in §209
Nietzsche presents Frederick the Great as an example of someone who developed a robust, experience-hardened skepticism. This is not the light, witty, or ironic skepticism of a salon intellectual, but the skepticism of a man who has commanded armies, governed a state, seen war and politics at close range, and therefore no longer takes abstract ideals (truth, virtue, happiness, God) very seriously — while still maintaining a certain cheerfulness and strength in the face of that recognition.

In Nietzsche’s view, this “more masculine German skepticism” is superior to weaker, more sentimental, or more moralistic forms of doubt because it is rooted in real experience of power, conflict, and command rather than in mere intellectual play or moral disappointment.

Summary
Nietzsche invokes Frederick the Great in §209 as a historical embodiment of a harder, more aristocratic, and more realistic form of skepticism — one born from the combination of military and political experience with intellectual refinement, rather than from moral idealism or philosophical abstraction. This stands in contrast to what Nietzsche sees as the softer, more “feminine,” or more superficial skepticism that he associates with French culture and with certain strands of 19th-century German thought.

Commanders and legislators

Mastery over drives and instincts

Section 217 – Clear Explanation (Ian Johnston translation)

This section is Nietzsche describing the ideal future attitude toward morality itself — specifically, what a higher, more refined type of person will eventually feel toward traditional moral demands.

Core Meaning
Nietzsche says that something “even higher and more sublime” is now possible:

We are learning to despise morality at the very moment when we love best — but we do this unconsciously, without drama, without moralistic fuss or “virtuous formulas.”

In other words:

A more advanced spirit no longer takes morality with deadly seriousness.
He can still appreciate its beauty, its historical role, and even feel affection for it (“when we love best”).
But at the same time, he has moved beyond it. He looks at it with a quiet, almost affectionate contempt — the way a grown adult might look back at the rules he followed as a child.
He compares this new attitude to how earlier generations (his “fathers”) finally outgrew religion: they stopped taking it seriously as a literal truth and began to find its pomp and solemnity distasteful. In the same way, the future “good European” will outgrow morality itself.

Nietzsche calls this new feeling:

“the music in our conscience”
“the dance in our spirit”
It makes all the old puritanical sermons, moral lectures, and bourgeois respectability sound ridiculous and out of tune.

Key Idea in Simple Terms
Nietzsche is saying:
The highest spiritual state is not to angrily reject morality or to rebel against it like a teenager.
It is to have outgrown it so thoroughly that you can look at it with a mixture of love and quiet contempt — the way one might look at a beautiful but childish game you no longer need to play.

This is part of his larger vision for the “good European” and the philosophers of the future: people who have moved beyond good and evil, not by hating morality, but by having transcended it with lightness and refinement.

Section 218 – Clear Explanation

This is one of Nietzsche’s sharper, more cutting observations about modern psychology and human behavior.

What the section means
Nietzsche is warning about a particular type of person: those who pride themselves on their moral tact and refinement — people who are very sensitive to moral distinctions and like to show off their subtlety in judging right and wrong.

His warning is this:

Be careful of them.
They never forgive you if they ever make a mistake in front of you — or even against you.
In such cases, they instinctively turn to slander and damage you, even if they still call you their “friend.”

In short: people who make a big show of their moral refinement and psychological subtlety are often the most dangerous when their ego is bruised. Their “refinement” is fragile. When it fails (when they look foolish or are proven wrong), they don’t respond with humility — they respond with resentment and covert attack.

Nietzsche ends with a bitter, ironic blessing:

“Blessed are the forgetful, for they are ‘done’ with their stupidities as well.”

This means: the only people who are safe from this kind of moralistic revenge are those who can quickly forget their own mistakes and move on. The refined moral psychologist, by contrast, cannot forget — and will hold it against you.

The broader point
This section continues Nietzsche’s critique of modern “psychologists” (especially in France) who take pleasure in exposing bourgeois stupidity and moral hypocrisy. However, Nietzsche suggests that these very psychologists are often still trapped in the same moral game. Their subtlety is not true strength — it is a refined form of vanity and ressentiment that becomes vicious the moment their own image is threatened.

In simpler terms
Section 218 is Nietzsche saying:
Beware of people who constantly advertise their moral sensitivity and psychological insight. They are often the quickest to turn nasty and vindictive the moment they feel exposed or inferior.
