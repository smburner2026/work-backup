# Deep Dive: Mercury Toxicity – Forms, Toxicokinetics, Mechanisms, Biomarkers & Chelation

**Date:** 2026-05-18  
**Domain:** IV. Applied Toxicology – Metals & Metalloids (Task areas: biomonitoring, susceptible subpopulations, treatment)  
**Prior Gap:** User began with minimal differentiation between elemental Hg⁰, inorganic Hg²⁺, and methylmercury. Key unknowns included transporter-mediated entry, thiol-binding chemistry, form-specific distribution and excretion routes, biomarker selection, and why certain chelators succeed or fail.

**Corrected Mental Model**
- Mercury toxicity is dominated by chemical form rather than total mercury concentration.
- All forms ultimately exert toxicity via soft-acid affinity for biological thiols (cysteine –SH groups), but the route each form takes to reach those thiols differs dramatically.
- Selenium can sequester mercury as insoluble HgSe, providing partial protection in some fish species, but this interaction is ratio-dependent and does not eliminate regulatory concern.

**Key Mechanisms / Definitions**
- **HSAB theory & thiol chemistry**: Hg²⁺ is a soft Lewis acid that forms extremely stable bonds with soft bases (thiol –SH). This is the molecular basis of “sulfhydryl poisoning.”
- **Transporter mimicry**: Methylmercury–cysteine complex structurally mimics methionine and uses the LAT1 amino-acid transporter to cross the BBB and placenta.
- **Form-specific distribution**:
  - Elemental Hg⁰: lipophilic vapor → rapid lung absorption → crosses BBB → intracellular oxidation to Hg²⁺.
  - Inorganic Hg²⁺: charged, binds thiols rapidly → poor BBB penetration → concentrates in kidney.
  - Methylmercury: efficient BBB/placental transfer via LAT1 → long-term bioaccumulation in neural tissue.
- **Toxicity pathways**: Direct enzyme inhibition (glutathione reductase, thioredoxin reductase), ROS amplification after glutathione depletion, microtubule disruption (developmental neurotoxicity).
- **Biomarkers**:
  - Urine Hg: best for elemental/inorganic (renal excretion).
  - Hair Hg: best for methylmercury (chronological record via hair growth).
  - Blood Hg: reflects recent methylmercury or mixed exposure.
- **Chelation logic**: Only dithiol soft-base chelators (DMSA, DMPS, BAL) compete effectively with biological thiols. EDTA (hard base) is ineffective for mercury and is a common exam distractor.

**Integration Points**
- Connects to lead toxicity deep dive via shared thiol-binding and chelation principles, but contrasts sharply in distribution and biomarker choice.
- Links to risk assessment (Domain III) through selenium–mercury interaction modifying exposure assessment and susceptible subpopulations (pregnant women, children).

**Answer Key for Retention Questions** (for future drill use only)

1. Very little methylmercury is excreted in urine until demethylation occurs; most remains bound in blood and tissues.
2. DMSA (succimer) – oral, well-tolerated dithiol. EDTA is a hard-base chelator that does not bind soft-acid Hg²⁺ effectively.
3. Formation of a methylmercury–cysteine complex that mimics methionine and uses the LAT1 transporter.
4. Insoluble mercury selenide (HgSe).
5. Urine (after oxidation to Hg²⁺ and renal handling).
6. Efficient placental transfer and long-term retention in developing neural tissue via amino-acid mimicry.
7. Better safety profile, oral route, and lower toxicity compared with BAL (IM, more adverse effects).
