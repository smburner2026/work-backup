# Deep Dive: Metals Toxicology — Foundations

**Date:** 2026-05-14
**Domain:** II (Mechanistic Toxicology) + IV (Applied Toxicology) — metals toxicity mechanisms, chelation therapy
**Prior Gap:** Abud was treating metals like organic xenobiotics (Phase I/II elimination model). No familiarity with Lewis acid-base theory, thiol chemistry, or the kinetic basis of chelator specificity. Clinical reasoning on metals was strong; regulatory thresholds and chelator pairings were weak.

**Corrected Mental Model:**
- Metals are indestructible elements — they cannot be metabolized to less toxic forms. Only three removal strategies exist: enhance excretion (chelators), sequester inertly (metallothionein, inclusion bodies), stop exposure.
- Metal entry/exit is transporter-mediated, not by passive diffusion. Promiscuous transporters for essential metals provide access to toxic mimics.
- Chelator selection is governed by HSAB thermodynamics + kinetic access + toxicity profile. Not arbitrary pairings — the logic predicts the matrix.

**Key Mechanisms / Definitions:**
- **HSAB:** Hard Lewis acids (Ca²⁺, Fe³⁺) bind hard Lewis bases (carboxylate –COO⁻). Soft Lewis acids (Hg²⁺, As³⁺) bind soft Lewis bases (thiol –SH). Rule: hard likes hard, soft likes soft.
- **Thiol (–SH):** Sulfur analog of –OH; cysteine provides the biological thiol. Soft metals attack enzyme –SH groups.
- **Four toxicity mechanisms:** (a) molecular mimicry, (b) ROS via redox cycling (Fenton/Haber-Weiss), (c) enzyme inhibition via sulfhydryl binding, (d) aberrant gene expression.
- **Chelator matrix:** Dithiols (–SH ×2) for soft metals (As, Hg): BAL (IM), DMSA (oral, first-line pediatric Pb). Hard chelators for hard metals: EDTA (Pb, Cd), deferoxamine (Fe).
- **Chromium exception:** Cr(VI) enters cells → reduced to Cr(III) → trapped. Toxicity from reduction process. No chelator works; treatment is ascorbic acid to accelerate extracellular reduction.

**Integration Points:**
- Connects to **Risk Assessment** via regulatory thresholds (OSHA blood lead: maintain <40 µg/dL adult occupational vs CDC pediatric reference: 5 µg/dL).
- Connects to **Toxicokinetics/ADME** via transporter-mediated entry, protein binding (MT, albumin), and the kinetic trapping concept.

**Retention Drill Questions:**

1. Q: Why can't EDTA (carboxylate-based, hard base) effectively chelate mercury?
   A: Hg²⁺ is a soft Lewis acid. EDTA's oxygen/nitrogen donors are hard/intermediate bases. The hard-soft mismatch means EDTA cannot outcompete biological sulfur ligands for Hg²⁺.

2. Q: Why is DMSA preferred over BAL for pediatric lead poisoning?
   A: Both are dithiol chelators (soft bases). DMSA is oral (BAL requires painful IM injection), has a wider safety margin, and does not significantly chelate essential metals. Practicality and safety, not binding chemistry.

3. Q: OSHA requires maintenance of blood lead levels below what threshold in adult workers?
   A: 40 µg/dL. Removal from exposure at ≥50 µg/dL. Common trap: confusing with CDC pediatric concern level (5–10 µg/dL).

4. Q: A worker uses cobalt-tungsten drill bits and develops chronic respiratory distress. Most likely diagnosis?
   A: Hard metal disease due to cobalt exposure. (Abud's hit — clinical reasoning is strong here.)

5. Q: Why doesn't chromium poisoning respond to chelation therapy?
   A: Cr(VI) enters cells → reduced to Cr(III). Cr(III) forms kinetically trapped octahedral complexes that cannot exit cells. Toxicity occurs during the reduction process. Clinical approach: ascorbic acid to reduce Cr(VI) extracellularly.

6. Q: Which chelator-metal pair is ineffective: dimercaprol-arsenic, dimercaprol-mercury, EDTA-lead, EDTA-mercury, penicillamine-copper?
   A: EDTA-mercury. EDTA is a hard-base chelator; mercury is a soft acid. Hard-soft mismatch.

7. Q: Methylmercury enters the brain by what transport mechanism?
   A: Molecular mimicry — the MeHg-cysteine conjugate is transported by amino acid transporters (looks like methionine).

8. Q: Cadmium's exceptionally long half-life in humans is approximately:
   A: 26 years. Cd induces metallothionein, which binds it in liver and kidney; only ~0.001% of body burden is excreted per day.
