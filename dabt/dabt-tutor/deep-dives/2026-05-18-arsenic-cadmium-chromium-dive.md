## Arsenic — Complete

**Two oxidation states, two mechanisms:**
- **Trivalent As³⁺ (arsenite):** attacks thiols (–SH in cysteine) → inhibits enzyme active sites, signalling, apoptosis
- **Pentavalent As⁵⁺ (arsenate):** mimics phosphate → short-circuits oxidative phosphorylation
- Entry via **aquaglyceroporins 7 & 9** (water/glycerol channels) → distributes with volume of distribution of water → systemic scattergun

**Toxicokinetics:**
- 80–90% GI absorption. Half-life ~10 h (inorg) / ~30 h (methylated). 50–80% excreted in urine within 3 days.
- **Methylation pathway:** As⁵⁺ → As³⁺ (arsenate reductase) → MMA → DMA (AS3MT + SAM). Methylation enhances excretion but **intermediate trivalent methylated species (MMA³⁺, DMA³⁺) are more reactive than arsenite.**

**Chelation logic:** Acute damage window is short and aggressive. DMSA (oral, safer) for peripheral toxicity. BAL (IM, crosses BBB) for CNS involvement / encephalopathy. Dithiols outcompete tissue thiols for As binding.

**Biomarkers (time-window model):**
| Matrix | Window | Detail |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Blood/urine | Current (h–d) | Half-life ~10h. Urinary As captures 50–80% of dose in 3 days. |
| Fingernails (Mees' lines) | ~6 wks–2 mo | Transverse white bands. Retrospective clock. |
| Hair | Chronic/months | Binds keratin. Forensic tool. |
| **Arsenobetaine trap** | — | Seafood-derived, excreted unchanged (up to 1,700 µg/L), nontoxic. |

**Clinical syndromes:**
- **Acute (70–180 mg fatal):** GI irritation, sensory peripheral neuropathy (Wallerian degeneration, 1–2 wk latency), anemia/leukopenia, torsade de pointes (K⁺ channel block → QTc prolongation), encephalopathy
- **Arsine gas (AsH₃):** Potent hemolytic → hemoglobinuria, renal failure, jaundice. 25% fatal.
- **Chronic:** Hyperpigmentation + palmar-plantar hyperkeratosis (6 mo–3 yr); Bowen disease-like SCC; black foot disease (Taiwan); QTc prolongation → atherosclerosis; diabetes; liver cirrhosis → HCC; peripheral neuropathy; immunosuppression
- **Therapeutic As₂O₃:** Acute promyelocytic leukemia → torsade de pointes

**Carcinogenicity:** Known human carcinogen (IARC, NTP). Skin, lung, urinary bladder. NOT directly genotoxic — no DNA binding. Epigenetic changes, DNA repair inhibition, p53 suppression, chromosomal instability.

## Cadmium — Complete

**The metal that never leaves.** Half-life >26 years. 0.001%/day excretion.

**Entry:**
- Ingestion: 5–10% absorbed (↑ with Fe/Ca deficiency, low protein — DMT1 upregulation in women of childbearing age)
- Inhalation: 10–60% absorbed (welding fumes, cigarette smoke → doubles lifetime body burden)
- Transporters: **DMT1** (Fe mimic), calcium channels

**Distribution & storage:**
- Liver + kidney. Cd induces **metallothionein (MT)** — cysteine-rich binding protein.
- Cd–MT stored in liver → slowly released → filtered by kidney → reabsorbed in proximal tubule → degraded in lysosomes → free Cd²⁺ released → **tubular cell damage**

**Signature toxicities:**
1. **Nephrotoxicity** (primary): Proximal tubular proteinuria. Biomarkers: β₂-microglobulin, NAG, retinol-binding protein. Critical renal cortex concentration: ~200–300 µg/g.
2. **Delayed acute pneumonitis** (exam classic): Welder inhales Cd fumes → 10–24 h latency → severe respiratory distress, pulmonary edema. Can be lethal.
3. **Itai-Itai disease:** Cd-contaminated rice + multiparous elderly women → osteomalacia + osteoporosis + renal dysfunction. Vitamin D deficiency as cofactor.
4. **Obstructive lung disease:** Chronic bronchitis, emphysema, fibrosis.
5. **Lung cancer:** IARC known human carcinogen (occupational inhalation).

**No effective chelation:** Cd–MT inaccessible; chelation causes more renal damage. Casarett: "no effective clinical treatment."

## Chromium — Complete

**Two forms, opposite toxicology:**
| Form | Entry | Toxicity |
|------|-------|----------|
| Cr(III) | Can't cross membranes — inert octahedral complexes | Essential nutrient. Nontoxic. |
| Cr(VI) | Sulfate/phosphate transporters | Toxic, carcinogenic, corrosive |

**Key mechanism:** Cr(VI) enters cells → cellular reductants (ascorbate, GSH, cysteine) reduce to Cr(III) → *during reduction*: free radicals, DNA adducts (Cr–DNA, DNA–protein cross-links, strand breaks). **Toxicity occurs during the rescue attempt.** Ultimate DNA-damaging species is Cr(III) formed *inside* the cell.

**Signature toxicities:**
- Nasal septum perforation (occupational: chrome plating, tanning)
- Type IV allergic contact dermatitis (cross-reacts Cr/Co/Ni)
- Lung cancer (IARC/NTP known human carcinogen)
- Acute renal failure (high-dose ingestion: proteinuria, hematuria, anuria)

**No chelation works:** Cr(III) is kinetically trapped intracellularly.
**Treatment:** Ascorbic acid — reduces Cr(VI) → Cr(III) *extracellularly* before cell entry.

## Retention Questions (Full Set)

1. Q: Why BAL instead of DMSA for arsenic encephalopathy?
   A: BAL crosses BBB; DMSA does not.

2. Q: Welder in confined space → severe respiratory distress 12 h later. Which metal?
   A: Cadmium (delayed acute pneumonitis from Cd fumes).

3. Q: High urinary arsenic, no symptoms. First question?
   A: Did they eat seafood? (Arsenobetaine — nontoxic, excreted unchanged.)

4. Q: Why isn't arsenic a direct genotoxic carcinogen?
   A: Does not bind DNA. Acts via epigenetic changes, DNA repair inhibition, thiol modification.

5. Q: Trivalent vs pentavalent arsenic mechanism?
   A: Trivalent → thiol binding. Pentavalent → phosphate mimicry.

6. Q: Why DMSA works for As but EDTA doesn't?
   A: HSAB — As³⁺ is soft acid; DMSA is soft base (dithiol). EDTA is hard base — mismatch.

7. Q: Arsenic trioxide → which dysrhythmia?
   A: Torsade de pointes (K⁺ channel block → QTc prolongation).

8. Q: Why no chelation for cadmium?
   A: Cd–MT complex inaccessible; forced chelation worsens renal damage.

9. Q: Why no chelation for chromium?
   A: Cr(VI) reduced to Cr(III) inside cells → kinetically trapped octahedral complexes.

10. Q: Chromium poisoning treatment?
    A: Ascorbic acid — extracellular reduction of Cr(VI) before cell entry.

11. Q: Why do women absorb more cadmium?
    A: Lower iron stores → DMT1 upregulation → more Cd absorption via Fe transporter.

12. Q: Cadmium biomarkers for nephrotoxicity?
    A: β₂-microglobulin, NAG, retinol-binding protein (low-molecular-weight proteinuria).
