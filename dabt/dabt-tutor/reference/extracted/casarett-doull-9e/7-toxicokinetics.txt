INTRODUCTION
Toxicokinetics refers to the quantitative study of absorption, dis-
tribution, metabolism, and excretion (ADME) of chemicals in 
biota through measurement and modeling of their concentrations 
or amounts in biological matrices (e.g., blood, plasma, excreta, 
exhaled air, tissues) as a function of time. Experimental studies of 
toxicokinetics facilitate the characterization of the temporal profile 
of the concentration of chemicals and their metabolites in the target 
tissue or other biological matrices, reflective of the net effect of 
the rate and extent of absorption through one or more exposure 
routes, distribution to tissues and organs via systemic circulation 
as well as disposition by metabolism and excretion (Fig. 7-1). The 
experimental and quantitative approaches used today for character-
izing toxicokinetics of xenobiotics were derived from such studies 
extensively conducted with drugs over the past decades (tradi-
tionally known as pharmacokinetics) (Gibaldi and Perrier, 1975, 
1982; O’Flaherty, 1981; WHO, 1986; Rescigno, 1997; Tozer and 
Rowland, 2006). Toxicokinetic data and analyses constitute an 
essential part of systematic approaches to safety/risk evaluation of 
xenobiotics and other substances including therapeutic drugs.
A fundamental goal of toxicokinetics is to facilitate the deter-
mination of dose delivered to the organism in view of understanding 
systemic exposure to the toxicant. For a scientifically sound interpre-
tation of toxicology studies and characterization of dose–response 
relationships, it is preferable to relate adverse responses to the dose 
delivered to the target organ (or to systemic circulation) in the form of 
putative toxic moiety. In fact, the relationship between administered 
dose and adverse responses is better understood when the toxico-
kinetic data or models are used to translate the administered dose 
in terms of a relevant measure of internal dose (i.e., dose metrics) 
(U.S. EPA, 2006, 2014). Consistent with the current understanding 
of the mode of action of a particular chemical, the dose metrics reflect 
the intensity and level of a toxicologically active form of the chemi-
cal in the target or surrogate compartment during a specific period

TOXICOKINETIC DATA
The interest in toxicokinetics arises from the need to interpret toxic-
ity and biomonitoring studies on the basis of how much of a chemi-
cal was absorbed, biotransformed, and distributed to the target site 
or excreted out of the body at various time periods. The use of data 
on the fraction of the dose absorbed represents one level of refine-
ment in relating to the onset and progression of adverse responses 
in a toxicology study. A more refined strategy would be to examine 
the internal dose of relevance to the mode of action of the chemical 
(e.g., maximal, average, or integrated concentration of the unbound 
chemical or its toxic metabolite in blood or tissue during a specific 
timeframe). In this regard, the whole blood and plasma are conve-
nient sampling matrices that also reflect the net impact of ADME 
processes in the organism. Furthermore, these biological matrices, 
in many cases, can effectively reflect the extent of target tissue 
exposure as the cellular matrix equilibrates with the free chemical 
in the extracellular fluid. The Cmax and AUC are the two dose met-
rics frequently obtained from quantification of chemical concentra-
tions in whole blood or plasma samples in toxicokinetic studies.
Fig. 7-2A presents the blood kinetic data collected in a group 
of animals as part of the toxicological evaluation of a xenobiotic 
following a single oral dose of 100 mg/kg body weight. The toxi-
cokinetic curve indicates an initial phase of increasing concentra-
tion determined by the rate of absorption, then a peak or plateau 
during which absorption rate equals elimination rate, followed by 
a phase dominated essentially by elimination. In this hypothetical 
example, the mean blood concentration of the parent chemical in 
the treatment group corresponded to 0, 1.32, 2.74, 3.37, 3.55, 2.89, 
1.67, and 0.37 mg/L at pre-exposure, or at 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12, and 
24 hours post-exposure, respectively. The Cmax,blood is 3.55 mg/L for 
this treatment group. Regarding AUCblood, a common method of cal-
culation involves summing the areas of trapezoids constituting the 
time-course curve (Gibaldi and Perrier, 1982; WHO, 1986). The 
trapezoidal method involves (i) averaging the blood concentrations 
found at two consecutive sampling times and (ii) multiplying it 
with the time interval between the two sampling periods (Figs. 7-2B 
and 7-2C). The lower panel in Fig. 7-2C illustrates the calcula-
tion of AUC for one trapezoid (4.56 (mg/L)·h), using the mean 
blood concentrations of 2.89 and 1.67 mg/L obtained at 10 and 12 
hours post-dosing. For this particular dataset, AUC0–24h (= 41.65 
(mg/L)·h) corresponds to the sum of areas of the seven trapezoids, 
that is, 1.32, 4.06, 6.11, 6.92, 6.44, 4.56, and 12.24 (mg/L)·h. For 
determining the AUC until complete elimination of the substance 
(i.e., 24 hours to infinity), the area of the last trapezoid is addition-
ally computed by dividing the last measured blood concentration by 
the rate of elimination (AUClast/Kel where Kel = first-order elimina-
tion rate constant, h−1). The trapezoidal rule can also be used to cal-
culate the area under the first moment curve (i.e., [concentration × 
time] vs. [time]). In turn, the ratio of the area under the first moment 
curve (AUMC) to the AUC is indicative of the mean residence time 
(MRT), that is, the average time spent by drug or chemical mol-
ecules in the body (Rowland and Tozer, 2011).
The timing of sample collection and the time intervals between 
samples in toxicokinetic studies should be appropriate to enable 
reliable calculation of the dose metrics of interest. The time-course 
data collected should (i) capture the critical kinetic phases in the 
exposed animals, (ii) allow the estimation of fundamental toxicoki-
netic parameters, and (iii) permit, to the extent possible, the charac-
terization of Cmax and AUC. In practice, at least two time points are 
chosen for sampling in the early phase to characterize absorption 
and distribution phases, and at least three time points are chosen for 
sampling during the terminal elimination phase, with the collected 
samples representing change in concentration by at least a factor of 
two or preferably by an order of magnitude. Chemical-specific toxi-
cokinetic properties, limit of detection of the analytical method along 
with statistical considerations can facilitate the determination of the 
number of samples, sampling time intervals, and volume of biologi-
cal samples to be collected. The number and volume of samples 
drawn should be such that they do not compromise the normal physi-
ological status of the animal (e.g., not drawing more than 10% of 
the blood volume during the AUC sampling interval (Cayen, 1995).
The accuracy of calculated dose metrics depends upon the 
number of data points collected in a toxicokinetic study whereas 
their magnitude depends upon the dose administered, exposure 
route, scenario (single or multiple doses), as well as the rates and 
extent of ADME influenced by factors such as age and pathophysi-
ological state of the test subject or animal. When all required time-
course data are not available or could not be collected in an animal 
or a human study to facilitate confident calculation of AUC or 
another metric of internal dose, the limited available data are subject 
to analysis or modeling to derive useful toxicokinetic parameters to 
gain a better characterization of the time course of the chemical in 
the biota. The choice of analysis or modeling method depends upon 
the underlying question(s) of the toxicological evaluation, the rich-
ness of the available data sets (toxicology, mode of action, and toxi-
cokinetics), as well as the precision and predictive ability required 
for the end-use. These approaches, enabling the understanding of 
the net impact of ADME processes on the time-course behavior of

TOXICOKINETIC CONCEPTS
Absorption and Bioavailability
The rate and extent of absorption, or the entry of a chemical into the 
systemic circulation, depends upon the dose, exposure route, as well 
as the volume and type of exposure matrix or dosing vehicle. Whereas 
human exposure to environmental chemicals occurs through the der-
mal, inhalation, and oral routes, laboratory animal experiments might 
additionally involve administrations by the intravenous (IV), intra-
peritoneal (IP), or intramuscular (IM) routes (Turner et al., 2011). The 
rate and efficiency with which a given chemical crosses the biological 
membrane barrier determine how quickly and how much of it appears 
in the systemic circulation. Whereas absorption might be incomplete 
when exposure occurs via extravascular routes, the intravenous dos-
ing assures full (100%) delivery of the dose into the systemic cir-
culation, and can therefore be used effectively as a benchmark of 
absorption by non-IV routes. The extent of absorption of a toxicant 
can be experimentally determined by comparing the time course of 
plasma toxicant concentration after IV and extravascular dosing. The 
AUC associated with the extravascular dose in reference to the AUC 
for the intravenous route is called bioavailability (F). In single-dose 
toxicokinetic studies, F is determined by using different IV and non-
IV doses according to the following equation, provided the toxicant 
does not display dose-dependent or saturable kinetics:

The impact of the rate of absorption on the resulting plasma 
concentration profile of a chemical is illustrated in Fig. 7-3 using 
three different values of Ka. As shown in Fig. 7-3, slowing the absorp-
tion rate of a toxicant leads to a decrease in the Cmax and a delay in 
time to reach Cmax (Tp) while maintaining the same extent of absorp-
tion or bioavailability (compare case 2 to case 1). The converse is 
true, that is, accelerating rate of absorption increases Cmax and short-
ens Tp. The dependence of Tp and Cmax on absorption rate has obvi-
ous implication in the speed of onset and maximum toxic effects 
following exposure to a chemical. Case 3 in Fig. 7-3 illustrates the 
peculiar situation when the absorption rate is so much slower than 
the elimination rate. In this case, the terminal rate of decline in 
plasma concentration reflects the absorption rate constant, instead 
of the elimination rate constant; that is, the washout of toxicant is 
rate-limited by slow absorption until the applied dose is completely 
absorbed or removed, beyond which time the toxicant remaining in 
the body will be cleared according to the elimination rate constant. 
This means that continual absorption of a chemical can affect the 
persistence of the toxic effect following an acute exposure, and that 
it is important to institute decontamination procedure quickly after 
overdose or accidental exposure to a toxicant. This is especially a 
consideration in occupational exposure via dermal absorption fol-
lowing skin contact with permeable industrial chemicals.
Volume of Distribution
The volume of distribution, Vd, corresponds to the apparent vol-
ume of a biological fluid in which a xenobiotic is diluted, and is 
expressed in units of volume (mL or L of blood, plasma, or plasma 
water). Vd would be approximately equal to the volume of blood 
if the chemical resided solely in blood and distributed uniformly 
among the blood components (plasma, erythrocytes). Hydrophilic 
substances exhibit smaller volumes of distribution, often close to 
0.6 L/kg (or 60% of body weight) in adults, approximating the 
total body water in plasma (4%), interstitial fluid (21%), and 
intracellular fluid (35%). Those substances that are highly bound 
to plasma proteins and not showing any permeation in the blood 
capillaries or tissue membranes would result in Vd reflective of 
the extracellular fluid. Xenobiotics exhibiting a marked affinity 
for tissue or fat depots have large Vd (exceeding physiological 
fluid spaces and body weight) leading to low plasma concentra-
tions. The mechanisms of tissue sequestration not only include 
partitioning of a toxicant into tissue lipids, but also involve bind-
ing to tissue proteins, concentrative uptake by active transport-
ers, and trapping in specialized organelles (e.g., pH trapping of 
amine compounds in acidic lysosomes) (Assmus et al., 2017). In 
the case of xenobiotics that are extremely lipophilic, their Vd can 
be approximated to the lipid content of the body. For example, 
the Vd of DDT is about 17.5 L lipid (i.e., 25% of body weight of 
70 kg) (Kirman et al., 2011). Conversely, the Vd in terms of L of 
whole blood can be calculated with the knowledge of tissue:blood 
partition coefficient (Ptb, in units of L blood/L tissue). The Ptb val-
ues reflect the relative distribution of a chemical between tissues 
and blood, at equilibrium. For example, Ptb = 1 for a chemical 
indicates that its concentration will be the same in the tissue and 
blood at equilibrium, whereas a Ptb value of 50 of a chemical in 
non-metabolizing tissue indicates that the concentration of this 
chemical will be 50 times greater in that tissue compared to the 
blood concentration at equilibrium. The Ptb along with the cor-
responding tissue volumes determines the extent of dilution of a 
chemical in the body. Thus, the sum total of blood volume and tis-
sue volumes (expressed as blood-equivalent volume, i.e., Ptb × Vt) 
would correspond to the Vd of chemicals (Poulin and Theil, 2002, 
2009; Rodgers and Rowland, 2007):

Clearance
Clearance (CL) is the apparent volume of a physiological fluid 
(e.g., blood, plasma, plasma water) that is cleaned of the toxicant 
per unit time (i.e., mL/min or L/h). Thus, a CL value of 1 L blood/h 
is interpreted as 1 L of blood being completely cleared of a toxi-
cant in systemic circulation per hour. Accordingly, high values of 
clearance indicate efficient and generally rapid removal of chemi-
cals from the systemic circulation, whereas low clearance values 
indicate slow and less efficient removal of a toxicant from the body. 
A formal definition of total body clearance (CLtotal) is the ratio of 
overall elimination rate of a toxicant divided by plasma concentra-
tion (i.e., amount eliminated per unit time/Cplasma); in effect then, 
CLtotal expresses the overall efficiency of the elimination mecha-
nisms operative in the body. Because this measure of elimination 
efficiency is with respect to blood or plasma toxicant concentration, 
it is also referred to as blood or plasma clearance.

Elimination Half-life
Elimination rate constant (Kel) represents the fraction of the 
amount of chemical in body that is removed from systemic circu-
lation per unit time. This is analogous to the fraction of the volume 
of distribution that is subject to elimination by various clearance 
mechanisms (i.e., CL/Vd). For example, if the CL = 10 L/h and 
Vd = 100 L, then the Kel equals 0.1 h−1 implying that 10% of the 
Vd will be cleared of the chemical per hour. However, the actual 
amount eliminated in 1 hour would be slightly less given that the 
amount in the body is declining continuously (from time zero to 
1 hour). Thus, in the case of first-order elimination kinetics, the 
fractional elimination is constant but the amount eliminated is pro-
portional to the amount remaining. For example, if the Kel equals 
0.3 h−1, the amount eliminated is less than 30% compared to the 
expectation based on the starting amount in the body (Table 7-5). 
Because the percent eliminated over a given time period remains 
constant regardless of the dose, it is more intuitive and convenient 
to refer to an elimination half-life (t1/2), that is, the time taken for 
the concentration or amount of a chemical (in a biological matrix 
such as blood or body) to be reduced by half. Thus, after one 
elimination half-life, the initial blood concentration [C0] would 
decline by 50%, that is, [C0] × 0.5. After the second half-life, the 
remaining concentration would diminish by 50% due to elimina-
tion processes, that is, (0.5 × 0.5 × [C0]) = 0.25 × [C0], for a total 
decline of C0 by a factor of 0.75 or 75%. After three, four, five, 
six, and seven half-lives, the initial concentration C0 would dimin-
ish by 87.5%, 93.75%, 96.9%, 98.5%, and 99.2%, respectively.

highly lipophilic persistent organic chemicals exhibit a large Vd

CLASSIC TOXICOKINETIC MODELS
Model Structure
Classic toxicokinetics involves empirical analysis of the time-
course data of a chemical and/or its metabolite(s) to derive param-
eters characteristic of their behavior in the exposed organism. This 
type of analysis uses one or more compartments to capture the 
toxicokinetics of a chemical in the organism (Fig. 7-5). The sim-
plest structure of the classic toxicokinetic model represents the 
body as a homogeneous one-compartment system. When the log 
[C] versus time plot of the toxicokinetic data yields a curve as 
shown in Fig. 7-6 (top panel vs. bottom panel), it would indicate 
that differential distribution and equilibration processes among 
the model compartments need to be accounted for. In such cases, 
more than one compartment is chosen to adequately model the 
toxicokinetics of the substance (Figs. 7-6 and 7-7). Basically, in 
classical toxicokinetic analysis, whether the model structure cor-
responds to one, two, three, or more compartments is determined 
by the time-course data. Even though the number and nature of 
the compartments may not have exact anatomical or physiological 
correspondence, the resulting model structure and mathematical 
representation for a chemical would consistently fit with the avail-
able empirical data on the kinetics in one or more biological matri-
ces such as whole blood, plasma, urine, exhaled breath, or tissues.

tissue group is much slower than elimination (right panel of Fig.

the later phase is controlled by the slow redistribution of the toxi-

to the central compartment, where it is eliminated (i.e., washout 
in the terminal phase is rate-limited by redistribution from the 
peripheral compartment).
The relative prominence of distributional phases in two-­
compartment models will often vary depending on the lipid 
solubility of the toxicant in each tissue grouping and any other 
sequestration and export mechanisms of a toxicant in particular 
tissues (e.g., tight binding to tissue proteins, active influx into or 
efflux out of the tissue cell types), as well as the competing influ-
ence of elimination from the visceral organs. For example, very 
rapid metabolism or excretion at the visceral organs would limit 
distribution into the slow or very slow tissue groupings. Also, there 
are times when equilibration rates of a toxicant into visceral organs 
overlaps with lean body tissues, such that the distribution kinet-
ics of a toxicant into these two tissue groupings become indistinct 
with respect to the exponential decline of plasma concentration, in 
which case two instead of three tissue groupings may be observed. 
The concept of tissue groupings with respect to uptake or washout 
kinetics serves to justify the seemingly simplistic, yet pragmatic, 
mathematical description of extravascular distribution by the classic 
two- or three-­compartment models.

PHYSIOLOGIC TOXICOKINETIC MODELS
The classic compartmental toxicokinetic models described in the 
previous section are developed by fitting empirical data on the con-
centration of parent chemical and/or metabolite(s) in the exposed 
organism. The parameter estimates obtained with these models 
are useful for toxicokinetic interpretations limited to the exposure 
duration, exposure route, dose levels, life stage, and species for 
which the experimental data were generated. In order to enhance 
the mechanistic basis of toxicokinetic analysis and to improve 
the application domain of the resulting parameter estimates, there 
is increasing interest in physiologically based pharmacokinetic 
(PBPK) models, also referred to as physiologically based toxico-
kinetic (PBTK) or biologically based biokinetic (BBBK) models.
The PBPK models describe the interrelationships among the 
key determinants of uptake and disposition to simulate the time-
course behavior of xenobiotics and their metabolites in blood and 
various tissues. These models are essentially quantitative statements 
of the current state of knowledge concerning the mechanistic deter-
minants of the toxicokinetics of a chemical in biota. The advantages 
of PBPK models in comparison with the classic compartmental 
models are that they permit: (i) the simulation of the concentration 
of chemicals and their metabolites not only in the blood but also 
in any other tissue of interest including the target organ, (ii) the 
evaluation of the nature and magnitude of the potential impact of 
physiological, physicochemical, and metabolic parameters on the 
toxicokinetics of chemicals, (iii) the prediction of the toxicokinet-
ics of chemicals across species and populations using relevant input 
parameters, (iv) accounting for temporal change in physiological 
and metabolic parameters, complex dosing regimens, and saturable 
processes, and (v) the simulation of toxicokinetic datasets collected 
under various dosing situations (exposure routes, doses, and sce-
narios) with a single set of input parameters and equations. The 
impediments to the use of PBPK models relate to the fact that not 
only these models contain more parameters compared to the classic 
compartmental models but also the mathematics and programming 
required are not trivial. Furthermore, the range of values and distri-
butions of input parameters are not always well defined for various 
chemicals and their metabolites in all species and pathophysiologi-
cal states of interest. Nevertheless, PBPK models are conceptually

toxicokinetics of chemicals in biota. While the former approach 
determines the number, behavior, and volumes of the compart-
ments based on the fitting exercise, the structure and complexity of 
a resulting PBPK model are determined by the critical determinants 
of toxicokinetics, available data sets, and intended end-use.
The practice of incorporating biological and physiological 
concepts into the analysis and interpretation of toxicokinetic data is 
not entirely new. The PBPK-type descriptions were developed for 
gaseous anesthetics as early as the 1920s and then applied to model 
the uptake and disposition of certain biopharmaceutics, antineoplas-
tics, and volatile organic chemicals (Haggard, 1924; Teorell, 1937; 
Kety, 1951; Mapleson, 1963; Riggs, 1970; Bischoff et al., 1971; 
Dedrick et al., 1972; Fiserova-Bergerova, 1975). Since the publica-
tion of a PBPK for styrene by Ramsey and Andersen (1984), there 
has been increasing interest in the development of PBPK models for 
xenobiotics and drugs (Reddy et al., 2005; Krishnan and Andersen, 
2010). Fig. 7-9 presents the key steps of PBPK modeling, with a 
specific end-use as the motivation for model development (e.g., 
chemical risk assessment) (WHO, 2010). Defining the scope and 
intended application of the PBPK model up-front is important to 
ensure that the model development process is concordant with the 
required level of precision and extent of evaluation. Overall, the 
construction of PBPK models begins with the development of a 
model structure, mathematical representation of key processes, and 
estimation of model parameters. The PBPK model equations and 
parameters are then written as a computer program, solved to pro-
vide toxicokinetic simulations, and compared with available data to 
guide further refinement or analysis (sensitivity, uncertainty, vari-
ability) in view of assessing the suitability of the model for the 
intended application.
Model Structure
The structure of a PBPK model for a given chemical corresponds 
to a diagrammatic representation of key processes and pathways 
determining its toxicokinetics in the organism (i.e., species or indi-
vidual). The PBPK model is represented as boxes and arrows, with 
the boxes corresponding to the organs (individual or groups) and 
the arrows reflecting the physiological flows and clearance pro-
cesses. The art of developing a PBPK model structure requires 
striking a balance between the principles of parsimony (i.e., mini-
mal but essential elements) and plausibility (i.e., reflective of physi-
ological reality), while being consistent with the current state of 
knowledge on the toxicokinetics and mode of action of the chemi-
cal or drug under study. In other words, the complexity of PBPK 
models increases only when it is required to explain the behavior 
contained in the toxicokinetic data. In PBPK models, increments 
of complexity generally involve the addition of compartments with 
different time-constants (i.e., blood flow divided by the distribu-
tion volume specific to each tissue compartment) or mathematical 
representations of mechanisms to account for specific accumula-
tion or disposition behavior of the chemical under study. The level 
of detail relates not only to the compartments (e.g., whole body, 
target tissue, or intracellular concentrations) but also to the chemi-
cal forms that are tracked within the model (e.g., parent chemical, 
metabolites, mixture components).
For a hydrophilic chemical whose concentrations across com-
partments are comparable, a one-compartment PBPK model might 
well be sufficient. However, for chemicals exhibiting different 
time-course profiles in various tissues, a multicompartmental PBPK 
description is required. Fig. 7-10 shows the structure of a four-­
compartmental PBPK model used for simulating the inhalation tox-
icokinetics of toluene, a lipophilic volatile organic chemical. In this 
particular case, the model compartments together represent ≈ 91% 
of body mass in adult rats. The remaining 9% (corresponding to 
the mass of the skeletal/structural components) is not included 
in the PBPK model because toluene does not exhibit significant 
affinity for, or accumulate in, skeletal and structural components. 
In this particular model, the “lung” compartment only represents 
the portal of entry, assuming no significant pulmonary metabolic 
activity toward toluene. The four compartments of this model cor-
responding to liver, fat, poorly perfused tissues, and richly perfused

Parameters: Significance and Estimation
The equations of the PBPK models contain a number of input parame-
ters, which are conveniently grouped as physiological, physicochemi-
cal, or biochemical parameters including rate constants of reactions 
and clearance processes relevant to the chemical being modeled.
Physiological Parameters  The physiological parameters con-
sist of volumes and flows; the volumes are required for the tissues 
or tissue groups represented as boxes whereas flows are associated 
with the arrows in the model which include alveolar ventilation rate, 
cardiac output, and organ-specific blood flow rates. Tissue volumes 
along with their composition, relative to that of blood, determine the 
extent to which chemicals are diluted within each compartment. The 
greater the volume of tissue and lipid content, the larger the Vd of 
lipophilic xenobiotics, thus leading to lower blood concentrations. 
For example, the volume of adipose tissue represents about 19% to 
25% of body weight in adult humans. This adipose volume along 
with the high Ptb for lipophilic organic chemicals (e.g., adipose tis-
sue Ptb for DDT = 199) contributes significantly to the Vd (e.g., for 
DDT 70kg × 0.25 L/kg × 199 = 3483 L) (Haddad et al., 1999; 
Kirman et al., 2011), and as a source of prolonged internal exposure 
to such chemicals. In contrast, the tissue blood flow influences the 
rate at which a chemical in systemic circulation is delivered to a 
tissue. Tissues with smaller volumes and high perfusion rates will

rate at which absorbed chemicals are distributed to and collected 
from all tissues, has been obtained from dye dilution curves using 
oximeters. The cardiac output is faster in smaller animals (e.g., 
mice) than in larger animals (e.g., humans), and again proportional 
to body surface area or BW0.7. The cardiac output in mammals is 
often comparable to the ventilation rate such that the ratio of perfu-
sion rate:ventilation rate is about 1 in resting individuals, but it can 
vary as a function of workload and physical activity.
While anthropometric data and breathing rates are often 
obtained in test animals/subjects, other physiological parameters 
required for PBPK modeling are obtained from existing litera-
ture or calculated on the basis of body weight for each species and 
life-stage (O’Flaherty, 1994; Brown et al., 1997; Corley et al., 
2003; Price et al., 2003a, 2003b; Dewoskin and Thompson, 2008).

Model Verification and Evaluation
Model verification focuses on whether the PBPK model is cor-
rectly coded (error-free) and the toxicokinetic simulations could be 
reproduced in a different programming software or computational 
platform. Model evaluation focuses on comparing the simulations 
(usually continuous) with experimental toxicokinetic data (usually 
discrete), and ascertaining whether the model with its structure and 
parameters is fit to provide output required for a specific end-use. 
Given that both the PBPK model simulations and experimental data 
are subject to uncertainty and variability, it is not realistic to expect 
an exact match between every single data point and the simulation 
output and therefore PBPK models providing predictions within a 
factor of 2 of the experimental data have generally been consid-
ered to be adequate. As such, the model evaluation focusing on 
fit-for-purpose and not simply on fit-to-data is becoming the prac-
tice. In this regard, WHO (2010) outlined a three-point approach 
to conduct evaluation of PBPK models with emphasis on the (i) 
biological basis, (ii) model performance, and (iii) reliability of the 
model output (i.e., predictions of dose metrics) for the intended end-
use (Fig. 7-16). Accordingly, the consideration of biological basis 
focuses on whether the model structure and range of the input param-
eters used in model development are biologically plausible, without 
contradicting the fundamental physiological principles or the mass 
balance (Table 7-14, column 1). The aspect of model performance 
focuses on whether the model is able to adequately reproduce toxi-
cokinetic data collected from more than one particular experiment 
using more than one dose and route of administration (Table 7-14, 
column 2). Finally, the consideration of reliability of dose metric 
predictions focuses on how well the PBPK model simulates the 
dose metrics of relevance to the end-use (e.g., AUCs for interspecies 
extrapolation in risk assessment), with particular emphasis on the 
results of uncertainty analysis and sensitivity analyses (Table 7-14, 
column 3). While the uncertainty analysis involves the evaluation 
implementation of these calculations is the integration of the differ-

In conclusion, the PBPK model is mechanistically based and 
more complex than the classical data-based toxicokinetic models 
but it is still a simplified representation of the biological system 
under study. The PBPK models, as other mechanistic models, only 
account for those determinants and processes that are hypothesized 
to be critical by the investigator(s). The complexity and number of 
compartments are thus not reflective of the accuracy and usefulness 
of a given PBPK model, but are considered to be critical for simu-

TOXICOKINETIC TOPICS 
AND APPLICATIONS
The interpretation of toxicology studies has sometimes been con-
ducted on the basis of administered dose or absorbed dose when 
such data were available. However, the dose to the target site is the 
more desired and relevant dose metric. In this regard, the toxicoki-
netic models are useful for estimating systemic exposure, in terms 
of the toxic form of a chemical (parent or metabolite) over a specific 
duration in blood or the target organ. Such a measure reflective of the 
dose to the target is critical not only for interpreting the toxicity data 
but also for conducting various extrapolations in the case of systemi-
cally acting chemicals (i.e., those chemicals that induce toxicity at 
target sites distant from the portals of entry by accessing them via 
systemic circulation). The following sections describe these topics 
and applications facilitated by toxicokinetic parameters and models.
Nonlinear Kinetics
The distribution and clearance of most chemicals follow first-order 
kinetics in the low-dose region. Accordingly, the Kel, Vd, CL, and 
t1/2 are expected to be same in the low-dose range (i.e., no dose-
dependent change in kinetic parameters occurs under first-order 
condition). In other terms, under first-order conditions, the dose 
metrics (e.g., Cmax, AUC) will be nearly identical when compared 
on the basis of unit dose (e.g., per µg/kg/day) or exposure con-
centration (per ppm). A semilogarithmic plot of blood concentra-
tion versus time over a range of doses will show a set of parallel 
lines such that blood concentration at a given time or the AUC0
∞ 
remain proportional to dose. Thus, a twofold change in dose 
(µg/kg/day) will result in a twofold change in blood concentration 
at a given time after dosing or in the value of AUC0
∞ if first-order 
conditions prevail in the dose range. However, for some toxicants, 
as the dose increases, its Vd and/or CL may change, showing non-
linear or dose-dependent kinetics (Fig. 7-18). Enzyme-mediated 
metabolism, active transport processes, and protein binding have 
finite capacities and therefore can be saturated. For instance, most 
metabolic enzymes operate in accordance to Michaelis–Menten 
kinetics (Table 7-10), such that the rate of metabolism (amount per

Single versus Repeated Dose Kinetics
Continuous or chronic exposure to a chemical can lead to its accu-
mulation in the body depending upon its elimination half-life and 
interval between the doses. For example, lipophilic chemicals with 
large Vd and very small CL exhibit long half-lives and accumulate 
as the exposure continues. On the contrary, for a chemical with a 
very small t1/2 compared to the time interval between doses, it is 
likely to be eliminated almost completely before the next dose such 
that there would be no significant accumulation. Let’s consider a 
chemical with t1/2 of 4 hours is administered to experimental animals 
once every 24 hours. In this case, the amount eliminated would be 
proportional to the body burden (i.e., first-order elimination kinetics) 
in the dose range of interest. Thus, the amount eliminated after one 
half-life or 4 hours = 50%, two half-lives = 75%, three half-lives = 
87.5%, four half-lives = 93.75%, five half-lives = 96.875%, six 
half-lives = 98.438%, seven half-lives = 99.219%, and eight half-
lives = 99.61%. If the second dose is administered at 4 hours, that is, 
after one half-life, then 50% of the chemical from the first dose will 
still be in the body to which the second dose adds leading to higher 
blood concentration than what was seen after a single dose. The nar-
rower the interval between doses or the shorter the dosing interval 
compared to the t1/2, the faster the attainment of steady state will be. 
Fig. 7-20 shows the same time to attain 50% steady state for various 
rates of intake; on the contrary, the steady-state blood concentration 
is strictly proportional to the intake rate. Typically, the rise in blood 
concentration is relatively rapid after dosing, as it is governed by the 
early (distribution) half-life, and becomes slower at later times when 
the terminal (elimination) half-life becomes dominant. Therefore, 
when the doses are given at certain intervals of time (and not in a 
continuous fashion), the fraction eliminated between two dosing 
intervals needs to be accounted for computing the dose metrics.

during a given interval of time (τ) and the maximal and minimal 
concentrations attained during repeated doses given at intervals of

time interval τ, whereas the product of the average Css and Vd equals 
the average body burden at steady state.
The compartmental models including the PBPK models 
described in the preceding sections are useful in the estimation of 
blood and tissue concentrations associated with a single dose or 
repeated doses given by various routes, and this can frequently be 
accomplished using a single set of parameters. In other instances, 
re-estimation or adjustment of parameter values might be neces-
sary to accommodate the observed or anticipated change in input 
parameters with time (e.g., change in Vd due to body fat content, 
change in CLint due to enzyme induction). Fundamentally, the rate 
of change in the amount of chemical in non-metabolizing tissues in 
PBPK models becomes equal to zero at steady state, such that the 
tissue concentrations remain stable during repeated exposures. The 
steady-state blood concentration obtained with the PBPK model 
will be the same as that obtained using Eq. (7-34) (Andersen, 1981; 
Gibaldi and Perrier, 1982). PBPK models also have been used to 
predict the kinetic behavior and tissue dosimetry of chemicals dur-
ing unusual exposure scenarios (e.g., adjusted threshold limit val-
ues during extended hours of work, physical activity/work load, 
pregnancy and lactation, or coexposure to chemicals) (Clewell and 
Andersen, 1987; Krishnan et al., 2002; Corley et al., 2003).

Kinetic Interactions During 
Mixed Exposures
Toxicokinetic interactions during mixed exposures result when one 
chemical alters the mechanistic determinant of another chemical 
at the level of absorption (e.g., alteration of ventilation rate, skin 
permeability coefficient), distribution (e.g., competition for binding 
to hemoglobin, albumin, metallothionein, alteration of the concen-
trations of binding proteins, alteration of tissue blood flow), metab-
olism (e.g., enzyme induction or inhibition), or excretion (e.g., 
GFR) (Krishnan and Brodeur, 1994). Such interactions result in an 
increase or decrease in the internal dose per unit administered dose 
during mixed exposures in comparison with the individual chemi-
cal exposures. For example, during mixed exposures, xenobiotics 
may compete directly with each other for an enzymatic binding site 
(competitive inhibition), bind directly to the enzyme-xenobiotic 
complex but not to the free enzyme (uncompetitive inhibition), or 
do both of these (noncompetitive inhibition) (Segal, 1975). In such 
cases, toxicokinetic analysis should account for the nonlinearity 
arising from not only the saturable metabolism of individual mix-
ture components but also the impact of the metabolic inhibition as 
a function of the concentration and affinity of one mixture com-
ponent (substrate) in comparison with the free concentration and 
potency of another mixture component (inhibitor). When induction 
of metabolizing enzymes occurs, the toxicokinetic analysis would 
indicate that the impact is more pronounced in cases where hepatic

Interpretation of Human 
Biomonitoring Data
Human biomonitoring or biological monitoring refers to the sys-
tematic sampling of body fluids (e.g., blood, urine, exhaled air), 
and at times body tissues, for the purpose of estimating an indi-
vidual’s internal dose from exposure to chemicals in the workplace 
or in the general environment (Paustenbach and Galbraith, 2006; 
Manno and Viau, 2010). Biomonitoring accounts for all exposure 
sources and routes (oral, dermal, and inhalation), individual differ-
ences in toxicant absorption and disposition, and critical personal 
or lifestyle variables, such as body size and composition, workload 
that affects pulmonary ventilation, or cigarette smoking that could 
affect the metabolic status of an individual. Although biomonitor-
ing provides a measure of internal exposure, in the final analysis 
the data obtained must be relatable to the health guidance values, 
point of departure underlying such values or health risks. For each 
chemical of concern, it is useful to establish a reference biomoni-
toring value below which a reasonable assurance of safety exists. 
In this regard, the tools for interpreting biomonitoring data make 
use of forward dosimetry or reverse dosimetry approaches (Clewell 
et al., 2008; Gurusankar et al., 2017). Forward dosimetry refers to 
the use of toxicokinetic data and models to develop concentrations 
of exposure biomarkers corresponding to the existing health guid-
ance values (e.g., occupational exposure limit, reference dose, tol-
erable daily intake) or point of departure in a dose–response curve 
(e.g., NOAEL). Well-known examples of the use of the concept 
of forward dosimetry in human biomonitoring include the devel-
opment of (i) biological exposure indices (BEIs) by the American 
Conference of Governmental Industrial Hygienists (ACGIH), (ii) 
biological tolerance values (BATs) and biological guidance values 
(BLW) by the German MAK Commission, as well as (iii) biologi-
cal monitoring equivalents associated with exposure guidance val-
ues or the underlying point of departure values (BE, BEPOD) for 
environmental chemicals (Hays et al., 2007; Angerer et al., 2011; 
Gurusankar et al., 2017). Reverse dosimetry, in contrast, involves 
the use of toxicokinetic parameters and models to reconstruct or 
estimate exposure levels associated with the measured concentra-
tions in biomarkers in biological matrices (urine, blood, plasma, 
exhaled air).

Reverse Dosimetry  Reverse dosimetry approaches use the toxi-
cokinetic parameters or models to convert the biomarker concen-
tration into the exposure dose. The sampling time relative to the 
exposure pattern would be a straightforward matter if exposure 
is continuous and constant, in which case blood, urine, or other 
samples can be collected at any time after steady state is reached. 
In reality, exposure is often episodic or intermittent, and in occupa-
tional settings exposure is dictated by the work shift. Systematically 
timed sampling is hardly feasible in a community population expo-
sure study, where sampling is typically random, and exposure 
comes from diffuse sources and may not occur with any defin-
able regularity. The only approach to overcome such shortcoming 
is to obtain samples at different times of a day to provide diurnal 
coverage, which introduces obvious logistical and cost challenges 
in a population surveillance program. It is not often appreciated 
that the observed level of a determinant for a chemical exposure 
is a function of not only current exposure, but also what happened 
some time before (Droz et al., 1991). This is particularly so when 
exposure is continuous or the interval between intermittent expo-
sures is on the order of the chemical’s elimination half-life (e.g., an 
elimination t1/2 of 24 hours or longer when exposure reoccurs on a 
daily basis). In other words, the elimination half-life of a biomarker 
or index compound dictates how far back in exposure history it 
reports. A corollary to this principle is the fact that short half-life 
biomarkers reflect more of the within day or day-to-day fluctuation 
in exposure, whereas daily fluctuation in exposure is dampened 
with long half-life biomarkers. Although the former cases may be 
viewed as undesirable because of variability in the measurement, 
it offers a better reflection of the oscillation in internal dose and 
particularly the transient peak exposures. The latter cases provide a 
less variable, time-averaged measure of exposure.

CONCLUSIONS
Toxicokinetic data and models are central to the selection of doses 
and species for toxicity testing, interpretation of dose–response 
relationships, and efficient design of experiments for focused and 
reduced animal use. The toxicokinetic principles are also key to the 
design and interpretation of human biological monitoring studies as 
established in occupational health research for many decades and 
more recently in screening level risk assessment of environmen-
tal pollutants. The emerging toxicity testing and risk assessment 
paradigms as well as the extensive ongoing human biomonitoring 
programs will require toxicokinetic data and tools for a large num-
ber of chemicals. In this regard, increasing focus on the develop-
ment of high throughput (HTP) approaches to derive toxicokinetic

parameters and PBPK models for new chemicals, mixtures, 
and particles is required. So far, there have been few successful 
attempts to integrate in vitro data and quantitative structure-prop-
erty relationships for partition coefficients within the PBPK frame-
work to simulate the inhalation toxicokinetics of chemicals (Peyret 
and Krishnan, 2011; Bessems et al., 2014). Further, there has been 
successful application of the HTP data on plasma protein bind-
ing (equilibrium dialysis) and intrinsic clearance (cryopreserved 
hepatocytes) to generate steady-state concentrations for the oral 
route, to facilitate the conversion of HTP toxicity study results into 
daily doses in humans (Wetmore et al., 2013; Wetmore, 2015). The 
emerging toxicity testing and risk assessment paradigms require 
HTP in vitro and in silico approaches that have broad capability and 
application domains in terms of chemical space, exposure route, 
and toxicokinetic parameters. In addition, advances in the develop-
ment of physiologically based cellular toxicokinetic models will 
facilitate simulations of dose metrics at macro and micro levels 
to better understand the relationship among the concentrations of 
chemicals causing perturbations in HTP screening assays, levels 
of chemicals and their metabolites found in population level bio-
monitoring studies, as well as the human exposure guidance values.
