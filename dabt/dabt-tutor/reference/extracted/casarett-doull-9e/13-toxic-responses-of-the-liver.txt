INTRODUCTION
The liver performs many functions that are critical to life. Among 
these are the processing of foods and other substances absorbed 
from the intestinal tract and the subsequent delivery of processed 
nutrients to other organs in the body. The liver is also an integral 
contributor of immunity that protects mammals from harmful 
pathogens. In addition to its immunological roles, it is the main 
organ where exogenous chemicals are metabolized, a process that 
hastens their excretion into bile and urine. As a consequence, liver 
cells are exposed to significant concentrations of these chemicals 
and their metabolites, some of which can cause liver dysfunction. 
Many industrial chemicals, plant toxins, environmental pollutants, 
food-borne agents, herbal remedies, and drugs (both pharmaceu-
tical and recreational) are known to be hepatotoxic. In the phar-
maceutical industry, adverse effects on the liver are one of the 
most frequent reasons for discontinuing the development of drug 
candidates and withdrawal of drugs from the market (Temple and 
Himmel, 2002; Stickel et al., 2011).
Although many hepatotoxic chemicals have been identified in 
the last several decades and much has been learned about mecha-
nisms by which liver toxicity occurs, liver injury from various 
chemicals continues to present major health concerns. The liver 
comprises several cell types, each with different functions, and it is 
nourished by a blood supply that is unique in the body. Knowledge 
of liver physiology and anatomy not only lends insight as to how the 
liver functions but also provides the underpinnings for understand-
ing how hepatotoxicants cause liver dysfunction. Therefore, our 
journey begins with an overview of liver anatomy and physiology. 
Next, the repertoire of hepatic responses to toxic insult and the vari-
ous ways of classifying and monitoring liver injury are discussed. 
Chemical-induced liver injury is typically initiated by one or more 
critical events, such as formation of a toxic metabolite, which trig-
ger intracellular responses that can progress to dysfunction or death 
of hepatic parenchymal cells (i.e., HPCs, hepatocytes). These intra-
hepatocellular events can in turn prompt secondary events involv-
ing activation of nonparenchymal cells that magnify or attenuate 
the initial injury. These events typically determine whether injury

LIVER ANATOMY AND PHYSIOLOGY
Hepatic Functional Anatomy
Livers of mammals typically have two (humans) or more (rodents) 
lobes into which blood vessels enter and exit. The liver is unusual 
among organs in that it has a dual blood supply (Fig. 13-1). As 
is typical of other organs, the liver has an arterial supply via the 
hepatic artery, which provides a minority of blood entering the liver 
(about 1/3 in humans, less in rodents). The major blood supply 
to the liver arises from the hepatic portal vein, which comprises 
venous drainage from the stomach and intestine. This unique anat-
omy positions the liver to have first contact with food-borne xeno-
biotic agents absorbed into the blood from the gastrointestinal (GI) 
tract, but it also means that the liver receives blood from which 
much oxygen has been removed after nourishing the GI tract.
These two blood supplies in part determine how liver is orga-
nized into smaller anatomical substructures within each lobe. Two 
concepts exist for organization of operational units, termed either 
“lobules” or “acini” (McCuskey, 2006b). According to the classical 
lobular concept, the liver is organized into hexagonal lobules that

are more or less apparent histologically. Each lobule is oriented 
around a central vein (also known as a terminal hepatic venule). 
At the corners of the lobule are portal triads (also known as portal 
tracts). As the name implies, each of these contains a branch of 
the portal vein (portal venule), a hepatic arteriole, and one or more 
small bile ducts (Figs. 13-1C and 13-2). The capillary equivalents 
in liver lobules are called “sinusoids.” Blood entering the portal 
tract via the portal vein and hepatic artery is mixed in the penetrat-
ing vessels, enters the sinusoids, and percolates along the chords of 
parenchymal cells (HPCs). The blood then collects into terminal 
hepatic venules (central veins), which coalesce to form the hepatic 
vein that connects to the vena cava (Figs. 13-1B and 13-2). Keeping 
in mind this general structure, the lobule is viewed as having three 
regions known as periportal (nearest portal triad), centrilobular (sur-
rounding the central vein), and midzonal (between periportal and 
centrilobular).
A somewhat different way of viewing the functional unit of 
the liver is the acinus. This concept is preferred from the standpoint 
that it better reflects the manner in which blood flows into the sinu-
soids; that is, some of the blood entering via the portal venule and 
hepatic arteriole mixes, then some flows laterally (between portal 
triads) before entering the sinusoid. The terminal branches of the 
portal vein and hepatic artery form the base of the acinus, which 
has three zones: zone 1 is closest to the entry of blood (i.e., cells 
near the portal triad), zone 3 abuts the central vein, and zone 2 is in 
between (Figs. 13-2 and 13-3). These zones correspond roughly to 
periportal, centrilobular, and midzonal areas of the classical lobule, 
respectively, but more closely align with the manner in which blood 
is delivered to the sinusoids.
Despite the greater functional accuracy of the acinar concept, 
lobular terminology is still used to describe location of pathological 
lesions of hepatic parenchyma. Acinar/lobular zonation is of consid-
erable functional consequence regarding gradients of components 
both in blood and in HPCs (Jungermann and Kietzmann, 2000). 
For example, as noted above blood entering the acinus comprises 
mostly blood from the portal vein that is poorly oxygenated relative 
to the blood entering from the hepatic artery. Enroute to the central 
vein, oxygen rapidly leaves the blood to meet the high metabolic 
demands of the HPCs. Therefore, HPCs in zone 3 are exposed to 
substantially smaller concentrations of oxygen than those in zone 
1. These anatomical substructures are also important because liver 
lesions caused by chemical exposure usually appear preferentially 
in one of them.
The sinusoids are endothelium-lined channels between cords 
of HPCs through which blood flows on its way to the central vein. 
The sinusoidal microvasculature of the liver differs in important 
ways from capillaries in other organs. Sinusoids are larger and more 
irregular than typical capillaries. Three major types of cells in the 
sinusoids are sinusoidal endothelial cells (SECs), Kupffer cells, 
and stellate cells (Fig. 13-3) (McCuskey, 2006b, 2008). In addition, 
the liver contains dendritic cells and a substantial number of lym-
phocytes, especially natural killer (NK) and NKT cells (Gao et al., 
2009). Each of these nonparenchymal cell types performs important 
functions (Heymann and Tacke, 2016; Xing et al., 2016).
Liver Cells and Their Functions
The liver’s location in the circulation between the intestinal tract 
and the rest of the body facilitates its performance as a primary con-
tributor to maintaining metabolic homeostasis of the body. Because 
venous blood from the stomach and intestine flows into the portal 
vein and then through the liver before entering the systemic circula-
tion, it is the first organ to encounter ingested nutrients, vitamins, 
drugs, and environmental toxicants, as well as bacterial products 
that enter the portal blood after being absorbed in the gastrointes-
tinal tract. Efficient scavenging or uptake processes extract these 
absorbed materials from the blood for processing, storage, and/or 
excretion into bile.
Hepatic Parenchymal Cells  Hepatic parenchymal cells 
(HPCs), also known as hepatocytes, are large epithelial cells that 
account for about 60% of the cells in the liver and about 80% of the 
liver volume. These cells occur in cords along the microvasculature 
of the liver (Fig. 13-3), with microvilli on their basal surface in 
apposition with the endothelium (Fig. 13-4). The apical aspect of 
these cells is in contact with neighboring HPCs, which are joined 
by tight junctions. Here a channel, or canaliculus, is formed into 
which bile is secreted by HPCs (Fig. 13-3); this bile ultimately 
flows into bile ducts and from there into the small intestine.
HPCs perform most of the many critical metabolic functions 
of the liver (Table 13-1). They process dietary carbohydrates by 
converting monosaccharides into energy through glycolysis and 
mitochondrial metabolism. HPCs are also involved in anabolic pro-
cesses, such as the synthesis of glycogen for carbohydrate storage. 
They respond to energy needs of other organs by releasing stored 
glucose into the circulation. Similarly, HPCs are a central hub for 
the regulation of lipids. They are capable of synthesizing lipids and 
removing them from the blood. HPCs can then process these lipids 
for energy production or for export into the circulation as lipopro-
teins for use by other organs. HPCs are also the source of many 
circulating proteins, such as plasma albumin and other binding pro-
teins, coagulation factors, and complement proteins. In addition, 
HPCs detoxify circulating substances produced during metabolism 
by other organs. For example, ammonia is a toxic product of protein 
catabolism, and HPCs detoxify it by forming urea, which is elimi-
nated by the kidneys. They also synthesize heme and are involved 
with the reutilization of iron. Additionally, HPCs synthesize bile 
acids that aid in absorption and processing of dietary lipids.

target organ for many toxicants. For example, metabolism of some 
xenobiotic agents by HPCs can result in toxic metabolites that 
directly injure these cells (see specific examples with acetamino-

contains other cell types (discussed in the next section) that per-

in pathogenesis following exposure to toxicants. Importantly, the 
many functions of HPCs render the liver a critical organ for main-
taining metabolic homeostasis of the entire organism. It follows that 
damage to HPCs and other liver cells from chemical exposure can 
lead not only to liver dysfunction but to dysfunction of other tissues 
(e.g., hepatic encephalopathy, coagulopathies [Lee, 2012; Kandiah 
and Kumar, 2016]).
Sinusoidal Endothelial Cells
Sinusoids are lined by thin, discontinuous endothelial cells with 
numerous fenestrae (or pores; Fig. 13-5) that allow molecules 
smaller than 250 kDa to cross the interstitial space (known as the 
space of Disse) between the endothelium and HPCs. In the space 
of Disse, sinusoidal endothelial cells (SECs) are separated from 
the HPCs by a basement membrane-like matrix, which is attenu-
ated relative to basement membranes in other tissues (Friedman, 
2000). However, this subendothelial extracellular matrix is impor-
tant for the normal function of all resident liver cells (Friedman, 
2000). The numerous fenestrae and the attenuated basement mem-
brane facilitate exchange of fluids and molecules, such as albumin, 
between the sinusoid and HPCs, but hinder movement of particles 
larger than chylomicron remnants. Endothelial cells are important 
in the scavenging of lipoproteins via the apolipoprotein E receptor 
and of denatured proteins and advanced glycation endproducts by 
scavenger receptors (Enomoto et al., 2004; Elvevold et al., 2008). 
Hepatic endothelial cells also secrete biologically active molecules 
such as cytokines, prostanoids, nitric oxide, and endothelins and 
express intercellular adhesion molecule-1 (ICAM-1) and vascular 
cell adhesion molecule-1 (VCAM-1) on the cell surface (Knolle 
and Wohlleber, 2016). These SEC-derived substances play impor-
tant roles in liver injury and repair.
Kupffer Cells  Kupffer cells are the resident macrophages of the 
liver and constitute approximately 80% of the fixed macrophages in 
the body (McCuskey, 2006b). They are situated within the lumen of 
the sinusoid, in apposition to SECs and with processes that extend 
through fenestrae to contact HPCs (Figs. 13-3 and 13-4). A pri-
mary function of Kupffer cells is to ingest and degrade particulate 
matter (e.g., bacteria). Also, Kupffer cells are important players in 
immune responses; as such, they are a major source of cytokines, 
eicosanoids, and reactive oxygen species (ROS) and can act as anti-
gen-presenting cells (APCs; Laskin, 2009).
Hepatic Stellate Cells  Hepatic stellate cells (HSCs) are 
located between endothelial cells and HPCs (Figs. 13-3 and 13-4; 
Friedman, 2008a). They are also known as Ito cells or by the more 
descriptive term of fat-storing cells. Stellate cells are the major sites 
for vitamin A storage in the body (Friedman, 2008a). Indeed, early 
responses to high-dose vitamin A therapy are HSC engorgement, 
activation, increase in number, and protrusion into the sinusoid 
(Geubel et al., 1991). These cells express smooth muscle actin; 
they are contractile and appear to control local flow of blood in 
the sinusoids. When activated, especially during chronic injury to 
the liver, stellate cells can assume a myofibroblastic phenotype, 
synthesizing and secreting collagen and other extracellular matrix 
proteins and thereby can initiate liver fibrosis (Friedman, 2000; Lee 
and Friedman, 2011; Schon and Weiskirchen, 2014; Weiskirchen 
and Tacke, 2014).
Other Immune Cells of Liver Sinusoids  In addition to resident 
liver macrophages (Kupffer cells), the liver is home to a population 
of immune cells of diverse lineage and function. Specific immune 
cell content and organization within the liver sinusoid makes the 
liver a true immune organ, capable of mounting both innate and 
adaptive immune responses both under normal conditions and dur-
ing disease (Shuai et al., 2016). Immune cells contribute to liver 
homeostasis and immunity. Thus, these cells may become engaged 
in the progression of liver damage driven by inflammation and/or 
become activated in response to injury-associated changes, includ-
ing inflammatory stimuli (McDonald and Kubes, 2016). An impor-
tant role of various immune cells is to mount a specific response 
to injury or disease. For example, dendritic cells (DCs; Fig. 13-3) 
and plasmacytoid DCs play a key role in antigen processing and in 
presentation of antigens to other immune cells along with produc-
tion of inflammatory cytokines, such as interferons (Chen et al., 
2016). Notably, although the cell surface protein CD11c is often 
considered a DC marker, an increasing body of knowledge of DC 
and macrophage heterogeneity suggests more comprehensive cell 
surface marker analysis is required to identify DCs definitively. 
Interestingly, DCs may play a protective role in certain experimen-
tal settings, including in resolution of fibrosis (Jiao et al., 2012; 
Almeda-Valdes et al., 2015) and in acetaminophen hepatotoxicity 
(Connolly et al., 2011).

a variety of cytokines that modify the activity of several other cell 
types in the liver. Both NK and NKT cells can display quite dichot-
omous functions in various disease or injury settings. Invariant 
NKT cells have been shown to drive alcohol-induced liver injury

Bile Formation
Bile is a yellow fluid comprising water that contains bile acids, 
GSH, phospholipids, cholesterol, bilirubin and other organic 
anions, proteins, metals, ions, and xenobiotic agents (Arrese and 
Trauner, 2003; Trauner and Boyer, 2003). Formation of bile is a 
specialized function of the liver. Adequate bile formation is essen-
tial for uptake of lipid nutrients from the small intestine, for protec-
tion of the small intestine from oxidative insults (Aw, 1994), and 
for excretion of endogenous and xenobiotic compounds. Bile is 
secreted into canaliculi by HPCs where it functions to transport 
various substances out of the liver and to help solubilize lipids 
in the intestine. As noted above, the canalicular lumen is a space 
formed by specialized regions of the plasma membrane between 
adjacent hepatocytes (Fig. 13-3). The canaliculi are separated from 
the intercellular space between HPCs by tight junctions, which 
form a barrier permeable only to water, electrolytes, and to some 
degree to small organic cations. Under physiological conditions, 
tight junctions are impermeable to organic anions, which allows 
high concentrations of bile acids, GSH, bilirubin diglucuronide, 
and other organic anions to accumulate in bile. The structure of the 
biliary tract is analogous to the roots and trunk of a tree, where the 
tips of the roots equate to the canalicular lumens. Canaliculi form 
channels between HPCs that connect to a series of increasingly 
larger channels (intrahepatic bile ducts) within the liver. The large 
extrahepatic bile ducts merge to form the common bile duct. Bile 
originating in the lobules travels from the intrahepatic bile ducts to 
the common bile duct and then into the gallbladder to be stored and 
concentrated before its release into the duodenum. However, the 
gallbladder is absent in several species, including the horse, whale, 
and rat, so that bile flows directly into the duodenum from the com-
mon bile duct in these species.
Bile Formation and Transport  HPCs begin the process of bile 
formation by active transport into the canalicular lumen of bile 
acids, GSH, and other osmotically active compounds including 
xenobiotics and their metabolites (Klaassen and Watkins, 1984). 
These molecules are the major driving force for the passive move-
ment of water and electrolytes across the HPC epithelium into 
the bile. Concentration of solutes in bile depends on their active 
transport at the locus of the canalicular membrane. With the iden-
tification of specific transporters, substantial progress has been 
made in the understanding of the molecular mechanisms of bile 
formation (reviewed by Jansen and Groen, 2006; Pauli-Magnus 
and Meier, 2006). On the basal (sinusoidal) side of the HPCs, 
there are sodium-dependent and sodium-independent uptake sys-
tems (Fig. 13-6). Most conjugated bile acids (taurine and glycine 
conjugates) and some unconjugated bile acids are transported into 
HPCs by sodium/taurocholate cotransporting polypeptide (NTCP) 
(Trauner and Boyer, 2003; Hagenbuch and Dawson, 2004; Stieger 
et al., 2007). Sodium-independent uptake of conjugated and uncon-
jugated bile acids is performed by members of the organic anion–
transporting polypeptides (OATPs) (Hagenbuch and Meier, 2004; 
Csanaky et al., 2011). OATP1B1 and OATP1B3 are predominantly 
expressed in liver and are capable of transporting conjugated and 
unconjugated bile acids, steroids, and many other organic anions. 
Furthermore, the OATPs transport numerous drugs and some 
hepatotoxicants such as phalloidin, microcystin, and amanitin 
(Hagenbuch and Gui, 2008; Roth et al., 2012). There also exist 
several organic-anion transporters (OATs, in humans). These trans-
port small organic anions, the movement of which is coupled to 
exchange of another organic anion; this transport differs from that 
of OATPs, which tends to transport larger, more lipophilic anions 
in an ATP-dependent manner (Nigam et al., 2015). Organic cat-
ions are transported across the basolateral membrane by organic 
cation transporters (OCTs, especially OCT1 in humans). The driv-
ing force for transport by OCTs is the concentration gradient of 
the cation across the HPC membrane, so transport can be in either 
direction (Koepsell and Endou, 2004). In addition to the uptake or 
exchange systems, there are ATP-dependent efflux pumps located 
on the basolateral membrane of HPCs. These carriers are members 
of the multidrug resistance-associated proteins (MRPs; ABCC), 
which are multispecific transporters for many different anions 
(Homolya et al., 2003).
All unconjugated bile acids in HPCs are conjugated before 
being transported by the bile salt export pump (BSEP) across the 
canalicular (apical) membrane. Bile acid excretion is a major driving 
force of bile fluid formation (i.e., “bile salt-dependent bile flow”). 
Other solutes in bile are transported by members of the multidrug 
resistance (MDR) P-glycoprotein family such as MDR3 (ABCC2), 
which transports phospholipids, and the heterodimeric transport-
ers ABCG5/ABCG8, which transport cholesterol and plant sterols 
into bile (Pauli-Magnus and Meier, 2006). In addition, MRP2 trans-
ports GSH, which is the main compound responsible for the bile 
salt-independent bile flow, as well as sulfated and glucuronidated 
bile acids, glutathione disulfide, glutathione conjugates, bilirubin 
diglucuronide, and many other conjugated drugs and chemicals 
(Borst et al., 2000; Gerk and Vore, 2002). Other transport systems 
of the canalicular membrane include the breast cancer resistance 
protein (BCRP; ABCG2), which can contribute to the biliary excre-
tion of bile acids and xenobiotics.
Canalicular lumen bile is propelled forward into larger chan-
nels by dynamic, ATP-dependent contractions of the pericanalicular 
cytoskeleton (Watanabe et al., 1991). The biliary epithelial cells 
that comprise bile ducts, once regarded as passive conduits, mod-
ify bile by absorption and secretion of solutes (Lira et al., 1992). 
Bile acids are taken up into biliary epithelial cells (cholangiocytes) 
by OATP1A2 (sodium-independent uptake) and by the sodium-
dependent bile acid transporter ASBT (Fig. 13-6; Obaidat et al., 
2012; Roth et al., 2012). These bile acids are then secreted on the 
basolateral side mainly by MRP3 and heterodimeric organic solute 
transporter (OSTα/OSTβ) (Ballatori et al., 2009). The bile acids 
secreted from biliary epithelial cells return to the portal circulation

Cell Death
Cell death from chemical exposure is known to occur by sev-
eral different molecular pathways. Resulting modes of cell death 
include oncotic necrosis, apoptosis, pyroptosis, necroptosis, etc. 
(Fig. 13-7). Differences among these are explained in Chap. 3, and 
therefore only some salient features of a couple of these will be 
covered here. Oncotic necrosis, often referred to simply as “necro-
sis,” is characterized by cell swelling, leakage of cellular contents, 
nuclear disintegration (karyolysis), and an influx of inflamma-
tory cells. Cell contents released during oncotic necrosis include 
intracellular enzymes such as alanine aminotransferase (ALT) and 
aspartate amino transferase (AST), which appear in the plasma 
and are used as biomarkers of hepatocellular injury (see below). 
In contrast to oncotic necrosis, apoptosis is characterized mor-
phologically by cell shrinkage, chromatin condensation, nuclear 
fragmentation, and formation of membrane-bound cell fragments 
termed “apoptotic bodies.” Because the latter are phagocytosed and 
digested by Kupffer cells or other neighboring cells, apoptosis is 
often not accompanied by an inflammatory response. The pathway 
to apoptosis involves activation of caspase enzymes that results in 
the cleavage of nuclear DNA. Oncotic necrosis and apoptosis as 
well as other modes of cell death typically occur as a result of acti-
vation of one or more intracellular molecular signaling pathways 
that result in cell death. These pathways are described in Chap. 3 
and in many reviews (Jaeschke et al., 2004; Jaeschke and Bajt, 
2006; Malhi et al., 2006; Schulze-Bergkamen et al., 2006; Luedde 
et al., 2014; Iorga et al., 2017). Liver injury from acetaminophen 
overdose is an example of a toxicant-induced cell death pathway in 
HPCs, and this is discussed below.
Signaling mechanisms of apoptosis have been elucidated in 
detail (reviewed by Jaeschke and Bajt, 2006; Malhi et al., 2006; 
Schulze-Bergkamen et al., 2006). In the extrinsic pathway of apop-
tosis, ligands (e.g., Fas ligand, TNF-α) bind to their respective 
death receptor (Fas receptor, TNF receptor type I), which triggers 
the trimerization of the receptor that prompts the recruitment of 
various adapter molecules and procaspases to the cytoplasmic tail 
of the receptor. The assembly of this death-inducing signaling com-
plex (DISC) leads to the activation of initiator caspases (caspase-8 
or -10). In HPCs, the active initiator caspase cleaves Bid, a member 
of the Bcl-2 family of proteins, and the truncated Bid (tBid) translo-
cates together with other Bcl-2 family members such as Bax to the 
mitochondria. These proteins form pores in the outer membrane of 
the mitochondria and cause the release of intermembrane proteins 
such as cytochrome c and the second mitochondria-derived activa-
tor of caspases (Smac). Cytochrome c, together with apoptosis pro-
tease activating factor-1 (APAF-1), ATP, and procaspase-9, form the 
apoptosome, causing the activation of caspase-9, which then pro-
cesses (and activates) downstream effector caspases, for example 
caspase-3 and caspase-7. The effector caspases can propagate the 
apoptotic signal by activating caspase-activated DNase (CAD) to 
initiate nuclear DNA fragmentation and by cleaving numerous cel-
lular proteins critical to cell function and to the structural integrity

In addition to downstream substrates, caspase-3 can also pro-

Although HPCs constitutively express Fas and TNF receptors, the 
death signal generated with ligation of the receptor is in most cases 
insufficient to trigger apoptosis in healthy cells. Inhibitor studies 
and experiments with gene-deficient mice support the hypothesis 
that only the amplification of the receptor-derived signal through 
multiple mitochondrial cycles can successfully induce apoptosis 
in HPDs (Yin et al., 1999a; Bajt et al., 2000). In addition to the 
direct propagation of the apoptosis signal by mitochondrial cyto-
chrome c release, the simultaneous release of Smac ensures that the 
cytosolic inhibitors of apoptosis proteins (IAPs) are inactivated and 
do not interfere with the promotion of apoptosis (Li et al., 2002). 
Thus, mitochondria are an important part of the extrinsic (receptor-­
mediated) apoptotic signal transduction pathway in liver cells after 
most stimuli. However, under conditions of strong Fas receptor acti-
vation (MegaFas ligand), downstream caspases are activated with-
out involvement of mitochondria (Schüngel et al., 2009).
In contrast to the extrinsic pathway, the intrinsic or mitochon-
drial pathway of apoptosis is initiated independent of the TNF 
receptor family, caspase-8 activation, and formation of the DISC. 
Despite the upstream differences, the postmitochondrial effects are 
largely similar to the extrinsic pathway. The intrinsic pathway is 
generally triggered by a cytotoxic stress or DNA damage, which 
activates the tumor suppressor p53 (Sheikh and Fornace, 2000). 
This protein acts as a transcription factor to promote the forma-
tion of proapoptotic Bcl-2 family members, for example, Bax. 
The increased Bax translocation to the mitochondria induces the 
release of mitochondrial intermembrane proteins including cyto-
chrome c, Smac, endonuclease G, and apoptosis-inducing factor 
(AIF) (Saelens et al., 2004). An intrinsic mechanism of apoptosis 
has been discussed for cell death in aging livers (Zhang et al., 2002), 
prolonged treatment with alcohol (Ishii et al., 2003), and toxicity 
of benzo(a)pyrene and acetaminophen in hepatoma cells (Boulares 
et al., 2002; Ko et al., 2004). For other hepatotoxic chemicals, such 
as carbon tetrachloride (Cai et al., 2005), galactosamine (Gómez-
Lechón et al., 2002), and microcystin (Ding et al., 2000a, 2000b), 
evidence for mitochondria-dependent apoptosis has been reported 
in cultured HPCs, and apoptotic cell death was observed after expo-
sure to these chemicals in vivo (Shi et al., 1998; Hooser, 2000; 
Gujral et al., 2003b).
A special form of cell death is the more recently described type 
of programmed necrosis termed “necroptosis” (Vandenabeele et al., 
2010; Grootjans et al., 2017). Necroptosis is generally initiated by 
death receptors, for example TNF receptor 1, and the formation 
of complex 1 with various adapter molecules including receptor-
interacting protein kinases 1 and 3 (RIP1 and -3). If caspase-8 is 
activated, it will cleave RIP1 and -3, and apoptosis will be initiated. 
However, if caspase-8 is inhibited, RIP1 and RIP3 activate a cas-
pase-independent execution mechanism involving the phosphoryla-
tion of mixed lineage kinase domain like pseudokinase (MLKL), 
which then translocates to the cell membrane and forms pores 
(Grootjans et al., 2017). Although there is an increasing number of 
papers published showing roles for RIP1, RIP3, and MLKL in the 
pathophysiology of many liver disease models in vivo, the results 
are variable, and it remains controversial whether some cell death 
is a classical necroptotic process or another form of programmed 
necrosis (Dara et al., 2016b).
Characterization of mode of cell death after chemical expo-
sure has been based on morphology (Fig. 13-7) and more recently 
on activation of various molecular factors described above. 
Importantly, commonalities in signaling pathways in different 
modes of cell death can occur, and this has led to controversy about 
the mode of cell death that applies to particular hepatotoxicants. For 
example, common molecular changes in mitochondria are involved 
in pathways to apoptosis as well as to oncotic necrosis (Jaeschke 
et al., 2012a; Pessayre et al., 2012). Partly for this reason, histologi-
cal examination of affected tissue remains the “gold standard” for 
characterizing cell death modes.
Fatty Liver (Steatosis)
Fatty liver (steatosis) is defined as an appreciable increase in lipid 
(mainly triglyceride) content of HPCs. Histologically, HPCs con-
taining excess fat appear to have many round, empty vacuoles in 
standard, solvent-extracted and paraffin-embedded tissue sections. 
The accumulation of these vacuoles can displace the nucleus to the 
periphery of the cell. Use of frozen sections and special stains or 
biochemical analysis of lipids is needed to verify the contents of the 
vesicles as lipid (Fig. 13-8). Based on the size of the fat droplets, 
one can distinguish between macrovesicular (large droplets) and 
microvesicular (small droplets) steatosis.
Fatty acids of hepatic and extrahepatic origin can play inte-
gral roles in the generation of steatosis. Free fatty acids (FFAs) are 
synthesized in HPCs, mainly from carbohydrate-derived acetyl-
coenzyme A. Also, FFAs can be generated in the liver from the 
hydrolysis of absorbed fat. Additionally, adipose tissue serves as 
an extrahepatic source of FFAs because adipocytes can release 
FFAs into the circulation. Once in the cytosol of HPCs, FFAs can 
be imported into mitochondria for degradation by β-oxidation or 
can be esterified into triglycerides for incorporation into very low 
density lipoproteins (VLDL), which transports FFAs to peripheral 
adipose tissue. Protein synthesis inhibitors can prompt the accu-
mulation of lipids by inhibiting the protein component of VLDL 
needed for export of lipids from HPCs. FFA uptake into mitochon-
dria depends on the activity of the mitochondrial enzyme, carnitine 
palmitoyl transferase 1, which can be downregulated by malonyl-
coenzyme A, the first intermediate of FFA synthesis. Thus, FFA 
synthesis, consumption, and storage are in a well-balanced steady 
state, normally with little accumulation of triglycerides in the liver 
(Pessayre et al., 2002). However, under conditions of excess uptake 
into HPCs of FFAs derived from adipose tissue and/or food, FFAs 
can accumulate and become esterified to form triglycerides that 
appear as steatosis (Pessayre et al., 2002; Neuschwander-Tetri, 
2010).

Steatosis occurs commonly from moderate alcohol consump-

the stimulus for it is temporary. However, livers with steatosis are 
more susceptible to additional insults, such as from hepatic isch-
emia (Selzner and Clavien, 2001) or hepatotoxicants (Donthamsetty 
et al., 2007). Perhaps more importantly, persistent steatosis appears 
to be a precursor to serious liver disease. When accompanied by 
inflammation (i.e., steatohepatitis), steatosis can progress to life-
threatening chronic liver damage, fibrosis (e.g., cirrhosis), and 
hepatocellular carcinoma (Farrell and Larter, 2006; Ibrahim et al., 
2011; Koo, 2013; Ferramosca and Zara, 2014; Angrish et al., 2016).
Although the most common cause of hepatic steatosis is insu-
lin resistance associated with obesity and sedentary lifestyle, expo-
sure to many hepatotoxicants can induce steatosis. Chemicals that 
produce steatosis associated with lethality include the antiepilep-
tic drug valproic acid (Scheffner et al., 1988), the antiviral drug 
fialuridine (Honkoop et al., 1997), the solvent carbon tetrachloride 
(Zimmerman, 1999), and protein synthesis inhibitors such as ethio-
nine, puromycin, and cycloheximide. Several drugs that accumulate 
in mitochondria and inhibit β-oxidation and mitochondrial respira-
tion have been associated with steatosis. These include 4,4′-dieth-
ylaminoethoxyhexestrol, amiodarone, tamoxifen, perhexiline, 
amineptine, doxycycline, tetracycline, tianeptine, and pirprofen 
(Berson et al., 1998; Larosche et al., 2007, Pessayre et al., 2012). 
The effects on mitochondria not only lead to steatosis but also to 
increased reactive oxygen formation and lipid peroxidation. In addi-
tion, drugs such as amineptine, amiodarone, tetracycline, pirprofen, 
and tianeptine can directly inhibit microsomal triglyceride transfer 
protein, which lipidates apolipoprotein B to form triglyceride-rich 
VLDL particles (Lettéron et al., 2003). Drugs with this dual effect 
on β-oxidation and VLDL secretion are generally highly steato-
genic. Ethanol is by far the steatosis-producing agent of most public 
health concern (Farrell, 2002; Pessayre et al., 2002; Stravitz and 
Sanyal, 2003; Saito et al., 2007; Neuschwander-Tetri, 2010) and is 
discussed as an example later in this chapter.
Canalicular Cholestasis
Cholestasis is characterized biochemically by elevated serum con-
centration of compounds normally concentrated in bile, particularly 
bile salts and bilirubin. This form of liver dysfunction is defined as 
a decrease in the rate of bile formation or an impaired secretion of 
specific solutes into bile (Padda et al., 2011). When biliary excre-
tion of the yellowish bilirubin pigment is impaired, it accumulates 
in skin and eyes, producing jaundice. Additionally, excess bilirubin 
increases in urine, which becomes darker yellow or brown. The 
histological features of cholestasis can be subtle and include dila-
tion of bile canaliculi and the presence of bile plugs in bile ducts 
and canaliculi. Toxicant-induced cholestasis can be transient or 
chronic; when substantial, it is associated with cell swelling, cell 
death, and inflammation. HPC injury is generally caused by the 
intracellular accumulation of chemicals normally secreted into the 
bile, such as drugs and their metabolites, other xenobiotic agents, 
cytotoxic bile acids, bilirubin, or other bile constituents. Many dif-
ferent types of chemicals, including metals, hormones, and drugs, 
can cause cholestasis (Zimmerman, 1999).
Molecular mechanisms of cholestasis are related to expression 
and function of transporter systems in the basolateral and canalicu-
lar membranes of HPCs described above (reviewed Wagner et al., 
2009; Padda et al., 2011). In principle, increased hepatic uptake, 
decreased biliary excretion, and increased biliary reabsorption (cho-
lehepatic shunting) of a xenobiotic agent can contribute to its accu-
mulation in the liver. For example, basolateral uptake by OATPs can 
contribute to the liver injury from various toxins, including phal-
loidin, microcystin, and amanitin (Pauli-Magnus and Meier, 2006; 
Lu et al., 2008). Some drugs that cause cholestatic injury, such as 
rifampicin, bosentan, and troglitazone, directly inhibit BSEP and 
can thereby lead to accumulation of bile acids (Stieger et al., 2000; 
Fattinger et al., 2001). Estrogen and progesterone metabolites 
inhibit BSEP from the canalicular side after excretion by MRP2 
(Stieger et al., 2000).
A substantial inhibition of excretion of bile salts can lead 
to their accumulation in HPCs and may thereby cause cell injury 
(Palmeira and Rolo, 2004). However, most bile acids that accumu-
late in rodent models are relatively nontoxic (Zhang et al., 2012); 
accordingly, it appears that inflammation caused by bile acid accu-
mulation in the liver plays a major role in liver injury from some 
forms of cholestasis (Gujral et al., 2003a; Allen et al., 2011). In 
contrast, due to the increased accumulation of the more hydropho-
bic glycine-conjugated bile acids in humans, direct cytotoxicity can 
occur in human cholestasis (Woolbright et al., 2015).
Increased bile acid concentrations in liver can trigger compen-
satory mechanisms that limit cholestatic injury (Zollner et al., 2006; 
Zhang et al., 2012). These involve alterations in transporter expression. 
Bile acids bind to the nuclear receptor, farnesoid X receptor (FXR), 
the activation of which stimulates the small heterodimer partner (SHP) 
that downregulates NTCP expression and thereby limits bile acid 
uptake (Denson et al., 2001). In addition, FXR activation increases 
expression of BSEP and MDR3, which enhances the capacity for bile 
acids and phospholipids, respectively, to be transported into the cana-
liculus and excreted from the liver (Ananthanarayanan et al., 2001; 
Huang et al., 2003). Furthermore, the FXR-independent upregulation 
of the basolateral transporters MRP3 and MRP4 reduces intracellular 
bile acid and drug concentrations (Schuetz et al., 2001; Wagner et al., 
2003; Fickert et al., 2006). Moreover, agonists of the nuclear xenobi-
otic receptors, constitutive androstane receptor (CAR) and pregnane 
X receptor (PXR), can induce MRP3 and MRP4 expression, result-
ing in enhanced export of bile acids during cholestasis (Wagner et 
al., 2005). In biliary epithelial cells, OSTα/OSTβ is upregulated at 
the basolateral membrane during cholestasis (Boyer et al., 2006a). 
This response, which is dependent on FXR, mediates the enhanced 
return of bile acids from bile to the plasma (Boyer et al., 2006a). Thus, 
such adaptation of transporter expression may counteract some of the 
detrimental effects of cholestasis (Zollner et al., 2006).
Bile Duct Damage
Another name for damage to the intrahepatic bile ducts is cholan-
giodestructive cholestasis (Cullen and Ruebner, 1991; Zimmerman, 
1999). A useful biochemical index of bile duct damage is a sharp 
elevation in serum activities of enzymes localized to bile duct epi-
thelial cells, particularly alkaline phosphatase (ALP). In addition, 
serum levels of bile acids and bilirubin are elevated, as observed with 
canalicular cholestasis. Initial lesions following a single exposure to 
cholangiodestructive chemicals include swollen biliary epithelium, 
debris of damaged cells within ductal lumens, and inflammatory cell 
infiltration of portal tracts. Chronic administration of toxicants that 
cause bile duct destruction can lead to bile duct proliferation and 
fibrosis resembling primary biliary cholangitis (PBC). Numerous 
drugs have been implicated in causing prolonged cholestasis with 
features of PBC (Zimmerman, 1999); however, only in rare cases 
does this result in permanent damage or even loss of bile ducts, a 
condition known as vanishing bile duct syndrome. Cases of this have 
been reported in patients receiving antibiotics, anabolic steroids, 
contraceptive steroids, or the anticonvulsant carbamazepine (Davies 
et al., 1994; Zimmerman, 1999).

Sinusoidal Endothelial Cell Damage
As noted earlier, the sinusoid is essentially a specialized capillary 
lined with endothelium with numerous fenestrae (holes) that allow 
for high permeability (Braet and Wisse, 2002). The functional 
integrity of the sinusoid can be compromised in two ways, by 
dilation or blockade of its lumen and by destruction of sinusoidal 
endothelial cells (SECs). Dilation of the sinusoid occurs when the 
downstream flow of blood is impeded. The rare condition of pri-
mary dilation, known as peliosis hepatis, has been associated with 
exposure to anabolic steroids and the drug danazol (Makdisi et al., 
1995). Blockade can occur when the fenestrae enlarge to such an 
extent that red blood cells become caught in them or pass through 
with entrapment in the interstitial space of Disse. Gaps between 
endothelial cells and overt injury clearly affect SEC function. Such 
gaps occur after exposure to acetaminophen (Ito et al., 2005), 
galactosamine/endotoxin (Ito et al., 2006), or an anti-Fas antibody 
(Ogasawara et al., 1993). They can be caused by direct injury to 
SECs or by their detachment from the extracellular matrix (DeLeve 
et al., 1997; Bajt et al., 2000; Ito et al., 2006). In general, matrix 
metalloproteinase inhibitors prevent gap formation (McCuskey, 
2006a). A consequence of SEC injury is the loss of barrier function 
with extensive blood accumulation in the liver parenchyma (i.e., 
hemorrhage).
Disruption of SECs is considered the early structural feature 
of the vascular disorder known as sinusoidal obstruction syndrome 
(formerly called veno-occlusive disease: DeLeve et al., 1999). 
Swelling and/or proliferation of SECs progresses to nonthrombotic 
occlusion of hepatic venules with cell debris and fibrotic material 
(DeLeve et al., 2003a). This results in congestion and medial hyper-
trophy of vessels and contributes to progressive necrosis of paren-
chymal tissue and fibrosis (Huxtable, 1989). Pyrrolizidine alkaloid 
plant toxins provide an example of a class of chemicals that cause 
pronounced SEC destruction and sinusoidal obstruction (discussed 
below).
Inflammation
Inflammation occurs in many organs as a response to injury that 
entails activation of the innate immune system. In the liver, the 
inflammatory response involves circulating blood cells as well 
as the resident cell types mentioned above (Fig. 13-9). Activated 
coagulation and complement cascades and alterations in micro-
vascular function are also components of an acute inflammatory 
response. The activation of resident macrophages (Kupffer cells), 
NK, NKT cells, and innate lymphoid cells plays a major role in 
liver inflammation. Additionally, the accumulation and activation 
of blood-borne cells including platelets, neutrophils, lymphocytes, 
and monocytes within the damaged liver are well-recognized fea-
tures of hepatotoxicity produced by many chemicals. Acute inflam-
matory responses participate in the removal of damaged tissue and 
in the initiation of liver repair. However, under certain circum-
stances, inflammatory cells and their products can aggravate exist-
ing liver injury by release of cytotoxic mediators or indirectly by

e.g., high mobility group box 1 [HMGB1]) from injured HPCs 
stimulates various receptors on inflammatory cells (e.g., toll-like 
receptors) (Schwabe et al., 2006; Bianchi, 2007; Kubes and Mehal, 
2012; Woolbright and Jaeschke, 2017). Activation of inflammatory 
cells in liver can also occur independently of direct hepatocellular 
damage; rather, hepatic inflammation can be driven by infection or 
pathologic events transpiring in other tissues that result in circu-
lating inflammatory triggers. For example, alterations in intestinal 
homeostasis can result in translocation of bacterial products such 
as lipopolysaccharide (LPS) from the intestine into the portal cir-
culation entering the liver (Giannelli et al., 2014). LPS and other 
pathogen-associated molecular pattern molecules (PAMPs) act at 
cellular receptors (e.g., toll-like receptors) to activate Kupffer cells 
and other sinusoidal cells. PAMPs also arise from bacterial or viral 
infections.
Kupffer cells and neutrophils are phagocytes that have a vital 
function in host defense and removal of cell debris. Kupffer cells 
can be activated not only by bacterial products such as LPS, but also 
by circulating particles and activated complement factors (Bilzer 
et al., 2006). Production of reactive oxygen species by NADPH oxi-
dase is a critical tool used by these cells for eliminating pathogens, 
but it can also contribute to damage of liver cells. Upon activation, 
Kupffer cells generate mainly hydrogen peroxide, which can diffuse 
into neighboring liver cells and create an intracellular oxidant stress, 
ultimately causing cellular injury (Bilzer et al., 1999). A detrimental 
role of Kupffer cells in the pathogenesis of toxicant-induced liver 
injury has been suggested for several chemicals including ethanol, 
acetaminophen, CCl4, and 1,2-dichlorobenzene (Laskin, 2009), 
although the role of Kupffer cells in acetaminophen hepatotoxicity 
may be counterbalanced by their important role in liver repair (Ju 
et al., 2002; Holt et al., 2010; You et al., 2013; Krenkel and Tacke, 
2017).
In addition to their capacity to cause oxidant-mediated cel-
lular injury, Kupffer cells generate inflammatory mediators such 
as cytokines and prostanoids (Decker, 1990; Schwabe et al., 2006). 
These mediators can aggravate injury by recruiting neutrophils into 
the liver (Bajt et al., 2001); activation of neutrophils can cause cell 
death in susceptible HPCs (Jaeschke, 2006). However, Kupffer 
cells can also generate anti-inflammatory mediators, such as pros-
taglandin E2 and interleukin-10 (Decker, 1990) that downregulate 
production of pro-inflammatory cytokines and thereby attenuate 
inflammatory liver injury (Bourdi et al., 2002; Ju et al., 2002). Thus, 
Kupffer cells can promote progression of injury or inhibit it by 
assisting in removal of cell debris and apoptotic bodies by phago-
cytosis. In addition, mononuclear cells (macrophages) recruited into 
liver from the blood can function in a similar way as Kupffer cells. 
Although there is a capacity for additional damage by these cells, 
their recruitment into the damaged liver and the mediators they 
release are important signals for inducing repair of damaged tissue 
(Holt et al., 2008; Laskin, 2009; Adams et al., 2010).
As mentioned before, the location of the liver downstream in 
the circulation from the intestine can result in exposure to microbes 
or microbial products that translocate into the portal circulation. 
Neutrophils arrest in the liver vasculature and can become activated 
in response to bacterial infection or extensive cell injury. These 
cells remove bacteria and cell debris at least in part through inter-
actions with the resident macrophages (Gregory and Wing, 2002). 
Neutrophils generate the potent oxidant and chlorinating species, 
hypochlorous acid, through the combined activities of NADPH 
oxidase and myeloperoxidase (El-Benna et al., 2005). In addition, 
neutrophils can release proteolytic enzymes (e.g., elastase, cathep-
sin G) and bactericidal proteins (Wiedow and Meyer-Hoffert, 
2005). The capability of neutrophils to migrate out of the vascu-
lature, adhere to target cells and generate potent cytotoxic agents 
render this leukocyte an effective killer of invading microorgan-
isms and a remover of dead or dying cells. However, if directed 
against still viable liver cells, these agents can promote additional 
tissue injury or even liver failure (Jaeschke, 2006). Notably, neu-
trophil-induced liver cell injury is a multistep process (Jaeschke 
and Hasegawa, 2006; Ramaiah and Jaeschke, 2007). It requires 
exposure to inflammatory mediators that upregulate adhesion mol-
ecules such as Mac-1 (CD11b/CD18) on the cell surface, which 
cause neutrophils to accumulate in liver sinusoids and prime them 
for reactive oxygen formation. If a chemotactic signal is received 
from the parenchyma, neutrophils will extravasate, adhere to the 
target, and become fully activated to release oxidants and proteo-
lytic enzymes (Chosay et al., 1997; Jaeschke, 2006; Jaeschke and 
Hasegawa, 2006). These neutrophil products can contribute to liver 
injury through the destruction of HPCs and other cells compro-
mised by toxicant exposure (Gujral et al., 2004; Jaeschke, 2006). 
Neutrophils are involved in liver injury from ischemia-reperfusion, 
alcohol, α-naphthylisothiocyanate, halothane, and other toxicants 
(see below and Ramaiah and Jaeschke, 2007).
Although this discussion has highlighted roles of Kupffer 
cells and neutrophils, several other cell types can contribute as 
well to acute and chronic inflammatory responses. A comprehen-
sive accounting of the complexities of inflammation is beyond the 
scope of this chapter, but greater detail about various aspects of 
liver inflammation is available in several recent reviews (Byrne, 
2010; Tilg and Moschen, 2010; Zhai et al., 2011; Liaskou et al., 
2012; McDonald and Kubes, 2012; Brenner et al., 2013; Hoque 
et al., 2013; Bishayee, 2014; Ceccarelli et al., 2014; Yang et al., 
2015; Zhu et al., 2016; Roth et al., 2017). The roles of inflammation 
in responses to specific hepatotoxicants are mentioned in sections 
below and in the section “Determinants of Susceptibility.”
Regeneration and Repair
The liver has a high capacity to restore lost tissue and function by 
regeneration. Loss of HPCs due to hepatectomy, either after surgi-
cal resection in human patients or modeled by major removal of 
liver (e.g., 70%) in rodents, triggers proliferation of all mature liver 
cells. This process is capable of restoring the original liver mass 
(Fausto, 2000; Michalopoulos, 2017). The mechanisms critical for 
restoring liver mass are not entirely understood. HPCs are normally 
quiescent, that is, they are in G0 phase of the cell cycle. In order to 
proliferate, they need to enter the cell cycle. This process is initi-
ated by cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6), which prime HPCs to respond 
to essential growth factors such as HPC growth factor (HGF) and 
transforming growth factor-α (TGF-α) (Fausto, 2000). In contrast 
to Fausto’s priming/progression hypothesis, Michalopoulos (2007) 
has argued that HPCs do not fit this two-stage model, rather there 
are primary direct mitogens for HPCs (e.g., HGF) and secondary 
proregenerative substances (TNF-α) that enhance the effect of the 
primary mitogen (Michalopoulos, 2007). Importantly, both cyto-
kines and growth factors are involved in the activation of a number 
of transcription factors that drive expression of cell cycle-regulating 
proteins such as cyclins, the activators of cyclin-dependent kinases 
(CDKs), and CDK inhibitors such as p18, p21, and p27 (Trautwein, 
2006). The coordinated expression of individual cyclins and inhib-
itors of CDKs guides the cell through the various phases of the

Fausto, 2000). This is allowed in part by the reduced sensitivity 
of oval cells to the proliferation-inhibiting cytokine, transforming 
growth factor-β (TGF-β) (Nguyen et al., 2007). For details of the

is referred to excellent reviews on this subject (Trautwein, 2006; 
Michalopoulos, 2010, 2014, 2017).
The partial hepatectomy animal model has provided abundant 
insights into the mechanisms of liver regeneration. It is important 
to note that although great similarity exists in the factors driving 
HPC proliferation after hepatectomy and after toxic liver injury, the 
stimuli for regeneration are distinct. As is the case for massive liver 
necrosis caused by acetaminophen, expansion of the HPC mass 
must be accompanied by removal of necrotic tissue, but the latter is 
not a key element of regeneration after partial hepatectomy. Thus, 
there is likely divergence in the precise role of different cell types, 
particularly macrophages, in repair of toxic responses in the liver 
compared to partial hepatectomy. The reader is referred to recent 
studies examining mechanisms of liver repair after acetaminophen 
overdose (Bhushan et al., 2014, 2016, 2017a, 2017b; Williams 
et al., 2014; Kopec et al., 2017).
Fibrosis
Hepatic fibrosis (scarring) occurs in response to chronic liver injury 
that overwhelms the capacity of the organ to repair. It is character-
ized by the accumulation of excessive fibrous tissue, specifically 
fibril-forming collagen types I and III, and a decrease in normal 
plasma membrane collagen type IV (reviewed by Bataller and 
Brenner, 2005; Rockey and Friedman, 2006; Gutierrez-Ruiz and 
Gomez-Quiroz, 2007; Friedman, 2008b). Fibrosis can develop 
around central veins, portal tracts, or within the space of Disse. 
This progressive collagen deposition, marked by interconnecting 
fibrous scars, alters the architecture of the liver. When the fibrous 
scars subdivide the remaining liver mass into nodules of regenerat-
ing HPCs, fibrosis has progressed to cirrhosis (Fig. 13-10), and the 
liver has limited residual capacity to perform its essential functions. 
The excessive extracellular matrix protein deposition and loss of 
sinusoidal endothelial cell fenestrae and of HPC microvilli limit 
exchange of nutrients and waste material between HPCs and sinu-
soidal blood.
The primary cause of hepatic fibrosis/cirrhosis in humans 
worldwide is viral hepatitis; however, biliary obstruction and, in 
particular, alcoholic and nonalcoholic steatohepatitis are of grow-
ing public health importance for the development of hepatic fibrosis 
(Bataller and Brenner, 2005). In addition, fibrosis can be induced 
by chronic exposure to drugs and chemicals and by heavy metal 
overload (Gutierrez-Ruiz and Gomez-Quiroz, 2007). Repeated 
treatment with carbon tetrachloride, thioacetamide, dimethylni-
trosamine, aflatoxin, or other chemicals has been associated with 
hepatic fibrosis in experimental animals and humans (Zimmerman, 
1999). Indeed, just about any chronic insult to the liver that results 
in a rate of HPC death that exceeds the ability of the liver to repair 
itself can result in liver fibrosis.
Central to the development of fibrosis is the migration and 
activation of hepatic stellate cells and their differentiation into 
myofibroblast-like cells. Thus, stellate cells become the main cell 
type producing extracellular matrix proteins in the liver (Bataller 
and Brenner, 2005; Gressner and Weiskirchen, 2006; Rockey and 
Friedman, 2006). Clarifying the mechanisms underlying stellate 
cell activation is important to understanding the onset of fibrosis; 
however, it is important to note that other cell types (e.g., peribiliary 
fibroblasts, bone marrow-derived cells) can also become myofibro-
blastic. The increased accumulation of stellate cells at sites of injury 
is caused by their migration as well as proliferation. The prolifera-
tion of stellate cells is induced by the formation of mitogens such 
as platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF) and the upregulation of 
the PDGF receptor, which further enhances the responsiveness of 
stellate cells to this mitogen (Pinzani, 2002). PDGF, monocyte che-
motactic protein 1 (MCP-1), and other chemokines are stellate cell 
chemoattractants (Marra, 2002).
Stellate cell activation is initiated by products formed during 
liver cell injury. For example, activating signals can be reactive oxy-
gen species and lipid peroxidation products generated in injured 
HPCs. In addition, Kupffer cells can release reactive oxygen and 
pro-inflammatory cytokines during the phagocytosis of cell debris 
or apoptotic bodies, thereby recruiting more inflammatory cells 
and enhancing the injury and oxidant stress (Tsukamoto, 2002). 
Stellate cell activation is also driven by the excessive production of 
extracellular matrix proteins induced mainly by TGF-β1, which is 
generated to a large degree by this cell type. The effects of TGF-β1 
are amplified by the increased expression of TGF-β receptors on 
stellate cells (Gressner and Weiskirchen, 2006). Damaged sinusoi-
dal endothelial cells contribute to the activation of stellate cells by 
generating a splice variant of cellular fibronectin and by releasing 
urokinase-type plasminogen activator, which processes latent TGF-
β1 (Friedman, 2000). Furthermore, platelets accumulated at the site 
of injury can produce TGF-β1 and growth factors such as PDGF 
(Rockey and Friedman, 2006).
Together, these and other stimuli cause the activation of stellate 
cells, which undergo phenotypic changes that involve proliferation, 
fibrogenesis, matrix remodeling, and pro-inflammatory mediator 
expression, including production of chemotaxins for inflammatory 
cells (Rockey and Friedman, 2006; Friedman, 2008b). Another hall-
mark of stellate cell activation is enhanced contractility due to the 
increased expression of α-smooth muscle actin. Increased expression

Liver Cancers
Chemically induced neoplasia can involve tumors that are derived 
from HPCs as well as other cell types within the liver (Sell, 2002). A 
detailed discussion of chemical carcinogenesis is beyond the scope 
of this chapter but is discussed in Chap. 8. Hepatocellular cancer 
has been linked to chronic abuse of androgens, alcohol, and con-
sumption of aflatoxin-contaminated diets. In addition, viral hepa-
titis, metabolic diseases such as hemochromatosis, α1-antitrypsin 
deficiency, and nonalcoholic steatohepatitis are major risk factors 
for hepatocellular carcinoma (Zimmerman, 1999; McKillop et al., 
2006; Wands and Moradpour, 2006; Aravalli et al., 2008). A pro-
nounced synergy between dietary aflatoxin exposure and hepatitis 
B virus infection is now well recognized (Henry et al., 2002). The 
prevalence of hepatitis B and C viruses and environmental factors 
make hepatocellular carcinoma one of the most common malignant 
tumors worldwide (Bosch et al., 2005).
Nonparenchymal cells are the precursors for other types of 
cancers. Rare but highly malignant angiosarcomas are derived from 
sinusoidal lining cells. Angiosarcomas have been associated with 
occupational exposure to vinyl chloride and arsenic (Zimmerman, 
1999). Exposure to radioactive thorium dioxide used as contrast 
medium for radiology (Thorotrast) has been linked to tumors 
derived from HPCs, sinusoidal cells, and bile duct cells (cholan-
giocarcinoma) (Zimmerman, 1999). The compound accumulates 
in Kupffer cells and emits radioactivity throughout its extended 
half-life. One study of Danish patients exposed to Thorotrast found 
that the risk for bile duct and gallbladder cancers was increased 
14-fold and that for liver cancers more than 100-fold (Andersson 
and Storm, 1992). Furan is a chemical that causes cholangiocarci-
nomas experimentally in rats (Hickling et al., 2010).
CLASSIFICATION OF LIVER INJURY
Liver injury can be characterized in a number of different ways 
based on morphology (histopathology), serum or plasma biomark-
ers (clinical chemistry), or the nature of the toxic response (intrin-
sic vs. idiosyncratic). Each of these provides a different conceptual 
framework for classifying liver injury, which can be helpful in 
experimental and clinical settings.
Histopathology
In experimental studies with animals, histopathological evalu-
ation remains the “gold standard” for characterizing liver injury. 
Histopathology of liver biopsies is sometimes used to aid in diagno-
sis of liver injury in human patients, but only under certain circum-
stances because the procedure involves some risk to the patient. The 
histological pattern of acute liver toxicity often follows the lobular 
zonation described above in section “Hepatic Functional Anatomy.” 
Many toxicants, especially those that are metabolized to more toxic 
metabolites, cause necrosis of HPCs that occurs in a centrilobu-
lar distribution, that is, necrosis distributed around the central vein 
(Fig. 13-7, bottom panel). This is often because enzymes such vari-
ous isoforms of cytochromes P450 (CYPs) that bioactivate many 
chemicals to toxic metabolites occur in greater concentration in 
HPCs residing in centrilobular (zone 3) regions than in periportal 
(zone 1) or midzonal (zone 2) areas. However, other determinants 
of zonal HPC heterogeneity such as supply of oxygen and con-
centration of cofactors for metabolism (e.g., NADPH, glutathione) 
can also contribute to distribution of lesions. Xenobiotic agents that 
cause centrilobular lesions include halothane anesthetic (Benjamin 
et al., 1985; Dugan et al., 2010), pyrrolizidine alkaloid plant toxins 
(Copple et al., 2002), solvents such as carbon tetrachloride (Brattin 
et al., 1985), acetaminophen (McGill et al., 2013), and other drugs 
and many other chemicals. In contrast, liver lesions from chemicals 
such as aflatoxin B1 and allyl alcohol occur in periportal regions, 
whereas midzonal lesions predominate after systemic exposure to 
bacterial endotoxin (Sneed et al., 1997; Barton et al., 2000).
Clinical Chemistry
When the liver is injured, various molecular constituents of dam-
aged cells can be released into the blood. These constituents can 
be quantified in plasma or serum samples and serve as biomarkers 
that track with the severity of ongoing liver injury (Giannini et al., 
2005). Examples that are used in both clinical and experimental 
quantification of hepatocellular injury are alanine aminotransferase 
(ALT) and aspartate aminotransferase (AST). These enzymes are 
normally contained selectively within HPCs but are released into 
the plasma when these cells are injured by chemicals or by liver 
diseases. Other enzymes that occur selectively in HPCs include 
mitochondrial glutamate dehydrogenase (GLDH), ornithine car-
bamoyltransferase (OCT), and various dehydrogenases (e.g., lac-
tate dehydrogenase [LDH]). There are differences in these enzyme 
biomarkers of HPC injury with respect to their concentrations 
along the sinusoid, organellar associations, longevity in the plasma, 
occurrence in different species, and selective presence in liver rela-
tive to other organs. These differences determine specificity and 
sensitivity in detecting and quantifying injury caused by different 
mechanisms and arising from different lobular locations or species. 
For example, ALT occurs relatively selectively in liver, whereas 
AST occurs also in muscle and other tissues; therefore, AST is less 
selective as a biomarker of HPC injury (Vroon and Israili, 1990). 
In recent years, there has been interest in liver-selective microR-
NAs (e.g., miR-122), high mobility group box-1 (HMGB1) protein, 
and keratin-18 in plasma as HPC injury biomarkers, but these have 
yet to gain utility in clinical settings (Antoine et al., 2012, 2013; 
Hornby et al., 2014).
Other serum biomarkers are used to detect cholestatic injury. 
For example, bilirubin is a breakdown product of hemoglobin and 
is eliminated by uptake into HPCs, where it is conjugated with 
glucuronic acid and secreted into the bile by the MRP2 transporter 
(Zollner & Trauner, 2006). Reflux of conjugated bilirubin into the 
plasma (“direct bilirubin”) occurs during HPC injury or biliary 
obstruction (Westwood, 1991). As noted above, bile acids are nor-
mally synthesized in HPCs and exported into the biliary system. 
Reflux of bile acids (e.g., cholic acid, chenodeoxycholic acid) into

Alkaline phosphatase is expressed on the canalicular mem-

clinically as a biomarker of hepatobiliary injury. Such increases 
are thought to arise at least in part from enhanced accumulation

increases in plasma GGT activity are used in the diagnosis of bili-

et al., 2007). 5′-Nucleotidase (5NT) is another such enzyme that 
is localized to the canalicular and sinusoidal membranes of HPCs 
and released into plasma during liver injury. Notably, all of these 
enzymes occur in other organs and are therefore not specific for 
detection of liver injury (Martin et al., 2007); nevertheless, they are 
widely used for the detection of cholestatic liver injury.
In addition to these constituents that are released into plasma, 
injection of certain dyes has been used to assess HPC and biliary 
injury. For example, indocyanine green (ICG) is rapidly excreted 
by HPCs into the bile and has been used to quantify hepatocel-
lular function as well as liver blood flow (Schneider, 2004). Other 
tests of hepatic function rely on the synthesis of plasma proteins 
by the liver. HPCs synthesize and release into the circulation many 
coagulation factors, and coagulopathies therefore occur in patients 
with liver injury because they are less able to maintain adequate 
plasma concentrations of these proteins. Accordingly, increased tis-
sue factor-induced clotting time (i.e., prothrombin time) indicates a 
high likelihood of hepatic failure (Stravitz et al., 2007).
Because values for enzyme activities in serum/plasma from 
normal (i.e., healthy) people can vary across clinical chemistry lab-
oratories that perform the measurements, increases in the enzyme 
activities are typically expressed as a ratio normalized to the “upper 
limit of normal” (ULN) for the particular laboratory in which the 
measurement was performed. In humans, serum ALT activity that is 
3 to 5 times or more above the ULN suggests liver injury. Clinically, 
drug-induced liver injury is often classified as hepatocellular, chole-
static, or mixed (i.e., both), depending on clinical chemistry results. 
For this classification, a so-called “R-value” is calculated from 
serum enzyme activities, where R = (ALT/ALT ULN)/(ALP/ALP 
ULN). If R is >/= 5, the injury is considered to be hepatocellular; 
if R </=2 cholestatic, and if R >2 but <5 mixed. This provides 
a conceptual framework for the diagnosis of liver injury (Table 
13-3). Useful in predicting clinical prognosis in patients with liver 
injury is “Hy’s Law.” This is based on an observation by Dr Hyman 
Zimmerman that the combination of serum ALT > 3XULN and 
total bilirubin > 2XULN in patients with drug-induced liver injury 
portends a poor outcome (i.e., a risk of 10% mortality) (Chalasani 
et al., 2014).
Finally, there are a couple of important points to be made about 
clinical chemistry biomarkers. First, neither they nor histopatholog-
ical evaluation of liver injury can distinguish toxicant-induced liver 
injury in patients from liver disease caused by pathogens, genetic 
predisposition, or other etiologies. This can render diagnosing drug-
induced liver injury a challenge for physicians, and consequently 
sets of criteria have been developed to aid in differential diagnosis 
(Corsini et al., 2012; Chalasani et al., 2014). Second, although it is 
common to refer to clinical chemistry biomarkers as “liver function 
tests,” most of them are not measures of liver function. For example, 
increased serum ALT activity is a biomarker of HPC injury, but it 
does not necessarily reflect overall liver function; in contrast, an 
increase in serum bilirubin does reflect a dysfunction in the hepato-
biliary disposition of bilirubin. This distinction becomes important 
in some clinical scenarios. For example, some patients with severe, 
drug-induced liver injury experience an early rise in serum ALT 
activity followed by an increase in serum bilirubin as the injured 
liver becomes dysfunctional. However, as injury progresses ALT 
activity can trend toward normal while bilirubin continues to rise, 
the latter marking a worsening of liver function and portending a 
poor outcome.
Intrinsic versus Idiosyncratic Responses
A commonly used classification of drug-induced liver injury 
(DILI) is based on the extent to which an individual’s susceptibility 
enables the toxic response. “Intrinsic” toxic responses (aka, Type 
A responses or “toxic hepatitis”) are those that have a well-defined 
relationship to the dose of the offending chemical and are capable 
of producing injury in all individuals (Licata, 2016; Iasella et al., 
2017). There is often some difference in sensitivity among animals 
or among humans, but all individuals respond with toxicity at some 
dose. Intrinsic liver toxicity is associated with distinctive liver 
lesions that depend on the specific chemical and that occur after a 
predictable latent period. These reactions are initiated by a direct 
effect of the chemical or its metabolite(s) and are reproducible in 
experimental animals. Acetaminophen (APAP) is an example of a 
drug that causes intrinsic hepatotoxicity. When rodents or humans 
are exposed to APAP in overdose, centrilobular hepatocellular 
necrosis occurs in a predictable time period (e.g., within 6 hours in 
mice and within 48 hours in humans). Differences among individ-
ual human patients in the APAP dose needed to cause liver injury 
have been reported, and pronounced species and strain differences 
in sensitivity are evident in animals, but APAP is hepatotoxic at 
some dose in all animals (and probably in all humans). APAP is 
but one example of the many chemicals that cause intrinsic toxicity 
(see below for details on APAP hepatotoxicity).
“Idiosyncratic” drug reactions (Type B reactions) are adverse 
responses that occur in a minority of patients taking therapeu-
tic doses of a drug, and the liver is frequently the target organ 
(Kaplowitz, 2005; Roth and Ganey, 2011). Large drug doses (i.e., 
overdoses) are not needed to elicit toxicity. Drugs from many phar-
macological classes have caused idiosyncratic hepatotoxicity in 
human patients as have herbal medications, and it is likely that some 
environmental and occupational toxicants can precipitate idiosyn-
cratic responses as well. Typically, these reactions occur only in a 
very small fraction of patients during drug therapy. Accordingly, 
individual susceptibility plays a major role in determining whether 
an idiosyncratic response occurs upon exposure to a particular drug. 
With these reactions, the relationship to dose is usually unclear. It

INITIATION AND PROGRESSION OF 
HEPATOTOXICITY
The many molecular mechanisms by which toxicants can cause 
injury to cells and tissues are discussed in detail in Chap. 3 of this 
volume. The unique functions of the liver and its position in the 
circulation predispose it to toxicity-determining factors that do not 
apply to other organs. Depending on the chemical insult and the 
nature of the exposure, these can contribute to hepatotoxicity and 
can lead to one or more of the toxic manifestations described above 
in section “Liver Pathophysiology.” The pathogenesis of chemical-
induced liver injury can be viewed conceptually as comprising 
various molecular initiating events that trigger other events within 
and outside of HPCs that result in progression to HPC death and, 
consequently, to liver dysfunction (Fig. 13-11).
The initiation of injury typically begins with entry of a chemi-
cal into HPCs. Lipophilic compounds, particularly most drugs 
and environmental pollutants, readily diffuse into HPCs, and this 
is facilitated by the fenestrated endothelium in the sinusoids that 
enables close contact between circulating molecules and HPCs. 
Other toxicants are rapidly extracted from blood because they are 
substrates for uptake transporters located on the sinusoidal mem-
brane of HPCs (Hagenbuch and Gui, 2008; Roth et al., 2012). Once 
inside the cell, some toxicants exert their effects directly by bind-
ing reversibly to receptors or other proteins, whereas others are 
bioactivated to toxic metabolites by CYPs or other enzymes (see 
Chaps. 3 and 6 and Park et al., 2011). It is usually oxidative (phase 
I) reactions that yield reactive, toxic metabolites, whereas conju-
gation (phase II) reactions generally (but not always) yield stable 
metabolites. If the rate of production of a toxic metabolite exceeds 
the capacity of the HPC to detoxify it, HPC injury can ensue (Park 
et al., 2011). Thus, the balance between rates of phase I reactions 
and conjugating phase II reactions can determine whether a toxic 
metabolite is detoxified or causes injury. Depending on the chemi-
cal and the relevant bioactivating enzyme(s), xenobiotic metabo-
lism can result in production of damaging free radicals or reactive 
electrophiles that bind covalently to macromolecules such as DNA 
and intracellular proteins. Although there exists ample evidence 
that covalent binding of some xenobiotic metabolites to proteins 
initiates cell injury, the specific protein(s) that are critical to the 
response have not been identified for most toxicants that act by this 
mechanism. An exception is the binding of microcystins to cyto-
skeletal proteins (see below).
These various initiating events lead to the disruption of HPC 
homeostasis, often by activating intracellular cell death signaling 
pathways. This culminates in cellular ion imbalance, mitochon-
drial dysfunction, altered gene expression, and/or DNA damage 
through activation of caspases or other mechanisms. In addition 
to these intrahepatocellular events, injured HPCs can release vari-
ous factors that promote progression of liver injury. For example, 
calpain is a calcium-activated protease that leaks from damaged 
HPCs, becomes activated in the extracellular milieu, and dam-
ages neighboring cells by cleaving membrane-associated proteins 
(Mehendale and Limaye, 2005; Mehendale, 2013). Many so-
called damage-associated molecular pattern molecules (DAMPs) 
are released from injured HPCs. DAMPs activate pattern recog-
nition receptors (e.g., toll-like receptors) present on nonparen-
chymal cells such as SECs, Kupffer cells, NK cells, and stellate 
cells. In addition, some hepatotoxic responses are associated with 
disturbance of the intestinal epithelium, which can result in the 
translocation into the portal circulation of pathogen-associated 
molecular pattern molecules (PAMPs; e.g., lipopolysaccharide, 
LPS) produced and released by intestinal flora. Like DAMPs, 
these PAMPs stimulate nonparenchymal cells, activation of which 
results in generation of ROS and NO and release of many inflam-
matory mediators including numerous cytokines, prostanoids, 
growth factors, proteases, etc. Expression of adhesion molecules 
and release of chemokines by activated nonparenchymal cells or 
by HPCs result in the infiltration into the liver of blood leuko-
cytes and platelets. Activation of these cells prompts additional 
production of inflammatory mediators. In addition, the coagula-
tion and complement cascades become activated. Together, these 
inflammatory events and other factors (e.g., SEC injury) can either 
promote or ameliorate the progression of HPC injury by activating 
various cell death or cell survival pathways, respectively.

EXAMPLES OF HEPATOTOXICANTS AND 
THEIR MECHANISMS OF ACTION
In this section a few examples are presented of the many toxi-
cants that injure the liver in people and animals. Xenobiotic agents 
were chosen for discussion based on their importance to human 
poisoning and/or their use in animal models of human disease. 
Acetaminophen and ethanol are emphasized because of their 
importance in causing liver injury in people and because their 
mechanisms of action have been extensively researched.
Acetaminophen
One of the most widely used analgesics, acetaminophen (N-acetyl-
p-aminophenol; APAP) is a safe drug when used at therapeutically 
recommended doses. However, an overdose can cause severe liver 
injury and even liver failure in experimental animals and in humans 
(Lee, 2004, 2013). About half of all overdose cases are caused by 
suicide attempts, but an increasing number of cases are reported 
with unintentional overdosing (Lee, 2013). Although the toxicity is 
rare relative to the millions of patients taking the drug daily, APAP-
mediated liver injury represents a significant clinical problem. 
During the last two decades, APAP-induced hepatotoxicity became 
the most frequent cause of acute, drug-induced liver failure in the 
United States and the United Kingdom (Lee, 2004, 2013).
APAP poisoning results in centrilobular hepatocellular necro-

used extensively to study APAP metabolism and mechanisms of 
APAP hepatotoxicity. At therapeutic doses, approximately 90% of 
APAP is conjugated with sulfate or glucuronide and excreted. This 
limits formation by CYPs of a reactive, toxic metabolite, N-acetyl-
p-benzoquinone imine (NAPQI). Most of the NAPQI is detoxi-

of NAPQI with GSH, any manipulation that reduces hepatic GSH 
levels, for example, fasting or protein malnutrition, enhances the 
toxicity of APAP. In contrast, interventions such as the supply of 
cysteine, the rate-limiting amino acid for GSH synthesis, promote 
the detoxification of NAPQI and limit cell injury (Mitchell et al., 
1973). Based on this fundamental insight into the mechanism of 
APAP hepatotoxicity, N-acetylcysteine (NAC) was introduced into 
the clinic as intervention therapy (Prescott et al., 1977; Smilkstein 
et al., 1988). This highly successful therapeutic approach, which 
saved the lives of many patients who consumed an APAP overdose, 
is still the most effective treatment available (Rumack and Bateman, 
2012; Waring, 2012). More recent evidence indicates that NAC 
treatment not only promotes cytosolic GSH synthesis to detoxify 
NAPQI but also replenishes the depleted mitochondrial GSH, 
which scavenges reactive oxygen and peroxynitrite (see below). In 
addition, excess NAC is degraded and supports mitochondrial ATP 
generation (Saito et al., 2010b).

formation and the release of mitochondrial intermembrane proteins 
including endonuclease G and apoptosis-inducing factor (AIF)

the nucleus after APAP exposure, initiate nuclear DNA fragmenta-

of GSH-depleted mitochondria to peroxynitrite results in nitration 
of mitochondrial proteins and mitochondrial DNA modifications 
(Cover et al., 2005). The prolonged oxidant stress eventually trig-

pore with consequent breakdown of membrane potential, mitochon-

(Kon et al., 2004; Ramachandran et al., 2011). These events lead 
to the loss of mitochondrial ATP synthesis capacity, more extensive 
nuclear DNA fragmentation due to the amplified release of inter-
membrane proteins after the MPT, and eventually oncotic necrotic 
cell death (Gujral et al., 2002). Because of the central role of mito-

could be an important therapeutic target (Du et al., 2016).

an important contributor to APAP-induced hepatocellular necrosis.

Intrahepatocellular events leading to cell death from APAP are 
clearly complex, involving numerous factors and pathways. Even 
though some of these factors are involved in apoptotic pathways, 
the predominant histopathologic presentation is oncotic necrosis 
(Jaeschke and Bajt, 2006). Factors common to a “necroptotic” cell 
death, such as receptor interacting kinase protein 1 and 3 (RIP1/3), 
the critical executioner of necroptosis, participate in APAP-induced 
hepatocellular death; however, mixed lineage kinase domain like 
pseudokinase (MLKL), another necroptotic factor, is not involved. 
This suggests that APAP-induced cell death cannot be considered 
as necroptotic cell death but rather should be termed “programmed 
necrosis” (Ramachandran et al., 2013; Dara et al., 2015, 2016b).
In addition to these intracellular signaling mechanisms that 
are triggered directly by NAPQI and lead to cell death, secondary 
progression events can expand the area of necrosis (Fig. 13-15). 
For example, necrotic HPCs release Ca2+-activated proteases (cal-
pains) that can promote injury in nearby cells (Mehendale, 2012). 
Likewise, the release of DNase-1 enhances nuclear DNA fragmen-
tation in adjacent cells and aggravates the injury after APAP over-
dose (Napirei et al., 2006). Some of the propagation of the injury 
might also progress through intercellular connexin channels (gap 
junctions) (Maes and Vinken, 2017).
Sinusoidal cells and blood elements can contribute as well 
to progression of hepatocellular injury and its resolution in APAP 
overdose. For example, during APAP-induced necrosis there is 
extensive release of various damage-associated molecular pattern 
molecules (DAMPs) including HMGB1, mitochondrial DNA, 
and nuclear DNA fragments (Antoine et al., 2012; McGill et al., 
2012b). The release of these DAMPs from necrotic cells can acti-
vate resident macrophages (i.e., Kupffer cells) and recruit additional 
leukocytes (neutrophils, monocytes-derived macrophages) (Fig. 
13-15). The precise role of the inflammatory response in APAP-
induced liver injury is an area of intense investigation and continu-
ing debate, for which consensus has not been reached. Likewise, 
deliberation is ongoing regarding soluble inflammatory mediators 
such as cytokines, studies of which employing similar approaches 
have produced different conclusions, as is the case for inflamma-
some involvement (e.g., see Williams et al., 2011, Xie et al., 2013; 
Woolbright and Jaeschke, 2017). Indeed, although strong evidence 
indicates that inflammatory cell activation plays a critical role in 
clearance of necrotic debris and repair of the injured liver (Jaeschke 
et al., 2012b; You et al., 2013; Woolbright and Jaeschke, 2017), 
other studies suggest that inhibition of these same cellular processes 
also reduces liver injury (Mossanen et al., 2016).
Toxic doses of APAP activate the hemostatic system and cir-
culating complement (Fig. 13-15). Pharmacologic depletion of 
complement or genetic deficiency in complement factor C3 reduced 
APAP-induced liver injury in mice, suggesting a contribution of 
complement to the progression of hepatotoxicity (Singhal et al., 
2012). Also, in mice given a toxic dose of APAP, tissue factor-­
mediated activation of the coagulation system led to thrombin acti-
vation and hepatic fibrin deposition, and anticoagulants reduced liver 
injury early (6 hours) but not at later times (24 hours) (Ganey et al., 
2007; Sullivan et al., 2013; Kopec et al., 2017). These results sug-
gest that an activated coagulation system contributes to both injury 
and repair. Thrombin not only leads to deposition of fibrin but also 
activates protease-activated receptors (PARs) present on SECs and 
platelets. Interestingly, studies in knockout mice suggested that the 
deposition of fibrin does not contribute to the progression of liver

A treatise on the dichotomy in findings with respect to inflam-
mation and hemostasis in APAP hepatotoxicity is beyond the scope 
of this chapter, but the reader is encouraged to recognize that inflam-
matory cells and hemostatic factors may not have singular roles in 
APAP-induced liver damage. The receipt of distinct extracellular 
cues can change the roles of these players from damaging to protec-
tive and vice versa. Additional understanding of how functions of 
inflammatory cells and blood elements can change under various 
circumstances might ultimately settle longstanding controversies 
about their roles in progression of injury and initiation of repair.
Although many details of mechanisms still remain to be eluci-
dated, the newly gained knowledge into signaling events in response 
to APAP overdose suggests two fundamental insights. First, necrotic 
cell death is in most cases not caused by a single catastrophic event 
but can be the result of a cellular stress that is initiated by metabolic 
activation of APAP and which triggers sophisticated intra- and inter-
cellular signaling mechanisms culminating in cell death. Second, the 
multitude of events following the initial stress offers many oppor-
tunities for therapeutic interventions at later times during injury 
progression. Because these events do not occur in all cells to the 
same degree and at the same time, delayed interventions might not 
completely prevent cell damage but might limit the area of necrosis 
enough to allow tissue regeneration and prevent liver failure. After 
APAP bioactivation in mice was complete, treatment with GSH or 
NAC to accelerate the recovery of mitochondrial GSH concentra-
tion effectively scavenged peroxynitrite, reduced the area of necro-
sis, and promoted regeneration, resulting in improved survival after 
APAP overdose (Bajt et al., 2003; Du et al., 2016). Equally effec-
tive in preventing APAP hepatotoxicity is the mitochondria-targeted 
superoxide dismutase-mimetic, Mito-TEMPO (Du et al., 2017). 
Overexpression of calpastatin, an inhibitor of calpains, also attenu-
ated APAP-induced liver injury and enhanced survival (Limaye et 
al., 2006). Delayed treatment with inhibitors of JNK or of upstream 
MAP kinases such as apoptosis signal-­regulating kinase 1 (ASK1) 
attenuated the mitochondrial oxidant stress and prevented the MPT 
and tissue injury in mice (Hanawa et al., 2008; Saito et al., 2010a; 
Du et al., 2015). Together these findings underscore the concept that 
the later stages of APAP-induced liver cell necrosis can be affected 
at the level of intracellular signaling in HPCs and during the propa-
gation of the injury to neighboring cells.
A significant sensitivity factor in APAP hepatotoxicity can be 
the consumption of alcoholic beverages. In addition to potential 
malnutrition in alcoholic individuals, ethanol is a potent inducer of 
CYP2E1, which is the main enzyme responsible for the metabolic 
activation of APAP in humans (Gonzalez, 2007). Whereas simulta-
neous exposure to ethanol and APAP competitively inhibits NAPQI 
formation at the CYP2E1 locus and thereby can reduce APAP-
induced toxicity (Sato and Lieber, 1981), the ethanol-induced 
increase in expression of CYP2E1 can enhance APAP toxicity after 
ethanol is eliminated (Gonzalez, 2007). Despite the clear evidence 
in experimental animals that alcohol consumption can increase the 
susceptibility to APAP (Sato et al., 1981; Sinclair et al., 2000) and 
the clinical observation of severe APAP hepatotoxicity in alcohol-
ics, it remains controversial whether alcohol can actually induce 
hepatotoxicity at therapeutic doses of APAP in people, as sug-
gested by some case reports (Zimmerman and Maddrey, 1995). An 
extensive review of the literature involving APAP consumption in 
alcoholics suggests no relevant risk for APAP hepatotoxicity at ther-
apeutic levels in this patient population (Rumack et al., 2012). In 
addition, a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial with 
multiple therapeutic doses of APAP showed no evidence of liver 
dysfunction or cell injury in alcoholics (Dart et al., 2010). Thus, 
alcohol consumption does not appear to increase the risk for liver 
injury after therapeutic doses of APAP. Nevertheless, consistent 
with experimental data and clinical experience, inducers of CYPs 
can aggravate liver injury after a hepatotoxic dose of APAP. This 
finding may apply as well to the potential interactions of APAP with 
other drugs and dietary chemicals.
Much of the understanding of mechanisms of APAP toxic-
ity has derived from studies in very useful murine models that 
recapitulate almost all aspects of APAP-induced liver injury in 
humans. Although the progress in understanding the complexity 
of APAP-induced liver injury during the last decade is encourag-
ing, the critical question as to whether these mechanisms of cell 
death translate to human pathophysiology has only recently been 
addressed (Jaeschke et al., 2014). Studies employing metabolically 
competent human hepatoma cells (HepaRG cells) and primary 
human HPCs showed that key events in APAP toxicity occur also 
in human cells. These events include reactive metabolite formation 
with GSH depletion and mitochondrial protein adduct formation, 
oxidant stress, JNK activation and translocation of phospho-JNK 
to mitochondria, mitochondrial dysfunction, and necrotic cell death 
(Gunawan et al., 2006; McGill et al., 2011; Saberi et al., 2014; Xie 
et al., 2014). Moreover, assessments of serum biomarkers in humans 
who overdosed with APAP provide evidence for mitochondrial 
damage and nuclear DNA fragmentation similar to mice (McGill 
et al., 2012b). Importantly, the observation that poor survival cor-
relates with release of mitochondrial biomarkers suggests that 
mitochondrial damage is critical for the injury process in humans 
(McGill et al., 2014). During APAP-induced necrosis in human 
patients, there is also extensive release of various DAMPs, includ-
ing HMGB1, mitochondrial DNA, and nuclear DNA fragments 
(Antoine et al., 2012; McGill et al., 2012a, 2012b). Evaluation of 
peripheral blood neutrophils from patients demonstrated enhanced 
phagocytosis and priming for ROS generation during the recov-
ery phase after injury (Williams et al., 2014). In addition, 
monocyte-derived macrophages recruited into the liver show an 
anti-­inflammatory and proregenerative phenotype (Antoniades 
et al., 2012). Moreover, activation of coagulation occurs in APAP-
poisoned people, and this might portend a poor outcome (Harrison 
et al., 1990; Kerr et al., 2003). Together, these results support the 
mouse model of APAP-induced liver injury as an appropriate reflec-
tion of the human pathophysiology.

As alcohol-induced liver disease progresses, appreciable cell death 
occurs alongside increasing hepatic inflammation (i.e., steatohepati-
tis). If left unchecked, these pathologic processes drive replacement 
of functional liver mass with scar tissue (Bataller and Gao, 2015). 
This results in impairment of many functions of liver, including a 
progressive reduction in capacity for biotransformation of drugs. 
People with hepatic cirrhosis due to chronic alcohol abuse frequently 
become deficient at detoxifying both the ammonia formed by catabo-
lism of amino acids and the bilirubin derived from breakdown of 
hemoglobin. Such hepatic dysfunction combined with defects in syn-
thesis of key proteins, such as albumin and clotting factors, can ulti-
mately drive multiple organ dysfunction and death (Gao and Bataller, 
2011). Thus, ethanol provides a highly relevant example of a toxicant 
to which exposure contributes significantly to liver-related morbidity 
(Gao and Bataller, 2011).
Morbidity and mortality associated with the consumption of 
alcohol is mainly caused by the toxic effects of ethanol and its 
metabolites on the liver (Stewart and Day, 2006), although effects 
of alcohol on other tissues are increasingly appreciated (Yeligar 
et al., 2016). This targeted toxicity is observed because >90% of 
a dose of ethanol is metabolized in the liver. There are three prin-
cipal pathways of ethanol metabolism (Fig. 13-16). Alcohol dehy-
drogenase (ADH) is quantitatively the most important pathway for 
alcohol metabolism. ADH oxidizes ethanol to acetaldehyde (Km of 
1 mM); the electrons are transferred to NAD+, which leads to the 
production of NADH. Acetaldehyde is further oxidized to acetate, 
also in a NAD+-dependent reaction, by acetaldehyde dehydroge-
nase (ALDH). This pathway is mainly limited by the mitochondrial 
capacity to utilize NADH and regenerate NAD+ (Stewart and Day, 
2006).
Both ADH and ALDH exhibit genetic polymorphisms and 
ethnic variations that contribute to risk of alcoholism and ethanol-
mediated liver damage (Agarwal, 2001; Day, 2006). One example of 
a toxicologically important polymorphism involves mitochondrial 
ALDH2, for which the ALDH2*2 form shows little or no catalytic 
activity. The resultant increased concentration of toxic acetaldehyde 
present in individuals who carry this polymorphism is thought to 
cause the uncomfortable “flushing” syndrome that accompanies 
ethanol exposure. This inactive form of ALDH is found in 50% 
of Asians but is absent in Caucasians. The discomfort experienced 
by people with this polymorphism might contribute the lower 
incidence of alcoholism in Asia compared to Europe and North 
America (Chen et al., 1999). However, heterozygotes of ALDH2*2 
were found to develop more severe liver injury in response to lower 
alcohol consumption, suggesting a higher susceptibility to alcoholic 
liver disease (Enomoto et al., 1991). Polymorphisms also exist in 
ADH, some of which could lead to more rapid production of acet-
aldehyde (Wall, 2005). These findings underscore the importance of 
acetaldehyde in the pathophysiology of alcohol toxicity.
Although most ethanol is metabolized in the liver by ADH, 
a second pathway of importance involves the alcohol-inducible 
enzyme, CYP2E1, which, like ADH, oxidizes ethanol to acetalde-
hyde (Fig. 13-16). This enzyme is located predominantly in HPCs 
of the centrilobular region and requires both oxygen and NADPH. 
Because the Km of CYP2E1 for ethanol is >10 mM, this reaction 
is most relevant for large doses of ethanol, and because expression

The mechanisms of alcohol-induced liver disease are complex 
and still incompletely understood. Evolution of the rodent models 
used to mimic alcohol-induced liver damage in people has been 
reviewed elsewhere (Mathews et al., 2014; Gao et al., 2017); there 
may not be a single animal model of ethanol-induced liver damage 
that recapitulates the entire spectrum of pathologies observed in 
human chronic alcoholism. Hepatocellular steatosis is a common 
feature of chronic alcohol consumption. Alcohol-mediated steato-
sis is caused by the excessive supply of acetate and NADH, which 
increases fatty acid synthesis. In addition, ethanol and acetaldehyde 
disrupt constitutive regulation of fatty acid metabolizing enzymes 
by impairing DNA binding of the transcription factor, peroxisome 
proliferator-activated receptor-α (PPAR-α) (Aoyama et al., 1998). 
In addition to this imbalance favoring increased fatty acid synthesis, 
ethanol exposure inhibits the transfer of triglycerides from liver to 
adipose tissue. Acetaldehyde inhibits the microsomal triglyceride 
transfer protein, which incorporates triglycerides into VLDL (Lin 
et al., 1997) and reduces VLDL release from HPCs by interfering 
with microtubular function (Kannarkat et al., 2006). These effects 
of ethanol and its metabolites can be compounded in the presence 
of a high-fat diet. Although steatosis alone is not considered a 
form of severe liver disease, it represents an early and permissive 
phase in the spectrum of liver pathologies caused by alcohol expo-
sure (Day and James, 1998a, 1998b; Tsukamoto et al., 2009). In the 
two-hit hypothesis of alcoholic liver disease, steatosis is considered 
the “first hit,” which requires a “second hit” to drive simple steatosis 
to the necroinflammatory change observed in severe alcoholic liver 
injury (Day and James, 1998a, 1998b). This should not exclude the 
possibility that lipotoxicity itself is a critical determinant of dis-
ease progression (Neuschwander-Tetri, 2010). Moreover, results of 
recent studies suggest that inflammatory events triggered by ethanol 
can occur independently of steatosis (Wang et al., 2017).
CYP2E1 is a source of reactive oxygen formation during etha-
nol metabolism (Dey and Cederbaum, 2006). The resultant intracel-
lular oxidant stress in HPCs can ultimately induce mitochondrial 
dysfunction and cell death of HPCs, but it also activates stellate 
cells and promotes fibrosis (Dey and Cederbaum, 2006). Direct 
evidence implicates CYP2E1 function in alcohol-induced patholo-
gies, including gut leakiness and liver pathology (Abdelmegeed 
et al., 2013). In addition to the intracellular events, alcohol exposure 
causes an inflammatory response, which contributes to the oxidant 
stress (Arteel, 2003; Hines and Wheeler, 2004). Gut-derived endo-
toxin and other bacteria-derived products leaking into the portal cir-
culation from a leaky gut can activate Kupffer cells through toll-like 
receptor activation to produce reactive oxygen species and cyto-
kines, such as TNF-α (Seki and Schnabl, 2012). The formation of 
these mediators can be amplified by feedback loops, which enhance 
cytokine and chemokine formation through priming of the redox-
sensitive transcription factor, NF-κB, in Kupffer cells (Arteel, 
2003). In addition, TNF-α can increase expression of inducible 
nitric oxide synthase (iNOS, NOS2) leading to the formation of 
peroxynitrite, a potent oxidant species capable of nitrating pro-
teins (Arteel, 2003). TNF-α can also directly promote cell death by 
acting on HPCs, which are rendered sensitive by ethanol-induced 
depletion of mitochondrial GSH (Colell et al., 1998; Nagai et al., 
2002).
Inhibition of the proteasome pathway, a well-recognized 
feature of chronic alcohol exposure, can enhance chemokine for-
mation in HPCs and promote inflammatory liver injury (McClain 
et al., 2005). Additional pro-inflammatory mediators and immune 
responses can be triggered by protein adducts of acetaldehyde and 
by lipid peroxidation products (Freeman et al., 2005) as well as 
by DAMPs released from HPCs and PAMPS (e.g., LPS) translo-
cated from the intestine into the portal circulation (Miller et al., 
2011) (Fig. 13-17). Interestingly, ethanol inhibits hepatic NK cells. 
Because NK cells can kill HSCs, the major cell type promoting 
hepatic fibrosis, ethanol may indirectly support fibrogenesis by 
preventing the elimination of activated stellate cells (Miller et al., 
2011). More recently, studies indicate that invariant NKT cells con-
tribute to alcohol-induced liver damage by recruiting inflammatory 
cells such as neutrophils (Cui et al., 2015; Maricic et al., 2015; 
Mathews et al., 2016). A defense mechanism that is activated dur-
ing alcoholic liver disease is autophagy, which can remove dam-
aged cell organelles and modified proteins (Ding et al., 2011) and 
thereby reduce the activation of the innate immune response. Thus, 
alcoholic liver disease is a complex interplay between the activa-
tion of pro-cell death mechanisms (reactive metabolite formation, 
oxidant stress, protein adducts, and stimulation of pro-inflammatory 
and profibrotic innate immune responses) and activation of defense 
mechanisms (antioxidants, autophagy, and NK cell activation) (Gao 
and Bataller [2011]).

Allyl Alcohol
Allyl alcohol is an industrial chemical used in the production of 
resins, plastics, and fire retardants and has been used as a model 
hepatotoxicant due to its preferential periportal (zone 1) hepatotox-
icity. The alcohol is metabolized by ADH to acrolein, a highly reac-
tive aldehyde (Fig. 13-18), which is then further oxidized by ALDH 
to acrylic acid. The observations that the toxicity depends on deple-
tion of hepatic GSH and is prevented by inhibitors of ADH but 
enhanced by inhibitors of ALDH suggest that acrolein formation 
is the critical event in liver injury (Jaeschke et al., 1987; Rikans, 
1987). Age and gender differences in allyl alcohol hepatotoxicity 
can be explained by variations in the balance between ADH and 
ALDH expression (Rikans and Moore, 1987).
The occurrence of allyl alcohol injury preferentially in zone 
1 HPCs (Fig. 13-18) is caused by the predominant uptake of allyl 
alcohol in the periportal region and the oxygen dependence of the 
toxicity (Badr et al., 1986). Although protein binding of the acro-
lein and subsequent adduct formation appears to be the main cause 
of liver cell death (Kaminskas et al., 2004), lipid peroxidation can 
become an important mechanism of cell injury under conditions 
of a compromised antioxidant status or in the presence of excess 
iron (Jaeschke et al., 1987, 1992; Pompella et al., 1988; Knight 
et al., 2003). Lipid peroxidation is caused by a reductive stress dur-
ing which excessive NADH formation produced by allyl alcohol 
metabolism leads to mobilization of redox-active iron from storage 
proteins (Jaeschke et al., 1992).
Carbon Tetrachloride
CCl4 was once a widely used solvent, but human exposure to it 
has been restricted due to recognition that it is a potent hepato-
toxicant. Acute exposure to CCl4 causes centrilobular necrosis in 
animals and humans (Fig. 13-19). It has been used widely to model 
liver injury in animals, with its current use heavily weighted toward 
studying fibrosis (Fig. 13-19). Cytochrome P450-dependent con-
version of CCl4 to trichloromethyl free radical (•CCl3) and then to 
the trichloromethyl peroxyl radical (CCl3OO•) is a classic example 
of xenobiotic bioactivation to a free radical capable of initiating 
lipid peroxidation by abstracting hydrogen atoms from polyunsatu-
rated fatty acids in phospholipid membranes (Fig. 13-19; Recknagel 
et al., 1989; Weber et al., 2003). Metabolic activation of CCl4 in 
vivo involves primarily CYP2E1 as indicated by the absence 
of CCl4 hepatotoxicity in CYP2E1 knockout mice (Wong et al., 
1998). CCl4-induced lipid peroxidation increases the permeability 
of the plasma membrane to Ca2+, leading to severe disturbances 
in calcium homeostasis and consequent necrotic cell death (Weber 
et al., 2003). CCl4 also induces significant mitochondrial damage, 
which is dependent on CYP2E-mediated metabolism and lipid 
peroxidation (Knockaert et al., 2012). In addition, the •CCl3 radi-
cal can bind directly to tissue macromolecules, as can some of the 
lipid peroxidation products such as 4-hydroxynonenal, which can 
form adducts with cellular proteins (Weber et al., 2003). In addition

Aflatoxins
Fungi of various species synthesize metabolites known as myco-
toxins of several types that cause injury to numerous organs. 
Aflatoxins are mycotoxins produced by Aspergillus molds that 
grow on nuts and crops such as corn, wheat, and rice (Fig. 13-20; 
Bennett and Klich, 2003; Bui-Klimke et al., 2014), and consump-
tion of these foods results in exposure to humans and animals 
(Eaton and Groopman, 1994). Aflatoxins have also been detected in 
milk of animals that consume contaminated crops, and this can lead 
to exposure of young children (Montesano et al., 1997; Creppy, 
2002). Exposure to aflatoxins is considered unavoidable, and the 
U.S. FDA has set tolerance limits in foods for human consumption 
(Groopman et al., 2008). Such regulations have typically resulted 
in minimal exposure from contaminated foods in most Western 
countries, but exposure is substantially greater in many develop-
ing countries, especially in Southeast Asia and Africa, where there 
is less regulation of aflatoxin concentrations in food (Schleicher 
et al., 2013).

has happened rarely in humans, but sometimes with devastating 
consequences. One poisoning outbreak in Kenya in 2004 to 2005

Chronic exposure to aflatoxins in feed causes hepatocellular 
carcinoma in animals, and numerous epidemiological studies have 
supported an association with cancer in humans (Ross et al., 1992; 
Qian et al., 1994; Liu et al., 2012). Although several aflatoxins 
occur in dietary sources, aflatoxin B1 (AFB1) occurs in the greatest 
concentration and in animal studies is one of the most potent muta-
gens and carcinogens known (Wang and Groopman, 1999; Creppy, 
2002). Chronic exposure to 15ppb AFB1 in the diet of Fisher rats 
resulted in a high incidence of liver tumors (Wogan et al., 1974). Of 
the various aflatoxins, AFB1 is also the most hepatotoxic acutely, 
producing periportal necrosis in rats (Clifford and Rees, 1967; 
Barton et al., 2000). Notably, there are marked species differences 
in sensitivity to AFB1 (e.g., rats, trout>>mice), and sensitivity to 
hepatotoxicity is influenced within an animal species by nutritional 
status, strain, age, and sex (Cullen and Newberne, 1994).
All aflatoxins are metabolized in liver by oxidation, hydro-
lysis, reduction, and conjugation reactions, some of which lead to 
toxic products and others to inactive metabolites. AFB1 is oxidized 
by various isoforms of CYPs. Although most of the products lack 
significant toxicity, oxidation of the furan ring leads to a reactive 
epoxide that alkylates DNA (Swenson et al., 1977; Eaton et al., 
1995; Lin et al., 2014) and that can be further metabolized to a dial-
dehyde that alkylates proteins (Fig. 13-20). The DNA adducts are 
thought to initiate liver cancer, whereas protein adducts appear to 
cause acute hepatotoxicity. Conjugation with GSH is an important 
pathway for detoxification of the reactive epoxide, which is also 
hydrolyzed both spontaneously and enzymically by epoxide hydro-
lases. The relative activities of the bioactivating and detoxifying 
enzymes appear to be an important determinant of DNA adduct 
formation and, by extension, to sensitivity to the mutagenic and car-
cinogenic effects of AFB1 (Buetler et al., 1992; Kew, 2013; Shen 
et al., 2014). In this regard, combinations of polymorphisms in sev-
eral genes encoding enzymes that metabolize AFB1 are associated 
with increased risk of developing hepatocellular carcinoma (Kirk et 
al., 2005). In addition, there exists a pronounced synergy between 
AFB exposure and infection with hepatitis B virus in causing hepa-
tocellular carcinoma in humans (Qian et al., 1994; Kew, 2013). 
Recent evidence suggests that hepatitis C virus might also synergize 
with AFB1 (Moudgil et al., 2013). Overall, it is clear that dietary 
AFB exposure causes acute hepatotoxicity and contributes signifi-
cantly to relatively high rates of liver cancer in areas where exposure 
is high, especially in regions where hepatitis B infection is prevalent.

size (Hoorn et al., 1993; Lappin et al., 1998). A prominent feature 
of pyrrolizidine alkaloid hepatotoxicity is the pronounced destruc-
tion of SECs as well as endothelial cells of central venules (Copple 
et al., 2002). The SEC destruction leads to marked hemorrhage (Fig. 
13-21) and is associated with centrilobular tissue hypoxia (Copple 
et al., 2006). Monocrotaline pyrrole is detoxified primarily by con-
jugation with GSH (Yan and Huxtable, 1996), and it is likely that 
monocrotaline bioactivation and profound GSH depletion in endo-
thelial cells renders them highly sensitive to injury from monocro-
taline (DeLeve et al., 1996; Wang et al., 2000).
Leukocytes such as Kupffer cells and neutrophils do not seem 
to play damaging roles in monocrotaline hepatotoxicity, but the 
SEC injury that occurs leads to activation of the coagulation system, 
which contributes to tissue hypoxia and is involved in the progres-
sion of liver injury (Copple et al., 2002, 2003, 2006). In addition, 
the activity of matrix metalloproteinases is increased in rodents 
after monocrotaline exposure, and inhibitors of these proteases pre-
vented injury, suggesting that they contribute to the degradation of 
basement membrane and consequent loss of SECs that progresses 
to sinusoidal destruction (Hanumegowda et al., 2003; DeLeve 
et al., 2003a). A reduction in production of protective NO by SECs 
appears to contribute to the increase in matrix metalloproteinase 
activity (DeLeve et al., 2003b).
The swelling and destruction of SECs and their dissection 
from HPCs lead to emboli comprising SEC aggregates, red blood 
cells, and monocytes (DeLeve et al., 2003a). These events underlie 
progression into the sinusoidal obstruction syndrome (see section 
“Sinusoidal Endothelial Cell Damage”) that characterizes chronic 
pyrrolizidine alkaloid toxicity. Although the sinusoidal obstruc-
tion syndrome can be produced experimentally in laboratory ani-
mals by a single administration of a pyrrolizidine alkaloid such 
as monocrotaline, human intoxication more typically results from 
subacute or chronic exposure to pyrrolizidine alkaloids (Huxtable, 
1989). Clinically, human sinusoidal obstruction syndrome can be 
divided into acute, subacute, and chronic phases (Stuart and Bras, 
1957). The acute phase is characterized by a rapid onset of nau-
sea, emesis, abdominal pain, hepatomegaly, and ascites (Stillman 
et al., 1977; Sperl et al., 1995; Coulombe, 2003). This phase can be 
relatively short in duration, either resolving or progressing to the 
subacute phase, which involves persistent hepatomegaly, prominent 
megalocytosis, and recurrent ascites. The chronic phase consists 
of cirrhosis and liver failure, which can occur many months after 
pyrrolizidine alkaloid exposure (Stillman et al., 1977; Coulombe, 
2003). This syndrome has a poor long-term prognosis, with death 
occurring up to 2 years after exposure (Coulombe, 2003).

and transport of ANIT into the bile compartment renders biliary 
epithelium a proximal target of ANIT. GSH conjugation plays a 
permissive role in the biliary transport and accumulation of ANIT. 
GSH concentration in bile increases in parallel with ANIT after 
oral administration, and GSH depletion limits ANIT transport into 
bile (Jean and Roth, 1995). Additional studies revealed that an 
ANIT-GSH conjugate produced within the HPC is transported into 
bile by the canalicular transporter, Mrp2 (Dietrich et al., 2001). The 
GSH S-conjugate of ANIT is unstable at physiological pH and dis-
sociates, resulting in large concentrations of free ANIT in the bile 
(Fig. 13-22). Depletion of GSH or Mrp2 deficiency reduced ANIT 
toxicity, highlighting the importance of biliary ANIT transport to 
its toxicity (Jean and Roth, 1995; Jean et al., 1995).
Accumulation of ANIT in bile damages intrahepatic bile ducts 
along with epithelial surfaces of extrahepatic tissues that contact 
bile, including gall bladder epithelium and extrahepatic bile ducts 
(McLean and Rees, 1958; Plaa and Priestly, 1976; Ungar and Popp, 
1976). A single, oral administration of ANIT produces acute biliary 
injury, disruption of bile flow, compensatory biliary hyperplasia, 
and portal neutrophilic inflammation. As the injury progresses, peri-
portal hepatocellular necrosis becomes evident. These lesions are 
formed as a consequence of neutrophil accumulation and activation 
and resemble so-called “bile infarcts” observed hours after experi-
mental ligation of the common bile duct (Plaa and Priestly, 1976; 
Roth and Dahm, 1997; Woolbright and Jaeschke, 2012). In mice, 
either prior depletion of neutrophils or neutralization of IL-17, a 
neutrophil chemoattractant, suppressed the hepatotoxicity of ANIT 
(Dahm et al., 1991; Kobayashi et al., 2010). In mice lacking the 
leukocytic beta-2-integrin, CD18, ANIT-induced HPC injury was 
reduced (Kodali et al., 2006), and studies in vitro indicated that 
ANIT causes bile duct epithelial cells to release a factor(s) that 
attracts neutrophils and stimulates them to injure HPCs (Fig. 13-22; 
Hill et al., 1999).
Persistent exposure of mice to ANIT for >4 weeks effectively 
recapitulates biliary fibrosis associated with chronic cholestatic 
liver diseases in humans, including primary sclerosing cholangitis. 
Much like Mdr2−/− mice, “onion skin”-like layering of collagen is 
observed surrounding intrahepatic bile ducts after chronic ANIT 
challenge. Strong evidence indicates engagement of immune cells, 
biliary epithelium, and the hemostatic system in this model of bili-
ary fibrosis (Tjandra et al., 2000; Sullivan et al., 2010; Golbar et al., 
2013; Joshi et al., 2016; Joshi et al., 2017).
Cyclic Peptide Hepatotoxins: Microcystins, 
Nodularins, Phallotoxins, Amatoxins
Several cyclic peptides synthesized by fresh water cyanobacteria 
or by toxic mushrooms are highly potent hepatotoxins, producing 
liver injury in humans and/or animals at doses below 1 mg/kg body 
weight.

80 cyclic heptapeptide toxins that are produced by cyanobacteria 
(“blue-green algae”; e.g., Microcystis aeruginosa) that bloom in 
freshwater lakes and ponds (Fig. 13-23; Labine and Minuk, 2009). 
Consumption of contaminated water from these blooms in several 
countries has resulted in injury to liver and other organs in fish, 
domestic animals, and humans (Bischoff, 2001; Svircev et al.,

The main toxicity target for microcystin LR is the liver, where 
acute injury is associated with blebbing and necrosis of HPCs, 
congestion, and hemorrhage (Hooser et al., 1989, 1990; Falconer 
and Yeung, 1992). These effects occur rapidly in rodents (Hooser 
et al., 1989). Microcystin dramatically deforms HPCs (Hooser et al., 
1991), and these deformities produce a secondary change in the 
structural integrity of the sinusoid. Chronic exposure is associated 
with hypertrophy, karyomegaly, and multinucleation of HPCs (Clark 
et al., 2007) and can result ultimately in fibrosis and/or hepatocellu-
lar carcinoma (Frangez et al., 2000; Svircev et al., 2010; Fujiki and 
Suganuma, 2011). Microcystin LR affects hepatocellular microtu-
bules, the function of which is critical to normal cell division and 
to maintaining normal cell structure. Acute exposure causes aggre-
gation of cytokeratin-containing intermediate filaments and actin 
microfilaments, which detach from the plasma membrane, resulting 
in bleb formation (Hooser et al., 1990; Chorus and Bartram, 1999).
Mechanisms of acute microcytstin LR toxicity have been stud-
ied mostly in cultured cells and in rodents, in which centrilobular 
necrosis as well as injury to bile duct epithelial cells occurs (Solter 
et al., 1998; Lu et al., 2008). Microcystin LR binds to cysteine resi-
dues on protein phosphatase (PP)-2A and PP-1 and inhibits these 
enzymes, which are involved in dephosphorylation of many proteins 
critical for intracellular signaling that controls cellular functions 
such as expression of proteins, cell proliferation, cell differentia-
tion, and cell death (Honkanen et al., 1990; Runnegar et al., 1993, 
1995; Garcia et al., 2003; Van Hoof and Goris, 2003). Small doses of 
microcystin that are insufficient to produce cellular structural defor-
mations diminish uptake and secretory functions of HPCs associ-
ated with hyperphosphorylation of the cytoplasmic motor protein, 
dynein (Runnegar et al., 1999). Dynein is a mechanochemical pro-
tein that drives vesicles along microtubules, using energy from ATP 
hydrolysis. Central to the hydrolysis of dynein-bound ATP is a cycle 
of kinase phosphorylation and phosphatase dephosphorylation. 
Thus, hyperphosphorylation of dynein freezes this motor pump. 
Microcystin LR also activates phosphorylase, which in turn can 
result in glycogen and glutathione depletion, oxidative stress, and 
increased intracellular free calcium that promote mitochondrial dys-
function and apoptosis in rat HPCs (Ding et al., 2000a, 2000b, 2001; 
Ding and Nam Ong, 2003; Bouaïcha et al., 2005). However, micro-
cystin LR caused necrosis in primary human HPCs (Woolbright 
et al., 2017). Activation of NF-κB and MAPK pathways has also 
been reported in primary HPCs exposed to microcystin LR at 
low nM concentrations, suggesting an immunomodulatory action 
(Zhang et al., 2013). It is to be expected that exposure to a chemical 
that inhibits phosphatases involved in many cellular signaling path-
ways would demonstrate numerous adverse effects on cell biology, 
and that certainly is the case with microcystins.
Importantly, other hepatotoxins are produced during blooms 
of cyanobacteria (Dittmann and Wiegand, 2006; Chen et al., 
2013). For example, nodularins are cyclic pentapeptides produced 
by Nodularia spumigena and are potent liver toxins (Chen et al., 
2013). They cause many of the same molecular alterations as 
microcystins, such as phosphatase inhibition, oxidative stress, and 
intracellular calcium changes (Bouaïcha et al., 2005; Dittmann and 
Wiegand, 2006; Lankoff et al., 2006; Solstad et al., 2008; Fujiki and 
Suganuma, 2011; Ufelmann et al., 2012; Ufelmann and Schrenk, 
2015). In addition, nodularin caused endoplasmic reticular stress, 
activation of MAPKs, and expression of TNF in HPCs in vitro 
(Meili et al., 2016). Thus, several hepatotoxins are produced by cya-
nobacterial blooms in freshwater sources. They disrupt numerous 
signaling pathways critical for maintenance of normal homeostasis 
and cause liver injury at very small doses. There is concern that 
global climate change will lead to more frequent and intense algal 
blooms and thereby enhance the risk of cyanobacterial poisoning in 
the future (Lürling et al., 2017).
Mushroom Toxins  There are several toxic, cyclic peptides that 
occur in various species of wild mushrooms. These mushrooms 
have been mistaken for edible species, and consumption of them 
has been responsible for numerous poisonings and deaths over the 
years, especially in Europe but also in North America. Of these 
mushroom species, Amanita phalloides (“death cap”) has been 
of greatest concern due to its ability to cause life-threatening tox-
icity (Fig. 13-23; Garcia et al., 2015). This mushroom produces 
several bicyclic heptapeptides known as phallotoxins and octa-
peptides known as amatoxins. Of these, phalloidin and α-amanitin 
(Fig. 13-23), respectively, have received the most attention as 
hepatotoxins.
Phalloidin is taken into HPCs by transporters of the OATP 
family (Lu et al., 2008) and causes cholestasis and hemorrhagic 
necrosis in livers after intraperitoneal administration to rodents 
(Vonk et al., 1982; Loranger et al., 1985; Ishizaki et al., 1997). 
Although phalloidin is a potent hepatotoxin, it is poorly absorbed 
after oral administration and might therefore contribute minimally 
to mushroom poisoning in humans (Wieland and Faulstich, 1978). 
Tight binding of phalloidin to actin filaments in HPCs prevents the 
disassembly phase of the normally dynamic rearrangement of the 
actin filament constituent of the cytoskeleton. This leads to strik-
ing alterations in the actin-rich web of cytoskeleton adjacent to the 
canalicular membrane of HPCs; the actin web becomes accentu-
ated and the canalicular lumen dilates (Wieland, 1977; Phillips 
et al., 1986; Yamamoto et al., 1988; Frimmer, 1987). In isolated 
HPCs, phalloidin increases intracellular free calcium, which can 
lead to alterations in hepatocellular homeostasis (Kawaji et al., 
1992).
Unlike phalloidin, α-amanitin is absorbed from the gastroin-
testinal tract and is rapidly eliminated in the urine (Homann et al., 
1986; Jaeger et al., 1993). As with other cyclic peptides, α-amanitin 
enters HPCs via OATPs, especially OATP1B3, on the basal mem-
brane (Letschert et al., 2006). Centrilobular hepatocellular necrosis 
and hemorrhage accompanied by a hepatorenal syndrome charac-
terizes severe poisoning in humans, and the latter portends a poor 
prognosis for survival (Pond et al., 1986; Bonnet and Basson, 2002; 
Garcia et al., 2015). The lethal dose in humans has been estimated 
to be 0.1 mg/kg, an amount that can occur in one night cap mush-
room (Karlson-Stiber and Persson, 2003).
A primary mechanism of toxicity of α-amanitin derives from 
its ability to bind to nuclear RNA polymerase II, thereby inhibiting 
RNA elongation with resultant impaired protein synthesis and cell 
death (Wang et al., 2006). In isolated HPCs, α-amanitin initiates 
apoptosis dependent on p53, and this might be connected to the 
inhibition of mRNA synthesis (Ljungman et al., 1999; Magdalan 
et al., 2011). In mice, knockout of p53 and BAK reduced α-amanitin 
hepatotoxicity, suggesting that a mitochondrial apoptotic pathway 
is important in death of HPCs in vivo (Leu and George, 2007). 
Leist et al. (1997) presented evidence that enhanced TNF expres-
sion occurs in mice treated with α-amanitin and is involved in the 
progression of injury. Some evidence suggests that oxidative stress 
is also involved, perhaps arising from free radical formation in 
α-amanitin itself (Zheleva, 2013).

Metals
Exposure to metals occurs via several routes, including diet and 
inhalation of metal-containing particulates from occupational 
exposure or cigarette smoke. Most metals target numerous organs, 
among them the liver, with a wide spectrum of consequences. A 
comprehensive treatise of the topic is beyond the scope of this chap-
ter, but some salient points about the toxicities of iron, cadmium, 
and copper are mentioned here. For more information about metal 
toxicity, excellent reviews are available (Fowler, 2009; Goering, 
2010; Corkins, 2011; Satarug, 2012; Patil et al., 2013; Rani et al., 
2014; Sharma et al., 2014; Zischka and Lichtmannegger, 2014; 
Matović et al., 2015; Pietrangelo, 2016).
Metals are excreted into bile by a series of processes that 
include (1) uptake across sinusoidal membranes of HPCs by mem-
brane transporters or receptor-mediated endocytosis; (2) storage 
in binding proteins or lysosomes; and (3) canalicular secretion via 
lysosomes, a GSH-coupled event, or by specific canalicular mem-
brane transport, for example via MRP2 (Ballatori, 2002). Biliary 
excretion is important in the homeostasis of several metals, notably 
copper, manganese, cadmium, selenium, gold, silver, and arsenic 
(Klaassen, 1976; Gregus and Klaassen, 1986). Species differences 
are known for biliary excretion of several toxic metals; for example, 
dogs excrete arsenic into bile much more slowly than rats.
Inability to export copper into bile is a central problem in 
Wilson’s disease, an autosomal recessive inherited disorder charac-
terized by a defect in or the absence of a copper transporting P-type 
ATPase (ATP7B). This carrier is located in the trans-Golgi network 
within HPCs and transports copper into the secretory pathway for 
binding to ceruloplasmin and then excretion into bile (Loudianos 
and Gitlin, 2000). Because biliary excretion is the only way to 
eliminate copper, a defect in ATP7B results in excessive copper 
accumulation in HPCs, which causes chronic hepatitis and cirrhosis 
(Loudianos and Gitlin, 2000). Copper toxicity involves production 
of reactive oxygen species that initiate liver damage characterized 
by centrilobular necrosis and cholestasis. However, activation of 
Kupffer cells by copper exposure and a consequent inflammatory 
response contribute to liver injury (Videla et al., 2003).
Cadmium hepatotoxicity becomes manifest when cells exceed 
their capacity to sequester cadmium as a complex with the metal-
binding protein, metallothionein (MT). This protective role for MT 
was documented by observations with MT transgenic and knock-
out mice. Overexpression of MT in the transgenic mice rendered 
them more resistant than wild-type animals to the hepatotoxicity 
and lethality of cadmium poisoning (Liu et al., 1995). In contrast, 
MT gene knockout mice were dramatically more susceptible to 
cadmium hepatotoxicity (Liu et al., 1996). Cadmium binding to 
sulfhydryl groups on proteins appears to be responsible for its toxic 
effect, but as with copper, activation of Kupffer cells and inflamma-
tion appear to play a role (Kyriakou et al., 2013).
Acute iron toxicity is most commonly observed in young 
children who accidentally ingest iron tablets (Chang and Rangan, 
2011). Iron poisoning can result in severe liver damage and results 
when free iron concentration in extracellular fluid and within cells 
becomes elevated. HPCs contribute to the homeostasis of iron by 
extracting this essential metal from the sinusoidal blood by a recep-
tor-mediated process and maintaining a reserve of iron bound to the 
storage protein, ferritin. The cytotoxicity of free iron is attributed to 
its capacity as an electron donor for the Fenton reaction, in which 
hydrogen peroxide is reductively cleaved to the highly reactive 
hydroxyl radical, an initiator of lipid peroxidation (see Chap. 3). 
Accumulation of excess iron beyond the capacity for its safe stor-
age in ferritin is initially evident in zone 1 HPCs, which are closest 
to the blood entering the sinusoid. Thus, the zone 1 pattern of HPC 
damage after iron poisoning is attributable to the location of the 
preferential uptake of iron and to the greater oxygen concentration 
in zone 1 that facilitates the injurious process of lipid peroxidation. 
Chronic accumulation of excess iron in cases of hemochromatosis 
is associated with a spectrum of hepatic disease including a greater 
than 200-fold increased risk for liver cancer (Ramm and Ruddell, 
2005).
DETERMINANTS OF SUSCEPTIBILITY
Pronounced variability in hepatotoxic response among individuals 
can occur upon exposure to xenobiotic agents. Underlying such dif-
ferences in susceptibility and magnitude of response are many fac-
tors including differences in metabolism, hepatoprotective systems, 
age, sex, tissue reserve and regenerative capacity, and inflammatory 
response (Table 13-5). Interactions of hepatotoxicant exposure with 
various diseases or with other xenobiotic agents can also influence 
susceptibility of individuals to liver injury. Several factors that can 
affect either the initiation or progression of xenobiotic-induced 
liver injury within individuals are discussed in this section.
Xenobiotic Metabolism
Many chemicals are metabolized by the various CYP isoforms and 
conjugating enzymes to metabolites that are either more or less tox-
icologically active than the parent molecule. In humans, CYP3A4 is 
the CYP isoform most commonly involved in metabolism of drugs, 
but many other CYP isoforms participate too, depending on the 
chemical. It follows that differences among individuals in the activ-
ity of such enzymes can markedly affect sensitivity to intoxication, 
and these differences result from both genetic and environmental 
factors. Polymorphisms in genes encoding metabolizing enzymes 
occur among individuals and have a major impact on disposition 
of toxicants (Zanger and Schwab, 2013). In addition, genetic dif-
ferences in receptors or transcription factors (e.g., AhR, PXR, 
CAR, Nrf2) that control the expression of these genes or epigen-
etic changes that regulate their expression can influence metabolic 
activity within individuals (Yokoi and Nakajima, 2011; Ishikawa 
et al., 2013; Smutny et al., 2013; Wallace and Redinbo, 2013; Wu 
et al., 2013; Elcombe et al., 2014; see also Chap. 6). Although 
there has been much emphasis over the years on how expression 
of CYPs influences bioactivation and toxicity, polymorphisms and 
expression differences in various xenobiotic conjugating enzymes 
also influence toxicity (for reviews, see Sim et al., 2008; Ginsberg

The expression of many xenobiotic metabolizing enzymes and 
their activities is influenced by dietary constituents, exposure to 
drugs and environmental chemicals, nutritional status, liver diseases, 
inflammatory conditions, sex, and age (Merrell and Cherrington, 
2011; Zanger and Schwab, 2013; Lu et al., 2014; Yeung et al., 2014; 
Yang, 2015; Guo et al., 2016). With some exceptions, inflammatory 
cytokines generally downregulate the expression of CYPs, conju-
gating enzymes, and transporters in HPCs and can thereby modify 
responses to chemicals that undergo metabolism (Renton, 2004; 
Fardel and Le Vée, 2009; Gandhi et al., 2012; Zanger and Schwab, 
2013). Moreover, xenobiotic agents can influence the metabolism 
and toxicity of other chemicals by inhibiting or inducing metaboliz-
ing enzymes (Hu et al., 2012). Xenobiotic transporters are subject 
to similar effects (Kobori et al., 2013; Nigam et al., 2015). Together, 
these factors determine metabolic capability within an individual 
and, by extension, influence hepatotoxicity of chemicals that are 
metabolized. Because many chemicals are subject to metabolism 
by several pathways and because there are so many factors that can 
affect metabolic activity, how metabolism and hepatotoxicity are 
likely to be influenced in a particular individual is difficult to predict 
(Palatini and De Martin, 2016).
Hepatobiliary Transporters
Toxicant-related impairments of bile formation are more likely to 
have detrimental consequences in populations with other condi-
tions in which biliary secretion is marginal. For example, neonates 
exhibit delayed development of multiple aspects of bile formation, 
including synthesis of bile acids and the expression of sinusoidal 
and canalicular transporters (Arrese et al., 1998). Neonates are 
more prone to develop jaundice when treated with drugs that com-
pete with bilirubin for biliary clearance. Individuals with genetic 
deficiency of certain hepatobiliary transporters are not only at risk 
for chronic liver injury and fibrosis but may also be more suscep-
tible to drugs and hepatotoxicants (Jansen and Sturm, 2003; Jansen 
and Groen, 2006). Patients with bacterial sepsis frequently develop 
cholestasis, which is mainly caused by downregulation of multiple 
canalicular transport systems (Geier et al., 2006). In addition, direct 
inhibition of the bile salt export pump (BSEP) (e.g., by the endo-
thelin receptor antagonist, bosentan) can lead to retention of bile 
acids in the liver and consequent liver injury (Fattinger et al., 2001). 
Biliary excretion is important in the homeostasis of several met-
als, notably copper, manganese, cadmium, selenium, gold, silver, 
and arsenic (Klaassen, 1976; Gregus and Klaassen, 1986). Because 
biliary excretion is the only way to eliminate copper, a defect in the 
ATP7B carrier (located in the trans-Gogi network) results in exces-
sive copper accumulation in HPCs, which causes chronic hepatitis 
and cirrhosis (Loudianos and Gitlin, 2000). Drug-induced inhibi-
tion of transporters also underlies some forms of toxic drug–drug 
interaction (Koepsell, 2015). Thus, the many transporters that occur 
in liver not only play important roles in elimination of xenobiotic 
and endogenous agents, but variation in their activities can be an 
important determinant of individual susceptibility to intoxication.
Protective Enzymes
As noted above, reactive oxygen species contribute to hepatotox-
icity from certain xenobiotic agents. Accordingly, polymorphisms 
and differences in expression of genes which encode enzymes that 
provide protection from reactive oxygen species can influence sus-
ceptibility to intoxication. For example, DNA polymorphisms and/
or variations in epigenetic or posttranslational control have been 
identified for GSH peroxidases (Hesketh, 2004; Lubos et al., 2011), 
superoxide dismutases (Miao and St. Clair, 2009; Becuwe et al., 
2014), and other enzymes as well as transcription factors such as 
Nrf2 that regulate protective cellular responses to oxidants and 
other insults (Osburn and Kensler, 2008; Wu et al., 2013).
Development and Aging
Age-related differences in hepatotoxicity have been recognized for 
many years and appear to have many underlying causes. For example, 
drug metabolizing enzymes generally are poorly expressed in new-
born animals and people, and the capacity to metabolize increases at 
various rates during maturity (Hines, 2013; Coughtrie, 2015). This 
results in differences in disposition of drugs and other xenobiotic 
agents, with consequent potential to affect toxicity (Rikans, 1989; 
Saghir et al., 2012). Elderly people typically have reduced liver blood 
flow, diminished xenobiotic metabolism, and differences in inflam-
matory responses, all of which have the potential to affect the toxicity 
of drugs (Tajiri and Shimizu, 2013). Importantly, elderly people also 
consume greater numbers of drugs than younger people, raising the 
risk of drug–drug interactions and hepatotoxicity (Adelizzi, 1994; 
Meier and Seitz, 2008; Stine et al., 2013).
Sex
Sex-related differences in hepatotoxic responses have been reported 
in many studies. For example, female sex is a risk factor for liver 
injury for several drugs and dietary supplements (Chalasani and 
Björnsson, 2010; Heidari et al., 2014; Korth, 2014; Kargar et al., 
2014; Katarey and Verma, 2016). An example is halothane hepa-
titis, for which female sex is a risk factor in humans; female mice 
are also more sensitive to this anesthetic, and the sex difference 
appears to be related to a difference in immune response (Dugan 
et al., 2010). For most drugs, it remains unclear whether this pre-
dilection is due to differences in xenobiotic metabolizing activity, 
drug dosing, body composition (e.g., lipid composition tends to be 
greater in females than males), or other factors (Anderson, 2008).
Tissue Reserve and Regeneration
There is a large reserve capacity in the liver inasmuch as a large 
fraction of the liver volume can be lost without a serious decre-
ment in function, and this organ also has a remarkable capacity for 
regeneration (Best and Coleman, 2011). Interindividual variations 
in either of these can affect the magnitude of hepatotoxic response. 
As noted above, proliferative repair is an important response of 
liver to injury (see section “Regeneration and Repair” on liver 
regeneration). In addition, such regeneration is not just a response 
to cell death but is a process that actively determines the magnitude 
of injury after exposure to many hepatotoxicants, such as thioacet-
amide, APAP, chloroform, bromobenzene, trichloroethylene, CCl4, 
galactosamine, and allyl alcohol (Anand et al., 2005; Mehendale, 
2005). Inhibition of mitosis with colchicine prevented tissue repair 
and aggravated liver injury after thioacetamide and other chemi-
cals (Mangipudy et al., 1996; Mehendale, 2005, 2013). In contrast, 
stimulation of repair by exposure to a modest dose of a hepatotoxi-
cant strongly attenuated tissue damage from a subsequent, larger 
dose of the same or a different chemical (autoprotection and hetero-
protection, respectively) (Mehendale, 2005, 2013). Tissue repair is 
dose-responsive up to a threshold, above which the injury becomes 
too severe, and cell proliferation is inhibited (Mangipudy et al., 
1995). In addition to the dose of the hepatotoxicant, other factors 
such as age, nutritional status, and disease state can influence tissue 
repair (Mehendale, 2005).

Inflammatory Stress
Inflammation is a common response to exposure to many hepato-
toxicants, and genetic or environmental differences in one or more 
of the many factors involved in an inflammatory response occur 
commonly among individuals. Large exposures to some agents, 
such as bacterial lipopolysaccharide (LPS, endotoxin) can directly 
initiate an acute inflammatory response that can cause serious liver 
injury (Hewett and Roth, 1995; Han, 2002). More common is mod-
est inflammation associated with alcohol consumption, pathogen 
infection, inflammatory diseases such as arthritis, surgery, etc., that 
by itself does not cause cell death or liver dysfunction. However, an 
independently initiated, modest inflammatory episode can qualita-
tively and/or quantitatively influence the magnitude of hepatotoxic 
response to drugs and other xenobiotic agents. As noted above, 
one way this can happen is via mediators such as TNF and other 
cytokines that regulate the expression of transporters and xenobi-
otic metabolizing enzymes which either bioactivate or inactivate 
chemicals.
An inflammatory stress can change the sensitivity of indi-
viduals to hepatotoxic chemicals independently of effects on 
metabolism. In human patients, inflammatory liver diseases such 
as hepatitis B and C have been linked to hepatotoxicity from anti-
viral drugs (Puoti et al., 2009), and these hepatitis viruses also 
synergize with aflatoxin exposure in causing liver cancer (see sec-
tion “Aflatoxins”). Experimentally, exposure of rodents to a small 
dose of LPS that causes modest liver inflammation (e.g., cytokine 
release, neutrophil accumulation) but no hepatocellular injury 
markedly increases liver damage from a variety of xenobiotic 
agents that cause intrinsic hepatotoxicity, including aflatoxin B1 
(Luyendyk et al., 2002), monocrotaline (Yee et al., 2000), carbon 
tetrachloride (Chamulitrat et al., 1995), galactosamine (Galanos 
et al., 1979), allyl alcohol (Sneed et al., 1997), cocaine (Labib et al., 
2002), and APAP (Maddox et al., 2010) (reviewed in Ganey and 
Roth, 2001; Ganey et al., 2004). The hepatotoxic interaction of 
LPS with these xenobiotic agents depends on many inflammatory 
factors. For example, in rats the interaction of monocrotaline with 
LPS requires participation by Kupffer cells, neutrophils, TNF, and 
an activated coagulation system (Yee et al., 2003a, 2003b, 2003c). 
Interestingly, both dose and the timing of the exposures are critical 
to the type of response in these models. For example, when LPS 
and monocrotaline were given 4 hours apart, liver injury occurred, 
but when the same doses were given 1 hour apart, animals died 
rapidly from another cause, likely NO-mediated circulatory shock 
(Yee et al., 2002). In contrast, mice given a nontoxic dose of APAP 
2 hours after a noninjurious dose of LPS experienced pronounced 
centilobuluar injury, whereas LPS given 24 hours prior to APAP 
afforded protection from liver toxicity (Liu et al., 2000; Maddox 
et al., 2010). The protection in the latter case was related to LPS-
induced downregulation of CYPs that bioactivate acetaminophen 
and could be influenced also by the production of anti-­inflammatory 
mediators that follow the flood of pro-inflammatory events that 
occurs shortly after LPS exposure. Collectively, these studies indi-
cate the complexities in how inflammatory stress can act as a deter-
minant of sensitivity to hepatotoxicants.
Nutritional Status
Nutritional status is yet another determinant of sensitivity to hepa-
totoxicants (Brautbar and Williams, 2002; Stickel et al., 2003; 
Bunchorntavakul and Reddy, 2013; Ingawale et al., 2014). For 
example, fasting decreases GSH concentration in liver; because 
detoxification of the toxic metabolite of APAP depends on GSH, 
fasting renders animals more sensitive to APAP hepatotoxicity 
(McClain et al., 1999). Deficiencies in many nutrients have been 
shown to enhance the toxicity of numerous hepatotoxicants, and 
dietary supplementation can sometimes improve liver function in 
affected individuals. Examples of such dietary nutrients include 
essential amino acids and lipids, S-adenosylmethionine, zinc, 
and antioxidants (Lieber, 2000; Basu, 2003; Stehbens, 2003). 
In contrast, excess consumption of some essential nutrients can 
exacerbate hepatotoxicity. An example of this is the hepatotoxic 
interaction of alcohol with iron overload (Tavill and Qadri, 2004).
The susceptibility factors discussed above are not meant to 
represent an exhaustive list. For example, interactions between 
chemicals can be an important determinant. Polymorphisms in 
genes controlling adaptive immune responses appear to be impor-
tant for some drug-induced hepatotoxic reactions (see section 
below). Importantly, almost any genetic or environmental difference 
among individuals can affect sensitivity to hepatotoxicants if that 
difference alters an enzyme or another factor that is critical in the 
molecular pathway to liver injury for a particular chemical.
IDIOSYNCRATIC, DRUG-INDUCED LIVER 
INJURY (IDILI): OCCURRENCE AND 
MODES OF ACTION
As discussed above, idiosyncratic reactions occur in a minority of 
patients during drug therapy, and these reactions often target the 
liver. Although idiosyncratic, drug-induced liver injury (IDILI) 
occurs infrequently in human patients, it has importance because 
it is sometimes severe, requiring liver transplantation or caus-
ing death. Moreover, drugs that are associated with human IDILI 
are often restricted in use or withdrawn from the market, result-
ing in the loss of otherwise efficacious drugs (Kaplowitz, 2005; 
Raschi and DePonti, 2015; Licata, 2016; Roth and Lee, 2017). 
Conventional preclinical safety testing in animals and in vitro that 
occurs during drug development fails to detect IDILI liability of 
drug candidates. Because these reactions are typically rare, they are 
not usually exposed in clinical trials that employ too few volunteers 
to detect them. Accordingly, much remains unknown about why 
and how IDILI reactions occur.
Many pharmaceuticals as well as herbal remedies and food sup-
plements have been associated with IDILI in humans (Stickel et al., 
2011). Listings of drugs can be found in recent reviews (Björnsson, 
2016; Björnsson and Hoofnagle, 2016), and NIH’s LiverTox Web 
site provides a searchable database for summary information about 
many drugs (https://livertox.nih.gov/). Antibiotics are the drugs 
most commonly associated with human IDILI in Western countries, 
but drugs from many other pharmacologic classes are also culprits 
(Katarey and Verma, 2016; Bunchorntavakul and Reddy, 2017). In 
Asia, IDILI is caused more commonly by dietary and herbal supple-
ments (Frenzel and Teschke, 2016; Ahmad and Odin, 2017).
DILI is difficult to diagnose because the clinical presenta-
tion is often indistinguishable from liver injury from other causes, 
so that diagnosis is largely by exclusion of other causes (Lewis, 
2015; Raschi and DePonti, 2015). Hepatocellular injury may be the 
most common phenotype, but cholestatic and mixed injuries also 
occur. Many patients recover from liver injury when exposure to the 
offending drug is stopped, but others progress to acute liver failure 
culminating in death or a need for a liver transplant.
IDILI reactions doubtlessly depend on characteristics of the 
offending drug as well as susceptibility of individual patients, but the 
mechanisms underlying them are poorly understood. Consequently, 
many hypotheses about the modes of action of IDILI have arisen 
(Chen et al., 2015; Roth et al., 2017). Each of these hypotheses is

currently supported by limited evidence from studies in humans 
and/or animals. A brief description of several of them follows; for 
greater detail, the reader is referred to the cited reviews.
According to the multiple determinant hypothesis, IDILI 
occurs from the intersection of several genetic and/or environmen-
tal susceptibility factors present simultaneously within a patient 
exposed to the offending drug. Such factors could be related to 
drug metabolism, transporters, nutrition, sex, age, concurrent expo-
sure to other xenobiotic agents, physical activity, underlying dis-
ease, concurrent inflammation, etc. (Li, 2002; Ulrich, 2007) (Fig. 
13-24). Each of the susceptibility factors is associated with a finite 
probability of occurrence (i.e., from 0 to 1), and the probability 
of an IDILI reaction would be related to the product of the indi-
vidual probabilities. This would result in a small number and could 
explain why IDILI reactions are typically rare. Consistent with this 
hypothesis is the observation that severe liver injury occurred in 
halothane-treated mice subjected to a combination of known risk 
factors for human halothane hepatitis (female sex, genetics, age, 
fasting) (Dugan et al., 2010).
Numerous drugs that are associated with human IDILI can 
cause mitochondrial stress or mitochondrial toxicity in vitro 
(Boelsterli, 2003; Pessayre et al., 2012; Han et al., 2013; Boelsterli 
and Lee, 2014). Moreover, human polymorphisms in enzymes that 
either bioactivate or inactivate drugs have been identified, and some 
appear to be risk factors for IDILI (Andrade et al., 2009; Czaja, 
2011; Markova et al., 2013; Ahmad and Odin, 2017). Similarly, 
many drugs that cause IDILI inhibit or downregulate expression 
of hepatocellular efflux transporters that move drugs and bile acids 
out of HPCs, and polymorphisms in genes that encode transport-
ers have associations with toxicity from some drugs (Corsini and 
Bortolini, 2013; Pedersen et al., 2013; Ulzurrun et al., 2013; He 
et al., 2015; Guo et al., 2015). Although each of these qualities alone 
might be insufficient to explain IDILI, they are likely important risk 
factors for certain drugs and could act in combination with each 
other or with other factors to precipitate hepatotoxicity (Aleo et al., 
2014; Köck et al., 2014; Ulzurrun et al., 2014; Zhou et al., 2017). 
Accordingly, these observations are consistent with the multiple 
determinant hypothesis. This hypothesis has raised the possibil-
ity that some idiosyncratic reactions are no different from intrinsic 
ones (e.g., dose-related) if viewed in the context of an individual’s 
sensitivity to hepatotoxicity being heightened by the presence of 
one or more risk factors (Roth and Ganey, 2010b).
The failure-to-adapt hypothesis proposes that therapeu-
tic doses of some drugs cause modest liver injury to which most 
patients adapt; those few who fail to do so progress to serious 
liver injury. For example, about 10% to 40% of patients taking the 
antibiotic, isoniazid, experience increases in serum ALT/AST that, 
in most patients, return to normal despite continued drug treat-
ment (see Dara et al., 2016a for review). However, isoniazid is 
well known to cause life-threatening liver injury in a small fraction 
(about 1%) of patients taking the drug, so it seems plausible that 
the livers of these patients fail to adapt to the modest initial insult. 
This and other observations (Watkins, 2005) led to the hypothesis 
that many drugs can cause modest, subclinical injury to liver and 
that IDILI occurs in people whose livers fail to prevent the progres-
sion of injury. Support for this hypothesis is circumstantial, and 
mechanisms are unknown. For drugs that cause IDILI via adaptive 
immune system activation (see below), adaptation might involve 
tolerance mechanisms that prevent progression of liver injury (Dara 
et al., 2016a). Alternatively, the liver typically adapts to liver injury 
by proliferation and replacement of injured HPCs, and several drugs 
that cause IDILI also inhibit cell proliferation (Roth et al., 2017).
Many conditions such as inflammatory diseases, alcohol con-
sumption, infection, etc., involve episodic exposure of the liver to 
PAMPs or DAMPs and consequent activation of the innate immune 
system (Fig. 13-25). According to the inflammatory stress hypoth-
esis, drug exposure interacts with a modest, noninjurious inflamma-
tory episode, resulting in liver damage. One can picture a patient 
on maintenance drug therapy progressing well until an inflamma-
tory episode interacts with the drug, precipitating liver injury. Such 
a scenario can account for many of the bizarre characteristics of 
idiosyncratic reactions (see Table 13-4; Ganey et al., 2004; Shaw 
et al., 2010; Roth and Ganey, 2013). Almost all of the animal mod-
els of IDILI in which pronounced liver injury occurs from IDILI-
associated drugs have been based on this hypothesis (Deng et al., 
2009; Roth and Ganey, 2011). In these models, rodents are cotreated 
with an inflammagen such as LPS that is noninjurious by itself and 
with a nontoxic dose of a drug. Only the combination results in liver 
injury. For example, trovafloxacin is an antibiotic, the use of which 
was curtailed due to several cases of fatal liver injury. Nontoxic 
doses of trovafloxacin given to mice cotreated with a modest dose 
of LPS experienced liver necrosis (Shaw et al., 2007). The drug 
prolonged the LPS-induced appearance of TNF in the plasma, and 
neutralization of TNF or knockout of either of the TNF receptors 
eliminated the liver injury (Shaw et al., 2009e). Indeed, drug inter-
action with the TNF response appeared to be the initiator of a chain 
of events involving other cytokines (interferon-gamma, plasmino-
gen activator inhibitor), neutrophils, and the coagulation system that 
contributed to the hepatotoxic drug–LPS interaction (Shaw et al., 
2009a, 2009b, 2009c, 2009d). With several other IDILI-associated 
drugs (e.g., chlorpromazine [Buchweitz et al., 2002]; sulindac [Zou 
et al., 2009]; amiodarone [Lu et al., 2012]; halothane [Cheng et al., 
2009; Dugan et al., 2010]; dicloxacillin [Higuchi et al., 2011]; 
diclofenac [Deng et al., 2006]; doxorubicin [Hassan et al., 2008]), 
cotreatment of rodents with an inflammatory stimulus led similarly 
to pronounced liver injury, consistent with this hypothesis.
The most widespread theory entails drug-induced activation 
of the adaptive immune system. According to the adaptive immu-
nity hypothesis (or hapten hypothesis), IDILI reactions are initi-
ated when drugs are metabolized to reactive metabolites that bind 
covalently to proteins (Fig. 13-25; Tailor et al., 2015; Thompson 
et al., 2016). These altered proteins are detected as antigens by 
antigen-presenting cells that process the antigens and present them 
to T-cells, which become activated, leading to an adaptive immune 
response that results in activation of inflammatory cells, release of 
cytokines, and other events (Fig. 13-25). Normally, this does not 
damage host tissue. However, in the presence of “danger signals” 
(e.g., DAMPs released from cells that are injured, perhaps by reac-
tive drug metabolites) and elimination of “immune tolerance,” the

Recent studies have demonstrated links between the adap-
tive immune system and drugs that cause IDILI. For example, for 
some IDILI-associated drugs (e.g., flucloxacillin), drug-specific 
T-cell responses have been identified in blood samples from IDILI 
patients (Ortega-Alonso et al., 2016; Usui and Naisbitt, 2017). 
Also, associations have been uncovered between certain polymor-
phisms in human leukocyte antigens (HLAs) and IDILI reactions 
to several drugs (e.g., flucloxacillin, ximelagatran, lapatinib, terbi-
nafine, fenofibrate, ticlopidine, amoxicillin-clavulanate, abacavir) 
(Fontana, 2014; Yip et al., 2015; Nicoletti et al., 2017). In some of 
these studies, almost all patients who suffered IDILI from a spe-
cific drug had a specific HLA genotype; however, most people who 
were exposed to the drug and had the HLA polymorphism did not 
develop liver injury. This suggests that additional susceptibility fac-
tors are at play.
An unanswered question regarding immune-mediated IDILI 
is what causes the death of hepatocytes. Both innate and adaptive 
immune responses culminate in the cellular expression and release 
of various cytokines. TNF is important in animal models that have 
been developed with several drugs that cause IDILI (see Roth and 
Ganey, 2013, for partial listing). In vitro, TNF interacts with numer-
ous IDILI-associated drugs to cause death of HPCs (Cosgrove 
et al., 2009; Zou et al., 2009; Lu et al., 2013; Maiuri et al., 2015, 
2017). Various hepatocellular stresses initiated by drugs appear 
to be important in cytotoxic drug–TNF interactions in vitro. For 
example, amiodarone and sulindac sulfide, the active metabolite of 
sulindac, cause oxidative stress in vitro that underlies the interaction 
with TNF (Zou et al., 2010; Lu et al., 2013). Diclofenac and chlor-
promazine cause endoplasmic reticulum stress that interacts with 
TNF to produce hepatocellular injury (Fredriksson et al., 2014), and 
cytotoxic interaction of trovafloxacin with TNF in vitro appears to 
depend on replication stress induced by drug-induced DNA damage 
(Beggs et al., 2014). For some drugs, interferon-gamma potentiates 
the drug–TNF interaction (Maiuri et al., 2015, 2017). Thus, there 
is mounting evidence that drug-induced, sublethal stress renders 
HPCs sensitive to the killing capacity of cytokines and that this 
might be important in IDILI reactions that are initiated by immune 
responses (Roth et al., 2017; Fig. 13-25).
In conclusion, IDILI likely occurs by several different mecha-
nisms depending the particular drug and characteristics of the 
patient. It should be borne in mind that the hypotheses described 
above are not mutually exclusive and that supporting evidence for 
each of them is incomplete. Paramount needs in this area include 
a greater understanding of factors that render people susceptible 
to IDILI reactions and developing better strategies to predict the 
IDILI liability of compounds during drug development (Roth and 
Lee, 2017).
CONCLUSION
The liver comprises several cell types and has numerous functions, 
many of which relate to metabolism of endogenous and xenobi-
otic agents, elimination of toxic substances, immune surveillance, 
and supplying other organs with energy sources. Its blood supply 
arising predominately from intestinal drainage exposes the liver to 
large concentrations of components in the diet that are absorbed 
into the blood, and this in part renders the liver susceptible to tox-
icity. Responses to chemical insults are several and include ste-
atosis, death of various cell types, inflammation, fibrosis, cancer, 
and proliferative repair. The mechanisms that result in liver injury 
are complex and, importantly, can be dose-related; that is, differ-
ent mechanisms may apply at small and large exposures to the 
same toxicant. Toxic mechanisms typically begin with an insult to 
HPCs that initiates intracellular signaling which can lead to cell 
death. Activated sinusoidal cells and blood elements can encourage

Progress in evaluating hepatotoxicity and its mechanisms has

greater understanding of mechanisms in the near future.
