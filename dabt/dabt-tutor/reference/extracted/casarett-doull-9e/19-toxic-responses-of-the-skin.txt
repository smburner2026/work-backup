INTRODUCTION
As the body’s first line of defense against external insult, the skin’s 
vast surface area (1.5 to 2 m2) is repeatedly exposed to chemical 
assaults and other types of injuries. As such, it may serve as a portal 
of entry for topical contactants. Recognizing the potential hazards 
of skin exposure, the National Institute of Occupational Safety and 
Health (NIOSH) characterizes skin disease as one of the most per-
vasive occupational health problems in the United States. In 2012, 
NIOSH ranked skin disease as the second leading work related ill-
ness based on frequency, and estimated its annual cost at greater 
than 1 billion dollars (NIOSH, 2017a). Although substantial reduc-
tion in the reported incidence has occurred in recent years due to 
better workplace environments and personal protective equipment, 
further improvements in prevention and management are clearly 
needed. Skin conditions resulting from occupational or consumer 
exposures not resulting in lost work are poorly recorded. Hence, 
their incidence is likely to be greatly underestimated.
NIOSH recently changed its cutaneous categorization to 
more accurately reflect the risks to workers after topical exposures 
(NIOSH, 2017b). Previously, inhalation exposures were thought to 
produce the most risk to workers, with skin exposure being only 
a secondary pathway. The new guidelines reflect increased under-
standing of several mechanisms by which skin exposure can lead to 
disease: systemic toxicity via skin absorption (SYS), direct effects 
that damage the skin (DIR), and immune-mediated responses to 
chemicals that contact the skin (SEN). The new notations utilize 
these categories to designate hazard specific skin exposures, and 
have incorporated standardized criteria to allow for consistency in 
how chemicals are designated. Determining the hazard potential of 
a particular chemical is based upon physicochemical properties of 
the substance, toxicokinetic studies, epidemiological data, in vitro 
or in vivo laboratory testing, and in silico computational predic-
tions. These criteria are specified in the revised chemical designa-
tion issued by NIOSH (NIOSH, 2017b).
SKIN AS A BARRIER
A large (10% of body mass) and highly accessible organ, the skin 
protects the body against external insults, thus maintaining homeo-
stasis. Its biochemical properties allow it to perform a myriad of 
functions beyond that of barrier. Physiologically, the skin partici-
pates directly in thermal, electrolyte, hormonal, metabolic, anti-
microbial, and immune regulation. Rather than merely repelling 
noxious agents, the skin may react to them with a variety of defen-
sive mechanisms preventing widespread cutaneous and/or internal 
injuries. If an insult is severe or sufficiently intense to overwhelm 
the protective function of the skin, acute or chronic injury manifests 
in various ways. The specific presentation depends upon a variety 
of intrinsic and extrinsic factors (Belsito, 1989) including body 
site, duration of exposure, other environmental conditions, and the 
physicochemical properties of the insult (Table 19-1).

ducts of the sebaceous gland provide a protective oil covering to

The interfollicular epidermis is stratified squamous epithe-
lium consisting primarily of keratinocytes. These cells are tightly 
attached to each other by desmosomes and to the basement mem-
brane by hemidesmosomes. Melanocytes, whose melanin granules 
give the skin its color, are interspersed among the basal cells and 
distributed in the papillae of hair follicles. Migrating through the 
epidermis are numerous Langerhans cells, which are dendritic, anti-
gen presenting cells involved in the immune response of skin to 
foreign xenobiotics.

The cellular composition of the epidermis is dominated by the 
keratinocyte. Keratinocytes of the basal layer comprise the germi-
native component. When a basal cell divides, one of the progeny 
detaches from the basal lamina and migrates upwards. As cells 
move toward the skin surface, they undergo a programmed ter-
minal differentiation, gradually expressing new protein markers, 
and accumulating keratin proteins. These keratins form insoluble 
intermediate filaments, which account for nearly 40% of the total 
cellular protein in the spinous layer of the epidermis. At the granu-
lar layer of the epidermis, keratinocytes undergo a striking mor-
phological transformation, becoming flattened and increasing in 
volume nearly 40-fold. Lipid granules fuse with the plasma mem-
brane at the granular layer/stratum corneum interface, filling the 
intercellular spaces of the stratum corneum with lipid, as opposed 
to the aqueous intercellular environment of the underlying viable 
epidermis. These surface lipids perform a variety of defensive func-
tions, both in preventing diffusion of water and ions out of the body, 
and preventing access of toxicants and bacteria into the skin (Elias, 
2005). Meanwhile, the plasma membranes of the now moribund 
keratinocytes become permeable, lose their reducing environment, 
and undergo extensive disulfide bonding of the keratin proteins. 
Cell organelles are degraded, while a protein envelope is synthe-
sized immediately beneath the plasma membrane. These membrane 
alterations are characterized by the loss of phospholipids and the 
addition of sphingolipids.
This programmed terminal differentiation and cell death, 
beginning as keratinocytes leave the basal layer, produces the outer 
most layer of the skin, the stratum corneum. No longer viable, the 
mature cells (called corneocytes) are approximately 80% keratin 
in content. They are gradually shed from the surface and replaced 
from beneath. Epidermal turnover typically takes 28 days: 2 weeks 
for basal cells to reach the stratum corneum, and another 2 weeks 
to be shed from the surface. In psoriasis, the migration of cells to 
the surface is nearly 10-fold faster than normal, resulting in a stra-
tum corneum populated by cells that are not completely mature. 
In instances in which the outer layer is deficient due to disease 
(e.g., atopic dermatitis) or physicochemical trauma, the barrier to 
the environment that the skin provides is suboptimal (Elias and 
Schmuth, 2009).
Percutaneous Absorption
During the past 50 years, the stratum corneum has been recognized 
as the primary barrier (Scheuplein and Blank, 1971). Diseases (e.g., 
atopic dermatitis) or other conditions (e.g., abrasions/wounding) in 
which this barrier is compromised can permit greatly increased 
uptake of poorly permeable substances, as does removal of the 
stratum corneum by tape stripping (under experimental conditions) 
or organic solvents such as toluene (in occupational settings). The 
viable layer of epidermis provides a much less protective barrier 
than does the stratum corneum, because hydrophilic chemicals 
can readily diffuse into the intercellular water, while hydrophobic 
chemicals can partition into the cell membranes, and each can gain 
access to the blood supply in the rete ridges of the dermis.
The stratum corneum is the perfect biologic membrane to 
prevent water loss from underlying tissues by evaporation. Its 
hydrophobic characteristics result from the lipid content of its 
intercellular spaces, approximately 15% of the total volume (Elias, 
1992). The lipids, a major component being sphingolipids, have a 
high content of long chain ceramides, removal of which seriously 
compromises barrier function as measured by transepidermal water 
loss (TEWL). The stratum corneum is hydrated (approximately 
20% water), the moisture residing in its protein content. However, 
the stratum corneum can take up a great deal more water upon pro-
longed immersion, thereby reducing the effectiveness of the barrier 
to hydrophilic chemicals. Indeed, occlusion of the skin with imper-
meable wraps, permitting the retention of perspiration underneath, 
is commonly employed to enhance uptake of chemicals, particularly 
medications, applied to the skin’s surface. Although penetration 
from air is generally too low to be of concern, protection from skin 
uptake may be advisable for some compounds (e.g., organophos-
phates) at concentrations sufficient to require respiratory protection.
Finding the rate at which the uptake of xenobiotics through 
the skin occurs is important for estimating the consequences of 
exposure to the many agents encountered environmentally. Uptake 
through the skin is now incorporated into pharmacokinetic model-
ing to estimate potential risks from exposures. The degree of uptake 
depends upon exposure conditions, and is typically proportional to 
solute concentration (assuming it has been diluted), time, and the 
exposed skin surface. In addition, intrinsic factors contribute to the 
absorption rate of a given compound; most notably, its hydropho-
bicity that affects its ability to partition into the epidermal lipid and 
its rate of diffusion through the corneal barrier. A measure of hydro-
phobicity is the commonly used octanol/water partitioning ratio 
(Kow). However, partitioning of a chemical into the skin is greatly 
affected by its solubility in, or adhesion to, the medium in which 
it is contained (including soil). Similarly, very hydrophobic com-
pounds, once in the stratum corneum, may diffuse only very slowly 
into the less hydrophobic regions below. Another important factor 
relates to molecular weight, where there is an inverse relationship 
with absorption. Thus, hydrophobic agents of low molecular weight 
permeate the skin better than those of high-molecular-weight, or 
low-molecular-weight hydrophilic agents. For small molecules, 
hydrophobicity is the dominant factor in penetration.
Although only small amounts of chemical may penetrate the 
stratum corneum, those particularly potent may lead to significant 
systemic effects (Table 19-2). Previous findings of percutaneous 
N-nitrosamines absorption (e.g., N-nitrosodiethanolamine) from 
cosmetics raised concerns, and led to their monitoring in consumer 
products (Fan et al., 1977). Children and adolescents harvesting 
tobacco have been found to be especially susceptible to poison-
ing by contact with nicotine, a natural pesticide present in the 
leaves (McKnight and Spiller, 2005). Conditions of topical treat-
ment of livestock for pest control must take into consideration not 
only the tolerance of the animals, but also the residuals in meat 
and milk resulting from skin penetration. High level skin exposure 
to chemicals considered safe at low levels can be dangerous (e.g., 
the nervous system toxicity and deaths of infants exposed to hexa-
chlorophene mistakenly added to talcum powder for their diapers 
[Martin-Bouyer et al., 1982]). Percutaneous toxicity in neonates 
is of particular concern given their increased body surface area to 
weight, the known skin barrier defects in premature infants, and 
controversy surrounding at what point in the neonatal process the 
cutaneous barrier is fully mature.

Because rates of transfer of various hydrophobic chemicals into the 
aqueous phase of the spinous layer are slow, saturation of the stra-
tum corneum can provide a depot leading to continued penetration 
into the body for relatively long periods after external exposure to 
a given chemical has stopped. Recognition that such estimates over 
predict uptake from short exposures has led to modeling of limited 
doses (Guy, 2010a).
Diffusion through the epidermis is considerably faster at some 
anatomical sites than others. A list in order of increasing permea-
bility gives the following: foot/sole, palm, forehead, and abdomen 
(Scheuplein and Blank, 1971). Scrotal skin has the highest perme-
ability for most topical chemicals (Fisher, 1989). Under ordinary 
conditions, absorption through the epidermal appendages is usually 
neglected, despite the ability of chemicals to bypass the stratum cor-
neum by this route, because the combined appendageal surface area 
is a relatively small fraction of the total available area for uptake. 
However, if loading of the stratum corneum is slow, penetration 
through the appendages can constitute an appreciable fraction of the 
total amount following short exposures. In some cases, the effects of 
appendageal absorption can even be dominant. For example, benzo[a]
pyrene penetrates the skin of haired mice severalfold faster than that 
of hairless strains (Kao and Hall, 1987). The hair follicles thus serve 
as important routes of entry for hydrophilic agents, and could poten-
tially be exploited for therapeutic purposes (Otberg et al., 2008).
Transdermal Drug Delivery  The ability of the stratum corneum 
to serve as a reservoir for exogenously applied chemicals is the basis 
for transdermal delivery of pharmaceuticals. Application of drugs 
to the skin can produce systemic affects, a phenomenon observed 
unintentionally before the ability of the skin to serve as a delivery 
system was appreciated. For example, topical exposure of young 
girls to estrogen has led to reports of pseudoprecocious puberty, 
while in young males, such exposure has produced gynecomastia 
(Amin et al., 1998). Transfer of topically applied hormonal medica-
tion from a treated to an untreated individual has been reported to 
cause adverse effects (Busse and Maibach, 2011).
Eloquently designed patches are currently used to deliver an 
increasing number of different drugs (including estradiol, testos-
terone, nitroglycerin, scopolamine, etc.) for therapeutic purposes 
(Guy, 2010b). The advantage of the transdermal approach over oral 
dosing include (a) providing a steady infusion of drug over extended 
periods, thereby avoiding large variations in plasma concentrations; 
(b) preventing exposure to the acidic pH of the stomach, thus pre-
venting biodegradation; and (c) avoiding biotransformation in the 
gastrointestinal tract from first pass removal by the liver. The con-
trast in plasma concentration kinetics between different methods 
of delivery is evident for chemicals that are rapidly metabolized, 
such as nitroglycerin, which has a half-life of minutes. A vari-
ety of chemicals, chosen to minimize irritation and allergenicity, 
have been incorporated into the delivery systems of pharmaceu-
tical preparations to enhance absorption and penetration. In addi-
tion, encapsulation of drug into small vesicles of phospholipids/
nonionic surfactants (micelles) and nanoparticle technologies can 
improve delivery through the epidermis and hair follicles (Desai et 
al., 2010). Finally, various microneedle systems (Prausnitz et al., 
2009) have been used to deliver drugs, vaccines, and gene therapy 
across the skin; these systems have been critical to the delivery of 
insulin to brittle diabetics (continuous glucose monitoring devices).
Measurements of Penetration  Traditionally, a pharmacoki-
netic approach to penetration has used experimental animals. To 
simplify determination of penetration kinetics, skin flaps have been 
employed and the capillary blood-flow monitored. For this purpose, 
pig skin was useful (Riviere and Brooks, 2005). Because penetra-
tion through rodent skin is usually faster than through human skin, 
the former can provide an overestimate for behavior of the latter. 
Therefore, human skin grafts onto athymic mice have been used 
to minimize species differences, since human skin survives well 
on athymic mice and retains its normal barrier properties. For 
risk assessment and pharmaceutical design, the most useful sub-
ject for experimentation is, of course, human. In pharmaceutical 
studies, volunteers are dosed, plasma and/or urine concentrations 
are quantified at suitable intervals, and amounts excreted from the 
body are estimated. Previously, isotopic labels were used coupled 
with an ultrasensitive detection by accelerator mass spectrometry 
(Buchholz et al., 1999). For in vitro work, there is one European 
Center for the Validation of Alternative Methods (ECVAM) 
model that has been accepted by the Organisation for Economic 
Co-operation and Development (OECD TG 428). In this procedure, 
skin static or flow-through diffusion cells consisting of rat, pig, or 
human skin are used under standardized in vitro conditions. This 
accepted OECD test guideline (OECD TG 428) is not only for cos-
metic testing, but also for regulatory issues. In this model, various 
concentrations of the test substance are typically used to reflect the 
realistic range of potential human exposures: normally 1 to 5 mg/
cm2 of skin for a solid and up to 10 µL/cm2 for liquids. The temper-
ature is kept constant because fluctuations could affect the passive 
diffusion of chemicals. The absorption of a test substance during a 
given time period (normally 24 hours) is measured by analysis of 
the receptor fluid and the distribution of the test substance within 
the skin. However, the guideline is not very specific and additional 
recommendations for the conduct of dermal absorption studies have

Biotransformation  The ability of the skin to metabolize chemi-
cals that diffuse through it contributes to its barrier function. Phase 
I metabolism of skin usually is only a small fraction (approximately 
3%) of that in the liver (Rolsted et al., 2008), but it is capable of 
affecting the outcome of exposure. Biotransformation influences 
the biological activity of xenobiotics and can result in skin sensiti-
zation, carcinogenesis, or other toxicities (Svensson, 2009). While, 
overall, these activities are normally low, they can be induced (or 
suppressed). In fact, a significant fraction of the pharmaceuti-
cals used in clinical dermatology are cytochrome P450 inducers, 
inhibitors, or substrates (Ahmad and Mukhtar, 2004). Well-known 
xenobiotic inducers of these isozymes include tetrachlorodibenzo-
p-dioxin (TCDD), polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons, and coplanar 
polychlorinated biphenyls. Exposure to such inducers could influ-
ence skin biotransformation, and even sensitize epidermal cells to 
chemicals that are not good inducers by themselves, a phenomenon 
observable in vivo and in vitro cell cultures (Walsh et al., 1995).
Biotransformation of a variety of compounds in the skin has 
been detected, including arachidonic acid derivatives, steroids, reti-
noids, amines, and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons, suggesting 
that multiple P450 activities are expressed. Using sensitive meth-
odologies, it is now evident that numerous distinct isoenzymes are 
expressed at varying levels in the skin. Greater than 30 CYP genes 
have been detected by DNA microarray and real-time polymerase 
chain reaction (Luu-The et al., 2009; Hu et al., 2010). In addition 
to the P450 cytochromes, other phase I enzymes expressed in the 
skin include flavin-dependent monooxygenases, aldehyde dehydro-
genases, carboxylesterases, and glutathione peroxidases (Hu et al., 
2010). The extent to which phases I and II metabolism influences 
sensitization to exogenous chemicals may rationalize species differ-
ences in the allergic response (Svensson, 2009).
Enzymes participating in phase II metabolism are expressed 
in skin. Several isozymes, such as UDP glucuronosyltransferase, 
sulfotransferase, and glutathione S-transferase, have been detected 
in human (Luu-The et al., 2009; Hu et al., 2010) and rodent skin 
(Oesch et al., 2007). In general, these activities occur at a lower rate 
than observed in the liver, but exceptions are evident, as in the case 
of quinone reductase (Khan et al., 1987). Various species express 
different relative amounts of the various isoenzymes, which could 
alter resulting target specificities or degrees of responsiveness.
A variety of other metabolic enzyme activities have also 
been detected in human epidermal cells, including sulfatases, 
β-glucuronidase, N-acetyl transferases, esterases, and reductases 
(Luu-The et al., 2009; Hu et al., 2010). In addition, the intercellular 
region of the stratum corneum has catabolic activities, for example, 
protease, lipase, glycosidase, and phosphatase (Elias, 1992). The 
epidermis and pilosebaceous units are important drug targets, and 
are the major sources of biotransformation in the skin. As a result, 
they are most susceptible to the toxic effects of external xenobiotics. 
However, other cell types (such as fibroblasts) can also actively bio-
transform, helping to rationalize observation that organotypic cell 
cultures containing underlying fibroblasts resemble natural skin to 
a better extent than those without fibroblasts, especially when using 
these cell cultures to estimate biotransformation (Gibbs et al., 2007; 
Luu-The et al., 2009; Hu et al., 2010). The development of these 
“3D skin models” has stimulated in vitro methods to replace animal 
testing of cosmetics and other potential skin toxicants.
CONTACT DERMATITIS
Overall, contact dermatitis falls into two major categories: irritant 
and allergic (Watkins and Maibach, 2009). Both involve inflamma-
tory processes and can have indistinguishable clinical characteris-
tics of erythema (redness), induration (thickening and firmness), 
scaling (flaking), and vesiculation (blistering) in areas of direct 
contact with the chemical. Biopsies from affected sites of allergic 
contact dermatitis reveal a mixed inflammatory cellular infiltrate 
of lymphocytes, histiocytes and eosinophils, as well as spongio-
sis (intercellular edema), but often are insufficient to distinguish 
this conditions from chronic cumulative irritant contact dermatitis 
(the most frequent subtype) or from other eczematous dermatitides. 
Indeed, allergic and chronic cumulative irritant contact dermatitis 
may coexist. For example, cement is alkaline and hygroscopic, pro-
ducing chronic irritation, and may contain hexavalent chromium, 
an allergen.
Irritant Dermatitis
Accounting for nearly 80% of all contact dermatitis, irritant derma-
titis arises from the direct action of agents on the skin. A chemical 
in this category is anticipated to give an adverse reaction to anyone 
if the concentration is high enough, the amount supplied sufficient, 
and the exposure conditions (frequency and duration) long enough. 
Irritant dermatitis can be broadly subcategorized as corrosive 
(chemical burns), acute, and chronic cumulative irritation.
Information on the irritancy of chemicals toward human skin 
has traditionally been obtained using laboratory animals (mice, rats, 
rabbits, and guinea pigs). The prototypical methodology has been 
Draize testing for irritation (Draize et al., 1944), which produces 
few false negatives (type II errors), but many false positives (type I 
errors). In this methodology, 0.5 ml (liquid) or 0.5 g (solid) of the 
target material is applied to a 1×1" gauze patch onto abraded and 
intact skin for 24 hours. Six or more albino rabbits or guinea pigs 
(Hartley) are used and the patches secured to immobilized animals 
wrapped with an impervious membrane. After 24 hours, the patches 
are removed and scored for the following endpoints:
	 1.	 Erythema and eschar: 0 = none; 1 = barely perceptible; 2 = 
well-defined; 3 = moderate to severe; 4 = severe w/ or w/out 
eschar
	 2.	 Edema: 0 = none; 1 = barely perceptible; 2 = slight with 
edges well-defined; 3 = moderate, less than 1 mm; 4 = severe, 
more than, or equal to 1 mm
The irritancy is determined numerically depending upon the 
final cumulative scores on abraded and normal skin: less than 0.9 = 
nonirritating; 1 to 1.9 = mildly irritating, safe with limitations; and 
more than or equal to 2 = irritating.
For development of new cosmetics, particularly in the 
European Union, a great need exists for an in vitro system to deter-
mine the potential for irritant responses. Since 2004, animal tests 
are prohibited in the EU for cosmetic products. Animal tests have 
also been banned for cosmetic ingredients (European Council and 
Parliament Regulation, 2009). The currently accepted in vitro meth-
ods for assessment of the different subtypes of irritant contact der-
matitis are reviewed below.

diate coagulative necrosis resulting in considerable tissue damage 
with ulceration and sloughing. Also referred to as a severe 2nd or 
3rd degree burn, it does not have a primary inflammatory compo-
nent. Examples of such include burns from strong acids and alka-
lies, ethylene oxide, and other severe dermatotoxicants. Examples

Given its severity, in vitro models for corrosion were con-
sidered essential given the EU’s stance on animal testing. Five 
validated in vitro alternatives are included in the EU regulations 
for skin corrosion testing, mainly the TER (rat skin transcutane-
ous electrical resistance test; OECD TG 430) and tests on recon-
structed human epidermis (EpiSkinTM, EpiDermTM, SkinEthicTM, 
and epiCS® (Netzlaff et al., 2005; OECD TG 431 and TG 435). 
In the TER, a liquid (150 μL) or solid (“sufficient to cover sur-
face”) target material is applied to rat skin for 24 hours. At that 
time, electrical resistance is measured and corrosivity assessed as 
follows: TER more than 5 kΩ, noncorrosive; less than 5 kΩ, corro-
sive. Unfortunately, TER is not applicable to surfactants or neutral 
organics. In the reconstructed epidermal models, liquid (500 μL) or 
solid (500 mg) target material is applied to the reconstructed model 
and either “breakthrough time” or cell viability is used as an index 
for corrosivity. Details of these methodologies can be found in the 
OECD documents and in Almeida et al. (2017).
Acute Irritation  Certain chemicals at sufficient concentration 
can produce an acute irritant response, sometimes called a 1st 
degree or mild 2nd degree chemical burn. These acute irritants 
include acid, alkalies, and oxidizing or reducing chemicals that 
substantially disrupt the cornified layer producing cytotoxicity 
directly. In 2003, ECVAM started analyzing skin irritation valida-
tion models using the previously described “3D models.” In 2007, 
the EpiSkinTM and EpiDermTM models were approved by ECVAM 
to replace the in vivo Draize irritation test. A number of in vitro skin 
irritation models have now been officially validated in the OECD 
guidelines (OECD TG 439), such as EpiSkinTM, EpiDermTM, and 
SkinEthicTM RHEA. In these systems, 25 μL/cm2 (liquid) or 25 
mg/cm2 (solid) is applied for 3 hours and cell viability assessed, 
typically using mitochondrial dehydrogenases to reduce the vital 
dye MTT. Depending upon the methodology, different viability 
endpoints are used. A detailed description of these models can be 
found in the OECD guideline and in Almeida et al. (2017).

In addition to chemical irritants, chronic friction and trauma

The skin at some anatomic sites is more easily irritated than at 
other sites, which can be demonstrated by measurement of TEWL 
(Murahata et al., 1986). The eyelids have a thin epidermis and are 
quite sensitive. Furthermore, the back is more sensitive than the 
forearm (Zhai et al., 2004). Individuals can vary greatly in their 
sensitivity to irritant dermatitis, especially the chronic cumulative 
variety. Although certain genetic contributions to irritant dermatitis 
have been more clearly defined (Liang et al., 2016), the influence 
of age and gender remain controversial (reviewed in Belsito, 2005).
Patients with atopic dermatitis (Fig. 19-2D) are especially 
prone to irritation. The incidence of atopic dermatitis is increasing 
rapidly in industrialized societies. Reports of its incidence in the 
pediatric population within these societies vary widely, but gener-
ally exceed 20% (Pols et al., 2016). Atopic individuals are not only 
more sensitive to irritants, but exhibit a propensity to produce spe-
cific IgE antibodies to allergens, and typically suffer from hayfever 
and allergic asthma. These individuals usually have a higher fre-
quency of persistent contact dermatitis than non-atopics (Nethercott 
and Holness, 1994). Atopic dermatitis has a strong genetic compo-
nent. In many atopics, defects in the intermediate filament aggregat-
ing protein, filaggrin, among other defective genetic barrier genes, 
are detectable (Palmer et al., 2006; Liang et al., 2016).
Allergic Contact Dermatitis
Allergic contact dermatitis (ACD) is a delayed (T-cell mediated) 
hypersensitivity reaction (Gell and Coombs Type IV immune 
response). To induce sensitization through the skin, chemical hap-
tens generally penetrate the lipid barrier and, to be detected by the 
immune system, attach themselves to carrier proteins. The complete 
antigens are processed by Langerhans cells (epidermal dendritic 
cells) and displayed on their surfaces with major histocompatibility 
complex II molecules. Following migration to the paracortical area 
of the draining lymph nodes, the Langerhans cells present the pro-
cessed peptide to naive T-helper cells (Th0) with specific receptors 
for the complexed allergen. This antigen presentation leads to the 
release of a variety of interferons, growth factors, and chemokines, 
which result in a proliferation of a specific clone of sensitized Th1 
(Belsito, 2008). This phase of the response is termed the induction, 
during which, over a 1 to 3 week period, memory T-cells are gener-
ated and enter the circulation.
Upon subsequent exposure to a specific antigen that has pre-
viously been recognized by the immune system, a more vigorous 
and rapid response occurs due to homing of specific memory T 
cells to the skin, where they release cytokines and chemokines that 
attract a variety of other nonspecific inflammatory cells. This phase 
of the immune response is termed elicitation, which may develop 
within hours to several days following antigenic exposure (Mark 
and Slavin, 2006). The “delay” in the response depends upon the 
potency and amount of allergen applied (Friedmann, 2007). The 
resultant epidermal spongiosis (intercellular edema) and perivascu-
lar dermal infiltrate of lymphohistiocytic cells is characteristic, but 
not pathognomonic, of ACD.

Unlike contact irritants, where the response is generally pro-
portional to the applied dose and time, contact allergens can elicit 
reactions at very small doses. In contrast, at significantly higher 
doses, saturation of the response, or even inhibition (tolerance), 
may become evident (Marker and Thomsen, 1986). Nevertheless, 
careful analysis from human and animal testing (Friedmann, 2007) 
show that a higher dose confers a greater likelihood of sensitiza-
tion to a given allergen. Moreover, the dose dependence for the 
induction of sensitization displays nonlinearity, suggesting that the 
response is sub-linear, and probably sigmoidal (Friedmann, 2007). 
These observations have practical implications: as the number of 
cosmetic preservatives becomes more restrictive due to regulatory 
activities, especially in the European Union, the likelihood increases 
that a given biocide will be used in more products and/or at higher 
concentrations in a given product, and that this higher cumulative 
exposure will result in sensitization to what otherwise are weak 
allergens, a phenomenon referred to as the Dillarstone effect.
Immunoregulation  Although animal studies have shown strain 
(presumably genetic) variation in cell-mediated immunity, the evi-
dence for a genetic influence in humans has been slight. Skog (1958) 
found that 5% of a defined population could not be sensitized to 
dinitrochlorobenzene and suggested that this was due to inheritance. 
In another study, significant genetic association with the capacity 
to become sensitized to para-nitrosodimethylaniline was reported 
(Walker et al., 1967). However, multiple attempts to correlate HLA 
haplotype and other factors (including T-cell receptor genes) with 
nickel sensitivity or other contact allergies have yielded conflicting 
results. In atopic dermatitis, gene variants are thought to contrib-
ute to the observed abnormalities in innate and adaptive immune 
responses (Liang et al., 2016). Nonetheless, definitive evidence of a 
genetic predisposition to ACD in humans has been meager, probably 
because of the diverse genetic pool and the limitations of technology.
The route of primary sensitization has a profound effect on the 
subsequent immunologic response. Sulzberger (1929) demonstrated 
that intracardiac injection of neoarsphenamine induced tolerance 
rather than sensitization. Tolerance induction has also been reported 
after primary oral ingestion of allergens (Chase, 1946), as well as 
after primary epicutaneous application of allergens to areas defi-
cient in HLA-DR-positive Langerhans cells (Elmets et al., 1983). 
The exact mechanism by which tolerance ensues is controversial, 
and may depend on the route of exposure (oral, intravenous, epi-
cutaneous, or intraperitoneal) and the precise subset of dendritic 
cells responsible for antigen presentation (Akbari and Umetsu, 
2005). There are data to suggest that the expression of indoleamine 
2,3-dioxygenase, an intracellular heme-enzyme that catalyzes oxi-
dative catabolism of indole rings by dendritic cells, results in sup-
pression of T cell responses and promotion of tolerance (Mellor and 
Munn, 2004). In general, either induction of hapten-specific CD4+/
CD25+ or Th3 suppressor T cells (Elmets et al., 1983; Girolomoni 
et al., 2004; Cavani, 2005) and/or clonal deletion of the responding 
T cells (Nossal, 1991) seems responsible for the observed tolerance. 
Readers interested in a better understanding of the mechanism(s) of 
tolerance induction are referred elsewhere. (Girolomoni et al., 2004; 
Akbari and Umetsu, 2005; Cavani, 2005).
Aged individuals have been shown to have various defects 
in the induction and/or elicitation of ACD (Kwangsukstith and 
Maibach, 1995). The precise reason is unknown and many aspects 
of cell-mediated responses are likely involved (Belsito et al., 1987). 
Experiments in which contact-sensitized aged mice were injected 
with naive young T cells and subsequently demonstrated normal 
responses upon antigenic challenge suggest that a failure of T cell 
amplification signals and/or the generation of sufficient T effec-
tor cells may be among the primary deficiencies in aged animals 
(Belsito and Possick, 1988).
The competency of immune responses in children is also con-
troversial (Kwangsukstith and Maibach, 1995). In the past, it was 
thought that children rarely developed ACD because of an immature 
immune system and that patch testing of children with standard 
concentrations of allergens resulted in a high percentage of irri-
tant reactions (Marcussen, 1963). However, other data suggest that 
patch testing of children with the allergens commercially available 
in the United States does not result in increased, and confounding, 
irritant responses (Rietschel et al., 1990; Zug et al., 2014). Of note, 
Strauss (1931) was able to sensitize 35 of 48 infants (1 to 4 days 
old) to Toxicodendron oleoresin, suggesting that the apparent hypo-
responsiveness of children may be due to limited exposure and not 
to deficient immunity. Similarly, the effects of gender on the inci-
dence of ACD seem related to the likelihood of exposure (Schubert 
et al., 1987; Kwangsukstith and Maibach, 1995).
In experimental models and clinical practice, downregula-
tion of ACD has been achieved with ultraviolet radiation (UVB or 
PUVA), glucocorticoids, and calcineurin inhibitors. Each of these 
acts somewhat differently. UVB and PUVA significantly decrease 
the density of epidermal LCs, induce epidermal hyperplasia (mak-
ing it more difficult for the antigen to reach any remaining epider-
mal antigen-presenting cells), upregulate IL-10, and induce CD4+/
CD25+ regulatory T cells (Belsito, 1989; Aubin and Mousson, 
2004). Glucocorticoids also inhibit the type IV reaction at multiple 
points (Belsito, 1989): epidermal LCs are decreased in number, 
the production and function of IL-1, IL-2, and IFN-γ are inhibited, 
and T cell proliferation is decreased. In contrast, cyclosporine A 
and other macrolactams act primarily to inhibit activation of cNF-
AT via inhibition of calcineurin (Bornhövd et al., 2001). Finally, a 
variety of other pharmacologic agents have been reported to inter-
fere with the induction and/or elicitation of ACD in murine models 
(Belsito, 1997).
Diagnosis and Testing  When a patient exhibits allergic con-
tact dermatitis, finding the responsible chemical is important to 
avoid continued exposure. For this purpose, patch testing is the 
gold standard that has been employed and refined since it was first 
described by Jadassohn (1895). On the washed back of patients 
who are not exhibiting dermatitis at the sites to be tested, nor using 
corticosteroids or other immunosuppressive treatments, patches 
are placed that contain a small amount of the putative allergen. 
Many of these materials are commercially available at standard-
ized concentrations too low to produce significant irritant reac-
tions, although induction of the allergic response has been reported 
to occur (Tomb, 1992). Certain chemicals are usually not tested 
because they induce too strong a response or might produce sensi-
tization (e.g., urushiol from poison ivy). After 2 days, during which 
time a reaction begins to develop, the patches are removed and the 
sites of exposure are scored for degree of response. These sites 
are additionally re-evaluated at any time from 1 to 5 days after-
wards (3 to 7 days after the initial application). The relevance of 
any positive reaction to the patient’s actual environment must be 
considered so that exposure to putative allergens in daily life can be 
minimized to appropriate chemicals. Interpretation of the results, 
and resulting modifications in lifestyle, should take into account the

Animal testing to predict allergenicity has an extensive history 
and the prototypical test has been the Guinea Pig Maximization Test 
by Magnusson and Kligman (1969). In this assay, a chemical is 
injected intradermally with or without Freund’s complete adjuvant. 
The reaction of the skin to subsequent challenge with a chemical 
can then be observed and graded. While this approach has been 
successful, the extrapolation of sensitivity measurements from labo-
ratory animals to humans presented large uncertainties. Therefore, 
in the last couple decades, the local lymph node assay (LLNA), per-
formed in mice, has become the predominant in vivo animal tool to 
measure the sensitization capacity of a specific chemical (Basketter 
et al., 2012). Not only does the LLNA use a smaller number of 
laboratory animals than the Magnusson-Kligman assay, it also has 
the capability to rank the potency of allergens from very strong to 
weak. Because sensitizers can differ in potency over four orders of 
magnitude, this quantitative assay has been extremely helpful.
As is the case with irritation, the European Union directive 
eliminating animal use in cosmetic testing has stimulated the devel-
opment of integrated in vitro and in silico testing strategies, where 
predictions for potential skin sensitization by a particular material 
include its physicochemical structure and its performance in vari-
ous in vitro tests. There are four key biological components in the 
“adverse outcome pathway” (AOP) for skin sensitization classified 
as: (1) the molecular initiating event; (2) activation of keratinocytes 
as detected by gene expression associated with specific cell signal-
ing pathways and inflammatory responses; (3) dendritic cell activa-
tion as determined by specific expression of cell surface markers; 
and (4) T-cell proliferation. There is general agreement in the sci-
entific arena that assessment of only one aspect of the AOP will not 
provide sufficient information to replace the use of animals for the 
assessment of chemical allergenicity (reviewed in Maxwell et al., 
2014). Rather, information from non-testing methods (in silico data, 
analogue based “read across” information) and in vitro approaches 
that evaluate the key events in the AOP will be needed. Information 
garnered from these approaches will need to be further combined to 
address the sensitization end point in a weight of evidence frame-
work, which is an area of intense research (Patlewicz et al., 2014; 
Tollefsen et al., 2014).
As far as addressing the key events in the sensitization 
AOP, four assays have been endorsed at the OECD level at this 
time. The first event in the AOP for sensitization can be assessed 
by the Direct Peptide Reactivity Assay or “DPRA” (Gerberick 
et al., 2004, 2008; OECD TG 442C), which measures the ability of 
a chemical to react with peptides, specifically histidine and cyste-
ine. To address the second step of the AOP, keratinocyte activation, 
KeratinoSens™ has been validated by ECVAM and has accepted 
OECD guidelines (OECD TG 442D). In the KeratinoSens™ assay, 
the sensitizing potential of a chemical is assessed using immor-
talized adherent human keratinocytes (HaCaT cell-line), trans-
fected with a plasmid to quantify luciferase gene induction as a 
measure of activation of Keap1-NRF2-antioxidant/electrophile 
response element (ARE)-1. Assessment of the third step in the 
AOP, dendritic cell activation, can be undertaken using either the 
ECVAM-validated MUSST or h-CLAT methodologies, the latter 
of which have OECD guidelines (OECD 442E). In the MUSST 
assay, expression of CD86 and CD54 on a myeloid dendritic cell 
line is measured, while h-CLAT measures only CD54 expression. 
Currently there is no validated methodology to assess the fourth 
component of the AOP, T-cell activation.
The integration of the results of testing the first three steps of 
the AOP to predict sensitization and hazard has yet to be resolved. 
It is an area of active research and various models (Bayesian net-
work, Artificial Neural Network and Weight of Evidence) have been 
proposed. Currently, the Weight of Evidence or the best “two out 
of three” approach in assessing sensitization based upon the testing 
of the first three key events in the AOP has found the greatest suc-
cess. This was initially proposed by Bauch et al. (2012) and later 
updated by Natsch et al. (2013) and Urbisch et al. (2015). In this 
approach, chemicals are identified as non-sensitizers if they are neg-
ative in both the DPRA and either the KeratinoSens™ assay or the 
MUSST/h-CLAT assays. However, using the current approaches 
does have drawbacks. An issue of significant concern among der-
matotoxicologists is the ability of the current in vitro test to detect 
pre-haptens (abiotically transformed materials; e.g., oxidized in the 
environment) and pro-haptens (biotically transformed materials via 
metabolism in the skin). Clearly, one or more of the current meth-
odologies must be adapted to detect pre-and pro-haptens (Patlewicz 
et al., 2016). Efforts to further improve these systems is already 
underway as evidenced by the peroxidase peptide reactivity assay 
“PPRA” (Gerberick et al., 2009; Gerberick, 2016), which looks at 
identification of pro-haptens via the use of peroxidase in the DPRA.

PHOTOSENSITIVITY
The ultraviolet and visible spectra of solar radiation that reach 
the earth’s atmosphere extend from 290 to 700 nm. Wavelengths 
beyond this range are either filtered by the earth’s atmosphere or 
are insufficiently energetic to cause cutaneous pathology. Adequate 
doses of artificially produced UVC (100 to 289 nm) or x-rays (less 
than 10 nm) can produce profound physical and toxicologic skin 
changes; however, these would occur only accidentally and more 
typically in the occupational or laboratory environment. The pro-
tective skin pigment, melanin, synthesized by the melanocytes, 
absorbs a broad range of radiation from ultraviolet B (290 to 320 
nm) through the visible spectrum (400 to 700 nm). Other chromo-
phores in the skin include amino acids, particularly tryptophan and 
tyrosine, and their breakdown products (e.g., urocanic acid), which 
absorb light in the UVB range. Biologically, the most significant 
chromophore in the skin is DNA and, as such, damage from radia-
tion can have lasting effects on the genetic information of the target 
cells, as outlined under the section “Skin Cancer,” below.
Phototoxicity
Phototoxic reactions from exogenous chemicals may be produced 
either by systemic or topical exposures. In acute reactions, the 
skin can become red and blistered within minutes to hours fol-
lowing ultraviolet light exposure, typically UVA (320 to 400 
nm). Occupational examples include coal tar, which, in combina-
tion with sunlight, can produce an immediate stinging sensation 
referred to as “tar smarts.” Over time, this damage can result in 
significant hyperpigmentation of the skin.
Phototoxic chemicals most commonly absorb in the UVA 
range, thereby assuming a higher energy, excited, triplet state; the 
subsequent transfer of electrons to other molecules or reduction 
to more highly reactive-free radicals results in the observed tissue 
damage (Moan and Peng, 2003; Deleo, 2004). Oxygen dependent 
photodynamic reactions commonly occur as these excited mole-
cules return to their ground state and transfer their energy to oxy-
gen, forming high reactive singlet oxygen. These reactive products 
then damage cellular macromolecules, especially unsaturated mem-
brane lipids, which results in cell death. The release of a variety of 
immune mediators from the dying keratinocytes and the recruitment 
of inflammatory cells to the skin are the histopathologic signs of 
phototoxicity. Those  chemicals most often associated with photo-
toxic reactions are listed in Table 19-7.
As opposed to many phototoxins that require oxygen radicals, 
psoralens are an example of chemicals producing phototoxicity with-
out production of singlet oxygen. Psoralens intercalate within DNA 
to form covalent adducts, and cross-link when activated by UVA. 
The result is inhibition of DNA synthesis and repair causing the 
cells to die. Psoralens can be found in various foods, including lime 
and celery. Production of photodermatitis in occupational settings 
where such foods are routinely handled has been reported (Seligman 
et al., 1987). An example of phototoxicity from furocoumarin pres-
ent in lime peel is given in Fig. 19-2F. Psoralens in combination 
with UVA (PUVA) have been used therapeutically to inhibit kera-
tinocyte growth and lymphocyte proliferation in diseases such as 
psoriasis and cutaneous T-cell lymphoma; however, this modality 
is infrequently used today because of better treatment options for 
these diseases, and an enhanced risk of both squamous cell car-
cinoma and malignant melanoma with prolonged PUVA therapy 
(Stern, 1992; Stern, 2001).
Nails, an appendage of the skin, may also suffer phototoxic 
reactions to drugs and, less commonly, to topically applied chemi-
cals. While various types of nail pathology may result, including 
changes in cell growth and pigmentation, detachment of the various 
layers of the nail plate from each other (onycholysis) is the most 
commonly observed effect of phototoxicity. Agents such as coal 
tar and tetracyclines produce photodynamic onycholysis, which 
requires generation of oxygen radicals. In contrast, topical or sys-
temic psoralens produce onycholysis by a non-photodynamic mech-
anism that does not require oxygen (reviewed in Epstein, 1999).
Photoallergy
Like phototoxins, most photoallergens respond to light in the 
UVA range (Table 19-8). Except for the need for photoactivation 
of the allergen, photoallergy is a typical delayed type hypersen-
sitivity response. The reaction may take hours to days to develop 
and can persist for several weeks following exposure if the agent 
is retained within the skin. Use of halogenated salicylanilides in 
consumer products led to thousands of cases of photoallergy in the 
1960s and 1970s. Subsequent to salicylanilides, fragrances, espe-
cially musk ambrette, in cosmetics, became conspicuous sources 
of photoallergy until these materials were banned. Nowadays, 
sunscreening agents and topical nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory 
drugs (NSAIDS, e.g., ketoprofen) are the more commonly found 
photoallergens (European Multicentre Photopatch Test Study 
(EMCPPTS) Taskforce, 2012). Although a photoallergic response 
to these agents is rare, they have become widely used as the long-
term risks effects of sun exposure and systemic intake of NSAIDS 
have become appreciated.

phototoxicity, because the former, like all delayed type hypersensi-

In Vitro Determination of Phototoxicity 
and Photoallergenicity
One way to assess the ability of a material to cause a phototoxic 
or photoallergic reaction is its ultraviolet absorption spectrum. If 
absorption is below the molar extinction coefficient (MEC) of less 
than 1000 L × mol−1 × cm−1, then the material has little to no 
phototoxic or photosensitization hazard (Henry et al., 2009). If 
absorption occurs, then phototoxicity and photosensitization are 
both possible. In the reactive oxygen species (ROS) assay, the gen-
eration of ROS (superoxide anions and singlet oxygen) following 
irradiation of the target material by light, while not measuring pho-
totoxicity directly, had 100% sensitivity (with some false positives) 
in predicting phototoxicity (Haranosono et al., 2014); however, this 
methodology has not been validated by ECVAM.
According to the OECD, the 3T3 Neutral Red Uptake in vitro 
phototoxicity test can be used to predict the phototoxic potential 
of a chemical (OECD TG 432). The 3T3 Neutral Red assay evalu-
ates cytotoxicity in BALB/c 3T3 mouse fibroblasts exposed to the 
test material in the presence or absence of UVA light, in order to 
determine the concentration-dependent reduction in Neutral Red 
Uptake by the cells. Although this method is accepted by the 
OECD, drawbacks do exist. According to Prieto et al. (2013), the 
following caveats must be considered: (1) a false negative result 
can occur since the toxicological hazard of a chemical is primarily 
due to a mechanism of action not captured by the cell line used; 
(2) a false positive can occur given a compound’s limited bioavail-
ability in vivo due to poor absorption and distribution, or a rapid 
biotransformation and excretion; and (3) the cell lines have a lack 
of metabolic capacity, thus some compounds that are metabolized 
in vivo to biologically active forms may be missed. As a result, 
intense efforts are underway to evaluate other in vitro phototoxicity 
test methodologies using photo-exposed “3D skin models” (Portes 
et al., 2002), as well as a fluorometric ROS assay (Seto et al., 2016).
As opposed to phototoxicity, there is currently no ECVAM-
validated or OECD approved guideline for photoallergenicity. 
However, a number of in vitro testing methodologies utilizing 
KeratinoSensTM and h-CLAT with the addition of exposure to 5 J/
cm2 of UVA have been undertaken. Tsujita-Inoue et al. (2016) have 
assessed the second step in the adverse outcome pathway (keratino-
cyte activation) using the photo-KeratinoSensTM assay. Additionally, 
Hoya et al. (2009) have evaluated the photo-h-CLAT assay. The 
development of in vitro systems to assess photosensitization is 
critical given the ban in Europe in testing cosmetic ingredients in 
animals, since testing for photoallergenicity in humans is consid-
ered unethical because of the potential for the induction of per-
sistent light reactivity, in which the individual remains extremely 
sensitive to sunlight even in the absence of the initially inducing 
photoallergen.
As is the case for non-photoactivated allergens, there remains a 
concern that in vitro testing may miss pre- and pro-photoallergens. 
To date, two models of photoantigen formation, the photohapten 
model and the pro-hapten model, have been reported (Tokura et al., 
1996; Tokura, 2000). In the induction phase of photoallergy, photo-
allergens can produce ROS and induce apoptosis in keratinocytes, 
but the use of either or both biomarkers would detect both photoal-
lergy and phototoxicity, and would not distinguish the two (Onoue 
and Tsuda, 2006; Kurita et al., 2007). Therefore, other investigators 
have focused on the in vitro formation of photo-antigens (Lovell 
and Jones, 2000). In these studies, the photobinding properties of 
putative photoallergens to human serum albumin have been used, 
similar to the approach used in the DPRA.
Endogenous Photosensitivities
Endogenous photosensitivity results from both endogenous and 
exogenous factors (reviewed in Bylaite et al., 2009). In a variety of 
genetic diseases, such as xeroderma pigmentosum, the cell’s ability 
to repair ultraviolet light induced damage is significantly impaired. 
Systemic lupus erythematosus, an autoimmune connective tissue 
disease, features an abnormal sensitivity to UVB; however, the 
exact reasons are unclear. In hereditary or chemically induced por-
phyria, enzyme abnormalities disrupt the biosynthetic pathways 
that produce heme (the building block for hemoglobin, myoglobin, 
catalases, peroxidases, and cytochromes), leading to accumulation 
of protoporphyrin precursors throughout the body, including the 
skin, which are activated by visible light (400 to 410 nm). One 
such porphyria, porphyria cutanea tarda, can be precipitated by 
alcohol, estrogens, or certain antibiotics in individuals with heredi-
tary abnormalities in porphyrin synthesis. Pellagra (photodermati-
tis, diarrhea, dementia, and death), due to alteration in tryptophan 
metabolism, can be induced by such drugs as isonicotinic acid and 
hydralazine; the treatment for pellagra is oral niacin (vitamin B3).

Other Responses to Electromagnetic 
Radiation
The dermatologic toxicity induced by electromagnetic radiation is 
not limited to the development of phototoxicity or photosensitiza-
tion (reviewed in Krutmann et al., 2017). Indeed, the most evident 
acute feature of ultraviolet radiation exposure is erythema (redness 
or sunburn). The minimal erythema dose (MED), the smallest dose 
of ultraviolet light that induces an erythematous response, varies 
greatly from person to person, largely dependent upon skin pig-
mentation. In the sunburn reaction, vasodilatation, responsible for 
the color change, is accompanied by the production of a variety of 
inflammatory mediators from injured keratinocytes. These media-
tors are responsible for the systemic symptoms that may be associ-
ated with sunburn: fever, chills, and malaise. UVB (290 to 320 nm) 
is the most effective wavelength for inducing erythema in human 
skin. Substantially greater amounts of UVA reach earth compared 
to UVB (as much as 100-fold greater); however, UVA’s efficiency 
in generating erythema in humans is about 1000-fold less than that 
of UVB. Both UVA and UVB have been implicated in the develop-
ment of melanoma and non-melanoma skin cancers. Because of its 
wavelengths and greater depth of penetration, UVA is more respon-
sible for some of the long-term effects of sun exposure: wrinkling, 
thinning (atrophy), and easy bruisability.
Overt pigment darkening is another response to ultraviolet 
exposure and is accomplished by enhanced melanin production by 
melanocytes and/or photo-oxidation of melanin. Tanning (increased 
pigmentation occurring within 3 days of ultraviolet exposure) is 
to be contrasted with the immediate pigment darkening of photo-
oxidation. The tanning response is most readily produced by expo-
sure to UVB and may be induced, along with erythema and DNA 
repair, by DNA damage. Tanning serves to augment the protective 
effects of melanin in the skin; however, this protective effect may 
be insufficient in preventing the damage it induces, especially in 
fair-skinned individuals (Sheehan et al., 2002).
Chronic exposure to radiation results in a variety of charac-
teristic skin changes (reviewed in Krutmann et al., 2017). These 
changes accelerate or mimic aging, but the rate depends greatly 
on the baseline skin pigmentation of the individual. Lighter skin 
people suffer from chronic sun exposure with a greater frequency 
than more darkly pigmented individuals. Photo-exposed areas 
(head, neck, backs of hands, and the sternal area of the chest) are 
more readily damaged by light. Pigmentary changes, such as freck-
ling and hypomelanotic areas (idiopathic guttate hypomelanosis), 
wrinkling, telangiectasias (fine dilated superficial blood vessels), 
pre-cancerous lesions (actinic keratoses and porokeratoses), and 
malignant skin lesions (basal and squamous cell carcinoma; malig-
nant melanoma) are all consequences of chronic exposure to ultra-
violet light. One significant pathophysiologic response of chronic 
exposure to light is a pronounced decrease in the Langerhans 
cells. Chronically sun exposed skin may have up to 50% fewer of 
these cells compared to photo-protected areas (Lim et al., 1983). 
This decrease may lessen immune surveillance of neoantigens on 
malignant cells, and may allow for such transformations to proceed 
unchecked. For these reasons, a tan obtained either naturally or in a 
tanning salon is not to be recommended (Lim et al., 2011).
Aside from the toxic nature of electromagnetic radiation, 
natural and environmental exposures to certain bands of light are 
vital for survival. Ultraviolet radiation is critical for the conversion 
of 7-dehydrocholesterol to pre-vitamin D3, without which normal 
endogenous production of vitamin D would not take place. Blue 
light (420 to 490 nm) can be lifesaving in premature infants due to 
its ability to photoisomerize bilirubin in the skin. In addition, the 
toxic effects of ultraviolet light have been exploited for decades 
through artificial light sources for treatment of skin disorders such 
as psoriasis.
URTICARIA
The skin is capable not only of delayed type hypersensitivity reac-
tions involving T-cells, but also IgE-mediated reactions resulting 
in allergic urticaria. For those allergens to which IgE antibodies 
have been elicited by previous or ongoing systemic or topical expo-
sures, subsequent cutaneous and/or systemic contact can lead to 
development of hives, typically in minutes, through an immediate, 
Gell and Coombs type I, hypersensitivity reaction. Hives are raised 
wheals that usually itch or sting and appear reddish. The symptoms 
of allergic urticaria result from degranulation of cutaneous mast 
cells induced by binding of IgE to receptors on the cell surface, 
leading to release of histamine and other vasoactive substances. 
Food allergies (Sicherer and Sampson, 2010) and pharmaceuticals 
(Limsuwan and Demoly, 2010) are major causes of acute allergic 
urticaria. Some agents (e.g., opiates) can bring about the direct 
release of histamine from mast cells without antibody mediation, 
while others (NSAIDs) do so through effects on arachidonic acid 
metabolism or other uncertain mechanisms. Urticaria lasting longer 
than 6 weeks, if not attributable to allergen exposure, frequently has 
an autoimmune basis, that is, the development of autoantibodies to 
the high infinity IgE receptor. True urticaria generally disappears 
within hours and, at the most, by 1 day. A hive persisting in a given 
location longer than 24 hours is more likely related to a non-urticar-
ial process, such as vasculitis. The lifetime prevalence of urticaria 
has been estimated at 9%, of which nearly 2% occurs chronically 
(Zuberbier et al., 2010).
Contact urticaria can arise from cutaneous exposure to a plant, 
animal, or other protein sources (allergic), but can also have a non-
allergic basis (Table 19-9). Allergic contact urticaria appears more 
commonly in atopic individuals and among certain occupational 
groups, for example, food workers due to proteins in food and the 
medical profession due to natural rubber latex proteins in gloves 
(Doutre, 2005). As opposed to the accelerators (carbamates, thiu-
rams, benzothiazoles, and 1,3-diphenylguanidine) and anti-oxidants 
(p-phenylenediamine derivatives) that cause delayed type hypersen-
sitivity, the hevein proteins in latex have caused severe, life threat-
ening reactions and deaths in some individuals following cutaneous 
or mucosal exposure. In response to the epidemic of latex hyper-
sensitivity, labeling for latex has been required on all medically 
related products in the United States. Additionally, manufactures 
have largely eliminated the more highly allergenic hevein proteins 
(Hev b1, Hev b3, Hev b5, and Hev b6.02) found in natural rubber 
latex from their products (Koh et al., 2005)
In addition to latex, other allergic contact urticants that result 
in massive IgE-mediated release of vasoactive agents from the mast 
cells have been reported. These include certain foods (e.g., nuts, 
fish, and shellfish) and pharmaceutical products (e.g., penicillins, 
cephalosporins, sulfonamides, macrolides, vancomycin, tetracy-
clines, and fluoroquinolones), among others. Insect allergens, such 
as those present in the stings of bees and wasps, are also known to 
cause an anaphylactic response.

ACNE
Acne is a common affliction of the pilosebaceous units especially 
of the face, chest, and back. The condition arises from blockage of 
the duct leading from the sebaceous gland to the cutaneous surface. 
This results in retention of sebum and enlargement of the gland. 
In its most common form (acne vulgaris), hormones associated 
with puberty lead to high sebum production, hyperproliferation 
and cornification of the ductal cells, plugging of the orifice, and 
retention of corneocytes in the follicular lumen (Cunliffe et al., 
2004). Proliferation of resident bacteria and inflammation within 
the blocked pore/sebaceous gland result. Topically, long chain 
fatty acids can give an acne-like response in animal models, and 
appear to do so in some individuals exposed to cosmetics. Likely 
acting in a similar fashion, petroleum products (oils, coal tar) in the 
workplace can give rise to acneiform eruptions (Ancona, 1986). 
Insoluble cutting oils used in the machine industry may have this 
effect, as illustrated in Fig. 19-2I. Such agents, which may contain 
chlorinated paraffins, stimulate excessive stratification of the duc-
tile cells thus staunching the egress of sebum from the gland.
Chloracne
The most disfiguring form of acne in humans, chloracne, is caused 
by exposure to halogenated aromatic hydrocarbons. Chloracnegenic 
chemicals, such as polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) and naphtha-
lenes, are often found together with the highly potent polychlo-
rinated dibenzofuran and dioxin contaminants formed during the 
synthesis of these agents. Chloracne is a rare disease, but still 
occurs sporadically from industrial and environmental exposures 
(Gawkrodger et al., 2009; Passarini et al., 2010). It was seen among 
returning veterans during the Vietnam War following exposure to 
“Agent Orange.” The most recent publicized example has been the 
poisoning of the Ukrainian President, Viktor Yushchenko; the diag-
nosis of chloracne and poisoning was made by a Swiss dermatolo-
gist, Jean Hilaire Saurat, and his colleagues (Sorg et al., 2009).
The recalcitrant nature of chloracne and its preventability mark 
it as an important occupational and environmental illness. Typically, 
blackheads (comedones) and cysts are present behind the ears and 
about the eyes, on the shoulders, back, and genitalia. As opposed 
to acne vulgaris, the nose is almost invariably spared. In addition 
to acne, increased hair in atypical locations (hypertrichosis), hyper-
pigmentation, brown discoloration of the nails, conjunctivitis, and 
eye discharge may be present. Because chloracne is a marker of sys-
temic exposure, concurrent effects in the liver and nervous system 
may accompany the cutaneous findings. Chloracne exhibits pro-
gressive degeneration of the sebaceous units with transformation

GRANULOMATOUS DISEASE
Foreign body reactions, isolating invading substances that cannot 
be readily removed, occur following the introduction of a variety 
of foreign agents into the skin through injection or subsequent to 
a laceration or abrasion. These reactions can take three forms: (1) 
nontoxic (the mere presence of an inert substance in the dermis); 
(2) toxic, in which the substance induces activations of macro-
phages; and (3) immunologic, in which T cells are activated. Toxic 
and immunologic granulomas can produce persistent lesions with 
abundant inflammatory cells resembling chronic infectious condi-
tions (e.g., tuberculosis, leprosy, Leishmaniasis, etc.), and can pres-
ent diagnostic challenges (Del Rosario et al., 2005).
The cutaneous response to graphite intradermally injected into 
the skin via puncture with a “lead pencil” is a common example of 
the nontoxic granuloma. The resultant “carbon tattoo” may persist 
as a grayish dot visible on the skin surface for years. However, there 
is no inflammatory reaction, and, hence, no sequela following this 
intradermal injection.
Silica or talc (magnesium silicate) is a prime example of an 
agent causing toxic granulomas. After a latent period of months to 
years following introduction into the abdominal cavity as a result 
of the use of powdered surgical gloves, the original large particles 
desegregate to assume a colloidal state that may incite significant 
macrophage activation with resultant granuloma formation, which 
has led to bowel obstruction and other complications. The US Food 
and Drug Administration (FDA) issued a final rule banning pow-
dered medical gloves beginning on January 19, 2017 (FDA, 2016). 
Injections of paraffin or mineral oil into the skin have long been 
known to induce sclerosing lipogranulomas (Heidingsfeld, 1906).
Delayed allergic sensitization (immunologic granulomas) may 
occur with beryllium. Analogous to the reaction in the lung, skin 
lesions have been reported in individuals intracutaneously exposed 
to beryllium. An example of beryllium granuloma of the skin is 
shown in Fig. 19-2J. Cutaneous gadolinium deposition and fibrosis 
are seen in the nephrogenic systemic fibrosis syndrome that arises 
following gadolinium exposure in patients with compromised renal 
function (Wilford et al., 2010). Metallic mercury and zirconium 
compounds, formerly used as deodorants, and tattoo dyes (includ-
ing cobalt, chromium, mercury, and others) can also induce immu-
nologic granulomatous reactions (Kaur et al., 2009) that, in rare 
cases, can be induced or intensified by intense light (laser) treatment 
(Tourlaki et al., 2010).
SCLERODERMATOUS SKIN DISORDERS
Scleroderma encompasses a group of rare diseases that involve 
the hardening and tightening of the skin and connective tissues. In 
some, scleroderma affects only the skin. But it may also involve 
structures beyond the skin, including blood vessels and internal 
organs (heart, lung, kidney, and digestive tract). Signs and symp-
toms vary, depending on which structures are affected.
Scleroderma results from the overproduction and accumula-
tion of collagen in body tissues. What prompts abnormal collagen 
production is not precisely known, but the disease appears to be 
autoimmune. In some genetically susceptible people, symptoms 
may be triggered by exposure to silica, polyvinyl chloride, epoxy 
resins, meta-phenylenediamine, bleomycin, and denatured rape-
seed oil. The most devastating epidemic of “toxic (rapeseed) oil 
syndrome” occurred in Spain in May of 1981, and is reviewed by 
Posada de la Paz et al. (2001).
TOXIC EPIDERMAL NECROLYSIS
Toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN) is a rare life-threatening skin dis-
ease with an incidence of one to two cases per million person years. 
In patients with acquired immune deficiency syndrome (AIDS), the 
incidence appears to be 1000-fold higher (Harr and French, 2010). 
At the most severe end of the spectrum of adverse cutaneous hyper-
sensitivity reactions to drugs, this syndrome involves detachment of 
≥30% of the epidermal surface from the dermis, commonly accom-
panied by severe erosions of the mucous membranes. Mucous 
membrane involvement can result in gastrointestinal hemorrhage, 
respiratory failure, ocular abnormalities, and genitourinary compli-
cations. Even with intensive treatment and prompt identification of 
causality, it may have a fatality rate of approximately 30%.
Stevens–Johnson syndrome is a milder, but still serious, condi-
tion that has been considered by some to fall within the spectrum of 
TEN; however, many dermatologists consider this a separate entity 
induced by drugs, viruses, and other often unknown precipitating 
factors. In Stevens–Johnson syndrome, up to 10% of skin surface 
area may be affected and there is a proportionally lower fatality rate. 
The two syndromes may be difficult to separate when 10% to 30% 
of the skin surface is involved.
TEN is most commonly associated with drugs; however, the 
disorder has other potential etiologies, including infection, malig-
nancy, and vaccinations. At onset, it may resemble an upper respi-
ratory tract infection (fever, cough, sore throat, and malaise), but 
prompt diagnosis when the cutaneous lesions develop several days 
later improves survival. Ongoing exposure to drugs first encoun-
tered during the previous month should be stopped, and treatment is 
initiated similar to that for severe burn patients: fluid and electrolyte 
replacement, protection from infection, and nutritional assistance. 
Nearly 200 drugs have been reported to cause this syndrome with 
the major contributors being the anticonvulsants, NSAIDs, sulfon-
amides, and allopurinol.

The mechanisms leading to TEN continue to be evaluated, 
but are not clear. Studies of genetic predisposition have revealed 
striking correlation between HLAB*1502+ in Han Chinese patients 
and susceptibility to carbamazepine (Chung et al., 2004). The U.S. 
Food & Drug Administration recommends that Asian patients 
be screened for this genotype before prescribing carbamazepine 
(Farkas and Feeney, 2007).
TEN is an immune-related, cytotoxic T cell reaction that 
causes apoptosis of keratinocytes, which presumably express a for-
eign antigen. In many ways, it mimics a hypersensitivity reaction, 
with its characteristic delayed reaction to an initial exposure and 
a more rapid response following repeated exposure. The proposed 
immunopathologic pathways leading to TEN include the follow-
ing: Fas ligand activation on keratinocyte membranes leading to 
death receptor–mediated apoptosis (Abe et al., 2003); release of 
destructive proteins (perforin and granzyme B) from cytotoxic T 
lymphocytes generated following an interaction with cells express-
ing major histocompatability complex (MHC) class I (Posadas 
et al., 2002); overproduction of T cell– and/or macrophage-
derived cytokines, such as interferon-γ, tumor necrosis factor-α, 
and various interleukins (Paul et al., 1996; Nassif et al., 2004); 
and drug-induced secretion of granulysin from cytotoxic T cells, 
natural killer cells, and natural killer T cells (Chung et al., 2008). 
In addition to treating the inciting cause (usually discontinuation 
of a drug), blockage of death receptor ligands using intravenous 
immunoglobin therapy can be extremely beneficial (Ardern-Jones 
and Friedmann, 2011).
HAIR
Hair, like the nail, is an “appendage” of the skin. It is a keratinous 
protein filament that grows from follicles found in the dermis of the 
skin. The highly structural and organized cortex, or middle layer 
of the hair, is the primary source of mechanical strength and water 
uptake. The cortex contains melanin, which gives the hair its color. 
Hair is one of the defining characteristics of mammals, where it 
primarily has thermoregulatory and tactile (whiskers) functions. 
The human body, apart from areas of mucosa and glabrous skin, is 
covered in follicles which produce thick (terminal) and fine (vellus) 
hairs. Human hair growth can last from 2 to 6 years and is divided 
into three phases: (1) anagen (growth) lasting 2 to 6 years; (2) cata-
gen (involution) occurring over 1 month; and telogen (the shedding 
phase) lasting 3 months.
Toxic effects on hair occur from systemic exposures affecting 
the anagen phase. The most commonly encountered toxic effect is 
hair loss (alopecia) and is referred to as anagen effluvium. The prin-
cipal causes are systemic cancer chemotherapeutics and heavy met-
als (mercury, bismuth, and thallium). Indeed, thallium salts were 
previously used in early 20th century for the treatment of ringworm 
of the scalp (tinea capitis) to induce hair loss and thus cure this 
fungal infection (Percival, 1930). This use has long ago disappeared 
due to its narrow therapeutic index and the development of safer and 
more effective treatment modalities. Kanwar and Narang (2013) 
have reviewed the multiple etiologies of anagen affluvium.
While loss of hair is more frequently seen as the toxic effect 
of chemicals on hair, overstimulation of the anagen phase can result 
in excess hair (hypertrichosis). Acquired, toxic hypertrichosis, may 
be seen with certain drugs (minoxidil and anabolic steroids), but 
has also been associated with various cancers and eating disorders. 
Hexachlorobenzene has also been reported to cause hypertrichosis, 
although this is usually seen in hexachlorophene-induced porphyria 
cutanea tarda (see “Endogenous Photosensitivities” section, above), 
where hypertrichosis is one of the manifestations.
SKIN CANCER
Both ultraviolet radiation and chemical exposures can result in skin 
cancers. Shortly after the discovery of radioactive elements at the 
turn of the 20th century, it was observed that x-rays could cause 
severe burns, squamous cell carcinomas, and basal cell carcinomas 
of the skin. In addition, the linkage between chemical exposure and 
scrotal carcinoma was long ago recognized among chimney sweeps 
(see Potter, 1963 that includes Pott’s original 1775 treatise).
UV-Induced Skin Cancer
Most skin cancers in the United States are UV-induced, and may 
be aggravated by genetic variations in the melanocortin receptor, 
which are associated with an increased skin cancer risk that is 
independent of hair type or skin color (Lynde and Sapra, 2010). 
The most common are the non-melanoma skin cancers, and less 
commonly malignant melanoma. In data summarized by the Skin 
Cancer Foundation (Skin Cancer Foundation: Skin Cancer Facts 
& Statistics, 2017), there were over 5.4 million cases of non-mela-
noma skin cancer in approximately 3.3 million people in the United 
States in 2012, the last year in which data was available. It is esti-
mated that one in five Americans will develop skin cancer in the 
course of a lifetime and that 40% to 50% of Americans who live to 
age 65 will develop at least one basal or squamous cell carcinoma.
Basal cell carcinoma (BCC) is the most common form of 
skin cancer with more than 4 million cases diagnosed yearly in the 
United States. Squamous cell carcinoma is the second most com-
mon form with more than 1 million cases per year in the United 
States. Organ transplant patients, especially those undergoing renal 
transplantation, are approximately 100 times more likely than the 
general public to develop squamous cell carcinoma. Actinic kera-
tosis is the most common pre-cancer, affecting more than 58 mil-
lion Americans (Skin Cancer Foundation: Skin Cancer Facts & 
Statistics, 2017).
Melanoma is the most lethal form of not only skin cancer, but 
most other malignancies. One person dies of melanoma every hour. 
An estimated 87,110 new cases of invasive melanoma will be diag-
nosed in the United States and 9730 people will die of melanoma 
in 2017. Thus, although melanoma accounts for less than 1% of 
skin cancers, it causes most skin cancer-related deaths (Skin Cancer 
Foundation: Skin Cancer Facts & Statistics, 2017). The vast major-
ity of melanoma is caused by ultraviolet radiation—both natural and 
artificial (tanning salons). In a UK study, 86% of melanomas could 
be attributed to UV exposure (Parkin et al., 2011). The estimated 
5-year survival rate for patients whose melanoma is detected early 
is about 98% in the United States. The survival rate falls to 62% 
when the disease reaches the lymph nodes, and 18% when the dis-
ease metastasizes to distant organs (Skin Cancer Foundation: Skin 
Cancer Facts & Statistics, 2017). The risk for melanoma appears 
to double in individuals who have had more than five lifetime sun-
burns (Pfahlberg et al., 2001).
Regular daily use of an SPF 15 or higher sunscreen reduces 
the risk of developing squamous cell carcinoma by about 40% 
(Green et al., 1999) and the risk of developing melanoma by 50% 
(Green et al., 2011). The annual cost of treating skin cancers in the 
United States is estimated at $8.1 billion: about $4.8 billion for 
non-melanoma skin cancers and $3.3 billion for melanoma (Guy 
et al., 2014). In the EU, there have been intense efforts to further 
elucidate, prevent, and recognize that skin cancer is an occupational 
disease among outdoor workers (John et al., 2016).
UVB induces pyrimidine dimers and 8-oxoguanine modifica-
tions, thereby eliciting mutations in critical genes (Melnikova and 
Ananthaswamy, 2005; Nishigori, 2006). The p53 tumor suppressor

Chemically Induced Skin Cancers
As previously mentioned, a landmark epidemiological investiga-
tion by Percivall Pott in 1775 connected soot with scrotal squa-
mous cell cancer, which was prevalent among chimney sweeps in 
England. Since that time, substances rich in polycyclic aromatic 
hydrocarbons (coal tar, creosote, etc.) have become recognized as 
skin carcinogens both in humans and animals (Voelter-Mahlknecht 
et al., 2007). With their variable content of polycyclic aromatic 
hydrocarbons and nitrosamines, mineral oils have a long history of 
causing skin cancer in the occupational arena among, for example, 
metal workers (Tolbert, 1997). Polycyclic aromatic compounds 
alone are relatively inert chemically, but they can accumulate in 
cellular membranes and thus perturb function if not removed. They 
are hydroxylated by a number of cytochrome P450 isoenzymes, 
primarily 1A1 and 1B1 in epidermal cells, and then conjugated 
for disposal from the body. Oxidative biotransformation, how-
ever, produces electrophilic epoxides that can form DNA adducts. 
Phenols, produced by rearrangements of the epoxide, can be oxi-
dized further to electrophilic quinones that generate ROS leading to 
DNA adducts, oxidized bases, and abasic sites (Park et al., 2006). 
Occupations at risk of skin cancer from exposure to these com-
pounds (e.g., roofing) often involve considerable sun exposure as 
well. As a major regulator of induction of CYP1A1, and to a lesser 
extent CYP1B1, the aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR) plays a criti-
cal role in the response of epidermal cells to polycyclic aromatic 
hydrocarbons (Swanson, 2004). While the role of AhR polymor-
phisms in the development of skin cancers has been well demon-
strated in murine models (Shimizu et al., 2000; De Souza et al., 
2009), screening efforts have not confirmed that this observation is 
relevant to humans.
Skin Tumor Promotion
As with all cancers, the development of skin cancers can be sim-
plistically viewed as a three-step model: initiation, promotion, and 
progression. Initiation is a rapid and irreversible event following 
limited exposure, and the cellular changes persist until cell death. 
Promotion is reversible and requires frequent exposure to the pro-
moter to enhance survival of the transformed cell. Progression 
follows sufficient exposure to the promoter and is irreversible, 
leading to chromosomal rearrangements during mitosis. It is this 
final trigger to genetic instability that subsequently leads to a cell’s 
malignant potential. In this model, the skin is treated once with 
a low dose of an initiator, such as a polycyclic aromatic hydro-
carbon. However, tumors do not develop unless there is subse-
quent treatment with a promoter, which must be applied numerous 
times and at frequent intervals (e.g., twice per week for 3 months). 
Application of the promoter need not start immediately after ini-
tiation, but if it is not continued long enough, or if it is applied 
before or without the initiator, tumors do not develop. Analysis of 
the results with this three-stage model can be extrapolated to more 
complicated (and likely realistic), multi-hit, multi-stage models 
(Owens et al., 1995)
A number of natural products are tumor promoters, many of 
which alter phosphorylation pathways. The best studied example, 
and one of the most potent, is the active ingredient of croton oil, 
12-O-tetradecanoylphorbol-13-acetate, which transiently stimulates 
then chronically depletes protein kinase C in epidermis (Fournier 
and Murray, 1987). A number of other phorbol esters have similar 
promoting effects.
Another groups of promoting agents, for example, okadaic 
acid, act as phosphatase inhibitors. Calcium channel modulators 
and free radical generators can also act as skin cancer promoters. 
Sensitivity to tumor promotion is an important factor in the relative 
sensitivity to skin carcinogenesis among different animal species 
(Poland et al., 1982). Much effort is currently focused on the sig-
nal transduction pathway(s) that is (are) perturbed by the treatment 
with these chemicals. The findings that chromate (a human lung 
carcinogen) in drinking water enhances tumor yield in mouse skin 
initiated by ultraviolet light suggest that generation of ROS can be 
an effective skin cancer promoter, and raises concerns about drink-
ing water standards for hexavalent chromium in the United States 
(Davidson et al., 2004).
Arsenic
Arsenic is an abundant element near the earth’s crust that is encoun-
tered routinely in small doses in the air, water, and food. It has long 
been known to affect human health (Hughes et al., 2011). In earlier 
times, high exposures occurred from medication (such as Fowler’s 
solution, potassium arsenite, for asthma) and from pesticides 
(sodium arsenite and lead arsenite), all of which have been phased 
out in the United States. Around the world, substantial exposures 
can occur as a result of dust from mining operations, smelting 
fumes, or from leachate in waterways. Glass manufacturing and 
food dried using coal with high arsenic content can also contribute 
to exposures. The environmental burden is increased by disposal of 
large volumes of wood preserved with chromated copper arsenic, 
among other sources. Well water derived from geological forma-
tions with high arsenic content is now recognized as a major health 
concern, highlighted by populations in the tens of millions at risk 
in Bangladesh and India (Tapio and Grosche, 2006). However, even 
in the United States, arsenic in drinking water remains a concern 
(Mayer and Goldman, 2016).
Studies in Southwestern Taiwan have shown an association 
between high arsenic exposure and altered skin pigmentation, 
hyperkeratosis of the palms and soles, impaired circulation reflect-
ing endothelial cell damage (“black foot” disease), and carcinoma 
(skin, lung, bladder, liver, and other organs). More recent concen-
tration dependent studies in Bangladesh show hyperpigmentation 
and keratoses occurring with drinking water concentrations close 
to 50 ppb (Argos et al., 2011), which was the former U.S. drinking 
water standard. In 2006, the United States reduced its standard for 
drinking water to 10 ppb. Furthermore, there appears to be syner-
gism between the toxic effects of arsenic and smoking (lung and 
bladder), sun exposure (skin), and fertilizer use (liver) (Melkonian 
et al., 2011).
Due to the observation that even low exposures to arsenic 
can have adverse health effects, much effort has been devoted to 
understanding its mechanism of action. Arsenic is a very weak 
mutagen in bacterial and mammalian cell lines, but it does induce 
DNA deletions and chromosome aberrations. It appears to act as a 
co-mutagen by interfering with DNA repair (Rossman et al., 2004).
