27.1  Introduction
Chemical carcinogenesis in rodents and humans is a multi-
factorial and multistep process that entails distinctly different 
mechanisms broadly involving DNA-reactive and epigenetic 
effects. The design of the standard rodent carcinogenicity bioas-
say (RCB) generally agreed upon by global regulatory authori-
ties usually does not address this understanding. Moreover, 
there are some aspects of bioassays which remain unresolved 
or are still under investigation. Also, related assays are of sub-
stantial importance in elucidating mechanisms of action and 
identifying hazard. These selected topics are discussed in this 
chapter. It is concluded that appropriate mechanistic data can 
in many cases replace carcinogenicity bioassays.
27.2  Introduction to RCBs
By the mid-20th century, compelling evidence had demon-
strated that contact with chemicals in the workplace could 
cause human cancer [1], and that administration of vari-
ous chemicals to animals produced specific tumors [2]. In 
light of these findings, the need for carcinogenicity test-
ing of chemicals, which might represent a cancer hazard 
to humans, was recognized. Accordingly, industry and 
government laboratories, notably the US Food and Drug 
Administration (FDA), began working on methods to iden-
tify the carcinogenic potential of chemicals [3]. Another 
activity along these lines was the establishment of the 
chemical carcinogen screening program at the US National 
Cancer Institute (NCI) in the mid-1960s [4]. In 1980, 
this program was transferred to the National Toxicology 
Program (NTP) at the National Institute of Environmental 
Health Sciences (NIEHS). In the ensuing years, in paral-
lel with contributions from industry, the standard RCB (rat, 
mouse) was developed. Details of the design of the 2-year 
RCB are available in many sources [5, 6], including NTP 
Reports. To date, several hundreds of chemicals have been 
screened [7]. The International Agency for Research on 
Cancer (IARC), using external experts, has systematically 
evaluated the carcinogenicity data from publicly available 
reports on over 1,000 chemicals, mixtures or processes 
(https://monographs.iarc.fr/agents-classified-by-the-iarc/). 
Approximately 120 of these have been judged to represent 
human cancer hazards [8]. The contribution of these to 
human cancer, however, except for lifestyle factors, such as 
cigarette smoking [9, 10], has not been quantified.
This chapter focuses on critical aspects of the RCB and 
related assays which warrant greater utilization, especially 
methodologies which address differences between effects in 
rodent studies and impacts from human intakes. The future of 
the carcinogenicity bioassay is discussed.

Chemicals elicit tumor development through a variety of 
mechanisms, ultimately affecting expression of genes critical 
for carcinogenesis, i.e., oncogenes and tumor suppressor genes

which are classified as carcinogenic to humans (Group 1) 
conducted by IARC resulted in the delineation of ten key char-

According to IARC, a carcinogenic chemical demonstrates one 
or more of the following properties: (1) act as an electrophile 
either directly or after metabolic activation; (2) be genotoxic; 
(3) alter DNA repair or cause genomic instability; (4) induce 
epigenetic alterations; (5) induce oxidative stress; (6) induce 
chronic inflammation; (7) be immunosuppressive; (8) modu-
late receptor-mediated effects; (9) cause immortalization; 
and (10) alter cell proliferation, cell death or nutrient supply 
[16]. These characteristics are now implemented by IARC as 
a framework for organizing evidence from mechanistic studies 
to conduct a more efficient cancer hazard classification. These 
key characteristics of carcinogens delineate mechanisms by 
which carcinogens induce acquisition of cancer traits by the 
normal cell [17].
Based on extensive data, carcinogens have been broadly 
categorized as the DNA-reactive or genotoxic type or the 
epigenetic or non-genotoxic type [18, 19]. These catego-
ries encompass the key characteristics recognized by IARC. 
Chapter 26 in this textbook discusses aspects of genotoxicity 
and epigenetic toxicity in detail.
27.3.1.1  DNA-Reactive Carcinogens
DNA-reactive carcinogens produce chemical-specific covalent 
adduct(s) in nuclear DNA in the target tissue(s) of carcinoge-
nicity [20]. This is no doubt the principal effect underlying 
their carcinogenicity. Measurement of adducts can be made 
with a variety of sensitive techniques [21–26], enabling detec-
tion of adduct levels as low as 1 adduct in 108 to 1011 nucleo-
tides, which represents about one or less adduct per cell, since 
nuclear human genome comprises approximately 3.1 × 109 
nucleotide pairs. The structures of DNA-reactive carcinogens 
have been extensively elucidated [27, 28]. The production of 
adducts by these chemicals is the consequence of the forma-
tion of electrophiles which react with nucleophilic sites in 
DNA. A consequence of this reactivity is production of breaks 
and mutations in nuclear DNA following repair or replicative 
DNA synthesis. For this reason, DNA-reactive carcinogens 
generally produce positive results in assays that measure geno-
toxicity or mutagenicity (see Section 4) [20].
The structures of five known carcinogenic electrophiles are 
depicted in Figure 27.2. Carbonium and nitrinium ions are the 
most common types of electrophiles formed by DNA-reactive 
carcinogens. The chemical structures of formed DNA adducts 
have been demonstrated for many DNA-reactive carcinogens 
[29–32]. Most DNA-reactive carcinogens give rise to more

DNA-reactive carcinogens are synthetic (e.g., the industrial 
chemical benzidine), naturally occurring (e.g., mycotoxins 
aflatoxins), or produced endogenously (e.g., formaldehyde) 
(Table 27.1). Most of the widely studied experimental organic 
carcinogens, such as polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons 
(PAHs), aromatic amines and nitrosamines, are DNA reactive 
after metabolic activation. Some are carcinogenic without bio-
transformation, that is, are activation independent (e.g., alkyl-
ating agents), but most require bioactivation to one of the five 
electrophilic structures shown in Figure 27.2 and hence are 
activation dependent [35].
Adducts produced in DNA by DNA-reactive carcinogens 
are subject to repair [36], but if cell replication occurs prior to 
repair, mutations and DNA breaks can be produced [27, 37]. In 
addition, some DNA adducts are resistant to nucleotide exci-
sion repair [38]. Thus, DNA-reactive carcinogens induce neo-
plasia largely as a consequence of their reaction with nuclear 
DNA, resulting in neoplastic conversion of the affected cells in 
the first sequence of carcinogenesis (initiation) (Figure 27.1).
DNA-reactive carcinogens also can react with other molec-
ular components of the cell, as documented by Miller and 
Miller [39] in their pioneering studies of protein binding of 
polycyclic aminoazo carcinogens. Binding to macromolecules 
other than DNA can exert cellular and tissue effects that 
facilitate the process of carcinogenesis either at initiation or 
promotion. Among the epigenetic effects produced by DNA-
reactive carcinogens that contribute to their carcinogenicity 
are enhanced cell proliferation and tumor promotion. Cell pro-
liferation is well established to affect the susceptibility of cells 
to both the mutagenic [40] and carcinogenic [41, 42] effects of 
chemicals. Cell proliferation can enhance neoplastic conver-
sion by creating the possibility for errors in the newly syn-
thesized strand formed during DNA replication. Enhancement 
of cell proliferation facilitates tumor promotion by disrupting 
cell–cell homeostasis allowing proliferation of preneoplastic 
or neoplastic cells [43]. Some of the differences in the poten-
cies of DNA-reactive carcinogens are likely to be due to their 
epigenetic effects [44], in addition to genotoxicity [45].
As a consequence of their mechanism of action, many 
DNA-reactive carcinogens exert potent carcinogenic effects in 
experimental systems, including induction of types of malig-
nant tumors not seen in control animals and tumors in several 
tissues and in more than one species, often with short latencies.
DNA-reactive carcinogens, due to their mechanism of 
action, have been assumed not to have a cancer threshold. 
However, current research using rigorous approaches reveals 
no-observed-effect-levels (NOELs) for DNA-reactive carcin-
ogens (see Section 11.1). Nevertheless, the features of DNA-
reactive carcinogens (Table 27.1) are indicative of a high 
degree of hazard to potentially exposed humans (see Section 
11) and it is probably for this reason that the majority of car-
cinogens recognized by IARC as having caused human cancer 
[46] is of the DNA-reactive type.
DNA-reactive carcinogens can be detected in short-term 
assays and limited carcinogenicity bioassays (LCBs) (see 
Sections 4 and 6).

27.3.1.2  Epigenetic Carcinogens
The epigenetic (non-genotoxic) type of carcinogen [18, 19] 
elicits neoplasia through a variety of molecular or cellular 
effects in the target tissue(s), which do not involve direct cova-
lent reaction with nuclear DNA [44, 47, 48]. The structures of 
epigenetic carcinogens and their metabolites do not contain 
moieties susceptible to the formation of a likely electrophilic 
reactant (Figure 27.3), other than generation of reactive oxygen 
species (ROS). For this reason, they are generally inactive in 
genotoxicity assays.
Epigenetic carcinogens are synthetic (e.g., the medication 
phenobarbital), naturally occurring (e.g., the plant compo-
nent d-limonene), or can form endogenously (e.g., estrogen). 
Some well-established examples of epigenetic carcinogens 
are provided in Table 27.2. While the chemical structures of 
these carcinogens vary widely (Figure 27.3), some groups of 
related compounds have common features. For example, sev-
eral chlorinated polycyclic compounds, including pesticides 
chlordane and dichlorodiphenyltrichloroethane, polychlo-
rinated biphenyls and 2,3,7,8-tetrachlorodibenzo-p-dioxin 
(dioxin) can act through liver tumor promotion [49], facili-
tating liver tumor development in rodents from pre-existing 
preneoplastic or neoplastic cells which are the precursors of 
background (spontaneous) tumors [50]. This is believed to be a 
consequence of slow metabolism of these compounds leading 
to their accumulation in the liver. Another example is phen-
oxyacetic acid moieties present in fibrates, which are involved 
in binding to the peroxisome proliferator–activated receptor 
alpha (PPAR−) leading to enhanced liver cell proliferation and 
development of liver tumors [51, 52].
In the process of carcinogenesis, changes in the epigenome, 
including altered patterns of DNA methylation and post-trans-
lational histone modifications as well as deregulated expres-
sion of non-coding RNAs, contribute to aberrant activation 
of tumor-promoting oncogenes, while inhibiting the activity 
of tumor suppressor genes, thereby leading to chromosomal 
and genomic instability [53]. These molecular changes have 
been reported for many established epigenetic carcinogens, 
including furan, arsenic, phenobarbital and the antihistamine 
methapyrilene [44, 54–56]. The latter, however, also produces 
mitochondrial dysfunction [57] and has liver tumor initiat-
ing activity [58]. Moreover, many DNA-reactive carcinogens, 
e.g., aflatoxin B1, 1,3-butadiene, tamoxifen, can also produce 
epigenetic alterations in the target tissues in addition to direct 
DNA reactivity [44, 54].
Modifications of gene expression profiles by carcinogens 
are achieved through a variety of molecular mechanisms 
(see Chapter 26). One of the best-studied epigenetic events is 
genome-wide and gene-specific DNA methylation. The addi-
tion of a methyl group to the five position of cytosine nucleo-
tide in CpG sites, which are distributed throughout the genome,

A common effect of many epigenetic carcinogens is 
enhancement of cell proliferation in target tissues, which is 
a contributing or even the major component in their carcino-
genic activity [62–64]. For several epigenetic carcinogens, the 
enhancement of cell proliferation is a consequence of the bind-
ing of the carcinogen to cellular receptors [65]. Among these is 
the aryl hydrocarbon receptor, which binds PAHs and dioxin 
[66]. The role of receptor binding in carcinogenicity is com-
plex; it may well be essential, but not sufficient for the carcino-
genicity of some ligands. For example, the carcinogenicity of 
PPAR− agonists may involve a combination of receptor-medi-
ated increased cell proliferation and oxidative damage to DNA 
resulting from induced peroxisomal generation of hydrogen 
peroxide [67].
Another mechanism that characterizes many tumor promot-
ers is inhibition of gap junctional communication [68–70]. 
This interferes with the signaling that maintains cellular and 
tissue homeostasis, allowing escape of altered cell populations 
from tissue controls [43].
A feature of some epigenetic carcinogens is that they 
increase production of ROS leading to oxidative DNA dam-
age, as reported for the monomer acrylonitrile [71, 72]. 
Oxidative DNA damage, however, is only weakly mutagenic 
[73] and tumor initiating activity has not been demonstrated, 
whereas tumor promotion has been reported, as in the case of 
the organochlorine pesticide pentachlorophenol [74].
The characteristics of the experimental carcinogenicity 
of epigenetic agents (Table 27.2), in general, are quite dif-
ferent from those of DNA-reactive carcinogens (Table 27.1). 
Epigenetic carcinogens typically require high-level or sus-
tained administration to produce the cellular effect lead-
ing to an increase in tumors. Epigenetic carcinogens which 
bioaccumulate due to slow biotransformation, such as the 
above-mentioned lipophilic polyhalogenated compounds, can 
produce a sustained internal dose even with administration 
of short duration. Tumors produced by epigenetic agents are 
often of the type occurring spontaneously, which indicates 
that carcinogens of this type are affecting tumor development 
of cryptogenic preneoplastic or neoplastic cells. Tumors pro-
duced by epigenetic agents often occur only later in life, i.e., 
after 1 year. The dependence of the carcinogenicity of most 
epigenetic agents on protracted dosing is a consequence of the 
necessity for them to exert, for some prolonged duration, the 
primary molecular or cellular effect that underlines their abil-
ity to increase neoplasia. As a consequence of their mechanism 
of action, epigenetic carcinogens are less potent compared 
to DNA-reactive agents. They often affect only a single tis-
sue where their epigenetic effect is produced. Unlike DNA-
reactive carcinogens, structurally dissimilar epigenetic agents 
do not usually exhibit additive effects, probably because their 
mechanisms of action are different from one another.
Only a few rodent epigenetic carcinogens (mainly hormonal 
or immunosuppressive agents) and some high-level occupa-
tional exposures have been recognized by the IARC as human 
carcinogens. These induce neoplasia only under conditions 
where they overtly produce their epigenetic effect. Often this 
requires high-level sustained intakes which are unlikely to 
occur in humans with most environmental exposures, includ-
ing properly controlled workplace exposure.
Epigenetic carcinogens can be detected in short-term assays 
and LCBs designed for this purpose (see Sections 5 and 6).
27.4  Short-Term Assays for Genotoxicity 
Used to Predict Carcinogenic Hazard
The objective of genetic toxicology is to investigate in short-
term assays whether a substance induces damage to the DNA 
in order to identify a genotoxic carcinogenic potential. Short-
term assays are used as surrogates for carcinogenicity bio-
assays until data from the latter become available. Genetic 
toxicity studies identify a hazard, while RCBs are used to 
identify a potential cancer hazard for humans (see Section 11). 
Usually, two in vitro assays and one in vivo assay are required 
by regulations [28, 75, 76]. These typically include detection 
of mutagenicity in the bacterial assay and clastogenicity/aneu-
genicity in the micronucleus or chromosome aberration assays 
[75–77]. New assays which offer specific advantages have 
been developed.
27.4.1  Genotoxic Endpoints
Genotoxic endpoints include DNA alterations, mutagenicity, 
clastogenicity and genome alterations (numerical alterations 
including aneugenicity). This differentiation is of importance 
as currently the quantitative cancer risk for mutagenic carcino-
gens often assumes a linear dose-response relationship. The 
cancer risk for clastogenic and aneugenic carcinogens, how-
ever, has a non-linear dose-response relationship, as this mode 
of action (MoA) is considered to be a threshold-related event.
DNA alterations: Structural changes in DNA, including 
DNA adducts, crosslinks or DNA strand breaks.
Mutagenicity: A detectable permanent change of the genetic 
material within a single gene or its regulating sequences. The 
changes can be point mutations, insertions or deletions. Such 
changes are typically detected in the bacterial reverse muta-
tion assay, in vitro mammalian gene mutation assay and in in 
vivo transgenic rodent (TGR) mutation assays.
Clastogenicity: A structural breakage of chromatids and/or 
chromosomes. These changes can be detected by the chromo-
somal aberration assay, the in vitro and in vivo micronucleus 
assay and by the Comet assay.
Genome alterations (numerical aberrations) are subdivided 
as either

27.4.2  In Silico Analysis (or (Q)SAR) 
Prediction of Mutagenicity
Continuous effort is being made to develop in silico predic-
tion tools for genotoxicity endpoints. Only the in silico tools 
for (quantitative) structure-activity relationship assessment that 
predict the Ames test result based on the chemical structure 
have achieved regulatory acceptance. In general, two differ-
ent systems are employed, one knowledge-based system such 
as Derek Nexus or Toxtree, and a second system, a statistical 
based system, such as Leadscope, CASE Ultra or Sarah Nexus. 
The requirements for acceptance of the systems are described 
in the International Council for Harmonisation of Technical 
Requirements for Pharmaceuticals for Human Use (ICH) M7 
guideline for pharmaceuticals. The objective is to reach high 
sensitivity and high negative predictivity in order to reach 
acceptance for not conducting a confirmatory Ames test in case 
of negative prediction, i.e., the number of false negatives should 
be as low as possible. The sensitivity and negative prediction 
may vary depending on the coverage of the chemical space in 
the knowledge bases. High sensitivity of 66–82% and negative 
predictivity of 91–95% were reported for impurities in pharma-
ceuticals using the commercially available systems [78]. The 
sensitivity and negative prediction for Toxtree, a freely avail-
able prediction software, showed a high sensitivity of 62% and 
negative predictivity of 92% for food contact materials [79].
27.4.3  Genotoxicity Investigations and Some 
General Rules for their Conduct
A wide variety of genotoxicity assays have been developed. 
Of these, only a few are widely used in regulatory acceptable 
studies for chemicals and pharmaceuticals, as shown in Tables 
27.3 and 27.4.
In all in vitro genotoxicity assays, a metabolic activation 
system needs to be added. This usually consists of an induced 
rat liver microsomal fraction and co-factor, called an S9 mix. 
Due to its cytotoxicity, this S9 mix can be used only in short-
term incubation experiments in the range of 2–6 hours. In 
general, continuous dosing of test substance (TS) for about 
24 hours is expected when the short-term incubation experi-
ments are negative in the in vitro mammalian genotoxicity 
tests. The maximum recommended concentrations in these 
tests are 1 mM or 0.5 mg/mL, whichever is lower for pharma-
ceuticals or 10 mM, 2 mg/mL or 2 ōL/mL, whatever is lower 
for chemicals. If the maximum recommended concentration 
cannot be reached, testing up to insoluble or up to cytotoxic 
concentrations is required.
Furthermore, in all genotoxicity studies, negative vehicle 
control and positive control compounds are included to pro-
vide evidence for the validity of each study. An exception is 
the in vivo micronucleus test, where positive controls can be 
omitted due to animal welfare consideration, when the labora-
tory has demonstrated proficiency in this test system.

27.4.3.1  Bacterial Reverse Mutation (Ames) Screen
Various forms of screens are used, such as the Ames assay 
with a restricted number of strains, typically the Salmonella 
typhimurium strains TA 98 and TA 100 with and without met-
abolic activation. Another frequently used assay is the 6-well 
Ames test where all regulatory relevant strains and condi-
tions are used as described in the Organisation for Economic 
Co-operation and Development (OECD) guideline No. 471, 
except for the use of 1/5 of all reagent volumes and 6-well 
plates [80]. There is a very good correlation between the 
6-well Ames and the standard Ames test among about 1,000 
substances tested with a false-negative rate of 0.1%.
27.4.3.2  Bacterial Reverse Mutation Assay 
(Ames Test) – OECD 471 Guideline
The objective of this assay is to detect mutagenic potential 
of TSs. S. typhimurium and/or Escherichia coli WP2 uvrA 
strains are used that have mutations in the histidine (His) or 
tryptophane (Trp) amino acids synthesis, respectively. These 
are so called, His/Trp-auxotroph, i.e., reverse mutations in 
genes coding for histidine or tryptophane synthesis are needed 
to enable outgrowth into colonies. S. typhimurium strains TA 
1535, TA 100, TA 102 or E. coli WP2 are sensitive to detect 
point mutations and S. typhimurium strains TA 1537 and TA 
98 are sensitive to detect frameshift mutations (Table 27.5).
All strains have defects in DNA repair (mutation in uvrB 
gene) to make them even more sensitive to mutagens. In addi-
tion, the strains have defects in the cell wall (rfa mutation) 
in order to allow higher molecular weight substances to reach 
the DNA in the bacteria. The principle of the Ames assay is 
that mutant bacteria cannot grow on His/Trp-deficient agar. 
If they are treated with a nonmutagenic substance, there is 
no colony growth detectable. If they are treated with a muta-
genic substance, and a reverse mutation occurs in the histidine 
(Salmonella strains) or tryptophane (E. coli strain) gene, lead-
ing to a functional protein. Colonies grow usually within 2 
or 3 days and can be counted as the number of revertants per 
plate. The revertant ratio of treated cultures versus negative 
control provides a measure whether a substance is mutagenic. 
In general, ratios of more than 2-fold for TA 98, TA 100, TA 
102 or E. coli or more than 3-fold for TA 1535 and TA 1537 
constitute a positive result. The maximum recommended con-
centration is 5,000 ōg/plate or up to insoluble or bacteriotoxic 
concentrations.
In general, a positive Ames result is indicative of a high 
probability that the RCB will also be positive for tumor forma-
tion. Together with the linear extrapolation of the cancer risk 
for mutagenic carcinogens, a positive Ames test triggers the 
control of the TS at very low levels, i.e., mutagenic impurities 
are required not to exceed 1.5 ōg/day for lifetime exposure 
(ICH M7). Well-conducted and scientifically justified appro-
priate in vivo follow-up studies allow assessment of the bio-
logical relevance and can outweigh the positive Ames result.
A collection of more than 500 chemicals that are classified 
as rodent carcinogens displayed for the Ames test a sensitiv-
ity of approximately 60% for the prediction of carcinogenicity 
and a specificity of about 74% or 75% based on 176 noncar-
cinogens [85, 86].

27.4.3.4  In Vitro Micronucleus Assay – 
OECD 487 Guideline
The objective of this assay is to detect a clastogenic or aneu-
genic potential of TSs. A variety of cell lines or primary cul-
tures including human peripheral blood lymphocytes can be 
used. The requirement is that cells should have undergone one 
mitosis in the presence of the TS. This is achieved by using 
exponentially growing cells lines or lymphocytes stimulated 
by a mitogen such as phytohemagglutin for 44–48 hours. 
Typically, the evidence for one mitosis is based on estab-
lishing the cytokinesis-blocked proliferation method, i.e., an 
actin polymerization inhibitor cytochalasin B is added prior 
to mitosis to inhibit cytokinesis. Considering only binucle-
ated cells for evaluation of micronuclei ensures that cells have 
undergone exactly one mitosis. At least 2,000 binucleated cells 
per culture are evaluated for the presence of micronuclei, the 
endpoint of this assay.
Micronuclei consist of DNA fragments or whole chromo-
somes which are not allocated to the two spindle poles during 
cell division but remain in the equatorial plane and are sur-
rounded by a nuclear membrane at the end of the cell cycle. 
In case of a positive outcome in the assay, micronuclei can 
be analyzed by fluorescent in situ hybridization (FISH) using 
centromeric probes or by the use of antikinetochore antibody 
staining (CREST staining) to differentiate between clasto-
genic substances (centromere negative, C′ ) and aneugenic 
substances (centromere positive, C+) (Figure 27.6).
The numbers of carcinogens and noncarcinogens investi-
gated with the in vitro micronucleus assay are as yet too small 
to define robust sensitivity and specificity for the prediction of 
carcinogenicity. A collection of about 26 noncarcinogens dis-
played a specificity of about 31% for the in vitro micronucleus 
assay [85].

27.4.3.6  HPRT Gene Mutation Assay – 
OECD 476 Guideline
The objective of this assay is to detect the mutagenic potential 
of TSs. Various cell lines with one functional X-chromosome, 
such as CHO, CHL and V79 lines of male Chinese hamster 
cells or human TK 6 cells, can be used as the hypoxanthine-
guanine phosphoribosyl transferase (HPRT) gene is located on 
the X-chromosome [87]. The principle of the assay is similar 
to that of the Mouse Lymphoma assay. Cells deficient in the 
HPRT gene are resistant to the cytotoxic effects of the purine 
analog 6-thioguanine (TG). A forward mutation at the HPRT 
locus, leading to deficiency, allows the cells to grow and form 
colonies in the presence of TG. After cells are treated with TS, 
an interval of 7–9 days is allowed for phenotypic expression of 
mutant colonies before they are quantified. This assay is more 
frequently used in the chemical industry and not listed in the 
genetic toxicity standard battery for pharmaceuticals.
27.4.3.7  In Vivo Micronucleus Assay – 
OECD 474 Guideline
The objective of the micronucleus assay is to detect the clas-
togenic or aneugenic potential of TS in vivo. The principle of 
this assay is similar to that of the in vitro micronucleus assay 
(Figure 27.7). A DNA fragment or whole chromosome, not 
allocated to the spindle poles during cell division, remains in 
the equatorial plane and is surrounded by a nuclear membrane 
at the end of the cell cycle. The detection of such micronuclei 
in the cytoplasm is facilitated in this assay, as bone marrow 
erythroblasts extrude their main nucleus during maturation to 
an immature erythrocyte (also named polychromatic erythro-
cyte [PCE] or reticulocyte [RET]). For detection of micronu-
clei in the bone marrow assay, a smear from femur or tibia 
bone marrow is evaluated for the presence of micronuclei in 
4,000 PCEs per animal by light microscopy. In the periph-
eral blood micronucleus assay, at least 20,000 RETs per ani-
mal are identified by labeling with CD71 (surface marker for 
immature RETs) and the number of micronuclei among them 
is quantified.
The assay, normally performed in mice or rats, is especially 
relevant for assessing genotoxicity as in vivo metabolism, 
pharmacokinetics and DNA-repair processes are active and 
contribute to the response. An in vivo assay is well suited for 
the verification of genotoxicity detected in an in vitro system.

than 14 days or up to 1,000 mg/kg for administration periods 
of 14 and more days. In case of toxicity or limited solubil-
ity, testing up to the maximum tolerated dose (MTD) or the 
maximum feasible dose is indicated. After administration of 
a single dose only, samples have to be collected at two time 
points, after two administrations one sampling time point is 
sufficient. Bone marrow is collected at 18–24 hours after the

27.4.3.8  In Vivo Comet Assay – OECD  
489 Guideline
The objective of the Comet assay is the detection of DNA 
single- and double-strand breaks, DNA adducts at alkaline 
labile sites in the DNA under in vivo conditions. Any cell type 
from rat, mouse or any other species can be investigated. The 
liver is the default tissue, and the gastrointestinal tract can be 
assessed as second organ after oral administration as site of 
contact. Any other tissue where high exposures is expected 
might be assessed additionally. The study design is similar to 
the in vivo micronucleus assay except that organ samples are 
taken 2–6 hours post-administration, depending on the time 
the maximum plasma concentration is reached. Single-cell 
suspensions are isolated from the organ of interest and cells 
are embedded in low temperature melting agarose and applied 
onto a slide. After lysis of the cells and unwinding of the DNA 
into single strands, the DNA fragments are migrated by elec-
trophoresis (pH ∈ 13). The DNA is stained with a specific fluo-
rescent dye resulting in a comet-like figure when observed by 
microscopy, consisting of the head region, representing the 
undamaged nuclear DNA and tail region, consisting of DNA 
fragments resulting from damaged DNA (Figure 27.8).
One hundred and fifty cells are analyzed per organ and 
animal to obtain the incidence of DNA damage measured as 
% Tail Intensity (% TI). Hedgehogs (nuclei without clearly 
defined “head” also named clouds or ghost cells) as indica-
tor for cytotoxicity should be recorded but excluded from the 
evaluation. Furthermore, the histopathological analysis of the 
investigated organs in combination with biomarker analysis 
for apoptosis or necrosis (e.g., cleaved caspase 3, TUNEL) may 
be helpful to determine a possible apoptotic mechanism of the 
TS to assess the biological relevance of positive Comet signals.
The Comet assay was shown to be able to detect genotox-
icity in a similar sensitivity as the transgenic mutation assay 
with a sensitivity of 92% [85, 88]. In the absence of a sufficient 
number of Comet tests for carcinogens/noncarcinogens, no 
sensitivity and specificity data are yet available for carcinoge-
nicity prediction.
27.4.3.9  In Vivo Pig-A Assay –OECD 470 Guideline
The objective of the Pig-A assay is to detect the mutagenic 
potential of a TS in vivo. Peripheral blood erythrocytes of mice 
or rats are investigated but it is acknowledged that these cells 
originate from the bone marrow, where mutations are fixed 
during mitosis of erythroblasts. Functional mutations in the 
phosphatidylinositol glycan class A (Pig-a) gene, located on 
the X-chromosome, result in the loss of the glycosylphos-
phatidylinositol (GPI) anchor from the surface of red blood 
cells (RBC). This loss prevents the binding of surface proteins 
such as CD59 that GPI tethers onto the surface. The CD59 
surface marker can be used as a reliable phenotypic marker 
of Pig-a mutation. The study design is similar to the in vivo 
micronucleus assay, but animals are treated for a minimum of 
28 days. Therefore, the Pig-A assay can be easily integrated 
into 4-week toxicity studies.
Flow cytometry with peripheral blood samples is used to 
detect the presence of CD59 negative cells together with CD71 
antibodies that allows a differentiation between RETs (CD71+) 
and mature RBC (CD71′ ). The Pig-a mutant frequency is mea-
sured in RET and RBC, with a signal expected in RET at much 
earlier time points. Due to lack of a sufficient number of Pig-A 
assay for carcinogens/noncarcinogens, no sensitivity and spec-
ificity data are yet available for carcinogenicity prediction.
27.4.3.10  TGR Mutation Bioassay (Muta™Mouse/
BigBlue® Mouse) – OECD 488 Guideline
The objective of the transgenic mutation bioassay (TGR) is to 
detect the mutagenic potential of TS in vivo. The study design 
is similar to the in vivo micronucleus assay with the differ-
ence that animals are treated for a minimum of 28 days and 
additional 3 days to allow for fixation of mutations (Figure 
27.9). The liver is the default target organ, but any other tis-
sue of interest can be assessed, including gastrointestinal tract, 
kidneys, lungs and urinary bladder. The assay is based on 
TGR strains containing multiple copies of plasmid or phage 
shuttle vectors integrated chromosomally. These shuttle vec-

27.4.4  Emerging Assays
27.4.4.1  In Vitro models
A large number of environmental chemicals that lack any data 
on carcinogenicity constitute the need for high-throughput 
assays [89, 90]. Implementation of emerging “-omics” tech-
nologies (toxicogenomics, epigenomics, transcriptomics, pro-
teomics and metabolomics) can address this deficiency. Such 
methods provide high-yield mechanistic information about a 
potential MoA of a chemical. These approaches can identify 
new biomarkers and events that contribute to carcinogenicity 
and differentiate between genotoxic and epigenetic carcinogens 
[91–93]. However, the complexity of high content data presents 
a challenge for data analyses and obtaining meaningful results.
Gene reporter assays measuring transcription of DNA dam-
age inducible markers, e.g., the GADD45a-GFP GreenScreen 
HC assay and GADD45a-Gluc Bluescreen HC assay, can be 
utilized as screening tools for indication of genotoxic hazard 
and the prioritization of chemicals for further testing [94–96]. 
Some novel tools, such as Multiplexed Flow Cytometric Assay 
and ToxTracker Assay, simultaneously investigate several end-
points indicative of DNA damage and cytotoxicity, thereby 
providing some mechanistic insight into potential genotox-
icity of chemicals [97, 98]. Assays currently accepted for 
the purpose of hazard assessment can be modified for high-
throughput screening and high content analysis. Examples of 
such assays include forward mutation assay in S. typhimurium 
based on fluorouracil resistance, Comet Chip, in vitro ćH2AX 
and micronucleus assays [99–103].
One of the major shortcomings of many in vitro assays is the 
utilization of organisms or cell lines which lack bioactivation 
capabilities. As such, these assays conventionally utilize S9 
fraction prepared from livers of rats dosed with enzyme-induc-
ing chemicals as an exogenous source for metabolic activation 
[104, 105]. Understanding of the importance of bioactivation 
in genotoxicity exhibited by chemicals increased interest in 
metabolically competent cell lines with bioactivation capacities 
similar to humans. Many studies describe the successful use of 
human hepatoma cell lines, HepaRG and HepG2, for assess-
ment of various genotoxic and epigenetic endpoints [106–109].
A more advanced and physiologically relevant approach 
is the use of organotypic models, 3D spheroids, such as 3D 
HepaRG liver or 3D reconstructed, primary human hepatocyte 
and skin models [107, 110, 111]. These models better resemble 
intact organs and represent relevant exposure sites for many 
chemicals.

27.4.4.2  In Ovo models
Avian eggs represent an intermediate stage between in vitro 
and in vivo systems. They utilize fertilized avian eggs as a non-
animal model for evaluation of multiple critical endpoints in 
the livers of the developing metabolically competent embryo–
fetuses either as a screening assay or as a follow-up to in vitro 
assays. The most commonly used species are chicken and tur-
key. Similarities between the development and histopathologi-
cal structure of the liver in avian embryos and in mammals, 
including rodents and humans [112, 113], and the gene expres-
sion patterns in chickens resembling those of rodents [114], 
provide evidence that avian species can be utilized along with 
rodent models. In particular, fetal avian livers express major 
phase I and Phase II biotransformation enzymes with activities 
comparable to those in postnatal rodent livers and which are 
induced by xenobiotics [115–117]. Accordingly, egg models 
have the potential to detect a wider array of genotoxic com-
pounds that require bioactivation, than in vitro assays with S9 
supplementation, which does not carry out Phase II metabolic 
reactions. Moreover, unlike in vitro systems, avian organisms 
in ovo are capable of detoxication and elimination of chemi-
cals. Several in ovo assays are available for the assessment of 
chemical genotoxicity and carcinogenicity.
The In Ovo Carcinogenicity Assessing Assay (IOCA) has 
been validated internationally [118]. It uses fertilized turkey or 
chicken eggs to measure the formation of liver preneoplastic 
lesions [118–120]. This assay is very effective in monitoring the 
processes of cell proliferation, differentiation, apoptosis, DNA 
damage assessment and developmental effects [120, 121].
Further developments of the IOCA are the Chicken 
Egg Genotoxicity Assay (CEGA) and related Turkey Egg 
Genotoxicity Assay (TEGA), for measurement of DNA dam-
age. Two complementary assays the 32P-postlabeling assay [24, 
122], for chemical-specific DNA adducts, and the comet assay 
[123, 124], for DNA strand breaks produced by either direct 
or indirect mechanisms are employed in CEGA and TEGA. 
A wide variety of prototype genotoxic carcinogens and struc-
turally related non-genotoxic or noncarcinogenic agents have 
been tested in these models [121, 125–129]. Findings in both 
models were highly concordant with outcomes in vivo, con-
firming that the avian egg model is a reliable screening tool 
for the assessment of potential genotoxicity. Furthermore, the 
chicken egg model has been utilized to analyze other end-
points, including genomic alterations, teratogenic effects and 
phenotypic anchoring of the molecular events to histologic 
changes [112, 115]. In addition, an avian egg model, the Hen’s 
egg test (HET-MN), can be used to monitor micronuclei for-
mation in erythrocytes of chicken fetuses [130–133]. Thus, 
egg models also can potentially contribute to the elucidation 
of several molecular mechanisms of chemical carcinogen-
esis. Since CEGA and TEGA primarily detect DNA damage 
endpoints, in ovo assays can be considered an “indicator test” 
according to the OECD guidelines [134]. Nevertheless, the in 
ovo assays provide commonly used and accepted endpoints of 
genotoxicity and therefore can serve as follow-up assays for 
materials that are positive in in vitro mutagenicity assay (i.e., 
Ames, mouse lymphoma assay) and/or in vitro clastogenicity/
aneugenicity assays (i.e., in vitro micronucleus assay).
27.5  Short-Term Assays for Epigenetic Effects
Specific assays for epigenetic effects (Table 27.2) are applied 
selectively, depending on the properties of the chemical (i.e., 
structure, biologic/pharmacologic action and toxicity). These 
assays provide information on an epigenetic mechanism 
that could result in an increase in neoplasms in rodents with 
extended dosing. Many can be conducted in cultured cells, 
particularly hepatocytes, which provide intrinsic bioactiva-
tion. Even when applied in vivo, the experiments are of short 
duration, except for assays for tumor-promoting activity, the 
detection of which is described further in Section 6. Positive 
results indicate potential oncogenicity. Moreover, the potency 
relative to that of established carcinogens with a similar mech-
anism and organotropism provides a guide to risk assessment.
The most extensively applied assay is inhibition of cell–cell 
communication, which is a property of many tumor promoters 
[68, 69, 135].
On the molecular level, various technologies are available for 
screening for effects on gene expression and function, includ-
ing assays for DNA methylation status, miRNA expression, 
chromatin characterization and protein levels [136–140] (see 
Chapter 26). The most commonly used methods for assessment 
of DNA methylation status are based on bisulfite sequencing 
[141], and chromatin immunoprecipitation-based assays are 
often utilized for the assessment of post-translational histone 
modifications [142]. The increased expression of specific genes, 
such as hepatic acyl-CoA oxidase (increased by PPAR− liver 
carcinogens) or hepatic cytochrome P450s (increased by a vari-
ety of liver neoplasm promoters), is linked to epigenetic carci-
nogenesis, and thus these methods have utility for screening.
27.6  Limited Carcinogenicity Bioassays
Changes that are antecedent to tumors (Figure 27.1) can be 
observed in subchronic studies and at the early stages of the 
RCB. These include organ toxicity, hypertrophy, hyperpla-
sia and preneoplasia. They can provide information on the 
potential mechanism of carcinogenicity [143]. A particularly 
valuable endpoint to monitor is cell proliferation because an 
increase in proliferation can lead to an enhancement of neo-
plasia [144, 145]. For example, liver enlargement is associated 
with potential for liver carcinogenicity [146, 147]. Conversely, 
the absence of histopathologic effects in subchronic studies is 
associated with absence of carcinogenicity [148, 149], provid-
ing other effects are not produced.
These subchronic effects have been incorporated into “lim-
ited” bioassays, which have as their endpoint preneoplastic 
changes that precede tumor development [150, 151]. These 
bioassays are referred to as “limited” because they are limited 
in the tissues sampled and the duration of testing.
LCBs can be applied as initiation assays, in which the TS is 
tested for its ability to induce the endpoint lesion, or as promo-
tion assays, in which the TS is administered after an agent that 
induces the endpoint lesion to determine the ability of the TS 
to enhance the development of the lesion [152]. An outline for 
initiation and promotion bioassays is shown in Figure 27.10.

In early experimental studies of initiation of skin carcinoge-
nicity, the carcinogens used induced skin tumors with single 
doses. Although this is possible with potent DNA-reactive 
agents, repeated exposure is required for an adequate assess-
ment of initiation by a TS. Because promotion requires an 
even longer time for manifestation, more extensive dosing, up 
to 6 months, is required for an adequate test. Essentially, an 
assay for initiating activity is directed largely toward assessing 
potential in vivo genotoxicity of the TS, whereas the assay for 
promotion activity assesses an epigenetic MoA.
The formation of preneoplastic lesions provides a biomarker 
of effect [153–156]. These non-invasive, usually benign foci of 
altered cells share many molecular and histological features 
with neoplasms, including mutations in oncogenes and tumor 
suppressor genes, as well as increased cell proliferation, i.e., 
hyperplasia [157, 158]. Preneoplastic lesions often precede for-
mation of tumors in many tissues in rodents and humans. As 
such, induction of preneoplastic lesions can be produced by 
lower dose levels and shorter duration of dosing compared to 
those necessary for induction of tumors [159]. Preneoplastic 
lesions can be detected using conventional histological evalu-
ation or with the help of histochemical and immunohisto-
chemical markers. Phenotypic instability (reversibility or 
progression potential) exhibited by foci should be taken into 
consideration in evaluation.
Positive findings for initiation are highly indicative of poten-
tial carcinogenic activity [160]. In fact, it has been calculated 
that an initiation/promotion assay (of 40-week duration) can be 
as sensitive as the 2-year RCB in detecting carcinogenic activ-
ity [155, 161]. Promotion activity also suggests a likelihood of 
carcinogenic activity [155, 160]. In either case, it is possible to 
establish dose-effect data and NOELs for the design of chronic 
bioassays or risk assessment.
The most extensively validated and used model for an LCB is 
the rat liver hepatocellular focus bioassay [150, 151, 162]. This 
bioassay takes advantage of the extensive capability of chemi-
cal biotransformation in the liver and the availability of sensi-
tive and reliable markers for preneoplastic lesions [156, 163]. 
The liver LCB model can also be used to study the dose effects 
of known carcinogens for risk assessment (see Section 11.2).
In the liver LCB, several series of selected chemicals have 
been evaluated. For example, a series of chlorinated alkyl com-
pounds [164], including dichloromethane, 1,2-dichloroethane, 
1,1,2-trichloroethane, trichloroethylene, tetrachloroethylene, 
1,1,1,2-tetrachloroethylene, 
1,1,2,2-tetrachloroethane 
and 
hexachloroethane, was tested [165]. These compounds were 
tumorigenic in mice, inducing mainly liver tumors [164, 166]. 
For some of these compounds, there is evidence for genotox-
icity, but in the rat liver LCB for induction of preneoplastic 
lesions, none exhibited initiating activity, whereas several dis-
played promoting activity comparable to that of the positive 
control, phenobarbital.
Other commonly used LCBs are the mouse skin papilloma/
carcinoma, the mouse lung adenoma/carcinoma and the rat 
mammary gland adenoma/carcinoma assays [150, 151, 167]. 
These have advantages for specific types of chemicals. For 
example, mouse skin is very responsive to PAHs and is appro-
priate for the assessment of topical products. However, arti-
facts resulting from chronic irritation must be considered.
The newborn mouse also has been used as a LCB [168]. In 
this model, newborn mice of any strain are administered the 
TS by intraperitoneal injection or oral intubation at days 8 and 
15 after birth and then held for observation for up to 1 year 
of age. The model exhibits high sensitivity to DNA-reactive 
carcinogens but is unlikely to respond to epigenetic agents 
because of the limited exposure that is provided.

27.7  Alternative Bioassays
Alternative bioassays employ transgenic mice [169, 170] in 
which the principal genetic targets in the transgene are spe-
cific oncogenes (H-ras model), tumor suppressor genes (p53 
model) or the entire genome as in DNA-repair deficient ani-
mals (XPA′ /′  deficient model). Four models that are the 
most studied are the p53 heterozygous mouse (p53+/′ ), the 
Tg.AC mouse, the CB6F1-Tg ras H2 mouse and the XPA′ /′ 
mouse. Other models, such as Min, p53/XPC double mutant, 
Eō-pim-1 and ARF-deficient, have shown responsiveness to 
carcinogens but have not been employed for regulatory stud-
ies [171]. The better studied transgenic models have responded 
appropriately to a variety of carcinogenic and noncarcino-
genic agents [172].
Two of the commonly used models are based on alterations 
in genes that are mutated in human and rodent neoplasms (e.g., 
the p53 tumor suppressor gene and the H-ras oncogene). In the 
case of the XPA′ /′ model, elimination of nucleotide excision 
repair provides an enhanced response to DNA damage. Each 
model has specific features. Mice heterozygous for p53 dif-
fer in response depending on the strains used as parents; for 
example, the C57BL mouse, which is the most widely used 
[173], has a low incidence of background liver cell neoplasms, 
whereas liver neoplasms in unexposed controls are more 
frequent in C3H mice. The Tg.AC model carries a v-Ha-ras 
oncogene fused to the promoter of ΄-globin gene in the FVB/N 
mouse strain [174], a strain not commonly used in toxicology 
and which is susceptible to audiogenic seizure. The Tg ras H2 
mouse carries five to six copies of human c-H-ras gene inte-
grated in tandem array in the genome of F1 mice of transgenic 
male C57BL/6J mice and normal female BALB/cByJ mice 
[175]. The C57BL/6J is not very susceptible to hepatocarcino-
genesis or lung carcinogenesis, whereas the BALB/c is suscep-
tible to lung tumor development [176].
Any route of exposure can be used with these models, 
but most data have been obtained by the oral route for the 
p53+/′  and Tg ras H2 assays, whereas topical application is 
the preferred route for the Tg.AC. In these models, neoplasms 
can be elicited within 6 months with few or no neoplasms in 
controls. Beyond 6 months, these animals begin to develop 
high incidences of genetically determined neoplasms as fol-
lows: the p53+/′  with a C57BL parent develops lymphomas 
and sarcomas [177], Tg.AC develops odontogenic tumors, 
erythrocytic leukemia and salivary gland and ovarian neo-
plasms [177, 178], H-ras2 develops benign and malignant 
lung neoplasms, splenic hemangiosarcomas and forestom-
ach papillomas [175]. The evaluation of increases in any of 
these neoplasm types must consider whether the increase is 
attributable to induction of neoplasia as opposed to accel-
eration of neoplastic development. The XPA-deficient mice 
do not show appreciably increased incidences of background 
neoplasms (only some liver neoplasms in the C3H-derived 
strain) [179, 180]. All of these models respond primarily to 
DNA-reactive carcinogens, although the Tg.AC model has 
the potential to identify epigenetic skin carcinogens [170, 
181, 182], and the Tg ras H2 has also responded to epigenetic 
carcinogens [170, 183].
The p53+/′ mouse clearly responds with accelerated 
development of thymic lymphomas (e.g., phenolphthalein, 
cyclophosphamide and melphalan [172]), but compared to 
the wild-type background, it has not exhibited an acceler-
ated response to DNA-reactive carcinogens targeting liver 
(diethylnitrosamine), mammary gland (9,12-dimethylbenz(a)
anthracene), colon (1,2-dimethylhydrazine) or lung (urethane) 
[176, 184]. This may reflect the fact that p53 mutation is not an 
early event in murine carcinogenesis for some tissues. Also, 
this model has, on occasion, failed to respond to the positive 
control p-cresidine [170, 172, 185]. In the Tg.AC model, the 
positive control generally used is the skin tumor promoter 
12-O-tetradecanoylphorbol-13-acetate [170, 172]. The fact 
that this chemical by itself elicits skin tumors in the Tg.AC 
mouse demonstrates the hypersensitivity of this model to skin 
irritants. For all of these models, testing may be enhanced by 
the measurement of cell proliferation in critical target organs. 
In the case of Tg.AC, stimulated cell proliferation can be the 
basis for skin neoplasm enhancement.
Currently, the Tg ras H2 model is by far the most commonly 
used model [186] and is favored by regulatory guidelines [187, 
188]. A critical aspect of application is the demonstration of 
assay sensitivity. In validation studies, this model detected 
mutagenic and nonmutagenic carcinogens but was negative for 
mutagenic or nonmutagenic noncarcinogens [183, 189]. The 
only exceptions were estradiol and the immunosuppressant 
cyclosporin A. This may indicate a limited susceptibility of 
this model to hormonal or immune-mediated modes of tumori-
genesis. Several retrospective comparisons of data from 2-year 
rodent bioassays and 6-month studies in the Tg ras H2 model 
were performed (e.g., [190]). They revealed that a combination 
of the 2-year rat bioassay with the Tg ras H2 model yielded 
a higher correct determination of 85% of human carcinogens 
than the 2 species 2-year rodent bioassay, which detected 69% 
of human carcinogens [190].
Transgenic mouse models may be more susceptible to genetic 
drift than wild-type rats or mice employed in the 2-year bioas-
say [191]. Therefore, positive controls are generally included 
in studies with Tg ras H2 mice intended for submission to a 
regulatory agency. Commonly used positive control chemicals 
are urethane or N-nitrosomethylurea (NMU), which produced 
a reliable positive response [192].
Studies in the Tg ras H2 model for submission purposes typi-
cally consist of three dose groups in addition to a positive and 
negative control group. A common study design includes 25 male 
and 25 female animals in the negative control group and the test 
article dosed groups. Group size may be as low as 10 males and 
10 females in the positive control. In most studies, toxicokinetic 
parameters are determined in satellite animals. Dose levels are 
usually determined based on results of a dose-range–finding 
study with 4 weeks of administration. An important difference 
to the 2-year rodent bioassay is the selection of the high dose. 
While in the 2-year bioassays, a high dose that results in drug 
plasma exposure at least 25 times higher than that in humans at 
the maximum recommended dose is considered sufficient, this 
rule is not generally accepted for the 6-month study in Tg ras 
H2 mice. Tumor rates are well characterized [193] and generally 
low when compared to the 2-year bioassay.

The use of the Tg ras H2 model for carcinogenic risk assess-

27.8  Design and Evaluation of RCBs
One of the most critical aspects of the RCB is the selection 
of the dosages of the TS to be used. Setting of the high dos-
age can be based on a number of endpoints, including toxicity, 
pharmacokinetics (relationship to human exposures), satura-
tion of absorption and maximum feasible dose [197, 198]. It is 
generally accepted (or required) that the high dosage should 
be the MTD. When the two sexes show different toxicities, 
which is frequent, different sets of dosages need to be admin-
istered in the RCB. Testing at the MTD ensures that potential 
carcinogenicity has been fully evaluated. Nevertheless, it cre-
ates issues in the interpretation of positive findings, because 
of high dose effects, as discussed below. The MTD is ascer-
tained in a 90-day study with the goal to identify a dosage to 
be used in chronic studies (104 weeks) that will reduce body 
weight gain by about 10% without producing life-shortening 
toxicity. Usually, a second dosage, which is one-half of the 
MTD, is used, in case the high dosage produces a reduction 
in survival (greater than 25%) which compromises evaluation 
of the results. In industry studies, a third dosage in the range 
of human intake is sometimes used to provide data for risk 
assessment should high dosages induce tumor increases. When 
enough animals survive at the MTD for the intended 104-week 
dosing to assess carcinogenicity and there is no increase in 
neoplasia, then it can be concluded that carcinogenicity has 
been rigorously evaluated.
Some investigators have advocated dosing for lifetime until 
natural death [199]. Such an approach, however, introduces 
several complexities beyond the longer duration of the study. In 
tissues with background incidences of neoplasms (see Section 
9), the incidence increases rapidly in older animals creating 
the possibility of numerical imbalances in tumor incidences 
between groups due to difference in survival. Also, allowing 
animals to become moribund creates the possibility that they 
will die unobserved and histology will be compromised by tis-
sue autolysis.
Several criteria have been applied to identify carcino-
genic activity. The most rigorous is the finding of unequivo-
cal evidence in either sex of the dosed animals of induction 
of a type(s) of malignant neoplasm not seen in contemporary 
controls [200, 201]. The incidence also should be above that 
recorded in databases of background neoplasms (see Section 
9), thereby indicating ab initio induction of neoplasia. Other 
criteria include a marginal increase in a very rare malignancy 
and an increase in the incidence of a type of malignant neo-
plasm present in controls, providing the incidence(s) in dosed 
animals exceeds the range of historical controls. A controver-
sial endpoint is an increase in the total incidence of all types 
of neoplasia with no increase in a specific type of neoplasm.
The malignant neoplasm can be of any histological type, 
epithelial or mesenchymal, and although a clear increase in 
the incidence of a malignant neoplasm is most persuasive, an 
increase in the combined incidence of benign and malignant 
neoplasms of the same cell type of origin, where the latter is 
not significantly increased, is generally accepted as reflecting 
carcinogenic activity [202, 203].
In addition to these criteria, an increase in the multiplicity of 
neoplasms above that in controls and a reduction in the latency 
period for the development of neoplasms have also been con-
sidered [201]. Although these latter findings can indicate an 
influence of the chemical or stress/toxicity produced by it on 
the development of neoplasia, they can reflect modulation of 
host control of background neoplasia and are less definitive 
evidence of carcinogenic activity.
The evidence of malignancy is best established by the pres-
ence of invasion or metastasis. For most rodent neoplasms, 
however, the diagnosis of malignancy is typically made by 
histopathologic evidence of cellular atypia. This can be prob-
lematic since some diagnoses are controversial (see anatomic 
pathology and cancer hazard evaluation). The finding of an 
increase in only benign neoplasms, especially if the type of 
neoplasm is not established to be premalignant, does not con-
stitute sufficient evidence for carcinogenicity but does provide 
some limited evidence.
These criteria apply to findings in any species, strain or sex. 
An effect in more than one sex or species, in more than one 
dose group or in independent experiments, of course, strength-
ens the evidence. It must be kept in mind that carcinogenicity 
in a rodent study is not proof of a cancer hazard to humans 
(see Section 11). Ultimately, in assessing human hazard (which 
is the reason why animal tests are done), scientific judgment 
is used in evaluating all the information available, including, 
importantly, mechanism (or mode) of action, including any 
known pharmacologic activity.
A conclusion by a regulatory body of a positive outcome in a 
RCB often reflects a conservative approach to hazard identifi-
cation in the interest of protecting public health. For example, 
a statistical increase in neoplasms is often accepted as valid in 
spite of artifacts in the study such as excessively reduced body

weight gain or poor or unbalanced survival. It is important to 
consider the possibility that a neoplasm increase can be a con-
sequence of the stress or toxicity of the dosing conditions [204] 
and not of a chemical action of the TS. Moreover, it is possible 
that the differences occurred by chance alone. This can be 
controlled to some extent by the use of two control groups to 
document in-study random variation [205, 206]. A statistically 
significant greater incidence in neoplasms in a dosed group 
that does not exceed that of both of the control groups can be 
regarded as a numerical imbalance, not a true increase.
A critical aspect in the evaluation is the interpretation of 
pathological diagnoses. Findings of increases in malignant 
neoplasms are universally accepted as evidence of carcino-
genic activity. Combining malignant and benign neoplasms 
of the same cell type of origin is also widely accepted, and 
guidelines have been published by the NTP [203]. It should 
be noted that in some tissues, certain benign tumors are not 
related to malignancies (e.g., benign mammary fibroadenomas 
and malignant mammary adenocarcinomas). When evaluating 
increases in neoplasms, attention must be paid to pathology 
in the tissue that is the site of neoplasms because cell injury 
and compensatory cell proliferation can facilitate neoplastic 
development.
When assessing the incidences of neoplasms in groups, 
both the incidence in animals surviving to termination and 
the combined incidences of decedent and terminal animals 
are considered. Evaluation should be made with regard to 
the ranges of commonly occurring neoplasms, as well as the 
percent incidence profiles expressed by time of death (i.e., the 
neoplasm distribution) [205, 207–216]. Among the commonly 
occurring neoplasms in rats, pituitary, mammary gland, and 
skin neoplasms occur predominantly after 50 weeks, whereas 
lymphoma occurs almost equally before and after 50 weeks 
[217]. In mice, lung, mammary gland, and liver neoplasms 
occur after 50 weeks, whereas lymphoma or leukemia occurs 
almost equally before and after 50 weeks [217].
A problem arises with the finding of a statistically signifi-
cant increase in a type of tumor found in controls (see Section 
11) present only in the highest dosage group which suffers 
from poor survival. This raises the question whether the tumor 
finding is only a numerical imbalance representing an artifact 
of toxicity. In this case, it can be helpful to apply Hill’s criteria 
of causality (see Section 11). These include consideration of 
other data, such as genotoxicity, findings in subchronic studies 
(e.g., preneoplasia), and mechanistic data (e.g., increased cell 
proliferation).
The assessment of the carcinogenic potential of a chemical 
or drug candidate is still in most cases based on long-term 
in vivo studies, usually in rats and mice. This is different for 
biotechnology-derived drug candidates, for which standard 
RCBs are usually inappropriate (ICH S6). Most carcinogens 
induce tumors in animals only after dosing for a considerable 
part of their lifetime. Chemical-induced tumors may resemble 
morphologically those that occur also spontaneously in the 
animal model used or, less frequently, may be morphologi-
cally distinct. Only strong and usually genotoxic carcinogens 
induce tumors in a high proportion of animals. Today, such 
molecules are usually identified by in silico, in vitro and in 
vivo assays for genotoxicity (see Section 4). Molecules with 
equivocal or weak genotoxicity may be expected to induce 
only a low incidence of neoplasms, even after lifetime admin-
istration. Thus, RCBs require a large number of animals, gen-
erally in the range of 500. The duration of these experiments 
is usually in the range of 3 years, including histopathological 
evaluation and thorough study interpretation. With mechanis-
tic follow-up experiments, the overall program for carcinoge-
nicity testing is in the range of 4 years. In consequence, these 
studies usually represent the most cost- and resource-inten-
sive studies in the toxicity testing program for a new chemical 
or drug candidate.
Two-year RCBs usually are performed at the end of a devel-
opment program, and the availability of the results may be on 
the time-critical path to submission for marketing authoriza-
tion. Also for drug candidates, the assessment of the carci-
nogenic potential is performed in most cases exclusively on 
nonclinical data. A false positive interpretation of a 2-year 
RCB may have major economic impact on the sponsor, while 
a false-negative result may represent a serious health risk for 
workers, consumers or the general population.
Thus, it is of utmost importance to include all informa-
tion with potential impact on the carcinogenic potential of a 
molecule in the interpretation of a carcinogenicity study. The 
spectrum of background neoplasms, their biological behavior 
and pathology as well as their incidence are important in the 
overall weight-of-evidence interpretation of a carcinogenicity 
study.
27.8.1  When to Use Historical Control Data (HCD)
According to draft guidance of the US-FDA, the statistical 
evaluation of a 2-year RCB requires the categorization of 
tumors as either common, with a background incidence >1%, 
or rare with a background incidence ≥1% [218]. The FDA’s 
thresholds for statistical significance of tumor incidences are 
less stringent for rare tumors than for common ones, reflecting 
the lower statistical power of the test applied to these tumors. 
Thus, a statistically significant increase in the incidence of 
a rare tumor is unlikely to be a false positive, indicating the 
importance of this categorization. Also, for rare tumors, HCD 
may assist in deciding whether a higher tumor incidence with-
out statistical significance is a true-negative or false-negative 
result.
For common tumors HCD may help to inform whether a 
marginally statistically significant difference is a true positive 
or a false positive.
Two-year bioassays are sometimes conducted with dual con-
current control groups. In case of disparate tumor incidences 
in the two controls HCD are of value in deciding whether the 
incidence in one group is unexpectedly high or in the other 
group unexpectedly low [210]. Comparison of tumor inci-
dences in the concurrent control group with HCD is also con-
sidered as a method of quality control of the bioassay [219].
27.8.2  Comparison of HCD with Study Data
It is common practice to determine whether study data are 
within the range of HCD. This was done in the US-FDA

review of liraglutide in support of interpretation of the nature 
of thyroid C-cell carcinomas in rats. Although there was only 
borderline statistical significance, incidences exceeded that of 
the concurrent control group and HCD. This was interpreted as 
evidence for a liraglutide-related effect. On the contrary, HCD 
may represent important evidence for the interpretation of sta-
tistical significance for a tumor type: in a case of high pituitary 
gland tumors in dosed rats, the incidence in the concurrent 
control group was extraordinarily low and incidences in dosed 
animals were within the range of HCD and only slightly above 
the mean [209]. This was taken as evidence for an incidental 
inter-group difference. These examples show how HCD can 
add to the weight-of-evidence assessment whether a numeric 
imbalance may be more likely indicative of a carcinogenicity 
effect or only incidental.
The range of HCD is dependent on the size of the HCD data-
base and does not consider data distribution, in particular stud-
ies with an extraordinary low or high background incidence of 
a given tumor. Replacing the upper range by an upper confi-
dence interval circumvents this issue [220]. Another option is 
to analyze HCD using a boxplot method [221].
Several statistical methods have been developed to enable 
the incorporation of HCD in the evaluation of a 2-year bioas-
say (reviewed by [221]). The methods published by Dunson 
and Dinse [222] and by Peddada et al. [223] offer the rigor 
of addressing differences in animal survival. Practically, 
however, these methods can be difficult to apply, as one 
requires a sophisticated setting of statistical parameters and 
the other the availability of individual animal survival data 
for HCD.
The comparison of actual study data with HCD represents 
only one aspect in the overall interpretation of carcinogenicity 
study results. HCD should always be considered in a weight-
of-evidence approach in conjunction with many other param-
eters [224].
27.8.3  Sources of HCD
Studies from which HCD are derived must be as similar as 
possible to the study being evaluated. This requires control of 
the many factors that can influence background tumor inci-
dences. Regulatory guidelines generally require that HCD 
are derived from studies using animals of the same strain 
and which were conducted under the same conditions as the 
study being evaluated [225–227]. To minimize the impact of 
potential genetic drift it is usually required to set a certain time 
frame for the generation of HCD, e.g., 5 years prior to study 
start. This time frame may be shorter or longer, depending on 
temporal stability of incidences for a certain tumor. HCD from 
other laboratories may be considered, if the relevant variables 
are sufficiently controlled [224]. HCD should always be ana-
lyzed for the effect of any parameter proven or likely to influ-
ence the background incidence of a given tumor [228]. Only 
those HCD should be compared to an actual study for which 
those parameters are similar.
All HCD used in the interpretation of a carcinogenicity 
study and the methodology of application of HCD should 
be a component of the study report and available for review 
[224].
27.9  Background Neoplasms
27.9.1  Nature of Background Neoplasms
The risk of developing cancer(s), particularly those of high inci-
dences, increases with advancing age, in laboratory rodents, as 
in any other mammalian species. These background tumors 
develop most likely from genetically abnormal cryptogenic 
neoplastic cells, although spontaneous mutations, which accu-
mulate with progressive age may play a role. In a population 
of rodents, these tumors represent the spectrum of background 
neoplasms of each colony of animals. The spectrum of back-
ground neoplasms must be considered when assessing the car-
cinogenic potential of a TS in RCB. An uneven distribution 
of background neoplasms among groups of a carcinogenicity 
study may be interpreted as a carcinogenic signal, which could 
result in a false positive interpretation of the study.
27.9.2  Common Background Neoplasms 
in Rats and Mice
The spectrum of background neoplasms differs between mice 
and rats, is sex-specific and depends on several additional fac-
tors. Table 27.6 lists the most common types of background 
neoplasms in Wistar rats and CD-1 mice. Incidences of back-
ground neoplasms in a model comprise HCD.
27.9.3  Factors That Influence the Incidence 
of Background Neoplasms
In addition to species and sex, other factors influence the spec-
trum of background tumors. Several parameters related to the 
animal, the experimental conditions and the histopathological 
processing and diagnostics can influence the pattern of back-
ground neoplasms (Table 27.7). Systematic investigations into 
the relevance of these factors have not been undertaken for 
most tumor types. In the absence of evidence from such inves-
tigations, special attention should be paid to factors which may 
have a causal relationship to the pathogenesis of a given tumor 
type. For example, factors that may influence the hormonal 
status of an animal may affect the background incidence of 
hormone-dependent tumors. The following paragraphs give 
examples of known relationships between certain experimen-
tal factors and their effect on specific tumor types.
27.9.3.1  Animal-Dependent Factors
Table 27.6 describes the effect of species on background tumors. 
An analysis of the database maintained by the industry consor-
tium Registry of Industrial Toxicology Animal-data (RITA) 
[209] revealed that pituitary gland adenomas of the pars dis-
talis were the most frequent background tumors in Wistar rats 
with an overall mean incidence of 27.6% in males and 52.1% 
in females. In CD-1 mice, the background incidence of this 
tumor type was much lower with 0.9% in males and 2.0% in 
females. Hepatocellular carcinoma was a common tumor in 
male CD-1 mice (mean incidence 10.6%) but occurred much 
less frequently in females (mean incidence 0.5%).

27.9.3.2  Animal Husbandry-Related Factors
A positive correlation between body weight and tumor inci-
dences has been documented for mice and rats (see Section 
8). A strong positive correlation between the body weight of 
B6C3F1mice at the age of 13 weeks with later liver tumor 
incidence [219] indicates that not only the final body weight 
may be of relevance. Mammary and pituitary gland tumors in 
F344 rats were strongly correlated with body weight [235]. In 
contrast to several tumors which increase in frequency with 
higher body weight, Leydig cell adenomas in rats showed the 
opposite behavior [228].
Body weight development reflects caloric intake and other 
diet-related factors. Diet may modify background tumor inci-
dences in two ways: (1) by a direct effect on certain tumor types 
and (2) by effects on longevity and the increased likelihood of 
animals to develop tumors if they live longer. Moderate caloric 
restriction consistently led to an increased survival (e.g., [235, 
237]). A reduced incidence and later onset of degenerative 
diseases such as cardiomyopathy and nephropathy was a 
major contributory factor. Moderate caloric restriction had no 
consistent effect on the overall tumor incidence at an age of 
106 weeks, but tumors were detected later in food-restricted 
rats [237]. When specific tumor types were considered in iso-
lation, there was a clear reduction in incidence and onset of 
pituitary gland adenomas and pancreatic islet cell carcinomas 
in males as well as pituitary gland adenomas and mammary 
gland tumors in females. The reduction of tumor incidences by 
food restriction may in part be due to a later time of detection 
for non-fatal and non-palpable tumors because of increased 
survival. The effects of food restriction on tumor incidences 
confirm the relationship between body weight and incidences 
of background neoplasms (e.g., [228, 235]). A retardation in 
the age-related loss of DNA-repair capacity and DNA repli-
cation fidelity due to caloric restriction is postulated to rep-
resent an important mechanism behind this beneficial effect 
of caloric restriction [238]. Dietary factors other than caloric 
intake seem to play a minor role, as long as there are no imbal-
ances that would result in malnutrition [239]. Obesity may 
result in contrary effects, i.e., reduce longevity due to earlier 
onset of age-related degenerative diseases and related earlier 
detection of non-fatal tumors as well as earlier onset of tumori-
genesis [240].
Data on the effect of environmental enrichment on back-
ground tumor incidences are lacking. It has been reported that 
basal adrenocorticotropic hormone and corticosterone con-
centrations were lower in animals housed with environmental 
enrichment when compared to those without [241]. It is pos-
sible that a lower level of activation of the hypothalamus–pitu-
itary–adrenal axis could result in lower incidences of adrenal 
neoplasms [224].
Group housing may influence the incidence of tumors 
that are hormone dependent. Individually housed F344 rats 
developed testicular Leydig cell tumors at a lower incidence 
and pituitary gland tumors at a higher incidence than group 
housed rats [235, 242, 243]. An analysis of the RITA database 
revealed a higher incidence of mammary gland carcinomas in 
group housed CD-1 mice when compared to individual hous-
ing (Boehringer-Ingelheim unpublished data). Male F344 rats 
dosed by the inhalation route developed more pheochromo-
cytomas of the adrenal gland, pituitary gland adenomas and 
pancreatic islet cell adenomas than those dosed via admixture 
in feed. Conversely, the incidences of Leydig cell adenoma and 
leukemias was lower in these animals [211]. Also female rats 
dosed by inhalation had a higher incidence of pituitary gland 
tumors. However, in that set of studies, inhalation studies dif-
fered from studies with admixture in feed also by housing 
and feeding conditions and the laboratory that conducted the 
studies. While the contribution of individual factors is unclear, 
it demonstrates the importance of animal husbandry-related 
factors.
The potential for induction of subcutaneous sarcomas by 
implanted microchips for animal identification is extensively 
documented [244–249]. In an experimental setting mimicking 
jet lag, repeat 8-hour advances of the 12-hour light/12-hour 
dark cycle increased the growth of osteosarcomas in a mouse 
tumor model [250].
27.9.3.3  Tissue Sampling and Processing
Generally, the likelihood of detecting a neoplasm increases 
with the area of tissue which is subjected to histological 
examination. This has been demonstrated for thyroid gland 
tumors [251] and testicular Leydig cell tumors [228]. The most 
relevant factors with impact on the tissue area available for 
examination are: (1) whether the organ/tissue is examined 
unilaterally or bilaterally; (2) the orientation of organ/tissue 
trimming and sectioning and (3) the number of tissue sections 
prepared or examined. In addition, histopathology procedures 
are of relevance: As an example, the study pathologist may 
request an additional tissue section if a certain structure is not 
present in the original section, e.g., the parathyroid gland in a 
section of thyroid/parathyroid gland. This may result in dif-
ferences in the number of thyroid gland sections examined, if 
the study pathologist had requested more additional sections in 
one group than in others.

Several organs routinely examined in carcinogenicity stud-
ies are of heterogeneous composition, in particular the brain, 
but also the kidneys. The pathologist will be able to detect 
small tumors of the pineal gland or of the renal pelvis only 
if the respective sub-structures are in the plane of the histo-
logical sections available for examination. Accordingly, it 
is of major importance to standardize the process of tissue 
sampling and processing. Under the guidance of the RITA 
consortium, pathologists and histotechnicians from various 
European countries and the United States addressed this issue 
and agreed on procedures for tissue sampling and trimming. 
The resulting guidelines have been published [252–254] and 
are also available online (https://reni.item.fraunhofer.de/reni/
trimming/index.php [accessed January 19, 2021]).
27.9.3.4  Histopathological Evaluation
Standard Operating Procedures specify histopathological 
procedures beyond definitions made in regulatory guidelines. 
Such procedures may influence the background incidence of 
tumors. Sarcomas that develop in association with a micro-
chip serve as an example. Although relevant guidelines recom-
mend the examination of all organs/tissues with a macroscopic 
abnormality, it has not been generally accepted to examine 
such findings histologically if associated with a microchip. 
Examining or not examining these tumors may have a major 
impact on the incidence of subcutaneous tumors and the over-
all tumor incidences as such tumors may develop in more than 
5% of the study animals [247]. In an effort at inter-laboratory 
harmonization, the RITA consortium recommended all sub-
cutaneous abnormalities be examined histologically, even if 
microchip-associated, and findings associated with a micro-
chip be recorded under the artificial organ “implantation site” 
[245].
Diagnoses made by pathologists can vary according to 
their education, training and experience [255]. It is, therefore, 
important that pathologists undergo a specialization resulting 
in board certification and further on-the-job training [256].
A clear definition and standardization of diagnostic criteria 
is a critical factor in a consistent evaluation of a carcinogenic-
ity study and even more in the comparability of background 
data between studies or even between different laboratories 
[257, 258]. Conversely, differences in diagnostic criteria or 
their interpretation may represent the most important source 
of variability of background tumor incidences [209, 259].
Different laboratories or pathologists sometimes use dif-
ferent terminologies (synonyms) for certain tumor types. 
Knowledge of such synonyms is important when comparing 
background tumor incidences from different sources. Tumors 
of the adrenal medulla may be termed “tumor, medullary” 
according to the WHO/IARC nomenclature [260] or “pheo-
chromocytoma” according to the International Harmonization 
of Nomenclature and Diagnostic Criteria for Lesions in Rats 
and Mice (INHAND nomenclature) [261]. In addition, the ter-
minology of an anatomic location can vary: for rodents with 
their composite stomach, “forestomach” has been used in the 
WHO/IARC nomenclature [262] but “nonglandular stomach” 
in the INHAND nomenclature [263].
An attempt to generate scientifically sound databases was 
the driver for the generation of the first international harmo-
nized systems of terminology and diagnostic criteria for rodent 
tumors. In the United States, this resulted in the Standardized 
System of Nomenclature and Diagnostic Criteria (SSNDC) 
guides (https://www.toxpath.org/ssndc.aspwhile [accessed 29 
January 2021]), while the WHO/IARC nomenclature was initi-
ated by the international RITA consortium. These nomencla-
ture systems were the basis for the development of a globally 
harmonized system, the INHAND terminology. This termi-
nology represents the current international standard ([264], 
www.goreni.org [accessed January 19, 2021]).
During histopathology peer review, a second pathologist 
evaluates a defined subset of organs and tissues of a study, and 
the final pathology report represents the consensus opinion of 
the study pathologist and the peer reviewing pathologist. This 
procedure increases the consistency of terminology and diag-
nostic criteria. Therefore, it is recommended that background 
data of tumor incidences be derived from peer-reviewed stud-
ies only [224].
27.10  Perturbations of Homeostasis 
in Carcinogenesis
In addition to effects on gene regulation [27], carcinogens 
disrupt metabolic homeostasis in rodents, particularly at high 
doses. It is important to monitor this in carcinogenicity stud-
ies as it has an impact on extrapolation of findings to humans. 
In addition, because both carcinogenic action and perturba-
tion are sustained chronically, major differences in the neuro–
endocrine–immune axis and target tissue microbiome between 
laboratory animals and humans have to be considered in order 
to build a framework of an effective and accurate compara-
tive extrapolation. Important variables in this extrapolation 
include: (1) exposure time; (2) metabolic weight or metabolic 
surface; (3) circulation volumes; (4) cell life cycle duration; 
(5) cell and tissue pattern of adaptation; (6) energy homeosta-
sis and (7) administered compound absorption, distribution, 
metabolism and excretion [265, 266].
The metabolic weight is expressed in kilo calories (kcal) per 
kilograms (kcal/kg), or kcal per square meter (kcal/m2). This 
value is extremely useful in dosing because it reflects more 
effectively the energy expenditure, work requirements of vari-
ous tissues and organs, and also xenobiotic metabolism by tis-
sues and microbiome [265, 266].
Thus, based on metabolic weight, for the same cell type, the 
energy requirements for processes, such as cell proliferation, 
migration and differentiation, are greater in larger species 
[265, 266]. For cell proliferation, the energy required for the 
same cell type across species is inversely proportional to the 
DNA excision repair capacity of target tissue [265].
In addition, the broad areas of organismal perturbation 
include: (1) maintenance of energy availability; (2) mainte-
nance of homeostasis; (3) state of development of cell and tis-
sue proliferation, differentiation, migration and growth and 
also (4) state of reproduction [267–271]. Moreover, for mean-
ingful extrapolation from laboratory animals to humans, the

The scope and behavior of the rodent microbiome and 
virome of the gastrointestinal tract, as well as the target tissues 
of toxicity in the range-finding studies, should be investigated 
before the bioassay commences, especially if the TS is to be 
administered orally.
The oxidation of nutrients, notably glucose, fatty and amino 
acids (both essential and non-essential), generates cellular 
energy in the form of adenosine triphosphate (ATP), which 
is the major energy supply of all cells [274] (Figure 27.11). 
Glycolysis is a central carbon metabolic energy pathway in 
nearly all mammalian organisms. In this pathway, cellular 
energy in an aerobic environment is generated by the degrada-
tion of glucose transported into mitochondria, where it is com-
pletely oxidized to CO2 and water (oxidative phosphorylation) 
via the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle, and the respiratory 
chain, generating up to 36 ATP molecules per glucose mol-
ecule [275]. Conversely, under anaerobic conditions, pyruvate 
is converted to lactate in the cytosol, followed by lactic fer-
mentation, and concomitant regeneration of oxidized nicotin-
amide adenine dinucleotide (NAD+), for continuing glycolysis 
without oxygen [275].
In addition to energy production, glycolysis provides impor-
tant precursors for the synthesis of amino and fatty acids, and 
nucleotides, which are required for cell proliferation, migra-
tion, differentiation and regulation of gene expressions by pro-
teins associated with adaptation [276].
Under impaired mitochondrial respiration, hypoglycemic 
and hypoxic histomicro-environmental conditions, resulting 
mainly from hepatic stress and injury, the hypoxia-inducible 
factor (HIF-1) is not degraded and instead is converted into 
a stabilized form − (HIF-1−), which operates under both 
aerobic and anaerobic microenvironments and leads to the 
Warburg phenotype and activation of oncogenes c-myc and 
c-ras [277, 278]. Subsequently, c-src and glucose transporter 
(GLUT) 1 and 3 are also activated together with hexokinase 
2 (HK2), lactate dehydrogenase and other enzymes in the 
carbon supplying pathways [279, 280]. In addition, c-myc 
activates expressions of genes involved in glutaminolysis 
as well as glycolysis [281–283]. Thus, when mitochondria 
are impaired, as a result of stress and/or injury, oxidative 
phosphorylation via TCA cycle produces ROS [284, 285]. 
Consequently, this accelerated glycolysis, which supplies 
carbon, and glutaminolysis, which supplies nitrogen, under 
stress, hypoxic and hypoglycemic conditions help with cell 
division and proliferation [286, 287]. Furthermore, lower 
intra and extracellular pH levels, which are enhanced by 
lactate production/fermentation, cause apoptosis of neigh-
boring, but not yet transformed normal cells [288], while pro-
moting growth and proliferation of transformed cells [289]. 
The TCA cycle usually persists even if other mitochondrial 
functions are impaired [290, 291]. Moreover, glutamine 
plays a significant role in the regulation of gene expression 
of metabolic energy proteins, as well as signal transduction, 
cellular maintenance and intracellular signaling pathway 
proteins [292]. In addition, glutaminolysis is a mitochondrial 
pathway process which is responsible for generating cellular 
energy from glutamine degradation [290], where glutamine 
is imported into cells via high-affinity glutamine transport 
protein (ASCT2), which is activated by c-myc [283, 290, 293, 
294]. Glutamine synthetase catalyzes and promotes gluta-
mine production located in the centrilobular hepatocellular 
areas of the liver lobule [295]. Furthermore, the glutamate 
generated by deamination of glutamine, which is catalyzed 
by a mitochondrial isoform of glutaminase 1 (GLS1), can be 
used either to generate glutathione (GSH) by GSH cysteine 
ligase to combat oxidative stress from ROS or can be con-
verted to −-ketoglutarate, catalyzed by isocitrate dehydroge-
nase (IDH), to serve as a substrate for the TCA cycle [293, 
296]. In the process, NAD is produced. IDH isoforms 1 and 
2 in the cytosol can produce NAD phosphate (NADPH), an 
important reductant in fatty acid synthesis, as well as oxida-
tive stress homeostasis (Figure 27.11).
One of the most important phases of histocellular metabolic 
energy disruption is oxidative stress, which can result from 
sustained focal hypoxia, hypoglycemia, hypohydration, mito-
chondrial dysfunction, extracellular matrix (ECM) injury or 
detachment, cellular and tissue stress and/or injury, shift in 
the metabolic energy substrate availability and accumulation 
of ROS. Sustained increase of ROS, in combination with some 
of the other factors, can initiate histocellular metabolic energy 
transformation that resembles histomorphologically dysplastic 
hyperplasia, anaplasia, preneoplastic-like initiation and pro-
gression [273, 297–301].
ROS-induced oxidative stress results in direct injury of 
histocellular components of both parenchymal and non-
parenchymal cells, and their ECM elements. These trans-
formed cells and normal adjacent cells develop a stepwise 
equilibrium in their microenvironment, which progressively 
shifts the ever-increasing proliferation and the dysplastic 
dedifferentiation of the transformed cells at the expense of 
the normal neighbors which die, being deprived of meta-
bolic energy substrates. Enhanced glycolysis by these trans-
formed cells with dysfunctional mitochondria and impaired 
oxidative phosphorylation propels glucose-6-phosphate 
(G-6-P) to enter the pentose phosphate pathway, which is a 
major source of NADPH, and which regenerates GSH [302–

form) from glutathione disulfide (GSSG) (the oxidized form) 
is catalyzed by GSH reductase using reduced NADPH [305]. 
More than 60% of NADPH is produced during the initial 
steps of the pentose phosphate pathway and the rest from 
the reactions following glutaminolysis [306]. The pathway 
deviates from glycolysis, using glucose-6-phosphate (G6P) 
as the entry substrate (Figure 27.11). Consequently, the con-
ventional glucose metabolic overflow is redirected to the 
pentose phosphate pathway in situations where glycolysis is 
interrupted. This increased overflow causes an increase in 
the NADPH production and ROS detoxification, as well as 
increased production of precursors for anabolic metabolism

to lactate after glycolysis, and the dysfunctional mitochon-

tion and, as a result, produce ROS [304]. GSH is one of the 
most effective cellular antioxidants capable of maintaining 
a redox balance [307, 308], which can detoxify xenobiotics 
and other toxic endogenous metabolites, even heavy metals 
[71]. Reduced GSH exists in interchangeable redox states as 
oxidized GSSG [309]. The first and third steps from G-6-P 
to pentose phosphate pathway are catalyzed by G-6-P dehy-
drogenase (G6PDH) and 6-phosphogluconate dehydroge-
nase (6PGDH), respectively (Figure 27.11). This metabolic 
redirection is especially important for hepatic stem and 
progenitor cells as well as neoplastically transformed cells, 
which have to regulate simultaneously glycolysis and ROS 
production [71, 274, 309, 310].
Another important function of GSH involves the post-
translational covalent modifications of proteins in a process 
called S-glutathionylation [311, 312]. This process is involved 
in cell growth, cell cycle progression, differentiation, tran-
scriptional activity, cell migration, structures of cytoskel-
eton and ECM and general cell metabolism [313, 314]. The 
modification of proteins is reversed by increasing reducing 
conditions, which promote removal of GSH from proteins via 
mechanisms involving direct thiol/disulfide exchange reac-
tions [311]. Several proteins undergo S-glutathionylation, 
including metabolic enzymes, cytoskeletal proteins, redox 
enzymes, stress proteins (e.g., heat shock protein-HSP-70) 
and fatty acid-binding proteins [315]. Furthermore, the ROS-
dependent regulatory mechanism involves pyruvate kinase 
M2 (PKM2) and 6-phosphofructo-2-kinase/fructose-2,6-bi-
phosphatase 3 (PFKFB3) enzymes. S-glutathionylation of 
these two enzymes causes inhibition of glycolysis and acti-
vation of pentose phosphate pathway [316, 317]. Thus, there 
is a shift of glucose overflow from glycolysis to the pentose 
phosphate pathway with facilitation of fallback and feedback 
loops [318]. Furthermore, O-linked μ-N-acetylglucosamine 
acylation (O-GlcNAc), in response to hypoxia, activates 
6-phosphofructo-1-kinase (PFK), which reroutes fructose-
6-phosphate (F-6-P) from glycolysis to the pentose phosphate 
pathway (Figure 27.11). This enables transformed cells to 
control ROS by dually regulating glycolysis and the pentose 
phosphate pathway, while modulating cellular UDP-GlcNAc 
[319]. In general, the end result is a decrease in glycolysis 
and an increase in pentose phosphate pathway end products, 
which regulate the energy needs of fast-growing cells and tis-
sues [320–322].
Thus, chronic disruption of energy homeostasis can be a 
feature of chemical-induced carcinogenesis. Homeostases 
are maintained by the circulation that connects all cells, tis-
sues and ECM. The latter is controlled by the brown adipose 
tissue, which is linked to the adrenal, pancreas, thyroid and 
regionally to nervous and lymphatic tissues. Brown adipo-
cytes allow their mitochondria to oxidase substrate rapidly 
without adenosine diphosphate (ADP) phosphorylation. Thus, 
uncoupling oxidation from phosphorylation promotes the 
dissipation of oxidation as heat. Older and female rats have 
a higher temperature than younger and male rats [323]. All 
components of circulation, i.e., blood and lymph, depend for 
trophicity on the sympathetic nervous system, together with 
pancreatic and uterine tissues. Uniquely among all tissues, 
circulation tissues possess two feedback loops, a positive 
and a negative, that operate back-to-back. This arrangement 
enables circulation to operate under physiologic and adaptive 
pathological conditions [265, 266]. The uncoupling proteins 
(UCPs) uncouple oxidation from phosphorylation, increase 
tyrosine hydroxylase activity and increase heat production 
and temperature [265, 266].
Thus, it is clear that some factors of metabolic perturbation 
need to be monitored in both, laboratory rodents and humans 
[265, 266]. For rodents, especially rats, in the range-finding 
studies, prior to the bioassay, potential changes that warrant 
attention are as follows : (1) changes in tissue volume (in mL) 
and plasma flow rate (in mL/min) in the liver, intestine and 
plasma; (2) the luminal pH (expressed as a log10 of viable 
counts) along the glandular stomach, small and large intestine; 
(3) increases in the pentose phosphate pathway; (4) increases 
in transaminases; (5) increases in alkaline phosphatase; (6) 
increases in lactic dehydrogenase; (7) increases in histone 
acetylation rate; (8) increases in DNA content of gastrointes-
tinal tract proliferating cells; and (9) increases in ribosomal 
RNA content. The parameters 3 to 9 usually decrease with 
aging but increase in cancer.
27.11  Relevance to Humans
27.11.1  Use of Mechanism of Action 
in Hazard Assessment
The cellular process of carcinogenesis is generally similar in 
experimental animals and humans [324, 325]. Based partly 
on this, a common assumption has been that all rodent car-
cinogens represent cancer hazards to humans. Carcinogens, 
however, differ in their MoAs, principally with regard to 
whether these mechanisms involve DNA-reactive (e.g., geno-
toxic) or epigenetic (non-genotoxic) effects (see Section 3.1). 
As a consequence, carcinogens of these two types differ in 
their characteristics (Tables 27.1 and 27.2). These differences 
direct the need for case-by-case means for assessing the 
potential risk to humans of any specific carcinogen intake. In 
human risk assessment, two considerations are paramount: 
(1) the relevance of the carcinogenic mechanism of action to 
humans and (2) the margin of exposure between the lowest 
active dose in RCBs and the highest human intakes, which 
are usually substantially lower than those used in rodent 
studies.
Some DNA-reactive carcinogens have been shown to pro-
duce DNA modifications which are similar in rodents and 
humans, e.g., 2-acetylaminofluorene [326] and benzo[a]pyrene 
[327], indicating similar bioactivation process. There are, how-
ever, exceptions where species differ in biotransformation; for 
example, tamoxifen glucuronidation [328] rendering humans 
insensitive to effects produced in rodents which underlie liver 
carcinogenicity. Also, single-point mutations in the tumor sup-
pressor gene, p53, are commonly observed in lung cancer sam-
ples from smokers [37], or liver cells from humans exposed to 
aflatoxin B1 [37], but are not frequently observed in rodents,

Most human carcinogens are DNA reactive (see Section 3.1.1), 
and thus, this type of carcinogen clearly represents a potential 
human cancer hazard [19, 330]. Not all intakes of such agents, 
however, lead to cancer [331], largely because human intakes 
are usually below thresholds for their critical effects in rodents 
(see Section 11.2).
Certain epigenetic carcinogens, either neuro-hormonal-
active substances or immunosuppressants, when administered 
to humans under therapeutic conditions, have been associated 
with increases in cancer (Table 27.2). These few chemicals 
were carcinogenic to humans at intakes at which they pro-
duced the cellular effects underlying carcinogenicity in ani-
mal models, often involving enhanced cell proliferation [332]. 
Nevertheless, humans have intakes of a variety of epigenetic 
rodent carcinogens, both synthetic (e.g., the antioxidant food 
additive butylated hydroxyanisole) and naturally occurring 
(e.g., the plant chemical d-limonene), without evidence of 
adverse effects. In some cases, this is due to the fact that the 
mechanisms of action for rodent carcinogenicity are not opera-
tive in humans, as with the plasticizer, diethylhexylphthalate, 
a PPAR− ligand [333, 334]. Table 27.8 presents mechanisms in 
rodents which are not relevant to humans. Also, most human 
intakes, with the exception of some drug treatments, do not 
exceed the threshold for the cellular or tissue effect underlying 
rodent carcinogenicity.
A variety of methods for risk extrapolation to humans 
have been proposed, ranging from simple linear no-threshold 
extrapolation to sophisticated physiologically based pharma-
cokinetic models [335–337]. With the current knowledge of the 
various mechanisms of carcinogenesis, risk assessment proce-
dures are increasingly based on mechanistic considerations and 
involve a case-by-case analysis informed by evaluation of all 
relevant biological effects [200, 338]. The US Environmental 
Protection Agency (EPA) [339] and other expert bodies have 
developed procedures involving mechanistic considerations in 
assessing risk. Mechanism-based methods for risk assessment 
are described in several publications [340–342].
In classifying chemical cancer hazards, expert bodies rely 
on the weight of evidence of carcinogenicity findings from 
human and/or animal studies. Greater weight is given to posi-
tive human findings, even though they are usually obtained 
under uncontrolled conditions and can constitute only an asso-
ciation. A set of nine criteria for assessing causality in epide-
miologic studies was elaborated by Hill [343]. These are as 
follows: (1) strength (effect size); (2) consistency (reproduc-
ibility of observation); (3) specificity (cause leads to a single 
effect); (4) temporality (the cause should precede the adverse 
event in time); (5) biological gradient (dose-response relation-
ship); (6) plausibility (biologic plausibility of the proposed 
mechanism); (7) coherence (the cause and effect should not 
conflict with already known information); (8) experimental 
evidence; and (9) analogy (effect of similar factors).
These criteria are also used in the interpretation of animal 
studies, along with assessment of molecular toxicology [344] 
as described above. For example, the Hill considerations were 
integrated into the causal analysis scoring approach for evalu-
ating the weight of evidence of potential cancer MoAs as a part 
of WHO/IPCS MoA framework [345].
27.11.2  Use of Thresholds for Preneoplastic Events 
Produced by DNA-Reactive Carcinogens
Extrapolation from RCBs that use high and toxic dosages (see 
Section 8) often does not take into account the possibility of 
thresholds. A growing body of literature, however, provides 
supportive mechanistic evidence that NOELs exist for the 
induction of cancer by all types of carcinogens (DNA-reactive 
and epigenetic) at toxicologically insignificant dosages.
Documentation of the dose-effect profile of the precarcino-
genic and carcinogenic effects of a chemical allows for more 
informed decision-making with regard to human hazard, in 
contrast to just a qualitative assessment of whether a chemi-
cal has shown experimental carcinogenic activity or not [346, 
347]. For example, identifying tumor NOAELs in rodents for a 
compound above potential human intake levels suggests a neg-
ligible risk to human health. Moreover, neoplasia is a multistep 
process; hence, experimental thresholds can be delineated for 
events that are antecedent to tumor development, such as DNA 
adduct formation, mutations and induction of preneoplastic 
lesions [156]. Such information can also assist with earlier 
detection of chemical hazard at lower dose levels, which are 
more relevant to real-world exposures [348], and which can 
significantly decrease the timeline of testing and reduce the 
number of animals required for testing.
Recognition is growing, that the use of mechanistic dose-
effect studies for genotoxic endpoints with comprehensive 
analysis of all data will provide critical information for deriv-
ing points of departure levels, such as benchmark dose and 
thresholds that can be used to estimate the risk of genetic dam-
age at low levels of intake [349].
As mentioned in Section 3.1.1, formation of DNA adducts is 
a crucial initial step in the process of carcinogenicity induced 
by DNA-reactive chemicals. DNA adducts are produced by the 
binding of electrophilic chemical species to nucleophilic sites 
in DNA [350, 351]. Each electrophile is the ultimate reactive 
form of a chemical produced either by chemical activation or 
metabolic bioactivation [27].
It should be recognized, however, that not every DNA 
adduct produced by a DNA-reactive chemical leads to muta-
tion which permanently affects cell function (Figure 27.12). 
Levels of DNA adducts formed are a function of the cellular 
concentration of a parent chemical as well as the bioactiva-
tion and detoxication capabilities of the exposed organism. In 
order to become of any biological significance, the levels of 
adducts in cellular nuclear DNA must be sufficient to escape 
or overcome repair processes. In addition, in order to perma-
nently impact cell function, mutations must occur in genes 
critical for carcinogenesis. As such, DNA adducts can be used 
either as an indicator of genotoxic potential or as a key initiat-
ing event.
The presence and amount of DNA adducts are related to 
the intake of a specific chemical, which is one of the major 
applications of adduct analyses in risk assessment. Thus, 
assessment of chemical-specific DNA adducts can be useful 
in the characterization of the mutagenic potential of mixtures. 
Many techniques can be used to detect DNA adducts, with 
some being highly sensitive, for example, 32P-postlabeling and 
advanced mass spectrometry methods can detect one adduct

genotoxic hepatocarcinogen, 2-acetylaminofluorene, has 
addressed the existence of thresholds for preneoplastic events 
and neoplasms produced by this chemical [357, 358]. The stud-

DNA adduct formation, hepatocellular toxicity, increased cell 
proliferation, preneoplastic foci and neoplasia (Figure 27.13).

the observed NOEL for DNA adducts formation was much

produce cytotoxicity and enhanced cell proliferation, thereby 
having a lower threshold. The lowest threshold and safety fac-
tors can be of great value for deriving benchmark doses for 
risk assessment tools, such as the margin of exposure approach 
used in safety assessment of cosmetic ingredients or food 
impurities. The delineation of thresholds also can serve as a 
basis for the elimination of the “as low as reasonably achiev-
able” requirement for the exposure to genotoxic carcinogens.
27.12  Future of Carcinogenicity Assessment
Carcinogenicity tests are not required for all chemicals for 
which there is human intake. For example, under the Federal 
Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act, food ingredients can be desig-
nated as Generally Recognized as Safe (GRAS) for specific use 
and do not require premarket approval by FDA [359]. GRAS 
designation is performed by a panel of qualified experts and is 
based either on evidence from scientific procedures (i.e., pub-
lished in vitro, in vivo, analytical, epidemiological and clinical 
studies) or experience of common use in food that demon-
strates to a reasonable certainty that no harm will result from 
the intended use of the ingredient [359–361]. This approach 
has been extensively used by panels of the Flavor and Extract 
Manufacturers Association of the United States to evaluate

the safety of flavors [362]. Also, in order to assess the prob-

[363, 364] is used by the Joint FAO/WHO Expert Committee

This approach utilizes the Cramer classification scheme [366] 
to establish exposure levels of concern (TTC) based on the 
presence of structural features that are suggestive of toxicity. 
The Cramer classification includes three classes [364]:
•	 Class I, substances with simple chemical structure 
and known metabolic pathways which suggest a low 
order of oral toxicity (TTC for this class is 30 ōg/kg

•	 Class II, substances that are intermediate between 
Class I and Class III (TTC for this class is 9 ōg/kg

•	 Class III, substances with chemical structures which 
may suggest a significant toxicity (TTC for this class 
is 1.5 ōg/kg bw/day).
The lowest TTC value of 0.0025 ōg/kg bw/day (or 0.15 ōg/per-
son/day) is assigned to potential DNA-reactive mutagens and/or 
carcinogens. Based on structural alerts and potential cumulative 
human intake, JECFA assigns Concern Levels, with Concern 
Level I being low and Concern Level III being high. RCBs are 
recommended only for flavors with the highest level of concern 
(e.g., Concern Level III and/or cumulative exposure at or above 
1 ppm [i.e., 3 mg/person/day]) [360]. While TTC concept was 
originally developed for substances with low exposure levels 
present in foods, other potential applications for this approach 
include evaluation of impurities in pharmaceutical preparations 
or substances present in cosmetic products [367, 368]. There is 
an ongoing effort to reassess and refine TTC values incorpo-
rating weight-of-evidence approach and in silico techniques to 
improve robustness and transparency of this approach [367].
The regulatory landscape of drug development also has 
gradually moved away from the default scheme of testing 
requirement in two rodent species toward a holistic carcino-
genicity risk assessment. Such an assessment may involve a 
carcinogenicity study only in one species or the carcinogenic 
potential of a molecule may be sufficiently assessed without 
a standard RCB. Compounds that are unequivocally geno-
toxic do not need to be tested for carcinogenicity, as it can be 
assumed that they represent trans-species carcinogens (ICH 
S1A).
RCBs are considered inappropriate for biotechnology-
derived products (ICH S6(R1)). Instead, a product-specific 
carcinogenic risk assessment is required for compounds with 
potential concern. Such investigations may include assessment 
of receptor expression in normal and neoplastic human tissues, 
cell proliferation in vitro and, if these investigations raise con-
cern, also investigations in appropriate animal models in vivo.
In 2012, ICH published a Regulatory Notice Document that 
considers an integrated holistic approach for carcinogenic-
ity risk assessment also for small molecule drug candidates. 
This approach considers all relevant features of the molecule, 
including pharmacology, pharmacokinetics, toxicities and 
information on carcinogenicity potential from other molecules 
of the same pharmacological class. In contrast to ICH S1A, 
this approach will not be limited to compounds considered 
trans-species carcinogens. An Expert Working Group is in the 
process of validating this approach in a prospective evaluation 
period and, according to the most recent update, will publish a 
Step 1 document soon (ICH S1(R1) EWG Work Plan).
Thus, various strategies are being applied to obviate the 
need for RCBs. With the development of new methodologies 
to assess potential carcinogenicity, as discussed here, it seems 
likely that the RCB will be increasingly less utilized as the 
primary means for assessing chemical carcinogenicity.
