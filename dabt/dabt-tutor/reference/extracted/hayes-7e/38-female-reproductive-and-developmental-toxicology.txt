38.1  Introduction
Developmental toxicology, also known as teratology, has been 
recognized as a distinct branch of toxicology since the early 
1960s. The discipline quickly coalesced after the seemingly 
innocuous morning sickness drug, thalidomide, was found to 
have caused serious congenital malformations in 10,000 or 
more surviving infants born between 1958 and 1961 [1]. While 
the discipline itself has a relatively short history, humans have 
had a profound interest in the causes of malformations for mil-
lennia. In the absence of the scientific method, ancient/medi-
eval populations noted malformations and recorded important 
events that occurred simultaneously. In ancient times, it was 
believed that terata predicted the future; in medieval to early 
modern eras, people acted on deeply held beliefs that assigned 
causes to these events and took actions to eliminate their ori-
gins, often resulting in tragic (and unwarranted) outcomes 
[2]. Within the last three centuries, there have been cases of 
women who gave birth to malformed babies being burned 
alive and of men who were suspected of bestiality that resulted 
in deformed livestock offspring being hanged. Thankfully, 
the formalization and acceptance of the scientific method 
gradually explicated the likely underpinnings of congenital 
malformations and debunked the groundless hysteria that con-
demned innocent victims to torture and death.
During the century that preceded the unfortunate tha-
lidomide incident, scientists had studied the embryology 
of vertebrates and its perturbation by external forces. They 
had demonstrated that external factors like X-irradiation [3], 
vitamin deficiencies [4, 5], and exposure to certain viruses 
[6], when applied during pregnancy, could cause congenital 
malformations. In 1959, James Wilson assembled the extant 
knowledge concerning the causes of birth defects and pub-
lished five principles of teratology that outlined what was 
known at the time about the genesis of terata by exogenous 
agents [7]; this list was updated and expanded to six prin-
ciples in 1973 [8]. With this background, the US Food and 
Drug Administration (US FDA) was able to quickly convene 
panels of experts who conceived a set of study designs that 
were subsequently promulgated as guidelines to ensure the 
safety of drugs for use during pregnancy [9]. It is a credit 
to those scientists who, despite the major advances in our 
knowledge over the next 50+ years, the principles within 
those guidelines continue to serve as the basis for DART 
testing today [10–13].

38.2  Reproduction
Because the lifespans of multicellular animals are finite, a 
means for producing successive generations is required for a 
species to survive. Sexual reproduction is the complex process 
whereby male and female vertebrates contribute specialized 
reproductive cells (called gametes), which merge via fertiliza-
tion to produce offspring that populate the succeeding genera-
tion. Gametes differ from the other cells of the body in that 
they are haploid (i.e., contain one-half the normal number of 
chromosomes characteristic of their species). Fertilization, 
which involves the fusion of a spermatozoan and an ovum, 
results in the formation of a new organism (the zygote) that 
has the total, diploid number of chromosomes, one-half being 
contributed by each parent.
The gametes of the two sexes differ dramatically. During 
their reproductively active phase of life, males produce mil-
lions of extremely small, motile cells called spermatozoa every 
day in their testes. In contrast, during embryonic development, 
females produce relatively few large ova (or eggs) that reside 
in their ovaries from the embryonic stage until the female’s 
period of sexual maturation, during which the ova are released 
(ovulated) or die. The time when the supply of ova is depleted 
marks the end of a female’s reproductive life. Females produce 
only a few thousand ova, and during their reproductive life, 
only a few hundred ova are ovulated and thus made available 
for potential fertilization.
Perturbations of the male reproductive cycle, which is ongo-
ing from puberty for the remainder of the male’s life, and 
manifestations of male reproductive toxicity are the subject of 
Chapter 37 [14]. Female reproductive biology is covered thor-
oughly in textbooks on the subject (e.g., [15–17]) and is sum-
marized below.
38.3  Female Reproductive System
38.3.1  Anatomy
While the specific anatomy in various species differs, the gen-
eral plan of the female reproductive system in mammals is 
similar [18–22]. The system consists of paired organs (ovaries) 
that release haploid gametes; a brood chamber (uterus) within 
which offspring develop; and a set of conduits that allow for 
translocation of ova (and young embryos) from the ovaries to 
the uterus (uterine tubes/oviducts) as well as for delivery of 
full-term offspring from the uterus to the external world (vagi-
nal canal). At specific times, the conduits also permit the entry 
and passage of male gametes in order to facilitate fertilization. 
Figure 38.1 compares the organization of the female reproduc-
tive tracts of humans and other primates with that of rodents 
and rabbits. Primates possess a single, midline (or simplex) 
uterus within which a single embryo/fetus develops. Multiple 
births are relatively rare. In contrast, the uteri of rodents and 
rabbits exhibit a “V”-shape (duplex) that encourages the devel-
opment of multiple offspring on each side.

side of the uterus (Figure 38.2). The surface of each ovary 
is ensheathed in a simple epithelium (called the germinal 
epithelium) that covers a thin connective tissue capsule (the 
tunica albuginea). The bulk of the ovary is the cortex, which

38.3.2  Physiology
Details of oogenesis, folliculogenesis, ovulation, fertilization, 
placentation, and embryonic development of humans and other 
species are presented in textbooks of embryology and devel-
opmental biology (e.g., [21; 23–30]). The information below is 
summarized from these sources.
38.3.2.1  Folliculogenesis and Ovulation
Early in gestation, a small population of primordial germ cells 
migrates from the yolk sac wall to the genital ridges, which are 
the precursors of the gonads located on the medial side of the 
mesonephric kidneys. In females, derivatives of the gonocytes 
give rise to the ova. Each oocyte (presumptive ovum) becomes 
associated with a single layer of somatic “nurse” cells. 
Together, the ova plus their shell of adnexal somatic cells are 
called follicles. The ovary has a finite number of primordial 
follicles at birth, which remain quiescent until they are stimu-
lated to resume development during reproductive life. The 
number of primordial follicles is greatest at birth and declines 
with age [31]. Due to the steady decline in the number of fol-
licles, it has been stated often that follicles are not renewed; 
however, ovarian stem cells that can give rise to functioning 
ova have been identified in numerous species, including mice 
and humans [32]. However, the genes that would control the 
development of these ovarian stem cells and their appropriate 
signals have not been elucidated. If they are active in the ova-
ries, their proliferative rate is insufficient to stem the steady 
decrease in follicles that occurs throughout a female’s repro-
ductive life.
For reproduction to occur, follicles must mature to release 
ova from the ovary. The maturation of follicles, termed “fol-
liculogenesis,” is a hormonally controlled, orderly process that 
culminates in ovulation. Changes in the female reproductive 
cycle are described in detail elsewhere (e.g., [33, 30, 34]) but 
briefly summarized here. Under appropriate hormonal stimu-
lation, groups of quiescent ova within the cortex of the adult 
ovary (called cohorts or clutches) begin to mature in concert 
with a female’s estrous (or menstrual) cycle, which controls 
receptivity of the uterus. Presently, the signals that stimulate 
a cohort of follicles to begin development while the remainder 
of the follicles remain dormant are not known. Importantly, 
while an entire cohort is necessary for successful ovulation

Folliculogenesis is characterized by an increase in the num-

follicular cells nourish the oocyte and begin to secrete estro-

an interior, fluid-filled chamber (the antrum), the size of which 
increases significantly in the successful ova. Eventually, the 
follicle appears as a large, fluid-filled sphere (Graafian follicle) 
with an internal peninsula of follicular cells surrounding the 
activated oocyte. Successful follicles come into contact with 
the ovary capsule and rupture to release the ovum with its sur-
rounding layer of follicular cells (corona radiata) into the peri-
toneal space. The ovum subsequently enters the opening of the 
uterine tube or oviduct. The shells of the ruptured Graafian 
follicles remaining within the ovary remain viable and are 
transformed into a corpus luteum. A corpus luteum appears 
yellow, and its cells secrete progesterone (important for main-
taining pregnancy). There is one corpus luteum formed for 
each ovum ovulated. Thus, the number of corpora lutea (CLs) 
can be used to measure the number of ova that were ovulated. 
Those follicles that do not ovulate regress and are resorbed. 
The remnants of atretic (unsuccessful) follicles appear white, 
and each is called a corpus albicans.
38.3.2.2  Hormonal Control of Female Cycles
The female reproductive cycle is controlled by the interplay of 
hormones secreted by the pituitary gland and those secreted 
by the ovary (Figure 38.3). In NHPs and humans, the cycle 
is approximately 28 days; in mice and rats, the cycle is about 
4.5–5 days. During the pre-ovulatory segment of the cycle, 
the pituitary secretes follicle-stimulating hormone, which 
stimulates (1) the growth and maturation of the follicular 
cohort developing within the ovary for that cycle as well as 
(2) changes in the uterine wall in anticipation of a possible 
pregnancy. As the follicles grow, the follicular cells secrete 
increasing amounts of estrogen, which peaks mid-cycle. Blood 
estrogen levels feedback to the pituitary, and when they reach 
an appropriate peak level, the pituitary is stimulated to secrete 
luteinizing hormone (LH). The sudden release of LH (known 
as the “LH surge”) signals ovulation to occur. After releasing 
the ovum, LH stimulates the conversion of the empty follicular 
shell to become a corpus luteum, which secretes progesterone. 
High amounts of progesterone maintain the preparedness of 
the uterine wall for implantation of a fertilized oocyte (zygote). 
If zygotes are not present in the uterus within a few days after 
ovulation, the progesterone level falls, and the cycle begins 
anew. The presence of zygotes within the uterus is signaled by 
a hormone released by the implanting trophoblast (chorionic 
gonadotropin), which maintains the functioning of the corpus 
luteum (now known as a corpus luteum of pregnancy).

cycle. Thus, there will be multiple CLs in each ovary. Because

epithelium in its state of receptivity so that the zygotes can 
implant. This signal comes from the hormone prolactin, which 
is released in a pulsatile fashion from the anterior pituitary 
gland approximately every 12 hours over the next 5–7 days. In 
rats, prolactin is the primary luteotropic hormone, the release 
of which is mediated by the striking of the uterine cervix 
by the penis during intromission. Consequently, a condition 
of pseudopregnancy can occur in the rat when intromission 
involves a sterile male or when the cervix is artificially stimu-

sized, lubricated rod).
38.3.2.3  Early Events
Ova are the largest cells in the mammalian body. The estimated 
diameter of ova is 0.070–0.075 mm in rodents (rats and mice); 
0.12–0.13 mm in rabbits; 0.11–0.12 mm in monkeys (rhesus); 
and 0.13–0.14 mm in humans [35]. Ovulation is the process 
that releases meiotic ova from the ovary (consult Figure 38.4). 
The ova are swept into the open, fimbriated end of the uterine 
tube (also called the oviduct or Fallopian tube), wherein they 
await the arrival of sperm. Fertilization, the union of a sperm 
and an ovum, takes place in the distal end of the uterine tube. 
Fertilization restores the diploid number of chromosomes, cre-
ates a new organism (the zygote), and initiates a rapid series of 
mitotic divisions (termed cleavage) that subdivides the cyto-
plasm of the ovum into successively smaller cells.
When a zygote reaches the uterine cavity, it is a fluid-filled 
sphere called a blastocyst. In primates, the blastocyst typi-
cally adheres to the upper posterior uterine wall. In rodents 
and rabbits, blastocysts are moved along the uterine horn by 
muscular contractions of the uterine wall, which in conjunc-
tion with chemical signaling distribute the zygotes to dis-
cretely spaced areas of attachment referred to as implantation 
chambers [36, 37].
38.3.2.4  Implantation and Placenta
During the several days that encompass the zygote’s journey 
through the uterine tube, the zygote receives its nutrition and 
disposes of metabolic waste by means of diffusion. The inter-
action between maternal and embryonic tissues establishes 
a temporary organ, the placenta, which is a more efficient 
mechanism by which nutritional delivery, waste disposal, gas 
exchange, and endocrine function can occur. In-depth studies 
of placentae are found elsewhere [37, 41].
While there are differences among the placentae of rodents, 
rabbits, and primates, these species all have chorioallantoic 
placentae that transfer materials from the mother to her off-
spring via a hemochorial interface. This means that, for all 
these species, maternal uterine vessels have been ruptured by 
the developing placenta and maternal blood comes into contact 
with the offspring’s capillaries. The blood streams do not mix, 
but they are very close, and transfer occurs by diffusion. This

the remains of the placenta. Such a site is called a “resorption.” 
If death of an embryo was very early, the site can be visualized 
if the uterus is stained with 10% ammonium sulfide. Loss of 
an embryo for which there is visual evidence of its presence in 
the uterus is termed “post-implantation loss.”
38.3.2.5  Development
The development of an embryo to a neonatal animal is a 
vigorous but meticulously coordinated series of concurrent 
processes that must transpire both in a precise sequence and 
during specific times in gestation. While the developmental 
schedule for each embryo must be maintained, it is important 
to recognize that each embryo develops at its own rate and, 
therefore, the gestational ages that have been tabulated for var-
ious developmental events (e.g., [42]) are the average times of 
observed events. Importantly, embryos from the same litter of 
a polytocous species are frequently at different developmental 
stages, especially during early embryogenesis. The variation 
in developmental stages of littermates can result from different 
times of fertilization and variation in the developmental rate of 
individual embryos.
Despite the many dissimilarities concerning such embry-
onic details as the length of gestation and the size of fetuses 
at parturition, the sequence of developmental events across 
species is remarkably consistent. Thus, the order of events in 
the following discussion is generic and avoids giving specific 
dates for events. Details of embryonic development are beyond 
the scope of this chapter. They can be accessed in embryology 
texts [21, 23-26, 28-30, 43].
As the blastocyst embeds itself in the uterine wall, the inner 
cell mass segregates into two layers: the epiblast, which is 
related to the developing amniotic cavity, and the hypoblast, 
which develops as part of the yolk sac. The epiblast quickly 
transforms itself through a specialized cellular migration pro-
cess (variously called invagination, ingression, or gastrulation 
[45, 27, 29, 30]) to form the three primary germ layers (ecto-
derm, mesoderm, and endoderm) as well as the notochord. 
These germ layers differentiate and combine to give rise to the 
specific tissues of the body. The ectodermal germ layer gives 
rise to the nervous system and skin. The tissues derived from 
the mesoderm include cartilage, bone, muscle, connective tis-
sue, kidneys, and blood. The endoderm cells form the linings 
of the gastrointestinal, respiratory, and lower urinary tracts. 
The primordia of the organ systems are formed from combina-
tions of tissues derived from the germ layers.
To accomplish proficiently and precisely the process of gen-
erating a healthy embryo, multiple coexisting mechanisms 
collaborate to synchronize embryonic schedules and influence 
the fates of different populations of cells. While the sequence 
of events is unalterable, there is some plasticity regarding the 
schedules. It is important to be mindful that successful dif-
ferentiation requires both the expression of specific genetic 
information that resides within the cell’s nucleus and provides 
the instructions regarding structural/physiological changes 
as well as the influence of appropriate environmental stimuli 
(e.g., inducer molecules; permissive and instructive signal 
molecules) that are released by disparate nearby embryonic 
tissues. Both conditions (preparedness for genetic expres-
sion and the presence of specific environmental stimuli) are 
present for finite periods, which must overlap as development 
proceeds. Thus, the development of a healthy embryo requires 
repetitive, well-timed interactions between (normal) environ-
mental stimuli and embryonic genes as they are expressed or 
repressed throughout development [44].
38.4  Concepts Important in DART Studies
A few concepts that are unique to DART studies will be 
described briefly below. The concepts include the dualism 
exhibited by embryonic potency versus differentiation; gesta-
tional milestones; critical (sensitive) periods for teratogenesis; 
response threshold; and the inverted yolk sac placenta.
38.4.1  Embryonic Potency and Differentiation
Potency is a concept used to describe all the possible fates of a 
cell at any time in development. Differentiation is the process 
whereby cells change sequentially until they attain the intrin-
sic properties and functions that characterize a particular adult 
tissue.
Embryonic cells are pluripotent during early development. 
That is to say, cells of young embryos can develop into a large 
variety of different tissue types depending upon their positions 
within the embryo and the signals in their local environment. 
As development proceeds, young embryonic cells change 
their characteristics as they become committed to a particular 
structural and/or functional role within the body. The concept 
of increasing cellular differentiation or specialization over 
time and the contemporaneous decrease in potency continues 
throughout the embryonic period and occurs in all species.

As development proceeds, various observable events must

38.4.3  Critical (Sensitive) Period for Teratogenesis
During the period of early organogenesis (when the embryo 
begins to undergo differentiation and the establishment of 
the germ layers), the organism is most vulnerable to devel-
opmental toxicity, including teratogenesis (see Figure 38.4). 
The onset of susceptibility is abrupt, and most developmen-
tal toxicants produce their highest incidences of malforma-
tions at this time. Not only do embryos exhibit a sudden onset 
of susceptibility, but also each embryonic organ has its own 
sensitive period for teratogenesis [8, 53]. This sensitive (or 
critical) period coincides with early developmental events 
and tissue interactions that transpire to initiate organ develop-
ment. Generally speaking, the susceptibility to teratogenesis 
decreases as differentiation and organogenesis proceed. This 
is because, during early stages of development, embryonic 
cells are morphologically and biochemically similar, and they 
have the potential to become nearly any type of cell in the 
body. As development proceeds, however, developmental deci-
sions are made concerning the fate of each cell. Thus, at later 
periods of gestation, groups of cells will have become dissimi-
lar and will respond differently to environmental challenges. 
This means that, as the embryo grows and matures, the popu-
lation of cells that is susceptible to a particular toxicant will 
comprise a relatively smaller proportion of the embryo. This 
also implies that when organs are first laid down (organogen-
esis), they undergo their “critical period” during which they 
are most susceptible to damage by environmental agents. As 
gestation proceeds and the tissues of organs become more dif-
ferentiated and the embryo’s susceptibility to grossly observed 
developmental toxicity thus tends to decrease. This led Wilson 
[7,8] to generalize that the time at which an agent will act on 
an embryo determines which tissues will be susceptible to the 
effects of the agent; consequently, susceptibility to a particular 
agent varies during gestation.
Thus, as embryos mature through progressive differentia-
tion, increasingly higher doses of a teratogen are necessary to 
elicit a malformation. This means that, as organ systems and 
the embryo progressively mature, they become increasingly 
resistant to teratogenesis. Importantly, for an adverse effect to 
be manifest in a developing organ, the concentration of the 
toxicant at that site must exceed a threshold, which is deter-
mined by the state of maturity of the organ rudiment. Below 
the threshold concentration, gross malformations will not be 
induced. Gestationally, more mature organs have a higher 
threshold than the same organ rudiment at a younger stage.
Most critical events in organ formation have been completed 
by the early fetal period. Nevertheless, developmental toxi-
cants can still adversely impact the fetus. During the remain-
der of pre- and postnatal development (PPND), progressive 
growth and functional maturation of each organ system con-
tinues. While the likelihood of gross malformations becomes 
less at this time, malformations of late-developing organs (e. 
g., kidneys, genitalia, brain), altered tissue differentiation, 
growth retardation, and postnatal functional deficits (includ-
ing neurobehavioral problems) may be caused by perturbation 
of events after the period of major organogenesis and should 
not be ignored.
Interestingly, even at high doses, exposure to toxicants 
during the earliest developmental period (from fertilization 
through blastocyst formation) typically does not cause mal-
formations. The apparent resistance of very young embryos 
is likely to occur because the zygote cells have not begun to 
differentiate and, therefore, are still pluripotent. All cells in 
the embryo at this time can become any embryonic cell type. 
Consequently, any affected cells can be replaced by surviv-
ing cells, thereby permitting normal development to proceed. 
Although the destruction of a number of undifferentiated cells 
may not result in a malformation, there does appear to be a 
critical limit beyond which damaging even nonspecialized 
cells cannot be tolerated if the embryo is to survive; if that 
critical limit is exceeded, the embryo will die.
38.4.4  Response Threshold
Adverse effects occur when a sufficiently high chemical con-
centration is present at the target tissue [54]. The dose required 
to establish the lowest effective concentration is considered 
the threshold dose. At doses below the threshold, the chemi-
cal concentration at the target tissue is insufficient to elicit an 
adverse response. In the case of developmental toxicity stud-
ies, the target tissue is the embryo, but dosing occurs to the 
pregnant animal. Consequently, it is important to consider 
the route and dosage to the maternal animal, especially if one 
wants to extrapolate findings to assess potential human risk.
Inorganic arsenic can be used to illustrate the complications 
in such assessments. A series of laboratory studies [55, 56, 57, 
58] were undertaken to determine if environmental exposure of 
pregnant women to inorganic arsenic was a risk for neural tube 
defects. In the experiments, mice, rats, or rabbits were dosed 
by relevant (oral and inhalational) exposure routes using a 
dose-response design. No arsenic-related structural malforma-
tions (including neural tube defects) were reported after either 
oral or inhalation exposure, although the highest dose levels 
caused severe maternal toxicity (including maternal deaths). 
Among surviving litters, the highest doses did cause low mean 
fetal weights and resorptions. In experiments using intraperi-
toneal injection of inorganic arsenic into rats and mice, higher 
doses were tolerated, and neural tube defects were observed 
in fetuses. When the results of these studies were integrated 
with the voluminous amount of ­published literature on the 
developmental effects of arsenic [59], the authors showed 
that neural tube defects (and other malformations) occurred 
only when maternal blood concentrations of inorganic arsenic 
exceeded a theoretical teratogenic threshold that was attained

38.4.5  An Overlooked Concept: 
Inverted Yolk Sac Placenta
The experimental animals used to assess potential develop-
mental toxicity in safety tests have much shorter gestations 
than humans, averaging 22 days (rat) and 31 days (rabbit) com-
pared to 266 days (human). The short gestational periods of 
experimental animals mean that developmental schedules 
are compressed, and all aspects of development, including 
the rapid formation of mechanisms to exchange nutrients, 
gases, and waste products with the maternal system, must 
occur much more quickly. The urgency to acquire a nutrient 
exchange mechanism in short-gestation species is driven by 
the fact that zygotes survive by diffusion during transit to the 
uterus. Remarkably, although much of the early development 
is completed during the next 10 days or so, the definitive pla-
centae in these species do not begin functioning until approxi-
mately gestational day (GD) 11–12.5. By this relatively late 
time in gestation, many significant developmental milestones 
have passed [42]. To meet their nutritional and metabolic needs 
during the early post-implantation period, these short-gesta-
tion species form a structure derived from the embryonic yolk 
sac, called the inverted yolk sac placenta.
Human embryos never develop an inverted yolk sac pla-
centa. The anatomic differences in rat, rabbit, and human 
placentation, the factors that influence transfer of substances 
between mother and offspring, and whether the responses of 
these species are representative of the human are described 
and assessed elsewhere [37, 39]. Briefly, early embryos of 
humans, rodents, and rabbits develop as a hollow sphere that is 
transected by a plate of cells (the embryo proper). The cavity 
above the embryo is the amniotic cavity, and the larger cavity 
below is the yolk sac cavity. In rodents and rabbits (but not 
humans), the yolk sac expands quickly and comes to surround 
the developing embryo proper. The configuration is much like 
that of a balloon surrounding a fist that has been pushed into 
it. The layer of balloon touching the fist is the visceral layer; 
the outer layer is the parietal layer. The parietal layer contacts 
the uterine wall but does not invade it. Shortly thereafter, the 
yolk sac cavity deflates, and the parietal layer of the yolk sac 
degenerates, resulting in apposition of the visceral yolk sac 
wall to the uterine luminal lining to form a temporary early 
placenta (Figure 38.5). It is this organization in the mature 
state (visceral yolk sac wall as the membrane that apposes the 
uterine wall) that leads to the description of this structure as an 
inverted visceral yolk sac placenta.
Nutrient-rich secretions from the glands of the uterine epi-
thelium are transported across the inverted visceral yolk sac 
placenta by pinocytosis, digestion by lysosomal enzymes, and 
diffusion of the breakdown products across the epithelium.

transfer nutrients and metabolic waste efficiently. Thus, there 
are substances such as trypan blue [61] that can produce mal-

sac placenta, but which will not affect species like humans 
and dogs that lack the inverted yolk sac placenta. Nearly half 
a century later this concept remains relevant as was demon-
strated clearly by a series of experiments using a hemoglobin-
based oxygen carrier that caused malformations in rat embryos 
[62] but was without effect in dogs [63], a species that lacks an 
inverted yolk sac placenta.
38.5  Test Guidelines (TGs) for Chemicals
The purpose of this section is to provide an overview of test-
ing guidelines for reproductive and developmental toxicity 
studies of chemicals such as pesticides, industrial chemicals, 
and other non-pharmaceutical chemicals for which there is 
a concern for human exposure. The testing guidelines most 
widely used internationally are promulgated by the European 
Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development 
(OECD), representing 37 member countries in Europe, the 
Americas (including the United States), Asia, and Oceania. 
The United States Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) 
also has testing guidelines for reproductive and developmental 
toxicity developed by the Office of Prevention, Pesticides, and 
Toxic Substances (OPPTS). These guidelines can be grouped 
into three broad categories: (1) prenatal developmental toxic-
ity [127, 128], (2) one- or two-generation reproductive toxicity 
[129, 130, 128, 131], and (3) reproductive toxicity screening 
tests [132, 133, 134, 135] (Tables 38.1 and 38.2).

38.5.3  Reproductive Toxicity Screening Tests
The OECD 421 guideline is for a reproductive/developmental 
toxicity screening test designed to provide initial information 
on the possible effects on reproduction and offspring devel-
opment from exposure of male and female rats for 2 weeks 
before mating, up to 2 weeks of mating, as well as during the 
gestation period and the first 2 weeks of the 3-week lactation 
period (Figure 38.8). The OECD 422 guideline combines the 
OECD 421 reproductive/developmental toxicity screening test 
with repeated-dose toxicity and neurobehavioral evaluations 
(Figure 38.8).
The OECD 421 and 422 guidelines are similar with regard 
to the number of animals, length of exposure, and reproductive 
toxicity endpoints; the OECD 422 also includes assessments 
of neurobehavior, clinical pathology, and pathology of non-
reproductive organs in parental animals. Because the OECD 
422 consists of both repeated-dose toxicity and reproductive 
evaluations, it can serve as an alternative to the OECD 421 and 
the OECD 407 repeated-dose toxicity study [140]. Generally, 
the OECD 421/422 studies are performed in one species, 
and the rat is preferred. The appropriate route of administra-
tion (RoA) is selected based on the expected route of human 
exposure, which is most commonly oral with administration 
by intubation (oral gavage), although administration in the feed 
or drinking water or other routes such as dermal or inhalation 
may be more relevant to human exposure. Dose levels may be 
based on information from acute toxicity tests or repeated-dose 
studies and should be tolerated in pregnant animals and nursing 
pups. For both the OECD 421 and 422 studies, 10 animals per 
group is recommended; however, to select 10 regularly cycling 
females (4–5-day cycles) at the start of the study, an additional 
2–3 females per group is suggested. An outline based on the 
OECD 421 and 422 guidelines is provided in Table 38.6.
The OECD 421 or 422 can be used to meet data require-
ments for specific chemicals under REACH or as a DRF 
study for the more extensive OECD 443 reproductive toxicity 
study. The OECD 421 and 422 are helpful when used as actual 
screens. Still, these study designs should not be used as api-
cal studies for hazard identification when there is a significant 
potential for human exposure; they lack specificity and sensi-
tivity due to the small group sizes, short dosing duration, and 
limited reproductive endpoints evaluated [141]. One significant 
limitation is that offspring are not exposed or evaluated after 
weaning to detect postnatal manifestations of prenatal expo-
sure or effects that may be induced during postnatal exposure.

The EOGRTS was adopted as an OECD Test Guideline (TG 
443) in July 2011 and updated in June 2018. In 2015, the study 
design was declared acceptable to fulfill the information require-
ments for reproductive toxicity assessments under REACH, 
a European Union regulation covering the production and use 
of various chemical substances and assessing their impact on 
human health and the environment. Acceptance of this TG 
under REACH thus increased its scope beyond plant protection 
products. While the TG provides a detailed description of the 
study’s operational conduct, various designs, triggered decisions, 
and endocrine endpoints are covered under OECD Guidance 
Documents 117 and 151. It should be noted, however, that inter-
nal triggers do not apply for chemicals tested under REACH or 
for classification according to the CLP (Classification, Labelling 
and Packaging) regulation and are applicable only to testing con-
ducted in North America and Canada..
Although guidelines for the conduct of EOGRTS were released 
only by the OECD, this study design is nonetheless widely accepted 
by reproductive health authorities across the globe, with a few nota-
ble exceptions at the current time. The general study design (Figure 
38.9) includes a 2-week premating regimen for both males and 
females in the parental generation. Following mating and gestation, 
F1 offspring are allocated to three cohorts before or at weaning and 
evaluated for effects on reproductive and developmental endpoints 
and the developing nervous and immune systems. Based on treat-
ment-related or equivocal effects observed in the F0 generation or 
F1 offspring, registrants may elect to extend the study to include 
the breeding of animals in the reproductive cohort to generate an 
F2 generation (often referred to as an F2 extension). Certain regula-
tory authorities, such as the ECHA, often request a modified study 
design, which includes a 10-week premating regimen based on the 
rationale that the 2-week premating exposure is insufficient for ade-
quate assessment of male reproductive effects. If a 10-week premat-
ing regimen is included in the study, an F2 extension is not required. 
Conversely, if the 10-week premating regimen is excluded, then 
breeding the F1 generation to yield F2 pups is considered standard 
practice. It is not the intent of this chapter to describe the OECD 
443 guideline in detail, and the reader is directed to review the pub-
lished guideline as well as published manuscripts on the topic [107, 
108, 109, 110]. Some of the essential study design elements are dis-
cussed in detail below.
38.5.4.2  Essential Study Design 
Considerations for EOGRTS
38.5.4.2.1  Route of Administration
For studies conducted on non-pharmaceutical substances, 
the RoA is based on the most relevant route of human expo-
sure. Although OECD TG 443 is designed for the dietary 
route of exposure, which is thought to best mimic human 
exposure for agricultural chemicals (e.g., pesticides and 
food additives), other routes may be considered when the 
diet is not expected to be the most relevant route of human 
exposure. Typically, the test substance is mixed in standard 
laboratory animal feed (either ground [milled] or pelleted 
feed to enable foraging or chewing behaviors in rodents). 
For non-nutritional test substances, no more than 5% of the 
feed should be replaced with the test substance [111, 112] 
because further substitution can lead to nutritional deficien-
cies. Palatability of the dietary admixes should be assessed 
in a small number of animals because particularly odor-
ous compounds (e.g., fragrance materials or strong organic 
chemicals) may cause reduced food consumption resulting 
in lower body weights, which can confound the identifica-
tion of actual test-substance–related effects. Compounds 
with low odor thresholds are also often highly volatile, 
which further complicates the use of treated diets. Unless 
a new diet is provided daily, volatile test substance con-
centrations tend to decrease over time, resulting in incon-
sistent exposure. The same rules and recommendations 
for dietary studies can also be applied to drinking water 
studies. However, dehydration often has more severe effects 
than reduced food consumption.

As an alternative to dietary exposure for most test sub-
stances, oral gavage administration in aqueous or non-aqueous 
vehicles can be used. In general, systemic doses achieved after 
bolus gavage are frequently higher than those attainable with 
ad libitum exposure via the dietary or drinking water routes 
of administration. It is important to recognize that a bolus 
dose can elicit a different toxicological response than might 
be observed after ad libitum (24-hour) exposure. Thus, careful 
consideration of the RoA is necessary based on each test sub-
stance’s unique characteristics and previously available data.
Inhalation is another route that can be used if relevant to 
human exposure (for example, for a pesticide inhaled when 
applied to crops). However, depending upon the physico-
chemical properties of the test substance, the methods used 
for inhalation exposure may vary. For example, for an aerosol-
ized fungicide, nose-only inhalation should be considered to 
reduce the deposition of aerosol droplets on other parts of the 
body, leading to both dermal and oral exposure in rodents via 
grooming. In contrast, for an easily volatilized substance, rel-
evant human exposure might occur primarily via its gaseous 
form; therefore, either nose-only or whole-body animal expo-
sure may be appropriate. In such a case, it may be necessary 
to determine the extent of systemic exposure via both whole-
body and nose-only inhalation to select the inhalation route 
that most appropriately mimics human exposure.
Regardless of the chosen route of exposure, it is essential 
to demonstrate whether pups are exposed continuously to the 
test substance via maternal milk during lactation. For test sub-
stances that are not (or poorly) excreted in maternal milk, it 
may be necessary to directly dose pups beginning as early as 
postnatal day (PND) 4 to assure continuous exposure [113]. On 
dietary or drinking water studies, pups begin direct exposure 
as early as PND 14, when their eyes are open, and they are 
mobile and strong enough to access the maternal food jar or 
water source. In these studies, the duration of direct dosing 
of pups during the preweaning period needs only cover the 
10 days between PND 4 and 14. In contrast, in oral gavage 
studies, pups usually only begin direct dosing at weaning; 
however, the dosing scenario is complicated in the case of 
poorly excreted test substances. Due to early consumption 
of feed by pups and poor elimination, the systemic offspring 
dose encompasses practically the entire preweaning period. 
The decision to direct dose pups should be based on analytical 
confirmation of the presence or absence of the test substance 
or its active metabolites in maternal milk and/or in pup blood 
during the preweaning period. Due to the immaturity of the 
pups, direct dosing can elicit offspring toxicity and/or changes 
in biomarkers that may not be notable in adult animals. Thus, 
careful consideration of the advantages and disadvantages of 
direct dosing is necessary. These determinations are best made 
on DRF studies before initiating the EOGRTS.
38.5.4.2.2  Number of Animals
In the parental (F0) generation, each test and control group 
should contain enough mating pairs to produce a minimum 
of 20 litters per group. However, given that either one, two, or 
all three F1 cohorts may be included in an EOGRTS, the ini-
tial number of animals can vary. The decision is based on the 
strain of rats being used and the anticipated sex ratios based on 
the laboratory’s historical control data (HCD). Table 38.7 pres-
ents the number of pups per sex required for each assessment 
cohort. In addition, the TG OECD 443 and associated guidance 
documents indicate that at least three pups/sex/group (where 
possible based on litter sex distribution) from at least 20 litters 
per group, should be selected to assess the attainment of sexual 
developmental landmarks – vaginal opening (VO) (patency) and 
separation of the prepuce from the glans penis (balanoprepu-
tial separation (BPS)). Depending on whether one, two, or all 
three F1 cohorts are included in an EOGRTS, surplus animals 
may need to be maintained on the study until the attainment 
of landmarks of sexual development (sometimes referred to as 
Cohort 1C). If these surplus animals are not selected for addi-
tional assessments, they can be removed from the study around 
PND 50–52. Table 38.7 shows that 40 pups/sex/group (2 pups/
sex/litter) are always selected for Cohort 1 (A and B). Thus, 
surplus pups are always necessary on an EOGRTS limited to 
reproductive toxicity assessments. The inclusion of Cohorts 2 
and 3 may reduce the need for surplus pups. However, the need 
may change depending on initial F0 group sizes and the strain 
of rats being used (Sprague Dawley rats, in general, have more 
consistent sex ratios than Han Wistar). With test materials that 
may impact sex ratios, or when test-substance–related mater-
nal/neonatal mortality is observed, maintenance of extra pups 
may become necessary. A starting group size of ∈30 F0 rats/sex/
group for Han Wistar rats and ∈25 F0 rats/sex/group for Sprague 
Dawley rats is thus recommended.

38.5.4.2.3  Triggered Decisions
OECD Guidance Document 117 lists the triggers that necessi-
tate breeding the F1 Cohort 1B animals to produce an F2 gen-
eration. These are test-substance–related or equivocal effects on 
(a) F0 fertility or fecundity (F1 litter sizes), (b) F1 postnatal sur-
vival, (c) F1 litter parameters or birth weights, (d) F1 reproduc-
tive developmental landmarks, or (e) any other equivocal effects 
on F1 parameters or unusual control data compared to historical 
background. Generally, effects on F0 estrous cyclicity, sperm 
parameters, or clinical/anatomic pathology do not trigger an F2 
extension because these endpoints are assessed for the F0 and F1 
Cohort 1A, which comprises littermates of Cohort 1B.

that examination of 3 pups/sex/litter for landmarks of sexual 
development is mandatory and is based on previous OECD

as well as several published manuscripts [114, 115, 116], state 
that the detection of structural anomalies depends not only on 
the numbers of litters examined, but also on the numbers of 
pups examined in each litter because all pups in a litter do not 
respond identically to any given test substance. Consequently, 
increasing the number of pups examined/litter dramatically 
enhances the detection of low incidence effects. In addition, 
to ensure analysis and interpretation of study results as well 
as comprehensive regulatory assessment, ECHA mandated 
the following: (1) F1 splenic immunophenotyping be con-
ducted in Cohort A (irrespective of inclusion in Cohort 3), (2) 
histopathology be performed on Cohort B, and (3) identical 
evaluations be conducted in F1 and F2 pups (if F2 extension 
was triggered). Additionally, read-down instructions following 
identification of target tissues in F0, and F1 and appropriate 
descriptions of assay and laboratory methods were included.
The most critical issue raised in the July 2021 document was 
dose selection. Based on the initial review, ECHA stated that 
dose levels that are too low, and hence fail to demonstrate clear 
evidence of an effect on sexual function and fertility, could 
result in the regulatory rejection of studies. The high dose 
must be selected to induce evidence of adverse effects, without 
mortality and severe pain and suffering (criteria established 
in OECD Guidance Document 19). In all instances, the high-
est dose must be selected to demonstrate whether CLP criteria 
for the most severe hazard category (Reproductive Toxicity 
Category 1B) for sexual function and fertility and develop-
mental toxicity apply. The documents emphasize that the aim 
is to demonstrate whether the criteria for very serious concern 
regarding endocrine disruption apply. According to the CLP 
regulation, a substance classified as a reproductive toxicant 
shows clear evidence of an adverse effect on sexual function 
and fertility, or on development, in the absence of other toxic 
effects, or if occurring together with other toxic effects, the 
adverse effect on reproduction is considered not to be a second-
ary nonspecific consequence of other toxic effects. If neither 
of these criteria (reproductive toxicity or endocrine disruption) 
is established, the limit dose (1,000 mg/kg/day) must be used, 
or the highest feasible dose without mortality and severe pain 
and suffering (i.e., the maximal tolerated dose [MTD]). While 
these instructions seem straightforward, they differ from the 
expectations of other regulatory health authorities and have 
the potential to impact mutual acceptance of data across vari-
ous regions of the world. Although ECHA has reiterated that 
this document does not reflect a change in agency policies, the 
goal now appears to be to set the maximum dose as high as 
possible to demonstrate whether CLP criteria for reproduc-
tive toxicity can be applied. A conservative way of addressing 
the concerns raised by ECHA is to utilize DRF studies to aid 
in selecting dose levels to establish an MTD and push doses 
as high as possible to demonstrate whether CLP criteria for 
reproductive toxicity can be applied to the test substance. Once 
the highest feasible dose is established, a descending sequence 
of doses can be selected for the pivotal study based on direct 
multiples or half-log increments to establish a dose-response 
and a no observable adverse effect level (NOAEL).
38.5.5  Endocrine Disruption Screening Assays
Under the US EPA’s Endocrine Disruptor Screening Program, 
a 2-tiered screening program exists for evaluating and iden-
tifying active endocrine compounds. Tier 1 includes 11 total 
assays (5 in vitro [cell-system–based] and 6 in vivo [live ani-
mal]) designed to identify compounds/chemicals that have the 
potential to interact with the estrogen, androgen, or thyroid 
pathways. Four of the in vivo assays are conducted in mam-
malian species and include the Hershberger, uterotrophic, 
and male/female pubertal development and thyroid function 
assays. The fish short-term reproduction and the (amphibian) 
metamorphosis assays make up the remainder of the in vivo 
battery. Two in vivo studies are conducted in female rats: the 
uterotrophic and the pubertal assay.
38.5.5.1  Uterotrophic Assay
A uterotrophic assay is recommended in guidelines published 
by the US EPA and the OECD for test substances of unknown 
estrogenic potential. This assay utilizes female rats with an 
immature or non-functional hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal 
(HPG) axis due to either sexual immaturity or surgical 
removal of the ovaries. It also evaluates their responsiveness to 
exogenously administered estrogens along with substances of 
unknown estrogenic potential.
Due to low circulating estrogen levels, the uterine tissues 
(in both immature and ovariectomized females) are quies-
cent, and the administration of an exogenous estrogen (e.g., 
17−-ethinylestradiol) elicits an estrogen-dependent uterotro-
phic response, measured as an increase in uterine weights. The 
increase in organ weight is a combination of fluid imbibition by 
the uterine epithelium and a secondary hypertrophic response 
of the uterine tissues. Although the assay is most commonly 
conducted to assess potential estrogenic activity, the guide-
line also includes methods to evaluate antagonism; that is, 
the ability of a test substance to diminish/deplete an estrogen-
mediated uterotrophic response. In an antiestrogenic assay, 
an antiestrogenic compound is administered in combination 
with 17−-ethinylestradiol to demonstrate antagonistic activity, 
measured as a reversal of the estrogen-dependent uterotrophic 
response (i.e., a reduction in uterine weights) when compared to 
17−-ethinylestradiol alone.
Both the adult ovariectomized and the immature intact 
female rats present comparable sensitivity and reproducibil-
ity in response to estrogens [117]. However, the OECD and 
US EPA differ in their preference for model (and RoA) for the 
uterotrophic assay. OECD prefers the immature intact female 
model due to animal welfare and surgical intervention con-
cerns; US EPA prefers ovariectomized adult females [118]. 
Each method presents drawbacks, as discussed below.
The immature rat model poses technical challenges given 
the smaller organ sizes, making dissection more challeng-
ing. In addition, in immature females, there exists only a nar-
row window of maximum sensitivity and responsiveness to

1918
Hayes’ Principles and Methods of Toxicology
estrogens (limited to the period between 17–25 days of age) 
when the uterus is in a developmentally quiescent period. 
Before this age, the uterus is relatively insensitive to estrogens. 
It has high circulating levels of −-fetoprotein (AFP), which 
binds estradiol, thereby reducing sensitivity of the assay [119]. 
Thus, the window of estrogen sensitivity in immature females 
is limited by the presence of AFP at a younger age. In con-
trast, the onset of puberty increases endogenous estrogens and 
a corresponding increase in uterine weight at older ages. The 
presence of littermates and a lactating dam necessary to main-
tain the normal health and welfare of immature rats may also 
confound the study data or its interpretation [119]. Lastly, the 
immature model may use a larger number of animals (versus 
the adult ovariectomized design), depending upon how animals 
are selected for the study. Because littermates are not statisti-
cally independent, and no littermates are assigned to the same 
group, a larger cohort of litters is needed to fulfill the needs of 
the study since only half the litter (females only) are usable.
In contrast, the adult ovariectomized model is more sensitive 
to detecting estrogen-responsiveness due to the lack of endog-
enous estrogens following ovariectomy. Surgical removal of 
the ovaries also provides greater specificity, as false-positive 
signals are unlikely to be produced by substances that may 
interact with other elements of an otherwise intact HPG axis. 
The successful conduct of the adult assay requires that suf-
ficient time be allowed for regression of estrogen-responsive 
tissues and circulating estradiol levels following ovariectomy. 
The most common practice is to allow 10–14 days post-surgery 
for regression. In addition, the animals must be monitored for 
estrous cyclicity to ensure that complete ovariectomy is per-
formed. Females that show evidence of cornified epithelial 
cells (i.e., vaginal estrus) should be excluded from the study 
because this implies the presence of ovarian remnants, hence 
the presence of circulating estrogens.
The study design for the uterotrophic assay calls for three 
days of dose administration for both 17−-ethinylestradiol and 
the test substance. In a normally cycling rat, the duration of 
the estrous cycle is 4–5 days. Therefore, the 3-day treatment 
duration is similar to an intact rat’s expected response time 
to increases in estradiol that produce the normal estrogenic 
response in uterine tissues [117]. Longer durations of dosing 
(i.e., up to 7 days) are sometimes used to improve detection sen-
sitivity for weakly estrogenic substances. Marty and O’Connor 
[118] discussed selection of the RoA of 17−-ethinylestradiol 
and the test substance in detail, particularly regarding OECD- 
and EPA-preferred routes and the benefits and disadvantages 
of subcutaneous injection versus oral gavage administration.
Notwithstanding which animal model and route of exposure 
are selected, verification of laboratory proficiency is required 
by both EPA and OECD guidelines. Laboratory proficiency 
can be established by conducting an initial baseline positive 
control (BPC) study to demonstrate assay performance, surgi-
cal success, tissue dissection proficiency, and the responsive-
ness of the test system. It is recommended that the BPC study 
be repeated should there be any change in assay conditions 
(e.g., change in laboratory diets, 17−-ethinylestradiol supplier, 
or change in laboratory personnel). The BPC study should 
include a minimum of four doses of the positive control to 
establish a dose-response and drift in laboratory procedures.
If a uterotrophic response is not evident in a BPC or a con-
current positive control group, the experimental conditions 
must be examined for potential confounding variables. It is 
important to note that the uterus is uniquely sensitive to estro-
gens, and any source of weak estrogens can confound the data 
and data interpretation. Separate BPC studies should be con-
ducted for each strain of rat or mouse used for uterotrophic 
assays. Laboratory diets should be analyzed for detectable lev-
els of isoflavones, as dietary phytoestrogens have been shown 
to have weak estrogenic activity in both in vitro and in vivo 
assays. Phytoestrogen levels >325–350 ōg/g total genistein 
equivalents (genistein, daidzein, and coumestrol) can reduce 
the responsiveness of weak estrogen agonists in a uterotro-
phic assay. However, this observation was limited to the intact 
immature rat model only [120].
Most laboratories house rodents in solid-bottom clear plas-
tic “shoe-box” caging containing contact bedding to allow for 
foraging and/or nesting behaviors in rodents. While these hus-
bandry practices are of little consequence in standard safety 
assessment studies, plastic caging and water bottles as well 
as animal contact bedding, often wood shavings or corncobs, 
can serve as sources of weak estrogenic chemical compounds 
or phytoestrogens such as those discussed above. It is prudent 
to ensure that animal caging used for uterotrophic assays be 
constructed with non-leachable heat-resistant materials (such 
as polyphenylsulfone) and plastic water bottles be replaced 
with glass or other inert materials. Contact bedding made with 
aromatic soft woods [121] and corncobs [122] should also be 
avoided due to their potential to influence pup survival and 
interact with the endocrine system. Instead, heat-treated hard 
wood such as Aspen, which is expected to be low in overall 
phytoestrogen content, is recommended.
Positive results in a uterotrophic assay (a statistically signifi-
cant increase in uterine weight) should generally be interpreted 
as evidence for estrogenic potential in vivo. A positive result is 
further supported by a dose-response. In general, for an assay 
to be considered acceptable, blotted vehicle control uterine 
weights should be less than 0.09% of body weight for imma-
ture female rats and less than 0.04% for ovariectomized young 
adult females. If the observed values exceed those above, the 
experimental conditions should be examined for confounding 
variables, as discussed above. Evidence of a positive response 
in a uterotrophic assay leads to further actions under the OECD 
Conceptual Framework for the Testing and Assessment of 
Endocrine Disrupting Chemicals. OECD Guidance Document 
150 provides various scenarios and combinations of results 
from existing in vitro and in vivo studies. It offers possible 
conclusions that can be drawn from the assay data and possible 
next steps to strengthen the Weight of Evidence (WoE) about 
the endocrine activity of the test substance.
38.5.5.2  Pubertal Assay
The purpose of the female pubertal assay is to identify test 
substances that can interact with the endocrine system, either 
by influencing pubertal development or function of the thy-
roid gland. Unlike a uterotrophic assay, this assay utilizes 
intact juvenile peripubertal female rats. A test substance 
with the potential to perturb the HPG axis via anti-thyroid,

1919
Female Reproductive and Developmental Toxicology
anti-estrogenic, or estrogenic activity (including alterations 
in receptor binding or steroidogenesis) may show a positive 
response in a pubertal assay. Juvenile female rats (weanlings, 
generally 22–42 days of age) are assigned to treatment groups 
and exposed daily for approximately 3 weeks until the attain-
ment of vaginal patency. Females are evaluated for VO, time 
to the first estrus after the day of VO, and any deviations in 
the pattern or regularity of the estrous cycle [123]. At termina-
tion, blood samples are collected for clinical chemistry and 
serum thyroid hormones (T4 and TSH). Samples should be 
collected at the same time each day to minimize diurnal varia-
tions. Reproductive and endocrine organs are weighed and 
preserved for histopathological examination. As in the utero-
trophic assay, the uterus is weighed wet and blotted to detect a 
uterotrophic response (measured as an increased or decreased 
uterine weight). The reader is directed to the published TG for 
details on the assay conditions and performance criteria [124].
While a BPC study is not required for laboratories perform-
ing pubertal assays, several capabilities are required before 
successful conduct is possible. Analytical assays for serum 
thyroid hormones must be validated. Evaluation of thyroid his-
topathology requires establishing a five-point scoring system 
for both follicular cell height and colloid amount, similar to 
that published by Stump et al. [123]. Lastly, because the puber-
tal assay is among the few published guidelines that require 
histopathological evaluation of the developing female repro-
ductive tract, pathologists must be familiar with the typical 
appearance of the developing ovary and uterus to allow for the 
identification of normal versus delayed reproductive develop-
ment and the determination of test-substance–specific lesions 
[125, 126].
To ensure limited exposure to environmental phytoestro-
gens, assay conditions like the uterotrophic assay must be 
maintained in the female pubertal assay. Laboratory diets and 
bedding should have low phytoestrogen content. Caging and 
water bottles should be made with non-leachable plastics or 
other inert materials.
Interpretation of pubertal assay data is challenging due to the 
animals being tested during a dynamic and sensitive develop-
mental window when integrated function of the endocrine sys-
tem is necessary for normal development; this is discussed in 
greater detail in Stump et al. [123]. In a female pubertal assay, 
endpoints include initial and final body weights, age at attain-
ment of VO, age at first estrus, patterns of estrous cyclicity, 
including the stage of the cycle at termination, organ weights 
(ovaries, wet and blotted uteri, and thyroid glands), serum 
thyroid hormones, and histopathology. Several endpoints are 
qualitative observations (estrous cycle staging, attainment of 
VO, etc.) that show considerable variability across personnel 
and testing laboratories. Some endpoints are confounded by 
inherent physiological variability. For example, termination of 
females at 42 days of age without regard of the stage of the 
estrous cycle results in organ weight differences within each 
group of females based on the stage of the estrous cycle at the 
time of termination, which may influence the determination 
of treatment-related differences in organ weights. The intro-
duction of scaling schemes for histopathological data appears 
to have improved objectivity in histopathological assessments. 
However, qualitative assessments such as histopathology do 
not conform well with numerical scaling. Changes in thyroid 
histopathology are best interpreted in conjunction with thyroid 
gland weights and thyroid hormone levels. Nonetheless, con-
siderable biological variability exists in control hormone lev-
els; thus, the group sizes on these studies must be large enough 
to allow for meaningful data interpretation. While the EPA 
guideline provides specific performance criteria for vehicle 
control animals, interlaboratory studies during the pubertal 
assay validation program showed that these criteria were dif-
ficult to meet for all endpoints. It has since been recommended 
that the EPA reevaluate published criteria; however, no update 
to the guideline has been issued. The accepted practice has 
been to comment on the percentage of endpoints that meet 
published criteria (especially organ weights and reproductive 
developmental landmarks) and report assay results regardless 
of what percentage of the endpoints met the published criteria.
The OECD has not published an official pubertal assay 
guideline. However, OECD Guidance Document 150 refers 
to the EPA guideline under its Conceptual Framework for 
Testing and Assessment of Endocrine Disrupting Chemicals. 
The pubertal assay is listed under Level 4, which pertains to in 
vivo assays that can be used to provide data regarding adverse 
effects on specific endocrine-relevant endpoints. The general 
approach outlined in the guidance document is similar to what 
was described for the uterotrophic assay. Data from existing in 
vitro and in vivo studies should be evaluated for each test sub-
stance to determine possible next steps to strengthen the WoE 
about its endocrine activity. Evidence of a positive response in

38.6  TGs for Pharmaceuticals
Evaluation of new drug candidates for DART is described 
in a series of guidelines set by the International Council for 
Harmonisation (ICH), a joint regulatory-industry body formed 
in 1990. ICH S5 (Detection of Toxicity to Reproduction for 
Human Pharmaceuticals) is the primary guideline related to 
DART, and it recently completed its third revision in 2020 [64]. 
Other safety guidances with critical inputs for DART testing 
for special situations include ICH S6(R1): Preclinical safety 
evaluation of biotechnology-derived pharmaceuticals [65] and 
ICH S9: Nonclinical evaluation for anticancer pharmaceuti-
cals [66, 67]. Lastly, the timing of guideline DART studies 
related to the progression of clinical trials and combination 
toxicity studies is outlined in ICH M3(R2): Nonclinical safety 
studies for the conduct of human clinical trials for pharmaceu-
ticals [68, 69].
Outside the ICH guidelines, region-specific guidance doc-
uments inform sponsors about topics not addressed in the 
ICH guidelines. Two of these, issued by the US FDA, offer 
recommendations to DART testing strategies for scenarios 
related to metabolite qualification [70] and combination 
drug development [71]. Similarly, the European Medicines

38.6.1  ICH S5(R3)
The primary guideline for DART drug development, ICH 
S5(R3), describes the phases of mammalian reproduction 
that must be assessed for potential risk related to pharma-
ceutical exposure (Table 38.9) [64]. It suggests in vivo stud-
ies to evaluate each phase but leaves room for flexibility 
in study strategy based on the individual characteristics of 
each drug program.
The reproductive life cycle is broken into six stages for in 
vivo characterization, termed Stages A–F, with three pro-
posed in vivo study designs. In toto, these three study types 
thoroughly assess a molecule for DART risk and represent the 
most likely testing strategy for pharmaceuticals, outside those 
for advanced cancer treatment [66] and biopharmaceuticals 
that are only cross-reactive in NHPs [65]. These studies include 
the fertility and early embryonic development (FEED) study 
for Stages A&B; the embryo-fetal development (EFD) study for 
Stages C&D; and the PPND study covering Stages C–F.
For most drugs, data from EFD studies are required to (1) 
support the enrollment of women of child-bearing potential 
(WOCBP) in outpatient clinical trials, and (2) to provide informa-
tion to investigators and clinical trial participants in the event of 
unintended pregnancy (exception: EFD data can be deferred to 
support Phase 3 trials in the US). The FEED study is required by 
the end of Phase 2, and the PPND study must be completed before 
submission for marketing authorization (Table 38.10) DART data 
is also used to guide decisions about contraption and pregnancy 
testing strategy during clinical trials..
ICH S5(R3) also includes multiple guiding principles appli-
cable to all DART study types related to sound study design 
and robust evaluation of the clinical candidate, as outlined in 
Table 38.11.

evidence of mating or for at least 2 weeks in the absence of

presence of sperm in the vaginal lavage sample, or the pres-

day because mating is limited to the dark period. In contrast, 
mice are only paired overnight and separated during the day

many FEED study designs continue dosing through GD 7 to 
ensure adequate overlap with the EFD studies, which initiate 
dosing on GD 6 in rodents. However, the last dose during early 
gestation can be adjusted as required for drugs with longer 
half-lives to ensure drug exposure through implantation with-

FEED study with once-a-day dosing, given the variability of 
the duration of the cohabitation period, individual females on 
study will be dosed a minimum of 22 days and up to a maxi-

after 3 weeks of cohabitation.
Maternal parameters evaluated throughout the study include 
(1) daily viability measurements and clinical signs and (2) 
weekly measurements of body weight and food consumption. 
Due to the confounding presence of the male rat, maternal 
food consumption is not evaluated during cohabitation.

maternal systemic exposure, qualify major metabolites, iden-

the risk assessment/clinical significance for any reproductive 
effects. However, these data can be extrapolated from general 
toxicity studies in the same species/strain.
Laparohysterectomy endpoints from presumed pregnant 
females in the FEED study are assessed at mid-gestation, typi-
cally on GD 13–15 for rodents. This developmental stage of 
pregnancy permits visual assessment of implantation numbers 
and embryonic viability. The uterus should be opened to allow 
a thorough evaluation of embryonic status. The pregnancy sta-
tus of apparently non-pregnant females should be confirmed 
by staining with ammonium sulfide [80] or by compression 
of the uterus between glass slides. The numbers and positions 
of implantations, numbers of CLs, and embryonic viability 
(live or resorption), as well as gross lesions in the thoracic and 
peritoneal cavities are reported. The presence of CLs in non-
pregnant females should be assessed to determine if there was 
an ovulation failure or impact on tubal transport/implantation.

or ovulation, additional studies to support risk assessment can be 
helpful. If warranted, reversibility of drug-related effects can be 
assessed by treating non-pregnant females for 2 weeks, followed 
by a recovery period of sufficient duration to allow for drug elim-

is also important to note that food restriction or metabolic chal-

15% can attenuate normal ovulation [81], which should be con-
sidered dose selection, and also in data interpretation if unavoid-
able. Multiple endpoints can be calculated from collected data 
(Table 38.12) and will facilitate data interpretation.

38.6.1.2  Embryo-Fetal Development Study (EFD)
Effects on maintenance of pregnancy, organogenesis, fetal 
development, and fetal growth (Stages C&D) are assessed in 
the EFD study. Uniquely, EFD data are required from stud-
ies in both a rodent and a non-rodent species, one of which 
must be pharmacologically responsive to the clinical candi-
date. EFD studies for small molecules are most frequently 
conducted in rats (typically, Sprague Dawley or Wistar Han) 
and rabbits (often New Zealand White or Dutch Belted). Mice 
and hamsters (rodents), as well as minipigs and cynomolgus 
monkeys (non-rodents), are possible alternative species when 
required due to tolerability, bioavailability, cross-reactivity, 
and/or physiology. Access to contemporaneous HCD for the 
chosen species/strain is essential for interpreting EFD data 
because malformations are low-frequency background events, 
and the concurrent control group will not be sufficiently pow-
ered to characterize the background malformation rate. While 
EFD studies are extensive and require time-intensive, highly 
technical fetal evaluations at termination, it is essential that all 
animals in a study be run simultaneously with only a minimal 
stagger-start to ensure high-quality, comparable data.
The EFD dosing period is guided by species-specific preg-
nancy and EFD milestones to capture the relevant duration of 
organogenesis from implantation to closure of the hard pal-
ate (Figure 38.13). GDs are counted from GD 0, the date of 
presumed/confirmed mating. In most cases, pregnant rats and 
rabbits can be ordered from the supplier timed-mated and 
arrive at the testing facility before the first dose administra-
tion. Following dosing, pregnancies are allowed to continue 
until near the time of expected parturition, at which time lapa-
rohysterectomies are conducted before natural delivery.
Maternal euthanasia should start early for rats and mice 
to avoid spontaneous parturition and loss of pertinent litter 
data. Species-specific pregnancy milestones are described in 
Table 38.13.
A range-finding or preliminary EFD study is often con-
ducted for rodent and non-rodent species to support optimal 
dose selection and provide information on maternal tolerabil-
ity, bioavailability, maternal exposure, and effects on EFD 
to guide subsequent study strategies. These types of studies 
evaluate smaller group sizes but administer test article for the 
same duration as definitive (e.g., Good Laboratory Practices 
(GLP)), fully powered EFD studies to cover organogenesis. 
Preliminary EFD studies [as described in ICH M3(R2)] or 
“enhanced” preliminary EFD studies [(as described in ICH 
S5(R3)] can also be used to support the enrollment of WOCBP 
into outpatient clinical trials pending the study design used 
as outlined in Table 38.14. Additionally, definitive EFD data 
from both species are needed in most cases by the end of 
Phase 2 clinical trials to initiate Phase 3 (exceptions: drugs

Fetal morphology exams must be carefully conducted by 
skilled and trained staff with experience in the species evalu-

fetus for shape, numbers of structures, and any defects to the 
head, palate, abdominal wall, limbs/paws, eyelids, tail, or other 
areas of the body is conducted shortly after fetal collection, 
before fetal euthanasia. Visceral exams [82] are conducted fol-

all major organ systems for correct position/orientation, shape, 
numbers, and potential malformations. The heart is carefully 
opened on both the left and right sides to evaluate for any 
alterations to the cardiac architecture, internal chambers, or 
valves. The head is cut to evaluate the brain for alterations; the 
scope of the evaluation is species-specific. In rabbits, a mid-
coronal cut in the skull during the fresh, visceral exam is made 
to permit visualization of the brain ventricles for shape and 
size. In rodents, the brain is fixed or frozen to enable a series 
of cuts through the skull and mandible to evaluate the palate, 
eyes, and brain ventricles for shape, size, and appearance. For 
skeletal evaluation, fetuses are eviscerated (and skinned, rab-
bits only), fixed in ethanol, processed in potassium hydroxide, 
stained with alizarin red, and cleared in glycerin solutions 
before visual evaluation of ossified bones by a trained fetal 
morphologist [83]. All bones are evaluated for position, shape, 
number, and degree of ossification. Alternatively, fetuses can 
be processed by combining alizarin red staining of bones with 
Alcian Blue staining to visualize cartilaginous structures [84]. 
Combined staining is technically more complex, but it permits 
a more complete evaluation of the fetal skeleton and can dis-
cern a developmental delay. Typically, visceral and skeletal 
examinations are completed on all rabbit fetuses; due to larger 
litter sizes relative to rabbits, 50% of rodent fetuses in each 
litter will be designated for visceral evaluation and the remain-
ing fetuses for skeletal assessment. However, if a rodent strain 
with smaller mean litter sizes is selected, an assessment of 
100% of fetuses for both visceral and skeletal alterations can 
be considered.
Fetal morphology findings are categorized as either malfor-
mations (permanent, structural changes to an organ or struc-
ture that are likely detrimental to development or survival) or 
variations (structural changes that are not considered to impact 
viability, development, or function) [85, 86, 87]. Fetal skeletal 
variations are often the result of delayed or uneven ossification, 
resulting in additional time to develop, and can be more clearly 
identified if fetuses are double-stained [88]. Common varia-
tions noted at the visceral exam in fetuses include minor vessel 
architecture differences, dilated ureters or dilated renal pelvis 
in rats, and small gallbladder or absent azygos lung lobe or 
innominate vessel in New Zealand White rabbits. Appropriate 
HCD will highlight the most common background variations 
observed for each species/strain. To improve data interpreta-
tion, derived values (which often normalize measured values 
to a common scale such as body weight) are commonly calcu-
lated. A list of derived parameters is presented in (Table 38.16).
The recently completed ICH S5(R3) guideline also intro-
duces the possibility to provide data from alternative assays 
(e.g., in vitro, ex vivo, or non-mammalian in vivo assays) 
designed to predict developmental hazards to defer or replace 
in vivo EFD studies in certain circumstances. In general, 
drugs with intended pharmacology/mechanisms of action with 
well-described adverse sequelae on development, or those 
with known class effects associated with teratogenicity, can 
be evaluated in a qualified alternative assay. A confirmatory 
positive signal may prevent conducting in vivo EFD studies. 
For drugs in development to treat severely debilitating or life-
threatening disease (outside advanced oncology indications) or 
use in patients with late-life onset of disease, alternative assays 
can be used as part of an integrated strategy to defer or replace 
one or both in vivo EFD studies.

The overwhelming majority of marketed drugs were not 
developed with intended use during pregnancy. Instead, 
DART testing has historically been required for pharmaceu-
ticals to provide physicians and patients information in the 
event of exposure during an unexpected pregnancy. Exposure 
to an unrecognized pregnancy is primarily the most suscep-
tible window for teratogenic effects during the first trimester. 
Potent teratogens require cautionary drug labeling and often 
require a negative pregnancy test before use (e.g., thalido-
mide, isotretinoin, mycophenolate). Recognizing the need 
to develop and provide therapeutics, including pregnant 
and nursing women, the requirement for robust nonclinical 
DART evaluations and risk assessment will be reinforced 
accordingly.
38.6.1.3  Pre- and Postnatal Development 
Study (PPND)
The PPND study is designed to evaluate for potential postna-
tal effects to the offspring through sexual maturity following 
maternal exposure to a drug throughout gestation and lacta-
tion (Stages C–F). Offspring are assessed for effects on sexual 
maturation, sensory function, motor activity, learning and 
memory, and reproductive capacity at adulthood. Data from 
the PPND are required to support applications for marketing 
authorization, so these studies are typically conducted during 
Phase 3 clinical trials. The species selected should have been 
evaluated previously for effects on EFD to obviate the need for 
range-finding studies. In most cases, the PPND is conducted 
in the rat due to practical experience and the availability of 
extensive HCD.
Maternal dosing is initiated at the time of implantation (GD 
6 for rodents) and continued through gestation and lactation 
until weaning (Figure 38.14). Offspring are never directly 
dosed; exposure of the test article to the conceptus or pup 
is indirect via the placenta or milk, respectively. Dams are 
allowed to deliver spontaneously. If dams fail to deliver by GD 
25, they are euthanized and evaluated for uterine contents and 
gross lesions.
Maternal endpoints for evaluation include viability, clinical 
observations, body weight, food consumption, observations 
at parturition, and maternal behavior. Body weights and food 
consumption are often evaluated daily in PPND studies to 
carefully profile toxicity/tolerability to the test article through-
out gestation/lactation and to ensure accurate dosing due to 
changes in body weights. Measurement of maternal food con-
sumption is discontinued on PND 14 to avoid the confounding 
effect of pups consuming the chow as they develop. There is 
no drug in the chow. All drug exposure is indirect via lactation 
from the dosed dam. Following weaning on PND 21, dams are 
euthanized and evaluated for gross lesions; uteri are examined 
for implantation sites.
TK data are generally included in PPND studies to gener-
ate an AUC profile in the early lactation phase. Pup expo-
sures are typically assessed at a single time-point; terminal 
blood samples can be collected from culled pups on PND 4 
without the need to add satellite rats to the study. When pup 
exposures are included, it is best to assess maternal systemic 
exposures to facilitate simultaneous comparisons. Collection 
of milk from dams for estimation of milk concentrations is 
rare, and these values are generally not considered helpful 
for direct, quantitative correlation to human milk concentra-
tions. When warranted, milk samples are collected around 
PND 10, the peak of lactation in rats [89], and a timeframe 
when the withdrawal of the pups for a few hours to facilitate 
the milk collection will not be impactful on pup growth or 
survival. For biologics cross-reactive in traditional DART 
species, evaluation of maternal and pup immunogenicity 
can be helpful to assist with data interpretation. Samples for 
immunogenicity can be conducted contemporaneously with 
a TK time-point for dams and pups.
Once delivery has started, no maternal dosing or pup obser-
vations should be conducted (except for removing dead pups to 
prevent cannibalization) until littering is considered complete. 
Pups found dead on the day of delivery can be assessed for 
the presence of milk in the stomach; a lung floatation assess-
ment can also be done to determine if the pup was stillborn or 
born alive. The day parturition is completed (considered PND 
0), all pups are weighed and assessed for external abnormali-
ties. Maternal behaviors indicating that littering is concluded 
include: (1) the absence of amniotic sacs, placentas, and umbil-
ical cords; (2) pup grooming; (3) self-grooming; (4) nesting 
behavior; and/or (5) pup nursing.

38.6.2  ICH S6(R1)
Biopharmaceuticals are products derived from specialized cells 
(e.g., bacteria, yeast, insect, plant, or mammalian cells) used to 
express a peptide, protein, or antibody of interest. These agents 
are distinct from small molecules in multiple ways, and their 
nonclinical assessment must consider these biological differ-
ences to ensure scientifically sound study design and interpreta-
tion. Most biotherapeutics will have a longer half-life, little to 
no off-target activity, and generate an immune response (anti-
drug antibodies (ADA)) in animals. These products are highly 
selective for human targets and are frequently cross-reactive only 
in NHPs. Accordingly, the ICH S6(R1) guideline addresses the 
principles and considerations for the toxicology testing of these 
drugs [65]. Traditional ICH S5(R3) DART studies are conducted 
at prescribed clinical milestones to support drug development 
for biologics demonstrated to be pharmacologically responsive 
in rodents and/or rabbits. For biotherapeutics cross-reactive only 
in NHPs, DART evaluations are conducted using a modified 
approach (Table 38.19).
Fertility data are gleaned from routine evaluation of the 
reproductive tracts (organ weights and microscopic changes) 
of sexually mature NHPs in repeat-dose toxicity studies of at 
least three months in duration. At study initiation, NHPs can 
be confirmed to be sexually mature by observation of menses 
or the presence of sperm in a semen sample. If scientifically 
warranted, additional DART endpoints, such as menstrual 
cyclicity, semen analysis, and/or reproductive hormone levels, 
can be added in these studies [94, 95].
The conduct of stand-alone EFD studies in NHPs is uncom-
mon. Antibodies (IgG) are transferred across the placenta via 
the neonatal Fc receptor (FcRn), predominantly expressed 
during the second half of gestation in NHPs and humans, 
resulting in fetal IgG levels equivalent to or slightly greater 
than maternal systemic concentrations [37]. Because minimal 
placental transfer of antibodies is expected during the period 
of major organogenesis (first trimester, GD 20–50), an EFD 
study in NHPs is limited primarily to an evaluation for effects 
on the maintenance of pregnancy.
Effects on development during gestation and lactation are 
assessed in the enhanced pre- and postnatal development (ePPND) 
study in which maternal monkeys (~16/group) are dosed from 
detection of pregnancy (GD 20) through parturition (~GD 160–
170) and infants are evaluated for ~6 months following delivery 
[96, 97]. Generally, one control and two drug-treated groups are 
evaluated. High-dose justification is typically based on doses 
associated with boundaries such as saturation of binding or maxi-
mum pharmacological response, rather than an MTD. During the 
study, pregnant monkeys are monitored biweekly using ultrasound 
to evaluate the viability of the conceptus; cynomolgus monkeys 
have a higher background rate of pregnancy loss/abortion, which 
is most notable during the first trimester and late third trimester, 
compared to traditional DART species.
Maternal viability, clinical observations, body weights, 
qualitative food consumption, TK, immunogenicity, serum 
chemistry, hematology, and other immune parameters (as 
appropriate based on the expected pharmacology of the drug) 
are monitored throughout the gestation and lactation phases. 
Following natural delivery, infants are housed with their moth-
ers throughout the postnatal phase and additionally monitored 
for viability, clinical observations, development of reflexes, 
body weights, TK, immunogenicity, hematology, serum chem-
istry, and other study-specific endpoints (e.g., safety pharma-
cology, ophthalmology, T-cell–dependent antibody responses 
(TDAR), lymphocyte phenotyping, and/or other immune 
assessments), as appropriate. At the end of the study, infants 
are euthanized and subject to routine toxicity study evaluation 
of organ weights and macroscopic and microscopic lesions. 
In general, maternal animals are not necropsied or subject to 
postmortem evaluations.
To incorporate teratology endpoints in an ePPND study, 
infants are evaluated shortly after birth for external malfor-
mations. Infants are sedated and radiographed in several ori-
entations to evaluate the skeleton at approximately 1 month 
of age. At termination, infants’ visceral tissues are assessed 
for changes in shape, position, and number; any alterations to 
the major organ systems are recorded. Because monkeys have 
singleton pregnancies, the screening power of these studies to 
detect potential drug-related structural changes in the offspring 
is low. Consequently, only significant developmental hazards 
will be identified for risk assessment of malformations.
Pregnancy enrollment for ePPND often takes several 
months. Due to gestation length and postnatal evaluation, 
ePPND studies usually take more than 2 years to complete. 
There are limited testing facilities with the technical capa-
bilities and available HCD to conduct these studies; there-
fore, ePPND studies must be planned and reserved years in

38.6.3  ICH S9
Nonclinical evaluation of anticancer pharmaceuticals is 
described in ICH S9, which provides guidance for drugs in 
development for oncology indications associated with advanced 
disease in patients with limited therapeutic options [66]. Given 
the risk/benefit for cancer patients with short life expectancies, 
the need for nonclinical data to support clinical use is attenu-
ated relative to drugs in development for other types of non-
life-threatening diseases. Further, many anticancer drugs are 
designed to impact pathways and targets associated with rap-
idly dividing cells to cause therapeutic benefit; by extension, 
these drugs are likely to cause adverse effects on embryonic 
development, a time of extensive growth. As such, EFD data 
from two species are typically expected to support the submis-
sion of a marketing application. However, if adverse develop-
mental toxicity at a clinically relevant exposure is identified in 
one species, an EFD study in the second species is not required. 
Further, FEED and PPND studies are generally not warranted 
for drugs developed in these indications.
38.6.4  ICH S11
Preclinical toxicity studies for new and novel pharmaceutical 
entities are traditionally conducted in adult animals to under-
stand systemic and reproductive toxicity. While these meth-
odologies are appropriate for generating safety data for the 
adult human population, the designs are generally considered 
inadequate for evaluating the safety of the same therapeu-
tic compounds for a pediatric population. Regulatory health 
authorities such as the US FDA (Pediatric Research Equity 
Act) and the EMA (EC No. 1901/2006) [98] require manufac-
turers to conduct studies and provide sufficient data and infor-
mation to support the use of their products in children. This 
applies to both new and previously marketed drugs intended 
for adult use. ICH Guideline S11 is proposed to provide direc-
tion on the nonclinical safety studies needed to support a 
pediatric development program. The ICH S11 guideline is 
meant to complement existing ICH guidelines (e.g., ICH M3, 
S5, and S9) and guidance documents (e.g., E11) and provides 
recommendations on the design and the timing of juvenile 
animal studies (JAS) in the context of an overarching clinical 
development plan. JAS are required only for pharmaceuticals 
intended for use in pediatric populations, including products 
with documented use in adult populations and those intended 
only for use in a juvenile population, often referred to as “pedi-
atric first” products. An understanding of the overall clinical 
development plan, developed according to ICH E11, allows 
for the development of a systematic nonclinical plan. The S11 
guideline discusses, in detail, a WoE approach toward deter-
mining if nonclinical JAS are warranted, based on the totality 
of the available evidence, including clinical, in vitro, and in 
vivo animal data. If a JAS is warranted, the study’s design and 
objectives should be based on the issues identified by the WoE 
approach, and an all-encompassing design, which includes all 
possible endpoints, is not recommended. The S11 guideline 
recommends a set of core endpoints (survival, clinical obser-
vations, growth, sexual development, basic clinical and ana-
tomic pathology, and TK) and a set of additional endpoints 
to address specific concerns identified by the WoE approach. 
Examples of additional endpoints include assessment of bone 
development, ophthalmology, and neurobehavioral or repro-
ductive assessments. While this chapter will not describe the 
ICH guideline in detail and the reader is directed to review 
the published guideline, essential study design elements and 
considerations for JAS are discussed in detail.
Although the ICH S11 guideline significantly improves 
upon prior guidance documents, it is not an all-encompassing 
document covering every scenario encountered when design-
ing or conducting JAS. These studies are usually extensive and 
difficult to repeat, so the study design must be well thought 
out and discussed with regulatory health authorities before 
being conducted. The most relevant information necessary to 
design a JAS are target human population, clinical indication, 
RoA, treatment duration, and test system (species) This infor-
mation is necessary to complete the initial WoE assessment, 
and although these elements are listed independently, all five 
must be considered together in the design of a JAS.
In general, JAS are required to be conducted only in a 
single species. The rat is typically used because it is also 
the most common species used in adult repeated-dose stud-
ies. JAS may be required in two species, but this is gener-
ally the case only for “pediatric first” or “pediatric only” 
clinical indications. The goal is to generate safety data for 
treating children when prior adult data are lacking. This situ-
ation could occur for diseases only in children or when the 
pharmaceutical cannot be given safely to adult volunteers. 
In these cases, the First in Human trials are conducted in 
children (instead of in healthy adult volunteers), and the non-
clinical JAS program generally includes a study in a rodent 
and a non-rodent species [99].
38.6.4.1  Age of Animals, Dosing 
Initiation, and Duration
The youngest intended patient age is one of the most important 
factors to consider in designing a JAS. It is widely accepted that 
laboratory animal species should be exposed from the young-
est feasible age to match the youngest anticipated human age 
in the clinic and continue through sexual maturation. Before a 
final decision is made on the age of initiation, all future and 
potential alternative clinical indications should be considered 
for each drug so that the youngest possible patient age has been 
taken into consideration.
Animals are generally exposed until the attainment of sex-
ual maturation. This serves to bridge the gap in toxicology

information between the age of initiation on a JAS and the 
standard age when general toxicology studies are initiated, 
i.e., in post-pubertal young adults. For diseases that are gener-
ally diagnosed in adolescents, JAS may not be necessary, and 
adequate information regarding potential treatment-dependent 
toxicities can be obtained simply by starting the general toxi-
cology program in animals slightly younger usually used on 
general toxicology studies. For example, the standard age of 
rats on a 13- or 26-week study is 8–9 weeks at study initia-
tion; for a drug intended for 12–16-year-old children, the non-
clinical toxicology program could be initiated with animals 
4–6 weeks of age, thus eliminating the need for a JAS.
The total duration of dose administration on a JAS can vary 
based on the clinical dosing regimen. However, animals are 
generally required to be exposed from the youngest feasible age 
through sexual maturation for small molecules. For example, 
in the case of an antibiotic that may only be administered for 
5–7 days in the clinic beginning as early as 2 years of age, it is 
essential to dose animals from the earliest possible age through 
the attainment of sexual maturity because a child might receive 
the treatment at any point between 2 years of age and adolescence. 
Even for compounds intended for chronic (lifetime) administra-
tion, the duration of administration on a JAS can be the same 
(i.e., earliest possible age through sexual maturation). However, in 
these instances, continued evaluation of animals during the post-
treatment period is generally required because exposure during 
the critical periods of development can result in long-term health 
effects.
Depending upon the molecular and human target organ 
systems, care should be taken to ensure the animals’ expo-
sure during the period of development most relevant to the 
pertinent organ system. It is also possible to adjust the dosing 
regimen to focus on the appropriate development period in 
these situations. For organ systems that may develop over a 
prolonged period of time, such as the CNS or endocrine sys-
tems, the expectation is to expose animals from the young-
est feasible age through sexual maturity. The dosing regimen 
must be designed not only to ensure exposure over a relevant 
period of organ system development but also to achieve mea-
surable and relevant exposures during the period of con-
cern. Thus, when the clinical dosing regimen is intermittent, 
daily dosing on a JAS may be necessary to assure adequate 
exposures. ICH S11 guidelines include an overview of organ 
system development by species for the most used toxicology 
species. This information is intended to aid in data inter-
pretation and selection of the relevant species, age at dose 
administration, and the relevant dosing durations for various 
developing organ systems.
Although the dosing period rarely extends past the attain-
ment of sexual maturation, a post-treatment assessment period 
is frequently included on JAS to allow for the evaluation of 
persistence, reversibility, and any latent effects that may arise 
because of early-life exposure. For example, a non-CNS-active 
compound in adults may enter the brain when administered 
before maturation of the blood-brain barrier in a pediatric 
population. It may thus be necessary to evaluate neurodevel-
opmental endpoints on a JAS, even though the brain is not 
identified as, or expected to be, a target organ for a particular 
compound.
38.6.4.2  Route of Administration
As with any nonclinical study, the RoA on a JAS should match 
the intended clinical route, where possible, although this may 
not always be feasible or appropriate. In such instances, it is 
essential to discuss with relevant regulatory bodies to deter-
mine the best path forward, as there is precedent for regulatory 
acceptance of alternative RoA based on practicability in the 
laboratory animal species. For example, dermal administration 
of topical creams and ointments, occluded or non-occluded, is 
frequently used as a RoA in adult animal studies. However, 
the dermal route becomes impracticable when neonates 
are housed by litter with a nursing dam. Although Sprague 
Dawley and Han Wistar rats generally make good mothers, 
topical application of a drug substance can adversely impact 
maternal nursing and nesting behaviors, making them aban-
don or cannibalize their pups. It thus may become challenging 
to document treatment-related effects because affected pups 
may be missing entirely or partly cannibalized, and litter data 
will be confounded. Alternatively, maternal grooming of the 
pups can result in exposure to the dam (not the primary test 
system) and reduced exposure to the pups (the primary test 
system), thus affecting the study findings.
Intravenous injections or infusions are routinely used RoA 
in adult rats and even mice. These can be administered daily 
via direct venipuncture, surgically implanted intravenous cath-
eters, or more recently, via vascular access buttons. In juvenile 
pups, intravenous infusion is usually feasible only after the 
animals have attained a sufficient size – generally around the 
time of weaning (PND 21 or older). With adequate training 
and practice, trained technicians can administer intravenous 
injections into the tail vein or the jugular vein as early as PND 
1 in the rat. However, repeated daily injection remains imprac-
ticable because of the animal’s size and the fragility of the skin 
and blood vessels. These practical concerns do not necessarily 
imply the impossibility of conducting a JAS but instead call 
attention to alternative approaches. For compounds with rela-
tively short half-lives (<12–24 hours) that may be administered 
daily in the clinic, it may be possible to use alternative routes 
of exposure (e.g., subcutaneous, or intraperitoneal injections) 
until it becomes feasible to administer the drug via intravenous 
infusion. Alternatively, in the case of compounds for which 
systemic exposure is sufficient after single injections, intermit-
tent administration via weekly intravenous injections has been 
used for JAS. For single-dose compounds, such as those used 
for cell/gene therapy applications, intravenous injections can 
be administered as early as PND 1 or 4, and the animals moni-
tored for the duration of the study.
38.6.4.3  Test Systems/Species (Animal Models)
As stated previously, the rat is the most commonly employed 
animal system in JAS mainly because it is the primary nonclini-
cal species. It offers several benefits beyond the relatively low 
cost of small animals, including ease of handling, large litter 
sizes, a short life span with rapid growth, and easy evaluation 
of sexual maturation. In addition, because the rat is the most 
frequently used rodent species in general toxicology and DART 
studies, there are extensive HCD available and a variety of

Female Reproductive and Developmental Toxicology
well-established and validated assays and measurements to mon-
itor development of the animals. Most laboratories have person-
nel proficient in dosing rat pups via oral gavage or subcutaneous 
injection beginning as early as the first week after birth. Other 
routes of administration, such as intramuscular or intravenous 
injection, require a higher degree of proficiency due to the size 
of the animals, and intravenous infusion for any length of time is 
generally only feasible once the animals have attained sufficient 
size – around the time of weaning (PND 21 or older).
The mouse is an alternative rodent species that has been 
used in JAS, although less frequently than the rat. Oral gavage 
administration to mice during the first postnatal week can be 
quite challenging because the pups are about a third of the 
size of rat pups (at birth, rats are 6–7 grams, mice are 2.0–2.5 
grams). The use of larger animal species may alleviate some 
of the challenges associated with dosing very small animals. 
However, the lack of adequate (or any) HCD at performing 
laboratories can be a downside.
Rabbit kits can be dosed either orally or subcutaneously 
beginning at birth. Although adult rabbits are generally dosed 
intravenously via the marginal ear vein, the ears of kits are 
not as well developed, thus precluding this as a RoA until the 
animals have matured. Although rabbits are often used to 
study large molecules, they tend to generate ADA, resulting 
in reduced or no systemic exposure. To avoid causing ADA, 
cohort designs can be conducted such that smaller groups of 
rabbits are exposed for short durations that, when combined, 
cover the entire period of clinical interest. In this way, drug 
exposure is more limited and can be maintained below the 
exposure duration/concentration threshold for ADA develop-
ment. However, cohort designs are complex, and the size of 
the study can rapidly become unmanageable. Generally, rab-
bits are used as a test system only when the drug’s molecular 
target is absent in the rodent.
Among other nonclinical species, dogs are perhaps used 
more frequently for JAS [100, 101] than the NHP or mini-
pig. Compared to NHPs that produce one (or very rarely, 
two) infants per pregnancy, dogs have larger litter sizes (up 
to 10 puppies/litter). The standard gestation length in dogs 
is approximately 63 days (as opposed to 5.5 months in NHP), 
and puppies can be dosed as early as the day of birth. JAS 
are rarely conducted in the NHP, even though primate infants 
can be dosed as early as at birth. An important reason for this 
is that time-mated NHPs are not commercially available, and 
the transport of juvenile NHPs (infants <1-year-old) is strictly 
regulated. The use of infants generally requires an in-house 
mating program that would require the maintenance of sexu-
ally mature adult males and females. With singleton births and 
relatively long gestation periods, it would be a prolonged pro-
cess to even attain small groups of infants at the appropriate 
age. Further, HCD are limited due to the small group sizes 
in NHP studies. Few laboratories globally have any depth of 
experience in conducting JAS with NHPs [102].
In recent years, the minipig has gained popularity [103, 104, 
105]. The gestation length (~115 days) is longer than that of the 
dog, but considerably shorter than the NHP, and litter sizes 
range from 4 to 8 piglets per litter. Because the minipig is a 
relatively robust animal (about 500 g at birth), even a JAS with 
small group sizes can require a considerable amount of test 
material. However, minipigs are often used for dermal stud-
ies, wherein the rodent poses practical limitations, as previ-
ously discussed. Piglets can be wrapped or dressed in porous 
onesies to prevent oral ingestion of the test material by sow or 
littermates. Further, regardless of the RoA, the minipig can be 
dosed as early as birth.
38.6.4.4  Endpoints for Evaluation
The S11 guideline recommends a set of core endpoints 
included on every JAS and a set of additional endpoints to 
address specific concerns identified by the initial WoE assess-
ment. Table 38.20 presents a list of core and supplementary 
endpoints included on JAS. The rat is used as a representa-
tive example, although the same or similar assessments can be 
included for any other species used for JAS. Depending upon 
the endpoints included, the number of animals in each group 
can vary considerably. Generally, ten pups per sex per group 
are considered sufficient for primary in-life and terminal 
assessments, including clinical and anatomic pathology. The 
inclusion of a post-treatment phase generally doubles the size 
of each group. Unlike repeated-dose toxicity studies in adult 
animals, the post-treatment phase on JAS typically includes 
all dose groups (as opposed to only the control and high-dose 
group). However, the initial number per group is also depen-
dent upon the age of the animals at termination. For example, 
although rats 8 weeks of age or older provide adequate blood 
volumes for clinical pathology assessments, the inclusion of 
TK or biomarker assessments might require additional ani-
mals or a set of satellite animals dedicated to these assess-
ments. Blood volume can often be a limiting factor if the 
primary necropsy is scheduled at a younger age.
Furthermore, as outlined in ICH S11, the inclusion of addi-
tional endpoints often dramatically increases the size of JAS. 
For reproductive or CNS assessments, the S11 guideline rec-
ommends the approaches outlined in existing ICH guidelines 
(e.g., ICH S5) and associated guidance documents, which 
require 16–20 animals per sex per group at a minimum to 
ensure sufficient statistical power for reproductive and CNS 
assessments. An experienced study team can optimize the 
assignment of animals to assure the collection of multiple in-
life and terminal endpoints from each group of animals.
In general, the expectations of regulatory health authori-
ties regarding the inclusion of supplementary endpoints on a 
JAS vary based on the modality, age at initiation of treatment, 
and clinical indication. For pharmaceutical entities intended 
for acute or short-term administration, assessing the core end-
points is generally considered sufficient. However, reproductive 
assessments are also requested for drugs intended for chronic 
(lifetime) administration, as drugs administered during repro-
ductive development and puberty have a higher potential for 
impacting reproductive performance. Similarly, CNS-active 
compounds are considered to have the potential to impact 
CNS assessments and would generally require, at minimum, 
an assessment of neurobehavior and tests for learning and 
memory. As discussed above, CNS assessments may also be 
requested for non-CNS-active compounds that may be admin-
istered before maturation of the blood-brain barrier in a pedi-
atric population.

The endpoints used to assess maternal toxicity in DART 
studies include maternal death, clinical signs, food consump-
tion, body weights, and abortion (especially in rabbits and 
guinea pigs). Maternal health can affect the numbers of CLs, 
viable (live) fetuses, and nonviable implantation sites (early 
and late resorptions, fetal death), fetal weights, and fetal mor-

female fertility, and implantation sites must be determined, in 
addition to the aforementioned maternal endpoints. In studies 
wherein the offspring are born (i.e., reproductive studies), the 
effects of maternal toxicity on parturition (birth) must be con-
sidered, as well as the influence of maternal care of offspring 
during the lactation period and the ability of the maternal ani-
mal to produce milk.
Several findings are often associated with maternal toxic-
ity. These findings include decreased fetal/pup weights in both 
reproductive and EFD studies, as well as reduced ossification 
in the skull and long bones, and wavy ribs. Establishing mater-
nal toxicity as being causative cannot be obtained from sum-
mary tables. Rather the individual maternal data for dams that 
carried offspring with such findings must be examined to link 
maternal findings (e.g., reduced weight gains, decreased feed 
consumption) to the affected offspring.
Animals in the high-dose groups of DART studies should 
exhibit mild, but not excessive, maternal toxicity. While find-
ings of maternal deaths or increased incidences of total litter 
resorption and abortions among surviving females are indica-
tive of maternal toxicity, dosages that produce a high propor-
tion of maternal deaths or abortions, total litter resorptions or 
offspring deaths are not desirable because they result in there 
being too few fetuses available to examine for developmental 
effects in an EFD study or too few offspring available to evalu-
ate or mate for production of the next generation of animals 
in a reproduction study. However, the dose-response curve for 
maternal toxicity may be steep with some test substances. In 
such cases, the difference between a dose that causes mater-
nal death, abortion, total litter resorption or offspring deaths, 
and dosages that induce less extreme maternal toxicity (e.g., 
decreased body-weight gain) may be small.
38.7.1.2  Maternal Deaths, Abortions, 
and Total Litter Losses
When evaluating the results of DART studies, it must be 
borne in mind that factors other than the test substance can 
cause maternal death or abortions, including maternal disease, 
environmental factors, and technical errors (such as intratra-
cheal intubation). When a pregnant animal dies, the necropsy 
records should be inspected to establish the probable reason 
for the death. For instance, if the necropsy records reveal that 
the female exhibited an inflamed (reddened) tracheal lining, 
pulmonary congestion, nasal discharge, and/or the presence 
of fluid in the lungs, then accidental intratracheal intubation 
(a technician error), or respiratory disease may be the cause 
of death. Another example would be the death of a pregnant 
rabbit for which the daily clinical signs included localized 
hair loss (alopecia), decreased or absent appetite (anorexia), 
and diarrhea. These signs are consistent with a trichobezoar 
(hairball in the stomach), a common occurrence in rabbits. 
Thus, maternal deaths cannot always be interpreted as test-
substance–induced toxicity.
Similarly, abortions and total litter resorptions may be 
caused by factors unrelated to test substance toxicity [143]. For 
instance, environmental stress from conditions such as exces-
sive noise in the animal room, deviations in light-dark cycles, 
and mishandling by technicians may induce abortions, par-
ticularly in rabbits. Although total litter resorptions happen in 
rabbits, they occur more regularly in rodents (e.g., mice, rats, 
or hamsters), which do not usually abort.
38.7.1.3  Clinical Observations
Clinical observations are objective, significant qualitative 
indicators of toxicity that well-trained technicians should note. 
In many cases, clinical observations may be a more sensitive 
and reliable gauge of maternal toxicity than changes in mater-
nal body weight and/or maternal body-weight gain (especially 
in rabbits). Clinical signs of toxicity induced by the test sub-
stance in DRF studies can give the investigator awareness 
of the clinical findings to be expected with treatment in the 
definitive study. When clinical signs are seen, the observed 
effect, its time of onset, its intensity, and duration should all 
be documented in the data. Examples of clinical observations 
that may be noted include convulsions, tremors, excessive 
salivation and/or mastication, alertness, listlessness, posture, 
movement within the cage, rales, color of mucous membranes, 
discharges from the eyes or nose, frequency and color of urine, 
presence of diarrhea, and hair loss (alopecia).
Behavioral changes reported during daily observations (e.g., 
animals that appear aggressive or hunched in a corner facing 
away from the observer) are similar to symptoms reported by 
human patients but not as objective as clinical observation data. 
Both behavioral changes in animals and symptoms in patients 
require a subjective interpretation by the observer. Although 
changes noted in animal behavior should be recorded, it is 
impossible to ascertain if these behavioral changes are early 
signs of subclinical toxicity that may be manifested as “objec-
tive” clinical signs at higher doses.
38.7.1.4  Maternal Body Weight and 
Body-Weight Gain
Most DART study guidelines require that maternal body 
weights be recorded at least twice weekly. However, the daily 
weighing of pregnant females during treatment may provide 
more beneficial information [142]. Maternal body weight and 
weight gain data are sensitive indicators of maternal toxicity 
and the most frequently used endpoints from DRF studies for 
selecting the highest dose levels to be administered in defini-
tive studies. In most test species, these endpoints are often the 
basis for determining the NOAEL for maternal toxicity.
Optimally, mean body-weight gain (or the percentage 
change in body weight) for all dose groups should be similar

during the predosing period. Decreases in mean maternal body 
weights observed during the treatment period usually result 
from the test article’s toxicity or decreased food consump-
tion. Reduced maternal body weight of ∈5% continuously for 
several days is generally judged to signal an adverse effect on 
maternal well-being [141].
Usually, test-substance–induced reductions in maternal body 
weight or weight gain are dose-related. Nevertheless, there are 
some situations in which mean body weight/gain values in low-
dose animals appear to be adversely affected, whereas values 
in the high-dose group are not. Such a situation can occur when 
the high-dose group experiences excessive maternal mortality, 
thus eliminating sensitive animals and/or reducing the number 
of surviving animals at that dosage available for comparison. 
In this situation, the absence of a dose-response in maternal 
body weight or body-weight gain does not imply the absence 
of a test-substance–related effect. Likewise, in EFD studies, 
maternal body weight or body-weight gain may not be dose-
related if a high proportion of surviving females have experi-
enced numerous resorptions per litter. For this reason, even 
though recording gravid uterine weight is optional in the new 
ICHS5(R3) [142] guidelines, we recommend that the females’ 
starting body weights be compared with their corrected mater-
nal body weights (terminal body weight minus gravid uterine 
weight) to minimize the variability introduced by the differing 
uterine weights between females who experienced total litter 
resorption (or a high proportion of resorption) versus those that 
carried a litter to term.
Any effect leading to decreased fetal or offspring body 
weight or reduced litter size will affect maternal body weight 
or weight gain. Thus, it is critical to assess individual relation-
ships among maternal body weight (or weight change), gravid 
uterine weight (if recorded), litter size, and fetal and offspring 
weights. It is essential to be aware of the changes that typically 
occur and compare the data in the treated groups with concur-
rent control and HCD when evaluating maternal toxicity.
38.7.1.5  Food and Water Consumption
Body weights and food consumption are closely linked param-
eters such that reductions in one endpoint will often be asso-
ciated with reductions in the other. Further, when the test 
substance is incorporated in either the diet or drinking water, 
reductions in consumption may be seen owing to unpalatabil-
ity. Therefore, food and water consumption must be measured 
daily (or at least every few days) to calculate test substance 
intake levels. Regardless of the RoA, food and water consump-
tion should also be monitored when the test substance is sus-
pected of causing appetite or excretory effects.
Reduced food consumption may be observed soon after the 
initiation of exposure; alternatively, repeated exposures may 
be required before reductions become apparent. Frequently, 
when reduced mean body weights have been observed dur-
ing the exposure period, treated animals will experience an 
increase in maternal body weight (adaptation or a “rebound” 
effect) during the post-exposure period. The rebound is com-
mon when the females have consumed less food during the 
exposure period.
To determine whether a reduction in food consumption was 
caused by a maternally toxic effect of the test substance or 
by the food’s unpalatability, the food efficiency index (FEI; 
the grams of food consumed per gram of body weight gained) 
for each group can be determined. The FEI is considered a 
measure of how effectively food is used by the animal (e.g., 
body-weight gain). A maternally toxic effect is unlikely if the 
FEIs for the treated and control groups are similar, and unpal-
atability is the probable cause for reduced food consumption.
38.7.1.6  Organ Weights and Clinical Chemistry
In an EFD study, current regulations only require the collec-
tion of maternal thyroid organ weights/histopathology and 
maternal serum thyroid hormones levels; other organ weights 
and clinical chemistry data are not required. However, infor-
mation regarding dose-related effects on the absolute and rela-
tive maternal organ weights can be helpful when assessing 
maternal toxicity. For example, the liver often exhibits early 
signs of adaptation and/or toxicity (e.g., induction of enzymes, 
fatty change, or hydropic change) associated with increased 
liver weights. Consequently, if a report provides data on mater-
nal liver weights, they should not be ignored but instead care-
fully evaluated.
Occasionally, clinical chemistry data (e.g., hematology and 
enzyme markers) are reported in EFD studies. When notable 
effects are reported, these data may help assess maternal 
toxicity, even without overt signs, such as decreased food 
consumption.
38.7.1.7  Additional Considerations
38.7.1.7.1  Rabbit Peculiarities
Rabbits provide several challenges to the determination of 
maternal toxicity. Pregnant rabbits often avoid food and 
thus frequently lose weight during a normal pregnancy. 
Additionally, it is not uncommon for pregnant rabbits to abort 
or deliver early when they experience substantial body-weight 
loss and decreased food consumption [144, 145, 146, 147]. 
Thus, maternal body-weight changes in rabbits are frequently 
difficult to interpret because of the pregnant rabbit’s inherently 
erratic body-weight gains and losses during gestation. Further, 
during the last week of pregnancy, rabbits will often reduce 
their food intake and exhibit abdominal hair loss as they use 
the hair to construct a nest before parturition.
38.7.1.7.2  Toxicokinetics
It is ideal to have some information on TK before initiating 
DART studies. Such information may inform the necessity to 
change species and/or revise the study design or dosing regi-
men to be used [148]. Likely, at the start of the DART studies, 
the only TK data available will be from repeat-dose toxicity 
studies conducted in animals that are not pregnant or lactat-
ing. Because the kinetics in pregnancy may differ from those 
in non-pregnant animals, obtaining additional TK data from a 
preliminary exposure study in pregnant or lactating animals or 
satellite groups in a DRF study can help determine the appro-
priate dosages for the DRF and definitive DART studies.

The collection of TK data from embryos, fetuses, and/or 
offspring on specified days during gestation and lactation may 
also be beneficial. Embryo-fetal TK analysis is not routinely

separately. In addition, pooling fetal blood and collecting fetal 
organs to evaluate test substance exposure could be requested

value of milk collection is limited due to species differences

the qualitative presence or absence of substances in milk from

direct dosing of pups will be required shortly after birth.

38.8.1  Estrous Cyclicity
Rats and mice are the most frequently used species in repro-
ductive toxicity testing due to their well-described short 
estrous cycle and the ease of handling to perform vaginal 
lavages (smears). The estrous cycle of rats begins between 
PND 32 and 36 [74], while in mice, the estrous cycle begins 
about PND 26 [78]. The normal estrous cycle lasts for 4–5 days 
when the vivarium conditions (notably, the photoperiod, tem-
perature, humidity, and noise) under which the rats and mice 
are housed are not altered.
Heape [150] coined the earliest use of the term “estrus” 
(“oestrum”) in the Quarterly J. Microscopic Science (see 
[151]) and further named and defined the stages of the 
mammalian estrous cycle into diestrus (“dioestrus”), proes-
trus (“proestrus”), metestrus (“metoestrum”), and anestrus 
(“anoestrum”). Knowing the length and stages of the estrous 
cycle helps evaluate the effects of a test article on reproduc-
tive function. The routine monitoring of estrous cyclicity in 
reproduction studies provides an additional valuable measure 
of whether the hypothalamic-pituitary-ovarian axis functions 
normally [74]. For further information on the interrelation 
between rodent vaginal smear cytology and endocrine altera-
tions see [152, 78, 151].
Proper training and experience are required to ensure com-
petence in using either a wet, fixed, or stained technique to 
identify cell types. We highly recommend the staining tech-
nique (e.g., methylene blue), which provides an improved abil-
ity to differentiate cellular morphology. We also recommend 
having technicians follow a group of animals for several days to 
understand the cyclical changes in the appearance of the vari-
ous cell types. Each vaginal smear should be evaluated without 
previous information on the individual female’s cycling status. 
However, do not hesitate to refer to earlier vaginal smears to 
help identify the overall cyclical pattern. Then, continuous 
monitoring and recording of the stages for two to three more 
cycles. During training or in a regulated reproduction study 
where estrous cycle observations are required, vaginal smears 
should be done each day concurrently to reduce variability in 
estrous cycle patterns. Also, the number of technicians observ-
ing either a wet or stained vaginal smear in a study should be 
controlled to lessen the potential for inconsistent observations 
between technicians. The technicians should simultaneously 
read the same vaginal smears to improve stage interpretation 
reliability if differences are observed.
The cell morphology observed in a vaginal smear – par-
ticularly when smears are taken during transitional stages – 
does not always fit clearly into the expected stages of diestrus, 
proestrus, estrus, and metestrus. Thus, it is essential to estab-
lish detailed requirements in the laboratory SOP for defining 
estrous cycle stages for recording proper descriptions of vagi-
nal smear cell types.
As mentioned previously, photoperiods must be well con-
trolled to maintain regular cycling. Typical photoperiods are 
12 hours of light and 12 hours of darkness; in some labora-
tories, 14 hours of light and 10 hours of darkness are used. 
Exposure to constant light can induce persistent estrus, lead-
ing to continuous sexual receptivity, constant vaginal corni-
fication, and cessation of ovulation [153, 154]. In contrast, if 
the test article disrupts estrous cyclicity by causing persistent 
diestrus, fertility will be reduced as female rats will not be 
sexually receptive during vaginal diestrus, and pregnancy will 
not occur [155].
In addition to deciding when to initiate mating (typically, 
on the evening of proestrus) and providing evidence that the 
female has mated (sperm-positive smear or plug), the vagi-
nal smear can also provide valuable information about the 
success of that mating and ensuing pregnancy. If pregnant, 
the female will no longer cycle, and the vaginal smear will 
present as diestrus until parturition. The vaginal smear may 
also differentiate confirmed pregnancy from pseudopreg-
nancy (prolonged luteal phase). This is determined by the 
number of days the vaginal smear remains leukocytic (in 
rats, pseudopregnancy is approximately 14 days; pregnancy 
is 21–22 days).
Because estrous cyclicity indicates hormonal status and 
reproductive function, significant disruption of cyclicity 
should be accompanied by other measurable reproductive 
changes (e.g., reduced receptivity, mating, and fertility). Subtle 
changes in estrous cycling without reproductive outcomes 
should not be considered an adverse reproductive effect. The 
complete termination of estrous cyclicity in response to test 
article exposure should be regarded as adverse. This termi-
nation may reflect abnormal fluctuations in the hypothalamic, 
pituitary, and/or ovarian endocrine environment. However, 
without evaluating additional critical reproductive endpoints 
using a weight-of-evidence approach, all estrous cycle staging 
changes should be cautiously interpreted before being deemed 
adverse.
38.8.1.1  Confirmation of Pregnancy
When the uterus is examined at laparohysterectomy, fetuses 
or resorption sites are considered evidence of pregnancy. The 
pregnancy (conception) index for each treatment group can be 
calculated by dividing the number of confirmed pregnancies

1936
Hayes’ Principles and Methods of Toxicology
in that group by the number of females mated. The pregnancy 
index is used to assess reproductive performance (similar to 
the fertility index in FEED and generational reproduction 
studies). Depression of this index relative to that of the control 
group might indicate a possible reproductive toxic effect, but 
only if treatment began before mating and implantation. Since 
treatment in EFD studies usually begins after implantation is 
completed, a low pregnancy index in this case instead suggests 
maternal health problems, poor animal husbandry, or that the 
time of initial dosing was miscalculated (i.e., begun too early, 
before implantation). Due to acclimation issues, pregnancy 
indices in pregnant animals shipped from suppliers are gener-
ally lower than those of animals bred in-house; this is espe-
cially true in mice. Definitive EFD studies will usually have 
to be repeated if substantial reductions in pregnancy indices 
occur among groups.
38.8.1.2  Number of Corpora Lutea (CLs)
CLs mark the sites on the ovary from which ova were emit-
ted during ovulation. Each corpus luteum contained a single 
ovum; thus, the number of CLs equals the number of ova 
available for fertilization. The number of CLs on each ovary 
is recorded for both rats and rabbits; however, this is unneces-
sary for mice because of the difficulty differentiating luteal 
tissue from the ovary. If the CLs outnumber the total num-
ber of implantations (i.e., the number of fetuses, pups, plus 
resorption sites), the litter experienced preimplantation loss. 
Because dosing is not initiated until after implantation in a 
standard EFD study, an increase in preimplantation loss in this 
study cannot be a test substance–induced effect. When such a 
finding is observed, reviewers (Study Director, report writers) 
should examine study data to verify (1) that dosing did not 
begin prematurely (i.e., before completion of implantation) and 
(2) that there is no evidence of environmental stress in the ani-
mal facility. However, in a FEED or generational reproduction 
studies, preimplantation loss is a primary endpoint to evaluate 
because dosing begins before mating.
38.8.1.3  Gravid Uterine Weight and 
Corrected Maternal Body Weight
Together with the ovaries, the pregnant uterus is considered 
the “product of conception.” In EFD studies, the uterus is 
excised by transecting the vagina inferior to the union of the 
uterine horns and incising the mesentery connecting them to 
the posterior body wall. The weight of these organs is a helpful 
measurement when the pregnant animal carrying few fetuses 
per litter is compared with other animals with many fetuses 
per litter. For instance, if a control animal were to have many 
more fetuses and/or offspring in a litter (reproduction studies) 
than is usually expected, then the average fetal or pup weight 
within that litter is likely to be less than the typical mean fetal 
or offspring weight. Conversely, in females with litters with 
only a few (e.g., two or three) fetuses or pups (but not a high 
number of resorptions), the average fetal or pup weight is fre-
quently much greater than the average fetal or pup weight. 
Even though the mean pup weights in the two preceding exam-
ples may be significantly different from each other, the gravid 
uterine weights often will not differ. Thus, the gravid uterine 
weight can determine how much the pregnant female gained 
from pregnancy, regardless of the number of fetuses.
The corrected maternal body weight (or carcass weight) is 
calculated by subtracting the gravid uterine weight (weight 
of the uterus plus contents) from the final body weight of 
the pregnant dam/doe before sacrifice. Comparing this mea-
surement among groups allows an investigator to determine 
whether test article–induced adverse body weight changes 
were caused primarily by toxic effects on the mother (in 
which case, corrected body weight will be affected) or on the 
fetal-placental unit (in which case, corrected body weight will 
likely be unchanged).
38.8.1.4  Numbers of Implantations, Resorptions, 
Living and Dead Fetuses, and Pups
The contents of the uterus are examined by incising the organ 
along the antimesometrial border. The numbers and locations 
of implantation sites are recorded. Implantation sites include 
viable and dead fetuses as well as resorptions. Resorptions are 
classified as early (unrecognizable embryonic tissue) or late 
(sites with recognizable fetal tissue). Dead fetuses are similar 
in development to live fetuses but are not viable at laparohys-
terectomy. In rodents, mainly rats and mice, dead offspring 
may present as resorptions or dead fetuses. This type of find-
ing is termed “fetal wastage.” Rabbits either resorb or abort 
their litters. Together, these findings are considered post-
implantation losses. They are expressed as a percentage of the 
total number of implantations per litter. The causes of fetal 
wastage include:
•	 Direct lethal effects of the test substance
•	 Lethal malformations of the fetus (whether sponta-
neous or test substance–induced)
•	 Maternal toxicity (whether test substance–induced or 
because of disease)
•	 Environmental stress
The presence of a dose-response relationship for fetal wast-
age strengthens conclusions about the developmental toxicity 
of the test substance. When one type of post-implantation loss 
predominates (either resorptions or dead fetuses), it may be 
possible to deduce the time or stage in development when the 
test article was toxic to the developing organism.
In addition to post-implantation losses, the number of live 
fetuses and pups in the litter is also recorded. When presented 
as the percentage of implantations per litter, this endpoint pro-
vides a measure of the developmental or postnatal toxicity of 
the test substance that includes its ability to kill offspring dur-
ing all stages of development.

38.8.2  Fetal and Pup Data
38.8.2.1  Fetal and Pup Weights
Offspring body weight is a sensitive endpoint for assessing 
toxicity. Because the sizes of fetuses and pups vary within any 
litter, the endpoint to be evaluated is the mean fetal or pup 
body weight. Reduced mean offspring body weight in treated 
groups compared with control values is evidence of develop-
mental delay. When the number of fetuses and pups per litter 
is similar between control and treated groups, decreased mean 
fetal or pup body weight in a treated litter generally implies a 
test substance–related effect. Often, reduced mean fetal or pup 
body weights may be the only sign of developmental toxicity. 
Typically, male fetuses and pups weigh more than females (so 
the similarity of sex distribution within groups should be veri-
fied). Also, individual fetal and pup body weights tend to be 
greater in litters with fewer fetuses.
Interpretation is unclear when reduced mean fetal or pup 
body weights are the only finding in EFD and reproduction 
studies. Modest fetal weight reductions may be only tempo-
rary; that is to say, pups that were smaller at birth may increase 
in weight, size, and maturation during the postnatal period, 
thereby abolishing any appreciable differences between 
treated and control pups. In other cases, the fetal weight reduc-
tion may be lasting (i.e., offspring fail to recover after birth). 
The possibility that fetal weight differences may disappear 
during the perinatal period can be assessed by considering 
available pup growth and viability data from reproduction 
studies wherein the pups are allowed to mature.
In reproductive toxicity studies, pups in the treated groups 
may experience decreased body weight during the latter half 
of the lactation period. Such weight losses can be directly 
attributed to an effect of the test substance on the pups as they 
start to eat the mother’s feed beginning around PND 10–12. 
Based on the pups’ relatively small size, their intake of the test 
substance can reach higher dosages than those intended for 
their mothers.
Extremely small fetuses, described as “runts” or “stunted” 
animals, are classified as malformed. The criteria used to clas-
sify runts vary from laboratory to laboratory; however, we 
suggest using runt or stunted classification when body weights 
are two or three standard deviations less than the mean control 
fetal body weight or 25–30% below the historical mean control 
body weight.
38.8.2.2  Fetal Examinations
Live offspring are usually inspected for the presence of exter-
nal, visceral, and skeletal alterations. Several detailed meth-
ods for examining offspring in preliminary and definitive EFD 
studies have been published [156–165]. Fetal morphology 
exams must be carefully conducted by a skilled and trained 
technical staff with experience in the species evaluated. Viable 
fetuses are removed from the uterus, weighed, examined for 
external malformations, and the sex is recorded. Sex in rodents 
is determined externally (rabbits internally) by observing the 
anogenital distance, which is longer in males than females; 
external sex is confirmed subsequently by internal examination 
that assesses whether the gonads have descended (males) or 
not (females). One-half of the viable rodent fetuses and all via-
ble rabbit fetuses undergo visceral examination by fresh tissue 
dissection, fixed-whole-body dissection, or the Wilson razor 
blade-sectioning technique (decalcified specimens) following 
fetal euthanasia. The remainder of the rodent fetuses or all of 
the rabbit fetuses after the visceral examination is prepared for 
the visualization of skeletal structures by staining with dyes 
specific for bone (alizarin red S) [166, 167] or bone and car-
tilage (alizarin red S—Alcian blue) [168]. To assess the brain 
for morphological changes, one-half of the rodent fetuses are 
decapitated and fixed or frozen to allow for freehand razor 
blade blade-sectioning sectioning of the heads. The remaining 
intact fetuses and all bodies are stained for skeletal visualiza-
tion. In rabbits, a variety of techniques have been used to eval-
uate the brain: (1) mid-coronal section of the fresh fetal skull, 
(2) a modification of the mid-coronal procedure whereby the 
fresh fetal whole brain is removed from the cranium and sec-
tioned, and (3) freehand serial sectioning of a fixed fetal rabbit 
head to permit visualization of the brain ventricles for shape 
and size, other brain tissues, and the eyes. It should be noted 
that fetal skeletal evaluations are optional in the preliminary 
EFD study [142]; in such cases, all the rodent fetuses may be 
prepared for visceral examination after external evaluation. 
Refer to the references provided above for further information 
on fetal examination procedures.
All morphological differences from the typical anatomi-
cal pattern should be recorded. The differences are classified 
as malformations or variations according to their severity. 
Malformations are extreme anatomical changes that inter-
fere with the fetus’s viability, health, or quality of life. Spina 
bifida, hydrocephaly, interventricular septal defect of the mus-
cular portion of the septum, cleft palate, absence of digits, 
and cyclopia are examples of malformations. Variations are 
structural alterations that commonly appear in control ani-
mals. They are considered to have no significant effect on the 
fetus’s well-being and occur frequently. Asymmetrical sterne-
brae in rabbits and rodents, bifurcated gallbladder in rabbits, 
and left umbilical artery in mice are examples of anatomical 
variations. Whether a particular alteration should be classified 
as a malformation and/or a variation is a subjective process 
that depends on the training of the prosectors, experience, and 
understanding of fetal morphology. Divergence in classify-
ing the same fetal alterations by different observers has led to 
significant disagreements in the conclusions reached by other 
laboratories studying the same compounds. The subsequent 
sections offer some guidance concerning the classification of 
findings during fetal examinations.
There were no officially recognized training programs or 
certification boards for technicians conducting DART studies, 
until recently. Laboratory technicians depended upon on-the-
job training to learn the morphology of the fetuses routinely 
examined in EFD studies [141]. Additionally, no universally 
accepted fetal pathology glossary [169, 86, 87] for offspring 
morphologic effects has been developed and/or adopted; 
instead, laboratories depend on their Standard Operating 
Procedures designed using the references noted. However, 
there is now a certification board, the International Register 
of Fetal Morphologists, governed by the Royal Society of

Biology. The mission of the Register is to assure competency 
in offspring external, visceral, and skeletal examination tech-
niques in two laboratory animal species, as required to satisfy 
the regulatory requirements for nonclinical safety evalua-

to provide evidence that fetal morphologists are competent, 
adequately experienced, and active in this field of work (fetal-
morphologists@rsb.org.uk).
38.8.2.2.1  Gross Structural Changes
At laparohysterectomy, the gravid uterus and fetuses should 
be manipulated gently. Rough handling of fetuses before an 
external examination can induce artifactual subcutaneous 
hemorrhages. Study Directors and report writers should be 
especially suspicious of studies in which hemorrhages are 
the only signs of developmental toxicity. A clue that should 
lead one to investigate possible technician-induced hemor-
rhages is their occurrence in both the experimental and con-
trol groups. The fetuses should be recovered and examined 
quickly to avoid possible artifacts. For example, rodent and 
rabbit fetuses that remain in the uterus for prolonged times 
may present with flexed wrists and ankles that can be readily 
mistaken for carpal flexures [171]. Similarly, fetuses linger-
ing on the examining table for long intervals before being 
examined can develop hyperextended or stiff joints [184].
38.8.2.2.2  Visceral (Soft Tissue) Changes
The viscera (organs) of the body are also susceptible to devel-
opmental toxicity. Improper cutting of the umbilical cord can 
result in the backflow of blood from the placenta into the fetus, 
causing intra-abdominal hemorrhage or an apparent hemor-
rhagic liver. Careless probing or pulling on delicate fetal 
organs can tear the tissues. The edges of all organs, whether 
malformed or normal, are smooth. Malformed organs do not 
present with jagged edges’ instead, the presence of rough 
edges on an organ is an indication that the tissue was damaged 
during the dissection.
Alterations in the viscera are not as readily observed as 
those of the external body and skeleton. Visceral malfor-
mations occur in the heart and great vessels (e.g., ventricu-
lar septal defects, transposition of the great vessels), lungs 
(fused lobes), kidneys (e.g., agenesis of kidneys), diaphragm 
(e.g., diaphragmatic hernia), and other organs. Knowledge 
of the normal anatomy of the test species provides an essen-
tial context within which to evaluate visceral alterations. 
For instance, prosectors should know the typical shapes 
of organs, their usual relation to vessels, nerves, and each 
other, and the accepted range of normal patterns. Thus, they 
should know that the diaphragm exhibits both a muscular 
and a membranous region and that the membranous area can 
be transparent. Fetuses can be erroneously reported to show 
a diaphragmatic hernia if the prosector can see through it 
and surmises that a portion of the diaphragm is missing 
without probing the area to discern whether the membrane 
is present.
The viscera of test species differ markedly in some respects. 
For example, rabbits and mice possess gallbladders, but rats 
do not. Furthermore, changes in the organs of one species may 
qualify as a malformation, but the same change may not nec-
essarily be a malformation when seen in others. For exam-
ple, the presence of a ventral pancreas, accessory spleen, or 
retrotracheoesophageal subclavian artery are malformations 
when seen in rats. Still, they are considered within the range 
of normal patterns observed in rabbits. Additionally, absent 
gallbladders in rabbits are classified as a variation [170] but in 
mice the condition is a malformation.
38.8.2.2.3  Skeletal Changes
Many differences among the skeletal structures of fetuses are 
so common that various patterns are considered “normal.” 
Examples of multiple skeletal patterns routinely observed in 
fetuses include 13 or 14 pairs of ribs in rodents, 12 or 13 pairs 
of ribs in rabbits, and delayed ossification of sternebra num-
ber 5 in rodents and rabbits [171]. Minor changes in skeletal 
patterns are called variations. Variations do not adversely 
impact the health or longevity of affected fetuses. Findings 
such as reduced ossification in phalanges or sternebrae [172], 
permanent supernumerary ribs in the lumbar region [171], 
wavy ribs (which can repair themselves during the postna-
tal development) [173], and bent long bones of the extremi-
ties [174] are examples of variations. Although the biological 
significance of variations has not been determined, super-
numerary ribs in mice and wavy ribs in rats have often been 
observed in fetuses delivered by dams that exhibited nonspe-
cific maternal toxicity [175].
Although skeletal variations such as wavy or supernumer-
ary ribs in rabbits are not considered adverse developmental 
effects, they may indicate maternal toxicity or maternal stress. 
Skeletal variations are often the consequence of transient 
delays or differences in developmental schedules. This delay 
is due to the bones of rodent fetuses beginning to ossify rapidly 
during the last 48 hours of gestation. If fetuses are removed 
by laparohysterectomy 12–24 hours earlier than recommended 
(e.g., on GD 19 or 20, rather than on GD 21 for rats), devel-
opment (especially ossification) of some bony elements will 
not be complete. Although normal for their gestational age, 
the bones will appear to exhibit what would be described as 
incomplete or absent ossification and are likely to be recorded 
as variations. Had the fetuses been examined later, ossifica-
tion would have appeared normal. Thus, scheduling mistakes 
or early sacrifice times can lead to the reporting of spurious 
increases in developmental variations.
38.8.2.2.4  Additional Considerations
Knowledge of the embryology of the structures being evalu-
ated will aid in determining the classification of any observed 
alterations. For example, in rabbits, when a bifurcated or dupli-
cated gallbladder is attached to a single bile duct, the finding is 
not considered to be a malformation because these alterations 
ensue from slight shifts in the branching pattern of the hepatic 
diverticulum during development. Similarly, accessory liver 
lobes, spleens, and pancreas are not considered malformations 
in rabbits because they also originate from slight deviations 
in normal developmental processes. In addition, double stain-
ing the fetal skeleton to evaluate bone and cartilage allows the

1939
Female Reproductive and Developmental Toxicology
examiner to determine the absence, presence, or delay in ossi-
fication, which may help determine the significance of either a 
malformation or a variation.
EFD test results can be confounded by the inclination for 
the fetuses of a given litter to exhibit similar findings, thereby 
artificially increasing the apparent fetal incidence of any 
given finding. This propensity has been designated the “litter 
effect.” The litter effect is caused by the fact that all fetuses 
in a single litter are exposed to the same maternal environ-
ment as their littermates (accounting for the similarity in out-
comes of that litter). In contrast, the maternal environment 
differs from litter to litter within the same treatment group. 
Another potential difficulty in the interpretation of results is 
that, owing to a large number of offspring that are evaluated 
in EFD and reproduction studies, findings may too quickly 
achieve statistical significance if (1) they are analyzed with 
the statistical methods routinely used in toxicology studies, 
(2) the fetus or pup is treated as the sampling unit, and (3) 
the number of malformations versus the number of mal-
formed fetuses are considered (i.e., one fetus with five struc-
tural malformations is counted five times versus five fetuses 
that present with one structural malformation each). Do not 
count the total number of malformations observed in each 
group and analyze totals for statistical significance. Instead, 
the observation should be the number of fetuses exhibiting 
malformation(s) divided by the total number of fetuses in a 
particular litter (the litter proportion).
Several statistical procedures have been developed to 
analyze fetal and pup data [176, 177, 178, 141]. For regula-
tory purposes, the litter is the appropriate sampling unit for 
laparohysterectomy, fetal, and postnatal data [179, 127, 142]. 
Some statistical procedures express fetal and pup endpoints as 
mean incidence per litter and analyze that value to address the 
challenges of varying numbers of fetuses and pups per litter 
and the litter effect; others analyze the number of litters with 
fetuses or pups that exhibit a particular endpoint.
The choice of statistical methods should be clearly stated 
in both the protocol and the final report. The results of sta-
tistical analyses alone are not sufficient to judge whether the 
test substance should be considered a developmental toxicant. 
When the level of statistical significance is set at a probability 
of p ≥ 0.05, 1 of every 20 comparisons may be statistically sig-
nificant by chance alone. Due to the large number of observa-
tions that are made and analyzed in EFD studies (e.g., all the 
external, visceral, and skeletal anatomy), it should be expected 
that several observations per study will attain statistical sig-
nificance by chance alone. Consequently, other factors in the 
study must be considered to resolve whether the statistically 
significant finding is cause for concern.
Over-calling fetal/litter observations, especially skeletal 
observations, is an issue that can affect study interpretation. 
When too many fetal observations are reported, especially in 
a control group, it is difficult to differentiate fetal effects due 
to treatment from background findings of little or no concern. 
Over-calling “incompletely or reduced ossification” of bones 
creates much “noise” in the data. These are subjective classi-
fications used in EFD studies that can vary between laborato-
ries. Smaller fetuses may have a normal external, visceral, and 
skeletal appearance for their reduced body-weight (or stage of 
development), even though their anatomy may differ from that 
of control fetuses with normal term body weights. Further, the 
bones may appear less ossified than that of fetuses with nor-
mal body weights. When the skeletons are normal for the fetal 
body weight (even if the fetus is smaller than normal), tech-
nicians should avoid using the term “incomplete or reduced 
ossification”; instead, the significant finding is a reduced fetal 
body weight.
When a statistically significant dose-related increase in the 
incidence of a particular endpoint (above the concurrent con-
trol incidence) is observed, the laboratory’s HCD should be 
evaluated to assess whether the study’s findings are outside the 
range of a larger population of controls. When exposure to a 
test substance is associated with a demonstrable increase in the 
incidence of any DART endpoint compared with the spontane-
ous incidence, the test substance can be suspected of being a 
developmental and/or reproductive toxicant. The spontaneous 
incidence is estimated primarily from concurrent control data, 
but HCD may also be used. The test substance is a suspected 
teratogen if the increased incidence is for a congenital mal-
formation. Because of the importance of developmental toxic-
ity as a noncancer endpoint for human health risk assessment, 
determining the definitive existence of a causal relationship 
between exposure to the test substance and the appearance of 
the endpoint is crucial.
An essential consideration in determining developmental 
and/or reproductive toxicity is whether the finding at issue 
displays a dose-response. A positive dose-response is strong 
evidence for DART when the finding is statistically signifi-
cant in the higher dose group(s). However, positive dose-
related trends in the incidence of fetal and pup effects may 
also appear without attaining statistical significance in the 
high-dose group. It is essential to know the spontaneous inci-
dence rate of the observed finding in the test species in such 
cases. If the finding rarely occurs in the test species, then the 
dose-related trend will be an important factor in determin-
ing the DART of the agent. However, if the finding is seen 
regularly among controls, it may not be considered evidence 
of DART.
A second consideration is that the major EFD endpoints 
(e.g., death, malformation, reduced fetal and pup body 
weights) may be related to each other. The presence of one 
precludes the existence of others. For instance, dead fetuses 
and pups are typically not weighed or evaluated for malfor-
mations. Therefore, increased embryonic death (post-implan-
tation loss) in the high-dose group can explain increased mean 
percent malformation incidences observed in the low- or mid-
dose groups but not in the high-dose group. In other cases, 
when the low- and mid-doses induce no adverse fetal effects, 
but the high dose elicits extensive post-implantation loss, the 
test substance’s teratogenic potential may have been masked. 
However, it should be noted that the mechanisms that cause 
post-implantation loss are not always the same as those leading 
to malformations (see discussion in 180). Thus, it is possible 
for the test substance to cause embryonic or fetal deaths but 
no malformations.
A third consideration is whether the dam or doe experienced 
maternal toxicity during gestation. Therefore, understanding 
the association between maternal toxicity and embryo-fetal and

pup toxicity is essential. However, demonstrating a relationship 
between maternal toxicity and embryo-fetal and pup toxicity is 
challenging. If maternal toxicity is suspected, one should try 
to match up findings expected to result from maternal toxicity 
with the clinical observations, body weights, and body-weight 
gains of the maternal animal in question. For example, devel-
opmentally toxic effects of a test substance are those produced 
without maternal toxicity. However, when adverse fetal or pup 
effects occur in litters from females that experienced signifi-
cant maternal toxicity, the fetal or pup effects may have been 
produced directly (by the test substance) or indirectly (second-
arily to maternal effects). This type of analysis cannot be com-
pleted by examining summary mean litter values. Rather, one 
must examine the available individual animal data throughout 
gestation to ascertain if maternal alterations were apparent dur-
ing the critical periods of gestation.
In addition, as alluded to previously, maternal toxicity can 
be associated with a low incidence of “nonspecific” altera-
tions such as wavy ribs, reduced ossification of sternebrae and 
phalanges in EFD studies, or decreased fetal/pup body weight 
in all types of DART studies. Occasionally, adverse offspring 
effects appear at doses that cause only minimal maternal tox-
icity. In such instances, the findings should not be interpreted 
to have been caused by maternal toxicity. Instead, the results 
suggest that the offspring and the dam or doe are susceptible 
to the same dosage of the test substance. Further interpreta-
tion of these outcomes involves measurement of test article 
exposure levels in the dam or doe which should be measured 
at defined days during gestation. Nevertheless, it is crucial to 
keep in mind that maternal toxicity in a pregnant dam or doe 
does not guarantee that adverse fetal or pup effects will occur 
because some test substances elicit profound maternal toxicity, 
while no apparent adverse effects on offspring are observed.
The final consideration is to realize that not all malforma-
tions are caused by test substance exposure. Malformations 
appear spontaneously [181], and a particular malformation can 
result from exposure to multiple test substances or conditions. 
Each test species has a background incidence of spontane-
ously occurring malformations. When malformations arise in 
the absence of a dose-response, they may be spontaneous in 
origin. When a rare malformation occurs, it is imperative to 
examine the laboratory’s HCD to establish whether the mal-
formation has been observed previously.
38.8.3  Historical Control Data (HCD)
DART studies (including multigenerational, extended one-
generation reproduction, and JAS) are conducted under regu-
latory testing guidelines to evaluate the effects of a test article 
on reproduction, EFD, and offspring. These evaluations gener-
ate data on many endpoints such as fertility, litter size, early 
and late resorptions, fetal and pup viability, fetal and pup body 
weights, frequency of fetal anatomical malformations (exter-
nal, visceral, and skeletal), and more [182]. Comparing data 
from groups dosed with the test article to a concurrent control 
group is considered the most relevant approach for interpret-
ing adverse effects. In a well-designed study, the concurrent 
control group will be of the same strain, age, and housed in 
the same environmental conditions (temperature, humidity, 
caging, lighting, feed, water, etc.) as the concurrently treated 
groups. Also, they will be exposed to the same amount and 
handling, including possibly dosing procedures. These prac-
tices control for possible confounding factors such as the type 
and amount of environmental “stress” (e.g., handling, dos-
ing, noise) to which animals are exposed in the laboratory. 
However, differences between the concurrent control and 
treated groups may arise by chance alone and on some occa-
sions, may even appear to be dose-related. For example, a con-
current control value for a given endpoint may be lower than 
expected, leading to what may seem to be an increase in the 
groups dosed with the test article.
Conversely, a higher than normal value in the concurrent 
control group may mask an actual increase in the groups 
dosed with the test substance. These limitations of the con-
current control group are of particular concern when inter-
preting fetal malformation data. Malformations are rare 
events (defined by the US FDA as “an endpoint that occurs 
in less than 1 percent of the control animals in a study and 
historical control animals”) [183] that can be better charac-
terized when incidences in both the concurrent control and 
treated groups are compared to a more extensive set of con-
trol values. Typically, HCD from the conducting laboratory 
and a time frame contemporary to when the study is done 
is best for comparison purposes. The confidence in a labora-
tory’s HCD increases as more data are accumulated. As vari-
ability decreases, the database becomes more representative 
of the population. A Study Directors’ challenge is to conduct 
enough studies to collect sufficient background data to have a 
reliable HCD to refer to when interpreting data. This process 
can take years for DART data overall and fetal malformations 
and variations. To accelerate this process, laboratories can 
design and conduct technical training studies to continuously 
train new technicians and collect data from control animals 
for use as historical data. In doing this, valuable embryo-fetal 
control data can be obtained in a few months. Longer and 
more complex reproductive toxicity study designs can be bro-
ken up into discrete phases that can also yield historical data 
within a relatively short time compared to an actual study, 
which can take 6–9 months or longer to complete. Such stud-
ies should be designed to mimic, as closely as possible, the 
design of the regulated EFD or reproductive toxicity studies, 
including strain, supplier, number of animals per group, mat-
ing procedure (in-house or timed-mated by supplier), age, 
RoA, duration and frequency of dosing, etc.
The prudent approach is to have HCD before initiating regu-
lated DART studies. These data can help one understand the 
normal range for a given endpoint and to assist in identifying 
the degree of biological variability over time in the endpoint. 
Variability may occur due to several external factors such as 
genetic changes in a strain, changes in the breeding facility, 
seasonal changes, change in the animals’ diet, the experience 
of the technical staff handling and dosing animals, and record-
ing fetal and pup observations. [184].
Animals housed in the testing facility but not part of the 
control group(s) on toxicology studies can be an additional 
source of background data for a laboratory. These data can be 
invaluable when no HCD are yet available. Some testing facili-
ties have “holding/training colonies” from which background

data in untreated animals can be obtained. Routine training of 
technical staff on animal procedures is one opportunity to col-
lect background data. For example, instead of discarding the 
vaginal lavage samples collected by trainees during routine 
training, the samples can be read, thus providing data on back-
ground animals housed under similar laboratory conditions. 
Data can be obtained on rat and mouse estrous cycle patterns, 
which vary considerably in animals housed under different 
environmental conditions. Keep in mind that these animals are 
not subjected to the same methods/conditions to which study 
controls are exposed; any differences should be considered 
when using such data for interpreting study findings.
38.9  Comments Regarding the 
Essentials of Report Review
The utility of the information presented in the preceding pages 
is intended not only to design and execute quality DART stud-
ies but also to assist in the evaluation of study reports intended 
for regulatory submission. In this section, we identify impor-
tant elements for inclusion in the report as well as consid-
erations or circumstances that can affect acceptance and 
interpretation of the study results in a regulatory environment.
38.9.1  Animals
38.9.1.1  Appropriate Test Animals
Disease-free animals of similar reproductive age, parity, and 
uniform genetic backgrounds should be used. Females should 
be nulliparous (virgin) because pregnancy confirmation in 
previously pregnant females cannot be accurately determined. 
Laboratory animal husbandry practices should comply with 
the regulation issued by the National Research Council [185] 
and the US Department of Agriculture [186].
We use laboratory animals to assess the potential effects of a 
test article on reproduction and embryo-fetal/postnatal devel-
opment to properly characterize the potential risks of exposure 
to the test article for women and men of reproductive age and 
children before and for children after birth. Consequently, it is 
critical to understand the comparative stages of embryogenesis 
and the physiological and functional development of the ani-
mal models used for human development.
38.9.1.2  Choice of Species
In EFD studies, the traditional test species are the rodents (rat 
or mouse) and one non-rodent species (typically, rabbit). The 
use of other species may be acceptable. However, a justifica-
tion for the species’ selection will be required (i.e., a robust 
HCD consisting minimally of fertility, fecundity, post-implan-
tation loss, fetal viability and malformation, and variation fre-
quency). Ideally, the species that responds to the test article 
most like humans should be used to estimate risk. However, 
when it is unknown which species reacts to the test article most 
like humans, the most sensitive species is used for assessing 
risk based on the rationale that humans are at least as sensitive 
as the most sensitive animal species [187]. Species selection 
for vaccine testing (with or without adjuvants), however, is dif-
ferent in that testing in only one species (e.g., rat, mouse, rab-
bit) is acceptable [142]. In this case, immunogenicity and the 
ability to administer a proper dose are critical factors in spe-
cies selection for vaccine testing. NHPs should only be used 
in vaccine EFD studies when the rat, mouse, and rabbit do not 
elicit an immune response. However, when biopharmaceuti-
cals are pharmacologically active only in NHPs, it is accept-
able to conduct EFD studies in this animal model.
Animal models of disease, genetically modified models, 
and surrogate molecules can help evaluate the pharmacologi-
cal effect on EFD and reproduction. Studies in disease mod-
els can be particularly crucial when the data obtained from 
healthy animals could be confusing or not applicable to the 
clinical disease conditions. As for the testing of biopharma-
ceuticals, the animal models used should be pharmacologi-
cally appropriate for the EFD and reproductive endpoints to 
be evaluated [142].
38.9.2  Test Article, Exposure, and Route
38.9.2.1  Purity of the Test Article
Impurities in the test article may play an essential role in 
potential reproductive or embryo-fetal toxicity. Therefore, the 
Study Director should include information on the purity of the 
test article and any measured impurities in the final report. 
Usually, the active ingredient (i.e., the technical material 
intended for commercial use) is tested; consequently, testing 
the final product formulation is considered unnecessary.
38.9.2.2  Dosing Formulations
Test articles are rarely dispensed neat but instead formulated 
in a dosing vehicle, drinking water, or feeding for the admin-
istration of test animals. The concentration of the test article 
in the exposure formulation must be accurate. Predosing and 
postdosing analyses of the exposure formulations should be 
performed to confirm the calculated concentration accuracy. 
In addition, homogeneity and stability analyses should be con-
ducted if the sponsor does not provide the information. A sig-
nificant problem in reproductive and EFD studies occurs when 
the administered dose of the test article is not the intended 
dose. This problem commonly occurs when the analyzed con-
centration of the test article in the exposure formulation is not 
the same as the nominal or target concentration specified in 
the protocol. The analytical concentrations of the exposure 
formulations should fall within ±15% of the target concentra-
tions. The study could be rejected if the analytical exposure 
concentrations are outside this target range.
38.9.2.3  Vehicle
When a vehicle is used to administer the test article, a control 
group of animals should be given an equal volume of the vehi-
cle without the test article. The vehicle should not cause either 
parental or EFD toxicity. If there is inadequate information

concerning the vehicle’s potential toxicity, justification for the 
choice of vehicle should be provided. A sham untreated con-
trol group may be required if the effects of the vehicle on the 
dosed animal are unknown.
Propylene glycol, a dosing vehicle commonly used in DART 
studies, has been associated with adverse EFD and reproduc-
tive effects. However, propylene glycol did not cause maternal, 
reproductive, or EFD toxicity when used in rat fertility stud-
ies or rat and rabbit EFD toxicity studies [188, 189]. Similarly, 
Gad [190] reported no maternal and embryo-developmental 
toxicity when propylene glycol (w/w 60/40: purified water/pro-
pylene glycol, in the rat at 5 mL/kg/day) was used in an oral 
EFD study in rats. (For further information on vehicles used in 
DART studies, see 190.)
38.9.2.4  Exposure & Route
The treatment route for EFD and reproductive studies is 
selected according to the expected route for the drug’s clini-
cal use or the intended route of human exposure to the drug. 
However, if adequate exposure cannot be reached using the 
clinical or expected route or if the clinical/desired exposure 
route is not achievable, a different route should be studied.
The animals should be dosed at the same time each day; 
if possible, no more than 2 hours should elapse between dos-
ing of the first and last animal. Embryological events typically 
occur during very narrow windows of time during gestation; 
this is especially true in species with short-gestation periods, 
such as those used in EFD studies [42]. Thus, the timing of 
exposure is critical.
38.9.3  Test Groups
38.9.3.1  Importance of a Concurrent 
Control Group
A concurrent control group’s objective is to represent the nor-
mal range of untreated and vehicle-treated animal values. 
Therefore, all treated and control groups should be run con-
currently. While staggering pregnancy induction within dose 
groups is acceptable mainly in EFD studies, the mean time 
of pregnancy induction should not differ significantly among 
the treated and control groups. Spreading the presumed preg-
nant animals over several days is done because there are many 
animals in each group to process, e.g., laparohysterectomies 
and fetal examinations. Therefore, staggering allows you to 
process the animals over a short period of 4–5 days, balanced 
across all dose groups. Prolonged periods before achieving the 
required number of presumed pregnant females in the study 
would suggest a mating problem. Such factors may cause poor 
health among the animals or a stressful environment in the 
animal facility.
38.9.3.2  Treatment Groups
There should be enough treatment groups to establish a dose-
response. For example, a control and three treatment groups 
should be minimally in a dose range-finding study. A control 
group and three treatment groups are acceptable in a definitive 
study. Furthermore, an adequate number of animals per group 
is essential for statistical power for results. The required mini-
mum number of pregnant rodents is 20 and pregnant rabbits 
is 16 [142, 191]. Typically, more animals are assigned to each 
group to ensure an ample number of pregnant animals at the 
end of the study.
38.9.3.3  Dosage Selection
Dosage selection should be based on dose range-finding stud-
ies and possibly data from previously conducted toxicological 
and pharmacological studies with the test article. Except when 
the test article’s biological, physical, or chemical properties 
limit the exposure amount, the highest dosage should cause 
minimal maternal toxicity, and the low dosage should cause no 
parental, fetal, or offspring adverse effects. When no maternal 
toxicity is observed, the highest dose should be the limit dose, 
1 gm/kg/day.
Dosages that produce excessive maternal toxicity may result 
in an unexpected number of maternal deaths and abortions. If 
this occurs, the study may need to be repeated. If the highest 
dosage causes no significant maternal toxicity, the study may 
also have to be repeated.
38.9.4  Documentation
The work is not over when the last animals are euthanized or 
the pathology results have been completed. Documentation 
of the entire study, from the early assessment of potential doses, 
through study design, day-to-day observations, any deviations 
from protocol, as well as integration and interpretation of results, 
is of paramount importance. The study report will memorial-
ize the outcome of the experiments and will form the basis for 
proximate regulatory review and, potentially, risk assessments 
decades in the future. This section lays out the expectations for 
the content in study reports to ensure that they are both accept-
able for current purposes and exhibit longevity for risk assess-
ments in decades to come.
38.9.4.1  Study Protocol
The protocol is a detailed description of all aspects of the 
planned study, including test substance, dosage levels, mode of 
exposure, numbers of animals per group, and all observations 
to be made during the study. Many of these aspects of the study 
design are addressed in regulatory guidelines, which should be 
referenced in the design and development of the study proto-
col. The study protocol should clearly define the timing and 
types of all parental, fetal, and pup observations, including all 
fetal and pup examination methods. If these methods are not 
adequately addressed in the protocol, they should be available 
in the laboratory’s standard operating procedures. These pro-
cedures must be clear and explicit.
Although the protocol must meet the appropriate regula-
tory agencies’ guidelines and testing requirements, the test-
ing requirements must be understood as minimum data needs.

Additional testing or modification of routine study designs 
may be required to assess a particular agent’s parental, 
embryo-fetal, and postnatal offspring toxicity potential. For 
example, deviations from basic protocols are acceptable with 
proper reasoning. Examples of this would be when a postnatal 
phase may be necessary to distinguish the dilated renal pel-
vis (a reversible condition) from true hydronephrosis (a kidney 
malformation) observed during fetal examinations. Or, when 
membranous ventricular septal defects were observed dur-
ing a fetal examination, the membranous septal defects were 
closed when the offspring were observed postnatally. When 
deviations are made, these should be specifically documented. 
Further, the documentation should include what the deviations 
were, why they were made, and what impact, if any, they might 
have had on the study’s integrity.
All experimental data must be accurately recorded and 
quality-assured. This can be achieved by performing data 
inspections and audits according to the GLPs regulations 
promulgated by the US FDA [192] and guidance that was 
subsequently developed by the European Chemical Industry 
Ecology and Toxicology Center [193], OECD, [194] and the 
US EPA [195, 196]. Compliance with GLPs provides a frame-
work for the practice of good science and helps facilitate the 
reconstruction of a study in the event it becomes necessary. 
Although these guidelines’ purpose is to ensure the data’s 
quality and integrity, they were not intended to limit informed 
scientific judgment when the data are inconclusive.
38.9.4.2  Final Reports
Report writing is not a simple undertaking. Before writing 
the final report, report writers and Study Directors must think 
about (1) who will review the report, e.g., client, regulatory 
authorities, (2) be familiar with the regulatory requirements 
where the report will be submitted, and (3) consider that the 
reviewers are not subject-matter experts.
All reports submitted as final must be signed and dated, 
minimally, by both the Study Director and the Director of 
Quality Assurance, and conform with the GLP Regulations. 
Any report that fails to be signed and dated should be consid-
ered a draft report that may be changed. It should be assumed 
that unsigned reports do not represent the final conclusions of 
the laboratory. Besides, it is incumbent on the Study Director 
to make sure the text and table formats are prepared to meet 
the reader’s and the regulatory reviewer’s needs.
38.9.4.3  Presentation of Findings
Well-organized, concise, correct, and transparent table formats 
should be used to present both individual animal and sum-
mary data because they facilitate both scientific and regula-
tory audits and reviews. The reviewer must associate reported 
parental, fetal, and pup findings with individual animals. 
Also, the data must be presented to allow for the identification 
of individual females that showed any given clinical sign(s) 
on any given day and to identify individual resorption sites, 
fetuses, and pups that presented with anatomical variations 
or malformations. The data should be displayed such that the 
reviewer can readily access the identity of any parent show-
ing clinical signs on any pre-GD, GD, and PND. All reported 
mean data should be carefully compared with the individual 
data from which the mean was calculated for possible incon-
sistencies. The appropriate use of statistical methods should be 
verified. A maternal NOAEL (NOEL) and developmental or 
pup NOAEL should be reported based on the data presented.
38.10  Conclusion
Developmental, reproductive, and juvenile animal studies are 
specialized sub-areas of regulatory toxicology and compo-
nents important for assessing a test substance’s potential risk 
to human development. Due to the complexities inherent to 
mammalian reproduction, interspecies differences in placen-
tation and gestation periods, and the uniqueness of the req-
uisite study designs, numerous interrelated endpoints must 
be evaluated to assess the potential adverse effects of a test 
substance in pregnant females and their offspring. The com-
ments and insights provided above are offered to help educate 
non-developmental and reproductive toxicologists about this 
vital subject-matter. The authors have endeavored to present 
an overview of female reproductive biology from the perspec-
tive of the information routinely evaluated in DART, extended 
one-generation, and JAS reports. The information presented 
herein focuses on interpreting the data collected from studies 
required during regulatory review.
