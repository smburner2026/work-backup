34.1  Introduction
The cardiovascular system is largely protected from xenobiot-
ics that must first pass through the gut, liver, lungs, or skin to 
gain access to the heart or blood vessels. Many prototypical 
cardiovascular toxicants are intentionally delivered as medi-
cines orally or intravenously. Despite the relative protection, 
the essential role of the cardiovascular system to mammalian 
biology means that toxic outcomes to the heart of blood vessels 
can be immediately life-threatening. Many toxicants can also 
have chronic impacts on cardiovascular function through the 
promotion of tissue remodeling that can lead to vulnerability 
to acute events, such as thrombotic vaso-occlusion (i.e., infarc-
tion or stroke) or ventricular arrhythmia, or decompensated 
heart failure.
Understanding toxicity to the cardiovascular system requires 
knowledge of the normal function of the major components, 
including the heart, vessels (arteries, capillaries, veins), and 
the blood. Assessment of toxicity thus arises from identifica-
tion of perturbations to normal physiology integrated with his-
topathological assessments.
34.2  Cardiac Function and Toxicity
The heart is a four-chambered organ principally comprising 
of cardiac muscle fibers, along with neural Purkinje fibers that 
guide the propagation of each conduction from the pacemak-
ing cells, and also the coronary vascular system. The heart 
beats approximately 2.5 billion times in a human lifetime, 
which requires a tremendous, continuous energy supply. 
Cardiomyocytes therefore have high content of mitochondria 
to provide that energy, principally through the metabolism of 
fatty acids, but also glucose and other substrates. Toxins that 
impact mitochondrial function are thus likely to have cardiac-
specific effects.
The chambers of the heart are designed to pump blood 
throughout the body, with the smaller atrial chambers pumping 
blood to fill the larger, more muscular ventricles, and the ven-
tricles then pumping blood to the lungs (right ventricle (RV)) 
or the rest of the body (left ventricle (LV)). There is an inti-
mate connection between ventricular function and pulmonary 
function. Impairments to cardiac output can lead to cardio-
genic pulmonary hypertension and edema, due to congestion. 
Similarly, toxicity or diseases affecting the pulmonary vascu-
lature can lead to pulmonary hypertension, which causes right 
ventricular hypertrophy and potentially failure. Timing of the 
atrial contraction precedes the ventricular contraction by a 
fraction of a second (typically 0.2 seconds in humans), such 
that there is time to fill the ventricles with blood in an optimal 
manner prior to ventricular ejection. This timing, controlled 
by a delay of the conduction propagation through the atrio-
ventricular (AV) node, can be altered by drugs and toxicants 
in a way that impairs efficiency of the heart. Furthermore, 
toxicants that stiffen or thicken the walls of the heart may also 
impede filling, an early outcome of diastolic heart failure.
Ejection of blood flows through either the pulmonary artery 
or the aorta. From there, blood travels in a pulsatile manner 
into sequentially smaller diameter vessels to the arterioles, 
then capillaries, venules, veins, and then out through the pul-
monary vein or vena cava and returns to the heart. The vas-
cular network spreads through every organ system and thus 
blood vessels are intimately involved in end-organ toxicity, 
either by the delivery of toxicants or as a target themselves. 
Numerous mathematical physiological relationships have been 
established for cardiac function and hemodynamics which are 
essential to our understanding of disease and toxicology; sev-
eral key equations are shown in Table 34.1.
34.2.1  Contraction and Pumping
Contraction of the heart begins with opening of L-type cal-
cium channels, which allows extracellular calcium to stimu-
late the release on intracellular stores of calcium from the 
sarcoplasmic reticulum in cardiomyocytes. Cytosolic calcium 
facilitates actin-myosin contraction; the magnitude of calcium 
flux from diastole to systole largely governs the strength of 
each contraction. Essential proteins contributing to pulsatile 
calcium flux include the ryanodine receptor, which enables the 
calcium-induced calcium release mechanism, the sarcoplas-
mic reticulum Ca2+-ATPase (SERCA2) pump, and phosphol-
amban. SERCA2 pumps calcium out of the cytosol into the 
sarcoplasmic reticulum after each contraction. Phospholamban 
is a direct inhibitor of SERCA2. Adrenergic stimulation can

34.2.2  Cardiac Hypertrophy
Cardiomyocyte growth can be stimulated by diverse stimuli. 
Growth can be triggered indirectly in response to increased 
vascular resistance, leading to sustained calcium increases 
that upregulate calcineurin and promote NFAT transcriptional 
activity (1). Hormonal factors such as angiotensin-II and endo-
thelin-1 can promote hypertrophy through histone deacety-
lase function, similarly downstream of calcium signaling. 
Inflammation that triggers cardiomyocyte NFkB pathways 
can also stimulate growth. Growth can be of a physiological 
nature, such as in response to sustained physical training (e.g., 
running a marathon). In pathological growth, however, the car-
diac thickening tends to be inward, reducing the volume of the 
cardiac volume. Concomitantly, the increased thickness of the 
ventricular wall impeded diastolic filling. Thus, pathological 
hypertrophy reduces the efficiency and reserve of the heart. 
Over time, toxic or pathological conditions can lead to cardiac 
cell death that weakens the muscle and causes dilatation of the 
ventricle.
34.2.3  Myocardial Cell Death
Toxic insults trigger a series of reactions in cardiac cells lead-
ing to measurable changes in calcium levels, mitochondrial 
function, and transcriptional outcomes. Mild injuries can be 
repaired; however, severe injuries will lead to cell death via 
accidental (necrosis) or regulated (apoptosis and other) cell 
death. If the cell survives the insults, structural and functional 
adaptations will take place. Cell death, however, leads to weak-
ening of the ventricular wall and the scarring that replaces 
regions of dead cells can be an impediment to diastolic fill-
ing. Apoptosis was found to be involved in cardiomyopathy in 
1994 (2). The loss of cardiac myocytes is a fundamental part 
of myocardial injury that initiates or aggravates cardiomyopa-
thy. Apoptosis is an important mode of myocardial cell loss, as 
has been demonstrated in heart failure patients (3). Myocardial 
apoptosis has been shown to play an important role in the car-
diac toxic effects induced by Adriamycin® (4, 5), an important 
anticancer agent whose clinical application is limited by its 
major side effect, cardiotoxicity. Exposure of primary cultures 
of cardiomyocytes to cadmium also induces apoptosis (6).
Many in vivo studies have shown that only a very small per-
centage of myocardial cell population undergo apoptosis under 
pathological conditions; for example, less than 0.5% of cells 
appeared apoptotic in myocardial tissue under the stress of 
dietary copper deficiency in mice (7). At first glance, this num-
ber seems to be too insignificant to account for myocardial 
pathogenesis; however, this would be a false statement. In a 
carefully designed time course study (8), it has been estimated 
that cardiomyocyte apoptosis may be completed in less than 
20 hours in rats. Because the heart is a terminally differenti-
ated organ, myocytes undergoing apoptosis will be lost; thus, 
the total cell loss can simply be accounted for by the rate of 
apoptosis plus necrosis. If apoptosis occurs at a constant rate 
of about 0.5% myocytes a day (7), the potential contribution of 
apoptosis to the overall loss of myocytes over a long period of 
time is significant.
Necrosis is a term that was widely used to describe myocar-
dial cell death in the past. Myocardial infarction, in particular, 
had been considered as a consequence of necrosis (9). Although 
it is now recognized that apoptosis contributes significantly to 
myocardial infarction (10), the significance of necrosis in myo-
cardial pathogenesis cannot be underestimated. The contribu-
tion of necrosis to cardiomyopathy induced by environmental 
toxicants and pollutants is particularly important. A critical 
issue is how to distinguish apoptosis from necrosis.
Apoptosis and necrosis were originally described as two dis-
tinct forms of cell death that can be clearly distinguished [36]; 
however, these two modes of cell death can occur simultane-
ously in tissues and cultured cells. In total, six specific types of 
cell death have now been identified in cardiomyocytes, includ-
ing apoptosis, necroptosis, mitochondrial-mediated necrosis, 
pyroptosis, ferroptosis, and autophagic cell death (11). These 
forms of cell death often overlap in terms of stimuli (e.g., TNFa, 
pathogen- or damage-associated molecular patterns, oxidative 
stress) and final signaling leading to death (e.g., caspase induc-
tion and plasma membrane disruption), but each has unique 
intracellular pathways that may be triggered. Necroptosis, for 
instance, depends on receptor-interacting protein kinase 1 
(RIP1) activation, while pyroptosis involves excessive NLRP3 
inflammasomal stimulation and gasdermin-induced lysis. The 
intensity and duration of insults may decide the outcome; thus, 
triggering events can be common for both types of cell death. 
A downstream controller, however, may direct cells toward a 
programed execution of apoptosis. If the apoptotic program is 
aborted before this control point and the initiating stimulus is 
severe, cell death may occur by necrosis (12). Alternatively, in 
acute injury, apoptotic cells can progress along a continuum to 
eventual necrosis. To distinguish the nature of cell death, there 
are specific markers that are prototypical for each that must be 
identified (11).
34.2.4  Electrophysiology
Pacemaking cells of the heart initiate the propagation of each 
cardiac cycle. Cells of the sinoatrial (SA) node are the normal 
pacemakers of the heart and initiate each beat with the open-
ing of voltage-gated calcium channels, that lead to a depo-
larization of the cytosol, followed by a rapid repolarization 
through the opening of potassium channels (Figure 34.1A). 
This depolarization is spread initially through the atrial tis-
sue via nerve-like Purkinje fibers and atrial myocytes. The 
conduction is delayed at the point of the AV node, which is 
the gate-keeper for transmission through to the ventricular 
cardiomyocytes. The AV node is composed of a similar cell 
type to the SA node pacemaking cells, but the AV node fires 
at a slower rate, allowing them to be entrained by the SA node 
cycle under normal circumstances, but acting as a pacemaking 
“safety net” should issues arise with the SA node function. 
The delay at the point of the AV node (only 0.1–0.2 seconds in 
a human) optimizes the ventricular filling prior to contraction.

From the AV node, electrical transmission proceeds through 
the Purkinje fibers of the bundle branch, located within the 
ventricular septal wall, then spreads evenly through to ven-
tricular cardiomyocytes. The monophasic action potential of 
ventricular cardiomyocytes differs from the pacemaking cells, 
being initiated in phase 0 by voltage-gated sodium channels 
and having a prolonged phase 2 wherein calcium is released 
into the cytosol to drive contraction (Figure 34.1B). Notably, 
drugs and toxicants can influence the shape and timing of the 
cardiac cells’ monophasic action potential, leading to changes 
in the heart rate and potentially predisposing cells to abnormal 
conductions (discussed below).
The ECG is a non-invasive method for determining the 
overall conduction of the heart. The overall ECG reflects 
a summation of all of these coordinated depolarization and 
repolarization events in the whole heart. The normal ECG 
propagation depolarizes atrial cardiomyocytes first, to allow 
well-timed filling of ventricles. Each normal ECG conduc-
tion begins with the P-wave, reflecting SA node and atrial 
depolarization (Figure 34.2A). Subsequently, the QRS com-
plex is typically the largest inflection of the ECG signal as 
the AV-node cells depolarize. The wave of depolarization to 
the ventricles must pass through the AV node, which delays 
the wavefront to allow for filling prior to ventricular depolar-
ization and contraction. The timing of each beat is a highly 
organized event, and can be altered by input from the nervous 
system (sympathetic and parasympathetic), as well as from 
the volume of blood returning to the heart. The overall repo-
larization of the ventricular mass is reflected as the T-wave. 
Human ECGs are most analogous to the ECG waveforms of 
larger animals, including non-human primates, dogs, and pigs. 
The most notable difference in rodent ECGs is the absence of 
the isoelectric ST segment (Figure 34.2B). The timing of each 
segment can be altered by physiological, pathological, or phar-
macological mechanisms. Adenosine, for instance, can pro-
long the PR-interval due to AV-node hyperpolarization. Many 
drugs, including sotalol and hydroxychloroquine, can prolong 
the QT interval. Excessive prolongation of the QT interval is 
a major concern for drug development, as this can become a 
substrate for a life-threatening ventricular arrhythmia known 
as Torsades de Pointes (French for “twisting of the points”).
In addition to Torsade de Pointes, many other negative 
electrocardiographic outcomes can be induced by toxicants 
(examples provided in Figure 34.2C–L). Ventricular arrhyth-
mias such as fibrillation and tachycardia pose the greatest risk 
of mortality. Atrial arrhythmias, such as flutter, fibrillation, 
or conduction block (slowing of conduction between the SA 
and AV nodes), may impair the efficiency of cardiac pumping 
and even predispose the heart to a major ventricular arrhyth-
mia. Notably, atrial fibrillation is also a major risk for blood 
stagnation and coagulation in the left atria, which can provide 
an embolic substrate for stroke. Thus, assessing the nature of 
electrocardiographic abnormality is important in terms of risk 
assessment.
In vivo measures of hemodynamics and ECG are a hallmark 
for toxicity and safety pharmacology studies and have been 
frequently employed for a variety of acute and chronic toxicol-
ogy studies. Surgical implantation of probes allows for either 
unrestrained (via radiotelemetry), jacketed, or tethered assess-
ments in real time in conscious, freely moving animals both 
large (non-human primate, pig, dog) and small (mouse, rat, 
rabbit). Numerous different approaches exist for blood pres-
sure measurement, but direct arterial catheters, connected to 
a force transducer, can be placed inside the lumen of major 
arteries (e.g., aorta and femoral), which affords a highly pre-
cise, continuous measure of pulsatile pressure. With direct

arterial measures, endpoints such as systolic, diastolic, and 
pulse pressure (difference between systolic and diastolic) can 
be easily obtained, along with heart rate.
Arrhythmias can arise from abnormalities at the cellular 
level, or from abnormalities related to tissue remodeling or 
interference with the normal conduction wavefront. Cellular 
events are typically due to metabolic impairment, such as 
during coronary flow obstruction (infarction), where insuf-
ficient oxygen is available to a region of cardiomyocytes. In 
such instances, the resting membrane potential is poorly main-
tained, and the calcium plateau (phase 2) is typically shorter. 
Such cells may have a more rapid firing rate owing to this 
abnormal monophasic action potential. Alternatively, certain 
drugs – especially potassium channel blockers like sotalol – 
can prolong the repolarization of cardiomyocytes excessively, 
to the point where phase 0 sodium channels reactivate prior to 
complete action potential recovery. In such instances, a phe-
nomenon called afterdepolarizations may occur. Early after-
depolarizations occur during phase 3 of the monophasic action 
potential; delayed afterdepolarizations occur during phase 4, 
but prior to the normal SA-originating conduction wavefront. 
In either case, the cell or cells that undergo afterdepolariza-
tions can be obstructive to normal wavefront propagation 
or may become ectopic pacemakers and capture the overall 
rhythm of the heart, typically leading to tachyarrhythmias 
such as Torsades de Pointes.
34.3  Vascular Function and Toxicity
The heart pumps blood through the aorta (outflow of the LV) 
and pulmonary artery (outflow of the RV). From these major 
conduit arteries, blood travels through successive bifurcations 
to smaller arteries, arterioles, capillaries, venules, veins, and 
then back to the main chambers of the heart from the vena 
cava (flowing into the right atrium) or the pulmonary vein (left 
atrium). Each successive stage of vessel has important but dis-
tinct physiological roles. Arterioles, for instance, are crucial 
to maintaining regional blood flow and vascular resistance. 
Capillaries are the major point of gas and nutrient exchange 
for distal organs. Venules and veins have a major role in bal-
ancing fluid volume and venous return, or cardiac preload. An 
essential function of arteries and arterioles is control of the 
internal diameter of the vessel via constriction or dilation of 
smooth muscles. Much of the regulation of this vascular tone 
is controlled by molecular communication between endothe-
lial cells that line the vessels and the smooth muscle cells that 
actively contract or relax.
34.3.1  Endothelial Cells
The endothelium is an essential homeostatic system with roles 
in target organ nutrient supply, regional blood flow, inflamma-
tion, coagulation, angiogenesis, and metabolism. Endothelial 
cells comprise the intimal, or inner surface lining, of all blood 
vessels in the body. Endothelial cells orchestrate responses 
to local injury, releasing cytokines to recruit leukocytes and, 
in the case of hemorrhage, vasoconstrictor agents to reduce 
flow and change the balance of coagulant (tissue factor) and 
antiaggregatory (prostacyclin) signaling. In vitro approaches 
to understanding endothelial responses to toxicants are numer-
ous and can include functional assays along with biomolecular 
(i.e., RNA, protein, metabolite) response assays. Novel co-
culture and 3D tissue models are constantly being developed 
to better understand the local environments relative to spe-
cific organs of interest, such as the blood-brain barrier of the 
cerebrovasculature or the gas exchange regions of the lungs 
or placenta. Such models are providing new insights into how 
toxicants may directly or indirectly impair endothelial func-
tion, although many essential aspects of vascular physiology 
are typically absent, including pressure and flow. As such, 
there is still considerable motivation to confirm outcomes in 
whole-animal models.
Endothelial nitric oxide synthase is a crucial enzyme for 
healthy vascular function. eNOS produces nitric oxide (NO), a 
major signaling molecule for the control of vascular tone, from 
the conversion of arginine to citrulline. eNOS is a dimerized 
enzyme that requires several cofactors, including tetrahydro-
biopterin (BH4). BH4 and NO react readily with superoxide, 
making this system highly sensitive, directly and indirectly, to 
many toxicants. In an intact vessel, NO diffuses to the smooth 
muscle to trigger relaxation and vasodilation. NO is also valu-
able as an inhibitor of platelet activation and inflammation. 
The ability of many toxicants to impair eNOS function is pos-
tulated to be an essential part of the broader risk for adverse 
cardiovascular events. The endothelium generates a number of 
other messengers that influence smooth muscle tone, including 
constrictors such as endothelin and thromboxane, and dilators 
such as hydrogen sulfide and prostacyclin.
34.3.2  Vascular Smooth Muscle Cells (VSMCs)
VSMCs, comprising the medial region of the vascular wall, 
primarily regulate the vascular tone through calcium-depen-
dent mechanisms, based largely on endothelial cell input and 
circumferential strain (i.e., blood pressure). Unlike cardio-
myocytes, which generate large intermittent contractions in 
response to a large, rapid cytosolic flux of calcium, the VSMCs 
regulated cytosolic calcium on a gradient of concentrations, 
permitting stable changes in contractile protein interactions. 
This means that blood vessels can maintain a specific inter-
nal diameter depending on the local blood flow needs of the 
tissue, rather than exhibit intermittent vasospasm. Calcium 
binds with calmodulin in VSMCs, which in turn activates 
myosin light chain kinase (MLCK). MLCK phosphorylates 
myosin light chain to increase interactions with actin fibers, 
driving contraction. The calcium release can be stimulated 
by a number of agents, including angiotensin-II and endothe-
lin-1. In certain pathologies, like hypertension, the contractile 
proteins can become sensitized to calcium, typically through 
activation of the RhoA-Rho Kinase pathway, which inhibits 
myosin light chain phosphatase, shifting the balance of sig-
naling toward force generation with lower levels of cytosolic 
calcium.
Under pathological conditions, VSMCs can dedifferentiate 
into a proliferative, migratory, and synthetic phenotype, which 
is central to the growth of atheromatous lesions. In chronic 
hypertension, VSMCs hypertrophy can leads to narrowing of

34.3.3  Atherosclerosis
The principal vascular pathology is a chronic inflammatory 
condition known as atherosclerosis, which is the root cause 
of many diseases, including coronary artery disease, angina 
pectoris, myocardial infarction, and many cerebrovascular/
transient ischemic attacks that can promote vascular dementia. 
Beginning in early life as lipid accumulation in the wall of the 
major arteries (aorta, coronaries, carotids), atherosclerosis is a 
progressive disease that can lead to fixed obstructions to blood 
flow and major coagulative events. Many risk factors exist for 
the development of atherosclerosis, including heritable predis-
position, sex, age, physical inactivity, and a diet low in fruits 
and vegetables. Inhaled toxicants such as particulate matter 
and cigarette smoke can promote atherosclerosis development.
The early atheroma lesions develop in response to mild vas-
cular injury, loosely termed, and involve the recruitment of cir-
culating leukocytes into the vascular wall. Once translocated 
into the vessel wall, the leukocytes differentiate into macro-
phages and begin to engulf accumulated lipids and cholesterol. 
Excessive uptake of lipids leads to further differentiation into 
inflammatory foam cells; thus, high levels of cholesterol are 
a constant promoter of atherosclerotic remodeling. As the 
lesion grows over decades, with accumulation of inflamma-
tory cells and (as described above) proliferation of VSMCs, 
the inner core of the lesion can become hypoxic, leading either 
to a necrotic core or angiogenesis to further supply nutrients 
and bolster the growth of the lesion. Furthermore, the endo-
thelial cells lining the lesion become unhealthy, reducing the 
generation of antiaggregatory molecules (NO, prostacyclin), 
and ultimately eroding away leaving behind a fibrous cap. The 
necrotic core and fibrotic cap are substrates of vulnerability for 
plaque rupture, an often fatal coagulative and vaso-occlusive 
outcome (infarction). Toxicants have been shown to play a role 
in many of the stages of atheroma development, from exac-
erbating the oxidative and inflammatory environment of the 
lesion to activating matrix metalloproteinases in the vascular 
wall that can lead to deterioration of the stabilizing extracel-
lular matrix fibers and promote rupture.
34.4  Essential Methodology
Unlike many organ systems of fields of biomedical science, the 
cardiovascular system demands that research extend beyond 
molecular tools to embrace a wide range of functional out-
comes (e.g., hemodynamics, cardiac pumping, ECG, vaso-
motor function). In addition, histopathology, imaging, and 
concrete knowledge of clinical/translation biomarkers are 
essential skillsets to undertake comprehensive screening of 
safety/toxicity of xenobiotics.
34.4.1  Ultrasound
Doppler ultrasound and flow measurements are a final essen-
tial tool for studying in vivo hemodynamics. Echocardiography 
applies ultrasound technology to permit imaging of the heart 
and large vessels in real time to assess the movement of the 
walls of the beating heart and flow of fluids (blood) through 
the chambers and vessels. Cardiac function, in terms of stroke 
volume and cardiac output, is easily obtained from even small 
laboratory models using high-frequency (~30 MHz) ultrasound 
probes. Additionally, alteration to the ventricular wall, in terms 
of hypertrophy or dilatation, can also be easily and accurately 
measured. Current technology permits advanced analysis of 
rapidly contracting mouse and rat hearts to assess indices of 
systolic and diastolic failure using tissue Doppler (13). High-end 
systems that allow for high-frequency ultrasound probes (30–
50 MHz) can even image fetal mouse hearts in the latter stages 
of in utero development (Figure 34.3). Notably, there are com-
plementary methods for in vivo imaging in preclinical models, 
including SPECT/CT and MRI. However, the rapid beating of 
the heart – at rates from 250 to 700 beats per minute depending 
on the model – makes such alternative methods expensive and 
time-consuming relative to the ultrasound technology. Vascular 
investigations may be more justifiable with MRI or SPECT/CT, 
and specific imaging markers of pathology can permit longi-
tudinal and clinically translatable studies. Gadolinium contrast 
imaging, for instance, can be used in conjunction with MRI to 
examine blood-brain barrier disruption (14).
34.4.2  Radiotelemetry
A limitation of Doppler ultrasound technology is the inter-
mittent measurements typically captured (e.g., once per day 
or once per week). Implantable flow probes can be utilized to 
capture continuous flow through arteries of interest (e.g., aorta 
or carotid) in an unrestrained (implantable or jacketed teleme-
try) or tethered preparation. While technology exists for rodent 
applications, such preparations can be exceptionally valuable 
for large animal research where reuse of the animal is possible 
for safety studies. Radiotelemetry methodology also allows 
for near-continuous collection of blood pressure, ECG, body 
temperature, and physical activity. Innovative technologies in 
this area are continuously being adapted, and devices can col-
lect numerous outcomes simultaneously. Blood pressure can 
be collected from various sites, including the carotid, aorta, 
and femoral artery. While a challenging surgical preparation, 
pulmonary pressure can also be collected in an instrumented 
model (Figure 34.4). The value of the continuous collection 
of unrestrained animals is in identifying relatively infrequent 
events, such as runs of tachyarrhythmia, bradyarrhythmias, 
skipped beats, or prolongation of QT interval (15). Measures of 
heart rate variability, which reflects many homeostatic inputs 
to the regulation of heart rate, including autonomic nervous 
system activity (16), are also readily obtainable from telemet-
ric recordings (13, 17).

34.4.3  Ex Vivo Isolated Organ Systems
Because the integrated hemodynamic system is highly capable 
of buffering stressors, it is often preferable to examine how a 
toxicant or drug can alter a more limited component of the car-
diovascular system. Isolated organ preparations have therefore 
been developed and optimized to eliminate feedback loops 
from neural networks.
34.4.3.1  Isolated Hearts
Isolated heart preparations have been used for roughly 
100 years to examine cardiac contractility, metabolism, and 
coronary flow (19). The Langendorff method uses a retrograde 
perfusion of oxygenated solution (such as Krebs-Henseleit 
solution or Tyrode’s) in the ex vivo heart, via the aorta. The 
retrograde flow closes the aortic valve and allows for coronary 
perfusion. The perfusion of nutrients keeps the heart pumping 
and enables dosing with drugs or toxicants directly, or hearts 
can be isolated from animals after exposures (20). A comple-
mentary model, the working heart preparation, relies on flow 
of blood/solutions in the normal physiologic direction but can 
also be utilized to study cardiac contraction and metabolism 
relative to toxic outcomes.
34.4.3.2  Isolated Vessels
Isolated vascular preparations have been a highly informative 
tool for toxicology research. Isolated preparations vary in the 
approach, allowing for in situ methods that leave much of the 
relevant physiology intact (21), isolated perfused vessel prepa-
rations at physiological (and controllable) internal pressures 
(22), to force-transduction methods of vessel rings or strips 
that eliminate a great deal of whole-body homeostatic feed-
back factors (23). These approaches can allow for the direct 
testing of toxicants by application in the vessel bath, or permit 
in vivo exposures with subsequent isolation of vessels from 
exposed animals. They also permit more concise and accurate 
dissection of molecular mechanisms of toxicity, by testing spe-
cific pathways of constriction (e.g., phenylephrine, thrombox-
ane, endothelin) or dilation (e.g., acetylcholine, shear stress, 
prostacyclin) or examining oxidative stress with antioxidants 
or radical scavengers.
34.4.4  Specialized In Vitro Approaches
34.4.4.1  Cardiomyocytes
In vitro approaches to understand electrophysiological effects 
of toxicants on monophasic action potential were very time-
consuming and challenging in terms of clinical/translational 
interpretation until the advent of inducible pluripotent stem 
cells (iPSCs), due to the differentiated and non-mitotic state of 
cardiomyocytes. A typical approach has been to use rat fetal 
cardiomyocytes, which still grew and divided in culture, or 
isolated adult cardiomyocytes which could be probed electro-
physiologically, but with very limited throughput. Adult car-
diomyocytes can be isolated after digestion of whole hearts 
using a Langendorff preparation, and used individually for 
electrophysiological assays, but these procedures are extremely 
time-consuming. However, electrophysiological assays permit 
the investigation of monophasic action potential disturbances 
and risk for afterdepolarizations and are therefore highly valu-
able for drug safety assessment.
The first advantage of the iPSC technique is that a consis-
tent cardiomyocyte population can be grown and utilized for 
electrophysiological tests of drug cardiotoxicity, especially in 
concert with new high-throughput multielectrode array tech-
nologies (24). The second benefit is the potential to utilize 
patient-derived iPSCs that may allow for a personalized medi-
cine approach to drug safety testing. Collectively, these tech-
nologies with iPSC-derived cardiomyocytes are transforming 
the methods for ensuring cardiac safety. Some studies, how-
ever, have failed to show a correlation between drug effects 
on subject-derived iPSC cardiomyocytes and subject QT pro-
longation (25). Rigorous protocols have been shown to mini-
mize variability, and improve prediction, while challenges 
remain, including varying methods for deriving pluripotency 
and redifferentiation into cardiomyocytes and intra-individual 
variability, the technique holds much promise for improved 
precision in cardiac safety testing.
34.4.4.2  Vascular Cells
In vitro approaches are often essential to understanding the 
impacts of toxicants on the eNOS/NO system and other vaso-
motor pathways. Direct toxicant impacts on eNOS can be iden-
tified as alterations in eNOS mRNA or protein expression, loss 
of dimerization of the eNOS protein, formation of peroxyni-
trite (and nitrosation of vulnerable protein tyrosine moieties) 
from the reaction of radicals superoxide and nitric oxide, and 
BH4 oxidation. Direct measurement of NO production, usu-
ally after experimental stimulation with acetylcholine, shear 
stress, or a calcium ionophore, is possible with sensitive and 
selective electrode detectors or spin-trap electron paramag-
netic resonance spectroscopy. Assessment of uncoupling of 
the eNOS dimer is also possible with modified electrophoretic 
techniques.
Endothelial cells can be obtained commercially or from 
relatively straightforward explant techniques. While numer-
ous cell lines exist and are used frequently (especially human 
umbilical vein endothelial cells), the ease of isolation makes 
primary endothelial cells a valuable option, as this approach 
allows for vascular bed-specific (e.g., cerebrovascular, coro-
nary, or placenta) research models. Furthermore, extracting 
and culturing primary cells from unique transgenic mod-
els or following in vivo exposures facilitates myriad creative 
study approaches to address mechanistic questions of toxicity. 
Emerging 3D tissue culture models are a further boon to the 
field, as endothelial cells are readily integrated with other sys-
tems to model the intracellular signaling at the vascular inter-
face with other organotypic cell types.
An essential role of endothelial cells is to maintain a barrier 
between contents of the blood and all tissues of the body. This 
varies between tissues, as endothelial cells of the liver have 
large gaps, known as fenestrations, that allow for cells and 
macromolecules and subcellular particles (e.g., lipoproteins) 
easy access to hepatocytes. Endothelial cells of the brain,

34.4.5  Animal Models
Cardiac hypertrophy can be manifested from a variety of 
approaches in animal models, typically through induction of 
hypertension, overuse of the heart, or through genetic models. 
Certain transgenic models, such as the CREBA133-expressing 
mouse or the spontaneous cardiomyopathic hamster model, 
develop heart failure with many classic translational histo-
logic, functional, and molecular features, such as ventricular 
wall thinning, reduced ejection fraction, and fetal isoform 
myosin heavy chain gene expression (26). Inducible mod-
els include angiotensin-II or eNOS inhibitor (e.g., L-NAME) 
infusion (typically using an osmotic minipump), or surgical 
models like thoracic aortic banding or arterial-venous fistula 
induction (27). Other long-term hypertension models will also 
induce cardiac remodeling that can in some models lead to 
failure, including treatment with doxorubicin and also genetic 
models (28, 29). Of note, many pulmonary hypertension mod-
els can be used to examine right heart remodeling, includ-
ing the toxin monocrotaline (described below) and chronic, 
continuous hypoxia (fraction of inspired air oxygen levels 
reduced to approximately 10%) (30).
Chronic vascular inflammation leads to remodeling and 
lesion development that can become obstructive to flow and 
a substrate for thrombosis, a pathology known as atheroscle-
rosis. Atherosclerotic changes in the large vessels in humans 
are the principal cause of life-threatening outcomes such as 
myocardial infarction and stroke. Numerous rodent mod-
els have been developed since the early 1990s that allow for 
examination of the potential for toxicants to chronically pro-
mote or acutely exacerbate atherosclerotic outcomes. The first 
transgenic mouse model, the ApoE∞/∞ mouse, was generated 
independently in separate labs in 1992 by Nobuyo Maeda (31) 
and Jan Breslow (32). By removal of this essential gene for 
cholesterol handling, mice exhibited levels of cholesterol far 
in excess of normal and, as a result, rapidly developed fatty 
streaks and more advanced vascular atheromatous lesions. A 
key study by Qinghua Sun in 2005 demonstrated the value of 
such vulnerable models for toxicology research, showing that 
chronic inhalation exposure to concentrated ambient particu-
late matter could augment the development and severity of ath-
erosclerotic plaques (33). Newer transgenic models, including 
the low-density lipoprotein (LDL) receptor knockout mouse, 
exhibit similar atherogenic pathologies but have a circulating 
lipid profile that better matches human disease. Prior to the 
ApoE∞/∞ mouse, however, the Watanabe heritable hyperlipid-
emic and New Zealand White rabbits were commonly used 
for atherosclerosis research, albeit only in limited studies for 
toxicology (34, 35).

34.4.7  Biomarkers for Human Research
34.4.7.1  Translational and Clinical Biomarkers
The development and progression of cardiovascular patho-
physiology and disease including atherosclerosis, left ven-
tricular remodeling, and hypertension is multifaceted. Driving 
these diverse pathophysiological outcomes include inflamma-
tion, oxidative stress, fibrosis, cardiomyocyte injury, and neu-
rohormonal regulation to highlight a few common processes. 
Animal models are developed to share similarities with human 
disease, but important aspects are typically missing, like the 
chronicity of disease and multimorbidities. It is crucial there-
fore to investigate biomarkers related to human cardiovascular 
pathophysiology, to support investigations of safety or toxicity 
in human populations.
Translational and clinical biomarkers provide essential 
and objective information that can be used to track changes 
in the physiological (pathological, pharmacological) state of 
single cells to whole-body models. Importantly, biomarkers 
allow investigators to track physiological changes not only 
from normal to diseased but also from diseased back to nor-
mal physiology. The use of biomarkers can help to understand 
the effects of a toxicological insult on molecular physiology, 
the mechanism of action of therapeutic counter measures, and 
ultimately clinical outcomes. There are many different types 
of biomarkers that can be employed, and biomarkers can be 
measured from single cells, tissues, and plasma to whole-body 
measurements such as blood pressure.
As mentioned, biomarkers consist of a diverse array of 
markers that can identify a subtle change in molecular path-
way to defining risk for a cardiovascular event in an individ-
ual patient. Many biomarkers that are used in a translational 
approach may not be validated as a clinical biomarker to assess 
risk or therapeutic effectiveness. However, these biomarkers 
are still useful and informative. For example, from labora-
tory work (animal/cellular) and human studies, superoxide 
dismutase is a known to help counter the effects of oxidative 
stress. However, it is not clear how changing levels of superox-
ide dismutase may either predict hard clinical outcomes (e.g., 
death) or prevent cardiac events. In contrast, elevation of the 
clinically validated C-reactive protein not only is predictive 
of poor clinical outcomes but also by decreasing C-reactive 
proteins with a therapeutic agent [i.e., HMG-CoA reductase 
inhibitors (statins)], clinical outcomes are improved (39). For 
the purpose of this discussion, biomarkers that are commonly 
employed in the clinical setting are highlighted in Table 34.2, 
and those biomarkers that are used more in a translational or 
mechanistic aspect are highlighted in Table 34.3. In exploring 
translational type of biomarkers (Table 34.3), these biomark-

34.5  Selected Cardiotoxicants
This section will focus on representative chemicals and drugs, 
which have been reported to have major cardiotoxic effects, 
and will not describe the adverse effects of cardiovascular 
drugs used therapeutically in the treatment of heart disease 
and disorders. For example, these drugs, such as beta-blockers, 
nitrates, calcium channel blockers, antihypertensive drugs, 
antiarrhythmic drugs, and digitalis and other inotropic drugs 
used for heart failure, are all prone to causing side effects that 
impact cardiovascular function, including promoting risks for 
arrhythmia. The remaining section discusses additional agents 
with known cardiovascular toxicity, divided into subsections 
of drugs (or self-administered substances), environmental and 
occupational exposures, and endogenous toxicants.
34.5.1  Drugs
34.5.1.1  Alcoholic Cardiomyopathy
Recognized for over 150 years, the abuse of alcohol in the 
form of ethanol has been associated with a variety of CVDs. 
Chronic, heavy use of alcohol may lead to nonischemic 
dilated cardiomyopathy or alcoholic cardiomyopathy, which 
results in decreased cardiac function and structural disease 
(40). Clinically, a patient with alcoholic cardiomyopathy will 
demonstrate low cardiac output, four chamber dilatation, wall 
thinning, ventricular dysfunction, and heart failure. Alcohol 
abuse may also be associated with arrhythmias, hypertension, 
stroke, and sudden death.
The pathogenesis of alcoholic cardiomyopathy is not clearly 
understood, but several sources of research suggest that altera-
tions in membrane stability of cardiac cells, decreased excita-
tion/contraction coupling, and formation of reactive oxidative 
metabolites may contribute to its pathogenesis. For example, 
the hepatic biotransformation of ethanol by alcohol dehydro-
genase and cytochrome P45O II EI to the toxic metabolite 
acetaldehyde may damage the membranes of cardiomyocytes 
(41). This reactive metabolite and other oxidative by-products 
may cause lipid peroxidation of the cardiac cell membranes or 
may damage cytosolic and membrane protein thiols.

The use of rats as an experimental model to investigate the 
chronic effects of ethanol on cardiac structure and production

alcohol on the human heart because of the shorter life span of 
the animals. However, a more recent study with a mouse model 
of alcoholic cardiomyopathy produced heart hypertrophy and 
fibrosis by genetically deleting the zinc-regulatory protein, 
metallothionein (MT) (42). This study and others suggest a 
role for zinc deficiency in the induction of alcohol-induced 
myocardial fibrosis and the possible involvement of oxidative 
stress in the fibrogenesis because of the antioxidant properties 
of MT. Other possible hypotheses of alcoholic cardiomyopa-
thy include inflammatory and immunologic changes, disrup-
tion of calcium-mediated contractile elements, inhibition of 
ATP formation, apoptosis, inhibition of protein synthesis, and 
receptor alterations.
34.5.1.2  Cocaine
Pharmacologically, cocaine is a local anesthetic, which blocks 
nerve impulses by reversible inhibition of Na+ channels and 
subsequent decrease in transitory Na+ conductance in nerve 
fibers. In addition, cocaine produces local vasoconstriction 
as the result of the inhibition of norepinephrine (NE) reup-
take. Cocaine may increase heart rate and blood pressure and 
stimulate the CNS, resulting in enhanced alertness and self-
confidence. Because of its euphoric-producing effects, cocaine 
may be abused chronically, resulting in altered behavior and 
addiction.
Chronic use of cocaine may have adverse effects on the 
heart because of its direct and indirect actions on myocardial 
contractility, increase in cytosolic calcium and disruption of 
NE reuptake, and subsequent stimulation of beta- and alpha-
adrenergic receptors. The net effect of the chronic use of 
cocaine is the production of cardiac arrhythmias, myocardial 
ischemia, myocarditis, aortic dissection, cerebral vasocon-
striction, and seizures. As pointed out by Kloner et al. (43), in 
their review on cocaine effects on the heart, cocaine abuse is 
a major risk factor for heart disease. It has been suggested that 
cocaine may directly injure cardiomyocytes, cause cell death, 
and result in myocardial infarction. The mechanism of cell 
death is not clearly understood, although it is postulated that 
coronary artery vasoconstriction, accelerated atherosclerosis, 
and thrombus formation may be factors in the production of 
myocardial infarction.
34.5.1.3  Antidepressants and Antipsychotics
Tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs) have been used since the 
1960s for the treatment of major depressive disorder. It is now 
well known that TCAs can have major cardiotoxic effects in 
overdose situations, and cardiovascular side effects are well 
documented (44). These adverse effects are linked to their 
inhibitory actions on cardiac and vascular ion channels, anti-
cholinergic- and quinidine-like actions, inhibition of reuptake 
of adrenergic amines, disruption of membrane permeabil-
ity, and direct myocardial depression. Clinical signs of TCA 
cardiotoxicity may include sinus tachycardia, postural hypo-
tension, QT prolongation and possible TdP arrhythmias, 
congestive heart failure, and AV block, leading to asystole 
and sudden death. Although TCAs are no longer considered 
the major therapeutic course of treatment for depressive dis-
orders, TCAs are still reported as one of the most commonly 
prescribed drugs implicated in overdose fatalities TCAs (44).
Several major antipsychotics have been associated with sud-
den cardiac death, QTc prolongation, and TdP. These effects 
by antipsychotics on the QT interval are thought to be medi-
ated by the blockade of the delayed rectifier K+ current. Other 
adverse side effects of the antipsychotics include tachycardia, 
orthostatic hypertension, and certain metabolic disorders, 
such as increased risk for weight gain, insulin resistance, and 
type 2 diabetes.
34.5.1.4  Androgenic–Anabolic Steroids
These agents are divided into structural classes: nonalkyl-
ated testosterone derivatives, such as testosterone, ester tes-
tosterone derivatives, nandrolone, and esters of nandrolone, 
and 17-alpha-alkylated androgenic–anabolic steroids (AASs), 
such as fluoxymesterone, methandrostenolone, stanozolol, and 
other related compounds. There is evidence for adverse car-
diac effects of AASs on the heart (45), resulting in pathologi-
cal changes such as hypertrophy, impaired diastolic function, 
myocardial necrosis and/or apoptosis, and possible ventricu-
lar fibrillation. Thus, the illegal use of exogenous androgens, 
especially in high doses, may induce cardiac hypertrophy 
and other cardiovascular problems. These actions on the 
heart are most likely associated with receptor and genomic 
mechanisms, but the fact that a variety of acute effects may 
be produced in a short period of time by androgens on the 
heart suggests that nongenomic mechanisms may be involved 
in their cardiotoxicity.
34.5.1.5  Antineoplastic Agents
Anthracyclines are natural products derived from Streptomyces 
peucetius var. caesius, with doxorubicin and daunorubicin as 
the major ones initially isolated from the fungus. Idarubicin 
and epirubicin are analogs of the latter two, differing only 
slightly in structure. Daunorubicin and idarubicin are used 
extensively in the treatment of acute leukemias, whereas doxo-
rubicin and epirubicin are more effective against solid tumors. 
Because of the quinone groups found as part of their chemical 
structures, anthracyclines can generate free radicals or semi-
quinone radical intermediates in malignant and normal tissues 
that can react with O2 to produce superoxide anion radicals, 
hydrogen peroxide, and hydroxyl radicals (46). Because these 
deleterious free radicals have a prominent action on cardiac 
tissue, cardiotoxicity is a major result of treatment of patients 
with these analogs. A rare form of cardiotoxicity is acute or 
subacute injury, resulting in transient arrhythmias, a peri-
carditis-myocarditis syndrome, or acute failure of the LV. A 
more serious adverse effect is the induction of cardiomyopa-
thy, which is the more common form of cardiotoxicity. Third, 
a type of toxicity may be shown years after treatment with 
the anthracyclines: arrhythmias and late-onset ventricular 
dysfunction.

34.5.1.6  Nonsteroidal Anti-Inflammatory Agents
The major NSAIDs are aspirin, propionic acid derivatives 
(ibuprofen, naproxen), acetic acid derivatives (indomethacin), 
selective inhibitors of COX-2 (celecoxib, etoricoxib, lumira-
coxib), and enolic acids (piroxicam) (47). Many of these agents 
have a variety of common and shared side effects, including 
GI effects, platelet actions, renal toxicity, CNS actions, uterine 
effects, hypersensitivity reactions, and cardiovascular toxici-
ties. The main cardiotoxic effects may be myocardial infarc-
tion, stroke, thrombosis, and closure of ductus arteriosus. It 
has only been most recently that the cardiovascular toxic-
ity of NSAIDs has gained much prominence with the with-
drawal from the market of rofecoxib and valdecoxib due to 
increased risk for serious cardiovascular adverse events. The 
other NSAIDs on the market have also been suggested to have 
the potential to increase the risk of heart attack and stroke. 
The most probable explanation for the cardiotoxicity of the 
NSAIDs is that the inhibition of COX-2 increases the forma-
tion of thrombi and elevates blood pressure, which would them 
enhance the potential for cardiovascular risk in humans.
34.5.1.7  Antimicrobial Agents
Of the macrolide antibiotics, erythromycin and clarithromy-
cin are associated with an increased risk of TdP as a result 
of QT prolongation (48). Telithromycin and azithromycin are 
classified as possible risks of TdP. Fluoroquinolones were the 
most widely prescribed class of antibiotics for many years but 
are now used less frequently (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin, and 
moxifloxacin). Sparfloxacin and grepafloxacin were removed 
from the market because of nonfatal and fatal arrhythmias 
secondary to QT prolongation with TdP. The azole antifungals, 
such as ketoconazole and itraconazole, have been reported to 
cause QT prolongation and TdP, and more attention should 
be paid to possible adverse cardiac events in patients taking 
these antibiotics. The antimicrobial agent, pentamidine, used 
to treat protozoal infections, has been reported to be associ-
ated with cases of QT prolongation and TdP. Similarly, certain 
antimalarial agents, chloroquine, hydroxychloroquine, and 
halofantrine, have been associated with significant QT prolon-
gation. Hydroxychloroquine has come under the microscope in 
2020 as a proposed therapy to treat SARS-CoV-2 (49). Despite 
extensive observational and randomized clinical trial reports, 
it was never established to benefit outcomes for Covid-19. The 
known QT prolongation effects for hydroxychloroquine were 
of uncertain role in Covid-19 therapy, and it has been postu-
lated that this may synergize with cardiovascular outcomes of 
Covid-19 to produce adverse outcomes that offset any poten-
tial antiviral or immunological benefit.
34.5.2  Environmental and Occupational Toxicants
34.5.2.1  Air Pollution
Cardiovascular morbidity and mortality are associated with 
numerous inhaled toxicants, both particulates and gases (50). 
Interestingly, recent research reveals associations between 
ambient air pollution exposure and adverse neurological 
and developmental/gestational outcomes, with toxicological 
research suggesting that circulating factors or particulates may 
impact endothelial homeostasis in organ systems remote from 
the lung. Evidence exists for both direct and indirect effects on 
the vasculature that may impact barrier function, drive inflam-
mation, and alter anticoagulation activity of the endothelium. 
Controlled exposures to gold nanoparticles confirmed a fairly 
rapid transit from inhalation to the presence of particles in the 
blood and carotid plaques in humans (51). On the other hand, 
pulmonary exposure to multiwalled carbon nanotubes leads to 
rapid generation of hundreds of circulating peptides that can 
induce endothelial inflammation ex vivo (52). Furthermore, 
inhaled reactive gases like ozone can also cause systemic vas-
cular effects (23). Importantly, evidence supporting these and 
related mechanisms of action do not suggest any pathways to 
be mutually exclusive. It is also important to note that many 
seemingly non-cardiovascular outcomes reported in associa-
tion with air pollution exposures, including adverse birth/ges-
tational outcomes, neurodevelopmental and neurodegenerative 
diseases, may require intermediate effects of the vascular sys-
tems of relevant target organs to manifest the indirect toxicity 
(53–55).
34.5.2.2  Heavy Metals
Cardiovascular health effects from metal toxicants are becom-
ing increasingly recognized in epidemiological and toxico-
logical studies. Arsenic, for one, has been shown to promote 
atherosclerosis, potentially through the oxidative impacts on 
atheroma components, including LDL cholesterol (38, 56, 57). 
Similar epidemiological and toxicological outcomes exist for 
cadmium (58). While a number of epidemiological studies 
have shown associations between urinary or circulating met-
als content and CVD, this is overall an understudied area (59). 
Molecular mechanisms of toxicity for heavy metals are quite 
well documented, including the generation of reactive oxygen 
species, inhibition of DNA repair pathways, and hapten-related 
behavior; thus, investigations using well-defined models of 
cardiovascular risk should provide deep insights into the role 
of heavy metals in promoting CVD.

34.5.2.3  Radiation
Radiation exposure most often arises from cancer therapy, but 
can also occur from unintentional exposures from nuclear plant 
accidents or nuclear weapons. Whole-body exposure to ioniz-
ing radiation has a specific effect on (primarily microvascular) 
endothelial cells that can induce a range of outcomes depend-
ing on the damage (60). Generation of DNA and protein dam-
age and reactive oxygen species can promote responses such 
as expression of adhesion molecules and cytokines, loss of 
barrier integrity, mitochondrial damage, and programed cell 
death. Survivors of such injury may be predisposed to athero-
sclerosis and vascular fibrosis. Early vascular effects resemble 
acute inflammation, as described above, but later effects may 
follow 2–6 weeks afterwards that are often due to an impair-
ment of healing pathways, especially in terms of loss of hema-
topoietic and endothelial progenitor cell availability from the 
damaged bone marrow.
34.5.2.4  Tobacco Smoke and Electronic Cigarettes
Hundreds of chemicals – gases and particulates – make up 
tobacco smoke, and collectively drive three major outcomes in 
heavy users: lung cancer, chronic obstructive pulmonary dis-
ease, and heart disease. Many of the components of tobacco 
smoke are independently associated with cardiovascular tox-
icity, most notoriously carbon monoxide (61, 62). Smoking 
as a habit is associated with the development of atheroscle-
rosis, hypertension, heart failure, myocardial infarction, and 
arrhythmia (63–65). Benzene is a key carcinogen in cigarette 
smoke and certain occupational environments, and limited 
epidemiological data suggests that benzene may be associated 
with hypertension and heart failure (66).
While e-cigarettes have been developed as a safer alternative 
to conventional tobacco smoking, evidence still points to risks 
for short- and long-term cardiovascular risks (67). The nico-
tine component of e-cigarettes causes short-term increases in 
vascular stiffness and blood pressure (68, 69) and limits flow-
mediated vasodilation (70). Nicotine has also been shown to 
prolong QT interval in Kcnq∞/∞ mice (71) and independently 
exacerbate cardiac remodeling in hypertensive models (27). 
Other chemicals present in e-cigarette vapor include alde-
hydes, metals, and tobacco-related contaminants, which may 
all promote some degree of cardiovascular response (72).
34.5.2.5  Monocrotaline
A contaminant of the Crotalaria spectabilis plant, mono-
crotaline, was first identified as a toxin in grazing cattle who 
developed cardiac and liver disease. The molecule is bioac-
tivated in the liver to a pyrrolic intermediate that is highly 
reactive and toxic to endothelial cells, primarily, due to the 
proximal relationship of the endothelium with the circula-
tion. Monocrotaline has become a widely used model for 
pulmonary hypertension in rodents. When injected intraperi-
toneally as a bolus, the high content leaving the liver through 
the venous circulation has a profound toxicity to endothelial 
cells of the subsequent capillary bed in the lungs. Endothelial 
toxicity leads to the development of neointimal thickening in 
pulmonary arteries, which over a 10–14-day period increases 
pulmonary vascular resistance and forces dramatic right ven-
tricular hypertrophy to compensate (Figure 34.6) (30, 73). In 
3–5 weeks, this right heart growth can lead to cor pulmonale, 
or right heart failure (74).
34.5.3  Endogenous Toxicants
As a final category, there are numerous endogenously derived 
chemicals that can promote significant cardiovascular pathol-
ogy. Typically, these are by-products of some other disorder 
and may contribute to a subsequent morbidity. It is important 
to note that many diseases lead to cardiovascular outcomes, 
such as chronic kidney and liver disease, obstructive sleep 
apnea, and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease.
34.5.3.1  Cholesterol/Crystals
Arguably the toxicant with the greatest impact on human 
health, excess cholesterol promotes atherosclerosis and ulti-
mately coronary artery disease and other related pathologies. 
Cholesterol is carried by lipoprotein particles and as LDL, 
which can translocate into the wall of great vessels. In the 
interstitium of the aorta, coronaries, and carotids, the LDL 
can be oxidized (to oxLDL), which triggers the recruitment 
of inflammatory cells into the vascular wall by triggering sig-
naling through scavenger and pattern recognition receptors. 
Macrophage uptake of LDL/oxLDL leads to transformation 
into foam cells, which are further prone to inflammatory sig-
naling. In addition, cholesterol in such high quantities can pre-
cipitate into crystal form in lysosomes, leading to lysosomal 
rupture and release of cathepsin B and triggering inflamma-
some responses, along with caspase 8 induction and apoptosis 
(75). This process is thought to contribute to plaque necrosis 
and instability, which can lead to rupture and fatal coagulative 
events.

34.5.3.2  Gut Microbiome Factors
Interesting data has emerged that small molecules derived from 
the gut microbiome are associated with atherosclerosis and 
hypertension. A specific example, trimethylamine-N-oxide 
can negatively affect platelet reactivity, vascular tone, and 
lipid metabolism (76). Gut exposures to various xenobiotics 
have been shown to not only alter gut microbiome composition 
but also lead to changes in circulating lipids and metabolites 
that are proatherogenic in nature (77). Further research in this 
area is needed to understand the interplay between the gut 
flora and cardiovascular health.
34.5.3.3  Uremic Toxins
When kidneys fail, a buildup of numerous circulating 
small molecule toxins occurs (78). These molecules include 
p-cresyl sulfate, indoxyl sulfate, and acrolein. With ele-
vated levels in direct contact with endothelial cells, numer-
ous inflammatory, calcification, and constrictive pathways 
can be triggered. Specifically, AhR, NFkB, and MAPK 
signaling pathways can be induced in the endothelial cells 
leading to upregulation of pro-coagulant proteins, adhesion 
molecules, and cytokines (79). Atherosclerosis and coro-
nary artery disease are major complications in chronic kid-
ney disease, and these uremic toxins are believed to play a 
major role. In a parallel manner, some volatile organic com-
pounds can build up in the circulation in respiratory dis-
orders; the clinical relevance of such compounds remains 
unknown.
