39.1  Introduction
Immunotoxicology evaluates adverse effects on the structure 
or function of the immune system that result from intended 
or unintended exposures to xenobiotics such as therapeu-
tic drugs, environmental chemicals, ultraviolet radiation, 
or biological materials. Xenobiotic exposure can alter nor-
mal immune system homeostasis by modifying immune 
cell production, activation, maturation, proliferation, and/
or cell-to-cell interactions, and by increasing or suppress-
ing the release of immune mediators. Immunotoxicity can 
result from either immune system activation or suppression. 
With inadvertent or exaggerated immune stimulation, the 
immune dysfunction can result in allergy/hypersensitivity, 
autoimmunity, or other inflammatory diseases. In hypersen-
sitivity immunopathologies, the immune system responds 
to the agent as an allergen with an exaggerated immune 
response. With autoimmunity, the xenobiotic directly or 
indirectly causes a break-down in self-tolerance and induces 
an immune response to autologous (self) constituents that 
results in pathology. In the case of immunosuppression, the 
immune decrement can result in increased incidence and 
severity of infections and tumors.
The immune system has a variety of weapons at its dis-
posal to defend the organism from pathogens and environ-
mental insults. Unfortunately, under certain circumstances, 
these same weapons can also turn against the host and result 
in serious pathologies. Luckily, to protect the host, the 
immune system has many redundancies. This overcapacity 
can help to buffer xenobiotic insults on this complex organ 
system so that a functional deficit often only manifests itself 
under influence of additional immune stressors such as an 
infection or tumor, or additional xenobiotic insult. As the 
immune system plays a critical role in immune surveil-
lance, host resistance to disease, and normal homeostasis of 
the host, identification of immunotoxic risk is important in 
the protection of human and animal health. To understand 
immunotoxicology, a knowledge of basic immunology is 
therefore necessary.
39.2  Immune Cells and Tissues
The immune system is a complex multicellular organ system 
consisting of granulocytes, macrophages (MØ), lympho-
cytes, and dendritic cells (DCs) with various functions and 
phenotypic characteristics, as well as various soluble media-
tors. These cells are generally of hemopoietic origin and, in 
adults, are found in the peripheral blood, lymphatic fluid, and 
organized lymphoid tissues, including bone marrow, spleen, 
thymus, lymph nodes, tonsils, Peyer’s Patches, and mucosa-
associated lymphoid tissue (MALT).
The immune system is in a constant state of self-renewal 
involving cell proliferation, differentiation, activation, and 
maturation. It exists to defend the body against invasion by 
infectious and opportunistic microorganisms and spontane-
ously arising neoplasia. This network of cells and soluble fac-
tors is highly regulated and interdependent, must discriminate 
self from nonself, and can react to nonself with many different 
(pleiotropic) defensive responses [1].
39.2.1  Organization, Differentiation, and 
Function of Primary Lymphoid Tissue
With the exception of follicular DCs which are of stromal origin, 
virtually all immune cells are derived from pluripotent hema-
topoietic stem cells, a unique group of unspecialized cells that 
have self-renewal capacity. These cells are found in the blood, 
islands of the embryonic yolk sac and in the liver of the fetus 
during fetal development and later in the bone marrow. The 
pluripotent stem cells differentiate along several developmen-
tal pathways, giving rise to erythrocytes, myeloid series cells 
(i.e., MØ and neutrophils), megakaryocytes (platelets), or lym-
phocytes. Maturation generally occurs within the bone marrow, 
although lymphoid progenitor cells are disseminated through 
the blood and lymphatic vessels to the primary lymphoid organs 
where they undergo further differentiation under the influence 
of the humoral microenvironment of these organs [2].

During the beginning of the second half of embryogen-
esis (days 12 and 13 in the mouse), stem cells migrate into 
the epithelia of the thymus and bursa-equivalent areas, where 
they differentiate independently of antigenic stimulation into 
immunocompetent T and B cells, respectively. The thymus is 
an organization of lymphoid tissue located in the chest, above 
the heart. Thymus development occurs during the 6th week of 
embryological development in humans and day 9 of gestation 
in the mouse. The thymus reaches its maximum size at birth 
or shortly thereafter in most mammals and then begins a slow 
involution that occurs with age that is complete between the 
ages of 5 and 15 years in humans. Thymic atrophy can also 
result from viral, bacterial, or fungal infections [3], as well 
as elevated stress hormone levels and xenobiotic toxicity. 
Histologically, the thymus consists of multiple lobules, each 
lobule containing a cortex (outer) and a medulla (inner) con-
taining lymphoid follicles (Figure 39.1). Lymphocyte precur-
sors from bone marrow proliferate in the cortex of the lobules 
and then migrate to the medulla. In the medulla, they further 
differentiate, under the influence of the thymic epithelium 
and hormonal factors, into mature T lymphocytes before emi-
grating to secondary lymphoid tissues. The neonatal/postna-
tal thymus has a significant endocrine function supported by 
nonlymphoid thymic epithelium cells. These cells produce a 
family of thymic hormones essential for T lymphocyte matu-
ration and differentiation.
In contrast, B cell differentiation occurs in the bursa of 
Fabricius in birds, a lymphoepithelial organ that develops 
from a diverticulum of the posterior wall of the cloacae. The 
mammalian bursa equivalent is believed to be the fetal liver, 
neonatal spleen, GALT, and adult bone marrow, depending on 
age. Mature B lymphocytes migrate from the bursa-equivalent 
tissue to populate the B-dependent areas of the secondary lym-
phoid tissues. The bone marrow functions as a primary lym-
phoid organ and serves as the principal source of uncommitted 
stem cells, including both myeloid and erythroid precursor 
cells. The bone marrow architecture is highly organized and 
complex, consisting of a matrix or cellular stroma derived 
from local mesenchymal cells, as well as cells of hemopoi-
etic parenchyma that are descendants of circulating stem cells. 
The bone marrow stroma supports hemopoietic stem cells and 
progenitor cells. These cells then differentiate and produce 
the major blood cell lineages. The bone marrow matrix con-
sists of reticular-DCs, fibroblast-like cells, and immune cells 
within the bone marrow microenvironment. The bone marrow 
stromal cells are organized in multicellular areas or “niches” 
and produce a variety of factors (e.g., cytokines, adhesion mol-
ecules, chemokines) that are important in the regulation of dif-
ferentiation and self-renewal [4].
Bone marrow stem and stromal cells have been shown 
to possess a significant capacity for metabolic activation 
because they contain cytochromes of the P450 and P448 fam-
ilies as well as peroxidases and can generate reactive oxygen 
species, which could also activate xenobiotics via oxidant-
dependent mechanisms [5]. This metabolic activity is thought 
to contribute to the sensitivity of bone marrow elements to 
toxicants such as benzene, which is extensively metabolized 
within the bone marrow. Considering the cell proliferation 
and differentiation occurring within the marrow, this tissue 
is also one of the most sensitive tissues to drugs or chemicals 
affecting cell division. Dose-limiting bone marrow toxicities 
are a significant problem with antiproliferative drugs, includ-
ing cytotoxic agents, antifolates, AIDS therapeutics, and cer-
tain cytokines [6, 7].
39.2.2  Organization and Function of 
Secondary Lymphoid Tissue
The organization and function of secondary lymphoid organs 
is extremely important for immune competence and host 
defense. The organized areas of secondary lymphoid tissue are 
the spleen, lymph nodes, tonsils, Peyer’s patches, and MALT. 
The anatomical organization of these tissues provides a micro-
environment for functional development of lymphoid cells and 
vital immune responses [8].
Lymph nodes are discrete, organized secondary lymphoid 
organs that serve as filtering devices for lymphatic fluid. 
Lymphocytes circulate between the blood and lymphatic fluid 
through lymph nodes where they encounter antigens and inter-
act with DCs. This cellular trafficking through lymph nodes is 
critical to immune surveillance [9]. Lymph nodes are divided 
structurally into three areas, cortex, paracortex, and medulla 
(Figure 39.2), and are served by several afferent lymphatic

antigenic stimulation, germinal centers are easily detectable 
as spherical or ovoid structures containing many large- and 
medium-sized lymphocytes, predominantly B lymphocytes. 
The paracortex, lying between the cortex and the medulla, is 
composed predominantly of T cells and is a major site of MØ 
and T cell interaction. Neonatal thymectomy or lymphocyte 
depletion by cytolytic drugs reduces the production of para-
cortical lymphocytes, leading to depressed immune capacity. 
In addition, the paracortex contains a specialized blood vas-
culature, termed postcapillary venules, that serves as a point 
of entry for recirculating lymphocytes from the bloodstream. 
The medulla of the lymph node is composed primarily of net-
works of cords and sinuses; it serves as an effective filter for 
removing particulate material from lymphatic fluid. Following 
antigenic stimulation, the majority of antibody that is pro-
duced comes from the PC found within these medullary cords 
of the nodes.
The spleen is the major filter of blood-borne antigens and 
the initial site of immunological response to these antigens. In 
addition, the spleen is a site of extramedullary hematopoiesis 
(non-bone marrow–red blood cell production) and responsible 
for the removal of damaged blood cells. There are two major 
histological regions within the spleen: the red pulp and the white 
pulp containing IgM-staining B and T cells (Figure 39.3a and 
b). These areas have been named for their colors in a freshly 
cut spleen. The white pulp consists of numerous white blood 
cell (WBC) aggregates and lymphoid follicles. The red pulp 
contains cords and venous sinuses analogous to the medullary 
region of lymph nodes. The region between the red pulp and 
the white pulp is referred to as the marginal zone in rodents or

systemic immunity, an assessment of MALT is an important 
aspect of immunopathology that should not be overlooked [14].
39.3  Immune Mechanisms Responsible 
for Host Defense
The host defense functions of the immune system can be 
classified based on the type of response to the immunologic 
stimulus and broadly defined as an innate or adaptive immune 
response. The innate immune response is evolutionarily con-
served, does not require prior sensitization with the inducing 
foreign agent to elicit a response, and responds to a wide range 
of antigens and damage- or pathogen-associated patterns to 
produce an immune response in the presence of these danger 
signals. On the other hand, the adaptive immune response is 
a tailored immune response specifically directed against a 
particular eliciting agent. The immune responses that char-
acterize adaptive host defense represent a series of complex 
events that occur following the introduction of foreign anti-
genic material into the body. There are two major types of spe-
cific immune response: cell-mediated immunity (CMI), which 
is initiated by specifically sensitized T cells and is generally 
associated with delayed-type hypersensitivity (DTH), rejec-
tion of tumors or foreign grafts, and resistance to viral agents, 
and humoral immunity (HI), which involves the production 
of antibodies by PC following sensitization to a specific anti-
gen and is important in resistance to extracellular pathogens. 
These antigen-specific adaptive immune responses also result 
in immunological memory and in a sustained and augmented 
response upon re-exposure to the specific antigen.
Penetration of the skin or mucosal defense barriers by a 
foreign material or invading pathogen results in activation of 
innate immune cells that recognize the damage- and patho-
gen-associated motifs via cell-associated pattern recogni-
tion receptors, such as toll-like receptors (TLRs), RIG-1-like 
receptors, NOD-like receptors, and C-type lectin receptors. 
Following activation, the receptors start a signaling cascade 
through various pathways which direct production of addi-
tional antimicrobial molecules as well as cytokines and che-
mokines. If the microorganism is not controlled and persists, 
specific adaptive immune responses involving antibody pro-
duction (HI) and the induction of effector lymphocytes (CMI) 
follow. The effector lymphocytes respond through cytokine 
mediators to seek out and destroy the invading microorganism. 
Both antibody-producing lymphocyte responses (B lympho-
cytes or B cells) and thymus-dependent lymphocyte responses 
(T lymphocytes or T cells) are triggered by the presentation 
of foreign antigen to appropriate lymphocytes by DCs, MØ, 
or other antigen-presenting cells (APCs). Antigen presentation 
and co-stimulatory signals are both required for activation of 
these cells. Following activation, B cells proliferate and dif-
ferentiate into PC and, with the support of T helper 2 (Th2) 
cells, produce large quantities of antigen-specific immuno-
globulins (antibodies). Antibodies enter the plasma where they 
bind the foreign material and either neutralize, lyse, or facili-
tate phagocytosis of the agent. Antibody–antigen interactions 
are expanded by actions of the complement (C−) system and 
other inflammatory mediators (e.g., prostaglandins and leukot-
rienes). With the support of Th1 cells, another population of T 
cells, referred to as cytotoxic T cells, proliferate and recognize 
virally infected cells that they can destroy before viral lytic 
replication can occur. As the immune response subsides, long-
lived memory cells are generated which facilitate mounting a 
more rapid and stronger immune response upon re-exposure 
to the specific antigen. This antigen-specific immune mem-
ory has traditionally been one of the hallmarks of adaptive 
immunity [1, 15]. However, it should be noted, that there is 
increasing evidence that indicates that innate immune cells 
can respond qualitatively or quantitatively with an increased 
response following secondary exposure [16]. This qualitative 
(non-antigen-specific) innate memory in myeloid immune 
cells has been referred to as “trained immunity” [17]. There is 
emerging evidence that natural killer (NK) cells can acquire 
antigen-specific immunological memory, a characteristic 
that was previously only associated with adaptive immune 
responses of T and B cells [18].
Recent work has shown that non-immune cells can be 
involved in both innate and adaptive immune responses and 
that some of the cells classified as innate or adaptive can have a 
functional role in both innate and adaptive immunity [19, 20]. 
Thus, while the innate and adaptive immunity classifications 
are useful, it should be noted that innate and adaptive immune 
responses are not mutually exclusive but part of an integrated 
continuum with complementary functions, where a defect in 
either can result in inappropriate dysfunction or increased 
vulnerabilities.
39.3.1  Innate Immune Response
Innate immune defenses include barrier surfaces (e.g., skin, 
mucosal surfaces, acidity of the stomach), myeloid and lym-
phoid effector cells, nonhematopoietic cells (e.g., epithelial 
cells), and a variety of mediators. Innate immune cells become 
activated in response to tissue damage or infection and can 
initiate defense mechanisms. This is typically manifested 
as inflammation that serves to contain the infection, recruit

immune cells and mediators to the challenge site, and contrib-
ute to healing when the infection is cleared. The mechanisms 
can be cell-dependent, as is the case with phagocytosis or cyto-
toxicity, or entail a variety of humoral mediators and secreted 
substances (e.g., complement, cytokines and chemokines, anti-
microbial peptides, and acute-phase proteins). The cells of the 
innate immune system include a broad range of myeloid and 
lymphoid cells such as monocytes, DCs, MØ, neutrophils, NK 
cells, and innate lymphoid cells (ILC) [21, 22].
39.3.1.1  Complement
The complement system is a key component of innate immu-
nity which provides a first line of defense against pathogens 
and promotes disposal of cell debris. It is also an important 
component of adaptive immunity through the augmentation of 
phagocytic function and B cell responses. Complement acti-
vation results in a complex cascade of proteases involved in 
opsonization, cytolytic membrane attack and disruption of 
bacterial cell walls, opsonization and removal of cell debris 
and apoptotic cells, augmentation of inflammation via che-
motactic agents, activation of leukocytes, and activation of B 
cells by aiding in costimulation. Due to the tremendous ampli-
fication capable in this cascade, complement is tightly regu-
lated. Beyond its role in innate immunity and its interface with 
adaptive immunity, complement is also involved in immune 
homeostasis through the removal of apoptotic cells and self-
antigens. The importance of the complement system is evi-
denced by disorders resulting from complement component/
regulator deficiency or dysregulation [23].
39.3.1.2  Phagocytic Leukocytes
Two categories of phagocytic leukocytes, polymorphonuclear 
neutrophils (PMNs) or granulocytes and mononuclear phago-
cytes or MØ, are involved in nonspecific mechanisms of host 
resistance. Both cell types originate from myeloid progenitor 
cells in the bone marrow. The inflammation associated with a 
splinter, for example, is typical of a nonspecific PMN and MØ 
response. Both PMN and MØ exhibit phagocytic activity toward 
foreign materials.
Neutrophils patrol blood and tissues and are the predominant 
immune cell in human blood. PMN readily traverses blood ves-
sels and provides an initial defense against infectious agents. 
Diseases resulting in neutropenia in which neutrophil num-
bers are decreased highlight the importance of these cells in 
the control of fungal and bacterial infections. Chemokines have 
been shown to modulate neutrophil release and retention [24]. 
Beyond their important role in innate immunity, neutrophils 
also have regulatory roles in adaptive immunity and have been 
shown to act as myeloid-derived suppressor cells [25] and sup-
press T cell proliferation and cytokine production. In addition 
to phagocytosis, neutrophils use neutrophil extracellular traps 
(NETs) to capture microbes and digest them via autophagy. 
Neutrophils can interact with a variety of immune and non-
immune cells on several levels. For example, T helper 17 (Th17) 
cells release IL-17 and chemokines to aid in neutrophil recruit-
ment, which in turn release chemokines that recruit additional 
Th17 cells, resulting in a positive feedback loop between these 
Th17 cells and neutrophils. Neutrophils are generally regarded 
as short-lived immune cells, thus ex vivo assays are typically 
performed within hours of collection; however, some reports 
indicate that human neutrophils have a lifespan of 5.4 days [26]. 
Currently, there is no clear consensus regarding neutrophil 
lifespan in humans, and many in vitro or ex vivo evaluations of 
neutrophil function err on the side of caution in scheduling the 
performance of these analyses on the day of collection.
Cells of the MØ/monocyte lineage are found in many tis-
sues, including liver (Kupffer cells), lung (alveolar and inter-
stitial MØ), skin (Langerhans cells), and brain (astrocytes and 
microglia). These cells, because of their proximity to portals 
of entry, are often the first cells to interact with drugs, chemi-
cals, and physical agents entering the organism via respiration, 
ingestion, or contact with blood. The capacity of mononuclear 
phagocytic system cells to carry out these functions is asso-
ciated with their activation state, which in turn is a function 
of both endogenous (e.g., interferon-gamma [IFN-ć]) and 
exogenous (e.g., bacterial lipopolysaccharide [LPS]) stimuli. 
Responsive MØ obtained from the peritoneal cavity are rela-
tively quiescent and require extracellular signals or priming, 
followed by a second signal induced by triggers such as LPS 
to be fully activated. MØ in the presence of specific opsonic 
antibodies and complement can destroy a wide array of micro-
organisms. MØ are recruited to target sites if PMN either can-
not contain or are destroyed by the infectious agent, as is the 
case with certain bacteria (e.g., Listeria). MØ can be activated 
to a state of enhanced bactericidal or tumoricidal activity by 
soluble lymphocyte products (e.g., cytokines) produced by T 
lymphocytes sensitized to the invading microbe. Beyond their 
direct phagocytic function, MØ monitor the tissues where they 
reside, help maintain tissue integrity and homeostasis, and 
can respond promptly to their environment (e.g., damage- or 
pathogen-associated motifs, microorganisms, and oxygen con-
centrations). Following activation, MØ can negatively regulate 
the innate immune response via anti-inflammatory pathways 
and phagocytosis of apoptotic neutrophils and debris. MØ 
have also been shown to coordinate the stress response via 
crosstalk and paracrine communication with other cell types, 
and to also assist in coordinating the tissue response to damage 
and injury [27].
Although the mononuclear phagocyte system is designed 
to protect the host, once a xenobiotic agent has gained entry, 
extensive and/or persistent tissue damage can result and is 
mediated, in part, by MØ products. Silicosis and asbestosis are 
two examples of diseases where MØ mediators are involved 
in the pathology [28]. In these conditions, tissue damage from 
an environmental or infectious agent results in an influx of 
phagocytic cells, mainly PMNs. In most instances, these cells 
effectively eliminate the agents by digesting them in internal 
vacuoles. If the foreign material persists (as, e.g., silica crystals 
or asbestos fibers), however, a chronic inflammatory process 
may ensue in which PMNs are replaced by monocytes/MØ as 
the predominant effector cells. These cells release a variety of 
active mediators (cytokines, nitric oxide, amines, lipid media-
tors, etc.) that recruit other inflammatory cells into the local 
environment, but also result in tissue damage. These processes 
may lead to the development of a granuloma, a collection of 
inflammatory cells surrounded by fibrotic tissue [1].

39.3.1.3  Other Granulocytes
Blood basophils, eosinophils, and tissue mast cells are involved 
in protection against parasites and important in the pathogene-
sis of allergic diseases. These cells produce a variety of media-
tors, can interact with both immune and non-immune cells, 
and are important in the regulation and dysregulation of tissue 
homeostasis [29].
39.3.1.4  ILCs and NK Cells
ILCs are a distinct arm of innate immunity and interact with 
hemopoietic cells as well as stromal and epithelial cells. ILCs 
are regulated by a variety of cell-derived factors (e.g., cyto-
kines, hormones, neuropeptides, alarmins, and eicosanoids) 
and contribute to immunity, inflammation, tissue homeostasis, 
metabolism, epithelial repair, and barrier function. Unlike T 
and B cells, ILCs do not have antigen-specific receptors. ILCs 
include the classic cytotoxic NK cell as well as non-cytotoxic 
ILCs that are grouped according to transcription factors and 
cytokine patterns. NK cells and the non-cytotoxic ILCs come 
from distinct lineages. When dysregulated, ILC responses can 
promote chronic inflammation, cancer, and metabolic disor-
ders [30, 31]. Since ILCs are relatively rare and are identified 
by cell surface markers and cytokine production, multicolor 
flow cytometry is used for purification, characterization, and 
functional assessment of ILCs [32].
NK cells are a population of non-B, non-T lymphocytes, 
and major members of ILCs. NK cells develop from hemopoi-
etic cells in the bone marrow and exhibit cytotoxicity toward 
a variety of target cells, including tumor cells and virally 
infected cells. Studies of individuals with NK cell deficiency 
states, most of which are associated with single-gene muta-
tions, have helped identify a role for NK cells in the defense 
against human infectious disease. A resounding theme of NK 
cell deficiencies is susceptibility to herpes viruses and other 
viral pathogens [33]. NK cells express a unique panel of cell 
surface markers (e.g., asialo GM1) and are morphologically 
distinct, being larger than other lymphocytes. These cells also 
express several unique receptors, including those producing 
activating or inhibitory signals upon recognition of target. 
Unlike CMI, NK cell–mediated cellular cytotoxicity is major 
histocompatibility complex (MHC) unrestricted and does not 
require prior exposure to the target.
Historically, NK cells have been seen principally as innate 
defense against viruses and tumor cell immune surveillance 
[34] In fact, the standard methodology for assessing NK cell 
function relies upon in vitro lysis of tumor target cells. However, 
NK cells have also been shown to participate in immunoregu-
lation and modulation of autoreactivity. In contrast to previous 
models in which NK cells were considered independent of the 
acquired immune response, it is increasingly recognized that 
these cells also have a vital role in the induction and regulation 
of acquired immunity [35, 36]. NK cells respond to, and pro-
duce, key immunoregulatory cytokines and thus play a signifi-
cant role in the normal immune response.
Related to NK cells are the Natural Killer T (NKT) cells, 
a unique subset of T cells that exhibit properties of both 
NK cells and T cells. These cells appear to have primarily 
immunoregulatory functions and serve in the coordination of 
functions between the innate and adaptive immune responses 
and are important in amplification or moderation of the 
immune response [37]. To date, this cell population has not 
been a target of immunotoxicology assessment, although it is 
conceivable that these cells would be as likely a target as NK 
cells. Moreover, NKT cells have been associated with immu-
notoxicologically important endpoints, including resistance to 
infection and autoimmunity [38].
39.3.2  Adaptive Immunity
Whether an antigen induces CMI, HI with antibody production, or 
both depends on the physical and chemical characteristics of the 
antigen, the mode of presentation of the antigen to lymphocytes, 
the pattern of antigen distribution within lymphoid tissue, and the 
molecular configuration of the antigen. In many instances, anti-
gen is initially phagocytized and processed by APC. Antigenic 
peptides are transported to the cell surface, where they are pre-
sented to lymphocytes through cell surface interactions via spe-
cific surface proteins (e.g., class II molecule antigens).
39.3.2.1  Humoral Immune Response
The principal function of B lymphocytes is production of spe-
cific antibody in response to antigenic stimulation. B cells rec-
ognize antigen via specific receptors, comprising membrane 
immunoglobulins associated with accessory proteins either 
directly or in the presence of an APC. Binding of the receptor 
with its cognate antigen triggers transmembrane signaling and 
subsequent activation of the B cell. The antigen is then internal-
ized, processed, and associated with class II MHC molecules. 
Antigen-derived peptides, along with MHC proteins, are then 
transferred to the cell surface for interaction with helper cells.
Within 3–5 days following antigen exposure, T/B cell inter-
action results in B-lymphocyte differentiation into blast cells, 
then into immature PC, and finally into antibody-secreting PC. 
The establishment of HI is characterized by an early rise in 
IgM antibody titer in the serum associated mainly with short-
lived PC, followed several days later by the appearance of IgG 
antibodies secreted by long-lived PC. During the differentia-
tion process, some of the lymphocytes develop into long-lived 
memory cells, ensuring that subsequent antigen encounters 
result in an enhanced (secondary) response. This secondary 
response is characterized by a shorter latency for antibody pro-
duction as well as an increased affinity and synthesis of IgG 
antibodies. Antibody molecules react with specific antigenic 
determinants (epitopes) on their target, facilitating its removal 
(e.g., lysis or enhanced phagocytosis).
Based on chemical structure and biological function, the 
five classes of antibody molecules in mammals are IgM, IgG, 
IgA, Immunoglobulin D (IgD), and Immunoglobulin E (IgE); 
some of the physical and biological characteristics of each of 
these classes are listed in Table 39.1. Antibodies operate via 
several mechanisms to protect the host from infectious agents. 
Some of these mechanisms include virus neutralization, in 
which antibodies bind and prevent virus particles from infect-
ing target cells; opsonization, the process by which antibody 
molecules react with infectious agents and thus enhance their

phagocytosis; and antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity, 
the process whereby antibody-coated target cells are killed by 
Fc-receptor–bearing lymphocytes.
The adaptive humoral immune response to pathogenic 
viruses and resulting production of virus-specific antibod-
ies are an important component of antiviral immunity. 
Paradoxically, for some viruses, the presence of antibodies 
at sub-neutralizing concentrations can assist viral entry into 
certain cell types and permit replication of the virus. This 
phenomenon is known as antibody-dependent enhancement 
(ADE) of infection and occurs when virus-antibody com-
plexes interact with complement or Fc receptors on the cell 
surface. This interaction promotes internalization of the virus 
and increases infection. ADE has been associated with exac-
erbation of viral disease and presents challenges to antiviral 
vaccines and intravenous antiviral immunoglobulins [39].
Natural antibodies (NAb) are immunoglobulins produced 
by a B cell subset (B1 cells) that are germline encoded and 
present in individuals in the absence of prior antigenic expo-
sure and can belong to the IgM, IgG, or IgA subclasses [40]. 
NAb bind to exogenous antigens (e.g., bacteria) and can be a 
first line of defense against infections. Natural autoantibodies 
are NAb that recognize new self-epitopes as well as necrotic 
debris and apoptotic cells. Nab are also involved in suppres-
sion of inflammatory responses and autoimmune responses, 
regulation of B cell development and B cell responses, and 
maintenance of immune homeostasis [41].
39.3.2.2  Cell-Mediated Immune Response
CMI refers broadly to any host resistance mechanism in which 
cellular elements play a direct role and are part of the acquired 
(adaptive) arm of immunity. This is in comparison to HI in 
which there are certainly cellular interactions but in which the 
final host resistance products are soluble factors such as anti-
bodies. There are several host defenses mediated directly by 
cells including MØ-mediated cytolysis, antibody-dependent 
cellular cytotoxicity, and NK cell cytotoxicity, although cyto-
toxic T lymphocytes (CTLs) usually predominate, particularly 
in the destruction of virus-infected cells.
Functions associated with CMI are commonly considered 
the province of T lymphocytes, although immune cells (e.g., 
B cells and MØ) as well as non-immune cells (e.g., fibroblasts 
and DCs) contribute to the development of CMI. As the pri-
mary effector cell in CMI, the T cell represents one of the 
most complex and multifunctional immune cells. Antigens 
that generally elicit CMI include tissue-associated antigens, 
chemicals, and drugs that covalently bind to autologous pro-
teins and antigenic determinants on persistent intracellular 
microorganisms. The route of exposure also plays a signifi-
cant role in the type of response generated. For example, 
sheep erythrocytes elicit antibody production (but not CMI) 
when injected intravenously in humans but elicit both when 
injected intracutaneously. The induction of CMI proceeds 
when small lymphocytes differentiate and ultimately divide, 
giving rise to cells responsible for effector function as well 
as immunological memory. In contrast to HI, which is more 
effective against extracellular pathogens, CMI helps protect 
against intracellular bacteria, viruses, and neoplasia and is 
responsible for graft rejection.
T cells can differentiate into populations responsible for 
either regulatory or effector function. For example, regula-
tory and inducer T cell functions are provided by CD3/CD4 
positive T helper cells. CD4-positive T cells are important in 
a regulated immune response to pathogenic agents. T helper 
function facilitates antibody responses by B cells and assists 
in other T cell responses. For most antigens, B cells require 
assistance from T cells for differentiation into PC. T helper 
cells are integral in the B cell response by participating in two 
distinct mechanisms: major histocompatibility-locus–restricted 
B/T cell collaborations and cytokine-mediated differentiation. 
Helper function is a result of interactions between surface mol-
ecules on T helper cells and B cells, as well as the production 
and secretion of immunoregulatory cytokines. Following their 
interaction with antigen-MHC complex, naïve CD4 T cells are 
activated and differentiate into different subsets based on the 
microenvironment and cytokine milieu and include T helper 
(Th)1, Th2, Th17, Th9, Th22, regulatory T cell (Treg), follicu-
lar helper T cell (Tfh). These subsets have characteristic cyto-
kine profiles, and particular phenotypes become differentiated

An important conceptual breakthrough in immunology was 
the finding that two major populations of T helper cells exist 
that have different, sometimes opposing, functions [44]. The 
Th1/Th2 paradigm was based on the induction of specific cyto-
kines, with one population (Th1 cells), producing interleukin-2 
(IL-2), IFN-ć, and lymphotoxin and the second population 
(designated Th2 cells) producing IL-4, IL-5, IL-10, and IL-13. 
In addition to their critical role as helper cells in immunity to 
pathogens, CD4 T cells play a role in the pathology of aller-
gies and autoimmune diseases and the Th1/Th2 paradigm has 
been important for immunotoxicology in that a certain immu-
nopathology has been associated with the predominance of one 
helper cell type over another, particularly in human disease 
states. However, more recent work and the identification of the 
existence of a more heterogeneous and complex T helper subset

an oversimplification of the complex contribution of T helper 
cells to multifaceted immune-related pathologies [45, 46].

ecules. Another effector function is the ability of T cells to 
mediate suppressor activity for both T- and B cell responses. 
The effector CD8 T cells were initially categorized as fairly 
homogeneous but more recent findings indicate that multiple 
subsets of CD8 T cells exist with distinct effector function 
and varying cytotoxic capacity. In addition to CD8 regulatory, 
suppressor, and follicular T cells, there are a number of other 
effector cytotoxic T cell subsets that have been identified and 
have differing roles in antitumor responses [47].
39.3.3  Other Immunoregulatory Circuits
39.3.3.1  Cytokines and Chemokines
Cytokines are low-molecular-weight extracellular glycopro-
teins that are generally produced in response to cellular acti-
vation. These mediators are produced primarily by immune 
cells (e.g., T cells, neutrophils, MØ) but can also be produced 
by non-immune cells [48]. Cytokines are phylogenetically 
ancient, highly conserved, and involved in fundamental bio-
logical processes apart from key roles in immunity and host 
defense. IL-1 and tumor necrosis factor (TNF) are illustrative 
of this concept as they are intrinsically involved in apoptosis 
and cellular proliferation, both fundamental biological pro-
cesses. Thus, cytokines should be recognized for their role 
as conveyers of bioinformation, rather than as simple effector 
molecules involved in a single physiological process such as 
immunity and host resistance.
Within the realm of immunity and host resistance, how-
ever, cytokines aid in the orchestration of complex immune 
responses, and the responses are tailored to the nature of the 
challenge encountered by the system. Most cytokines studied 
have multiple and overlapping actions, and they function by 
binding to specific receptors on target cells. Cytokines can 
bind receptors on their target cells and act in an autocrine 
(binds to its own receptors resulting in signaling), paracrine 
(binds to neighboring cell receptors resulting in signaling in 
these cells), or endocrine (cytokine reaches the circulation 
and triggers a more global response) manner [49]; and recep-
tor expression can vary based on cell type and activation or 
maturation state [50]. The complexity of responses initi-
ated by members of the cytokine family is compounded by 
the nature of cytokine-receptor interactions, which generate 
subsequent intracellular signaling cascades and downstream 
effects, including cell growth, differentiation, activation, che-
motaxis, apoptosis, and the generation of various products, 
including additional cytokines. Cytokine-receptor interactions 
are multitudinous, as cytokine ligands may associate with an 
array of receptors and receptor binding sites may bind mul-
tiple ligands; interactive, as cytokines may enhance, dimin-
ish, or otherwise influence the effects of other cytokines; and 
functionally diverse, as signaling within different cell types 
may influence the expression of the hundreds of genes upon 
activation [51, 52]. While a detailed review of each mediator 
involved in manifold immunological responses, both benefi-
cial and pathological, is outside the scope of this chapter due to 
the highly complex nature of the cytokine signaling network, a 
brief overview is presented below.
ILs and IFNs are often classified together as cytokines as 
each represents soluble immunomodulators, and many can be 
classified as pro-inflammatory, regulatory, type-1 (TH1/TC1), 
type-2 (TH2/TC2), or TH17/TC17 [53, 52]. Pro-inflammatory 
cytokines, including TNF-−, IL-1−, and IL-1μ, typically 
induce inflammatory effects; regulatory cytokines such as 
IL-10 are generally associated with immunosuppressive or 
anti-inflammatory responses; type-1 cytokines, including 
IFN-ć and IL-12, may regulate type-2 responses, are involved 
in microbicidal responses, and have been implicated in auto-
immune disease; type-2 cytokines, including IL-4, IL-5, IL-9, 
and IL-13, may likewise regulate type-1 responses and may 
augment allergic inflammation; and TH17/TC17 cytokines, 
including IL-17A and IL-17F, have been implicated in gen-
eral pro-inflammatory responses autoimmune processes [53, 
52, 54]. There are many additional cytokines that have been 
described and categorized under these and other classifica-
tions, though distinct classifications are difficult to achieve due 
to the functional overlap diversity of individual cytokines. For 
convenience, cytokines may be grouped into several classes 
(Table 39.2). These classifications are necessarily arbitrary due 
to the overlapping activity of these molecules and dynamic as 
new cytokines are identified [55].
Chemokines, or chemotactic cytokines, are critical to the 
function of the innate immune system. They are produced by 
almost all cells of the body and are involved in a multitude 
of biological roles (Table 39.2), and as with cytokines, signal-
ing is complex and balanced with agonistic and antagonistic 
responses. This chemokine family includes more than 50 
endogenous chemokine ligands and transmembrane signal-
ing receptors. Ligands interact with cells bearing appropriate 
receptors, and receptor expression varies depending on cell 
type, maturation status, and activation status. Chemokines are 
largely responsible for the recruitment and residence of spe-
cific cell types in target tissues during homeostatic conditions 
as well as inflammatory responses and are also key contribu-
tors to cell homing, maturation, and activation processes [56].

These small molecules are important in the generation of both 
primary and secondary immune responses and are involved 
in the priming of naïve T cells, the differentiation of effec-
tor and memory cells, and the function of regulatory T cells 
[57–59]. Chemokines are also associated with the modula-
tion of the Th1/Th2 balance implicated in autoimmunity and 
hypersensitivity, serve as mediators of allergic inflammation, 
and modulators of leukocyte function in disease states such as 
rheumatoid arthritis and asthma [60–62].
Chemokines are named and grouped based on the number 
and position of their cysteines. The C chemokines have one 
N-terminal cysteine, the CC chemokines have two adjacent 
N-terminal cysteines, the CXC chemokines have two N-terminal 
cysteines that are separated by one amino acid, and the CX3C 
chemokine (CX3CL1 or fractalkine) has two N-terminal cyste-
ines with three amino acids in between [63]. Chemokine recep-
tors for each respective group are indicated by the letter “R” 
following the CC, CXC, or CX3C classification. While the nam-
ing convention is structurally based, many chemokines addi-
tionally have origin or function-related designations. CXCL10, 
for example, is also referred to as IFN-ć-induced protein 10 (IP-
10), as the IFN-ć cytokine induces its expression.
While cytokine and chemokine signaling is intended to pro-
tect the host and overcome challenges presented by infectious 
agents, unchecked cellular activation and cytokine and chemo-
kine release have the potential to cause or exacerbate disease 
states or, in an acute setting, to cause a lethal “cytokine storm”. 
Severe cytokine storms are generally characterized by sudden, 
massive, systemic inflammation, tissue damage, edema, hypo-
tension, and multiple organ dysfunctions. The mechanisms 
underlying the initiation of a cytokine storm are not well under-
stood, though it is thought that highly virulent pathogens and 
superantigens are capable of triggering such an event [64–66]. 
TGN1412, a humanized anti-CD28 antibody manufactured by 
TeGenero that had undergone a Phase I clinical trial, is one 
such superagonist that has been implicated in initiating cyto-
kine storms. Due to the critically necessary as well as poten-
tially deleterious effects of cytokine and chemokine signaling, 
an understanding and appreciation of the pathways involved in 
homeostatic and dysfunctional states are of utmost importance.
39.3.3.2  Immune/Nervous/Endocrine/
Coagulation System Crosstalk
While the immune system is critical to protection of the host 
from infections and tumors and has historically been presented 
mainly as a defense armamentarium, it is also now recognized 
as an integral part of reproduction, development, wound heal-
ing, tissue homeostasis, and maintaining system integrity [67]. 
It has also been shown that the immune system, central nervous 
system, cardiovascular system, coagulation, and endocrine 
systems, rather than being separate in function and structure, 
share many features and appear to cross-regulate each other’s 
function [68, 69]. For example, the complement proteins that 
are generally considered part of innate immunity activate (and 
are activated) and cross-regulated by platelets and the coagu-
lation and fibrinolysis systems [70]. The dysregulation of each 
cascade system and the associated pathologies have been high-
lighted by the COVID-19 pandemic. This viral infection can 
result in a fatal inflammatory response, wherein the homeosta-
sis between complement and extrinsic and intrinsic coagula-
tion pathways is disrupted and can contribute to microvascular 
thrombi in the lungs and kidneys and organ damage [71].

39.3.4  Comparative Immunology
Comparative immunology evaluates the structure and func-
tion of the immune system and mechanisms of pathogen rec-
ognition, immune signaling, integrated innate, and adaptive 
immune responses across species. This information is critical 
to our understanding of similarities and differences that exist 
regarding the translatability of preclinical data between human 
and nonhuman systems. It is therefore important to carefully 
select and evaluate model species based on the relevant path-
way and triggering events. A drug or chemical that affects or 
targets a particular pathway in a given cell type of the selected 
strain and species should also trigger similar relevant signal-
ing in humans. Even with careful selection, however, there 
are complex interactions between immune (and non-immune) 
pathways that can make translation challenging. Despite the 
challenges, while fundamental differences between humans 
and animals used in preclinical studies exist, data obtained 
from lower species can be instructive and indicative of poten-
tial human responses [72].
39.4  Concepts and Approaches for Detecting 
Immunotoxicity in Experimental Animals
39.4.1  Immunosuppression
39.4.1.1  Basic Considerations in Study Design
For the most part, adverse effects observed in the immune 
system are due to the general properties of the chemical (e.g., 
interaction with specific macromolecules) but are amplified by 
the complex nature of immune system responses. Dean et al. 
[73] originally suggested a tier approach to immunotoxicity 
testing, such that each subsequent tier provides identification 
of a more defined effect on the immune system. Tiered screen-
ing panels have been the basis for several risk assessment 
guidelines [74]. Although the configurations of these testing 
panels vary by laboratory and species, they generally include 
measures for (a) altered lymphoid organ weights and histomor-
phology; (b) quantitative changes in cellularity of lymphoid 
tissue, peripheral blood leukocytes, and bone marrow; (c) 
impairment of cell function at the effector or regulatory level; 
and (d) increased susceptibility to experimental challenge 
with infectious agents or transplantable tumor cells. While 
it is generally agreed that functional tests provide the great-
est level of sensitivity, some guidelines do not suggest func-
tional tests until Tier II or on a case-by-case basis. Instead, 
these guidelines suggest reliance on careful histological and 
hematological evaluation, particularly inclusion of extended 
histopathology endpoints [75, 76].
There are a number of advantages and limitations to using 
such test panels in animals or humans. For example, although 
the sensitivity of these methods to detect immune system 
changes in animal models may be high, it is difficult to extrap-
olate the clinical significance of subtle immune changes to 
humans. Furthermore, some of the tests require invasive pro-
cedures such as immunization and either require additional 
animals in standard 28-day toxicology studies or are not fea-
sible or ethical for inclusion in human studies.
A variety of factors must be considered when evaluating 
the potential of an environmental agent or drug to adversely 
influence the immune system. Assessment requires validation 
of the endpoints to be measured (quality control and biologi-
cal relevance) as well as knowledgeable selection of animal 
models, exposure parameters, and consideration of general 
toxicological parameters, including metabolism, distribution, 
and toxicokinetics. The treatment protocol should consider the 
potential route and level of exposure expected in humans, the 
biophysical properties of the agent (e.g., protein binding, bio-
availability, and toxicokinetics of the agent), as well as infor-
mation on the agent’s mechanism of action. Doses selected 
should attempt to establish a clear dose–response curve from 
a no-observable-adverse-effect level, up to or near the maxi-
mum tolerated dose. While in some instances it might be ben-
eficial to include a dose level that induces overt toxicity, any 
immune changes observed at such a dose should be interpreted 
cautiously, as either severe stress or malnutrition can impair 
immune responsiveness or the immune change is secondary to 
other organ toxicity. It is often recommended that the highest 
dose used be considerably lower than a dose producing severe 
weight loss. Although laboratories routinely employ three dose 
levels, dose range–finding studies are recommended prior to 
a full-scale immunotoxicology evaluation. The selected expo-
sure route should parallel the most probable route of human 
exposure, which is most frequently oral, respiratory, or der-
mal. Since these major routes of exposure are also associated 
with local immunity, attention may need to be directed to 
assessment of local immune responses.
Selection of the most appropriate animal model for immu-
notoxicology studies has been a matter of some discussion. 
Ideally, toxicity testing should be performed in a species that 
will elicit chemical- or drug-related pharmacology and toxici-
ties like those anticipated in humans. For most immunosup-
pressive therapeutics, results from rodent studies have been 
predictive of clinical observations [77, 78] although differ-
ences exist (e.g., glucocorticoids are lympholytic in rodents, 
but not in primates). While mouse and rat data have provided 
insights and translated relatively well, exceptions have been 
shown to occasionally occur in immune responses to particu-
lar agents [79].
The quantitative and qualitative response of an experimen-
tal animal to an immunotoxic agent can be influenced by their 
genetic composition (genotype), indicating a need to consider 
not only species, but strain as well [80].

the immune system plays in protection of the host from infec-

39.4.1.3  Techniques for Assessing  
Immunosuppression
The basic historical approach to immunotoxicity testing is based 
on the work of Luster et al. [83–85]. This early work validated 
the concept of the tier approach in which test materials are evalu-
ated for effects on the immune system using a biphasic system of 
descriptive and functional assays and is shown in Table 39.4. Tier 
I includes tests that are incorporated into a standard 28-day safety 
study and include routine hematology, selected organ weights 
(spleen, thymus), and histology of lymphoid organs. The inclu-
sion of the T cell–dependent antibody response (TDAR) using 
either sheep red blood cell (SRBC) or keyhole limpet hemocya-
nin (KLH) requires the use of additional animals and special-
ized techniques if evaluated in the spleen. However, additional 
animals are not required if blood is collected and plasma/serum 
is analyzed by enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA). 
Additionally, measures of NK cell activity and/or immunophe-
notype profile may also be included in assessments, neither of 
which require additional sets of animals.
The following tests are commonly performed in mice and 
rats, and many of these are now available for use in nonhuman 
primates (NHPs) [86, 87]. While not yet as readily available as 
for rodents and NHPs, some tests/reagents are also available in 
canines [88–90] and minipigs [91–95].
39.4.1.3.1  Routine Tests and Extended 
Immunopathology
A hemogram (complete blood count [CBC] and differential) 
and examination of lymphoid organ weights, including the 
spleen, thymus, and lymph node, are useful for assessing 
the immunotoxic potential of a material [96–98]. Because 
of the structural division of the spleen and lymph nodes 
into thymus-dependent and thymus-independent compart-
ments, immunocytochemical staining may indicate preferen-
tial effects for T or B cells, though this is seldom conducted. 
Likewise, microscopic examination of the thymus may reveal 
a compound that affects thymocyte viability, although careful 
measurement of thymus weight is simpler and provides equal, 
if not added, sensitivity. Extended histopathology provides 
considerably more information than routine histopathology 
and does not involve any specialized stains. This involves a 
semiquantitative assessment to estimate histological changes 
within different anatomical compartments of the spleen, thy-
mus, and lymph node and considers changes in cell density 
and anatomical compartment size [99, 100]. Five endpoints are 
examined in the spleen: cellularity of periarteriolar lymphoid 
sheaths, lymphoid follicles, marginal zone, red pulp, and the 
number of germinal centers. Three endpoints are examined in 
the thymus: cortex cellularity, medullary cellularity, and the 
corticomedullary ratio. Four endpoints are evaluated in the 
lymph node: grade of cellularity in the follicles, paracortical 
areas, medullary cords, and sinuses.

39.4.1.3.3  Antibody-Forming Cell (AFC) Assay
In the AFC assay, only Tfhs are required for the anti-SRBC 
response; therefore, with respect to T cell function, the AFC 
assay alone may not allow determination of toxicity of TH 
cells other than Tfh [102]. Within a few days following in vivo 
injection of a foreign antigen, antibody molecules of the IgM 
class are produced. The AFC assay (alternatively referred to 
as the plaque-forming cell [PFC] assay) quantitates the pro-
duction of a specific antibody through the enumeration of 
antibody-producing cells in the spleen following a primary 
antigenic stimulus such as SRBCs [103, 104]. The plaque 
assay has been, for the most part, replaced by ELISA tech-
niques in which antibody levels are determined directly from 
serum [105] using T-dependent antigens such as tetanus tox-
oid, chicken gamma globulin, and KLH, with KLH most often 
used in lieu of SRBCs as the immunizing antigen [106], par-
ticularly in pharmaceutical development. The steps involved in 
the TDAR assay are illustrated in Figure 39.4.

39.4.1.3.7  Phenotypic Analysis of Cell Surface 
Markers by Flow Cytometry
There are both pros and cons for including immunophenotypic 
analysis as part of an immunotoxicology screen. It offers a rapid, 
sensitive, and quantitative measure of a heterogeneous cell pop-
ulation in blood or cell suspensions prepared from lymphoid 
organs. A major concern is that the test is not well validated in 
rodents, particularly with respect to sensitivity or defining the 
thresholds for which observed changes relate to disease. For 
mechanistic studies, however, they are of unquestionable rele-
vance, especially when considering the vast array of well-defined 
surface markers now available for rodent and human immune 
cells as well as NHPs and other non-rodent species [120–124]. 
The technique involves treating cells with monoclonal antibodies 
covalently bound to different fluorochromes. These antibodies 
recognize surface antigens, referred to as cluster of differentia-
tion (CD) antigens, which are unique to different cell types. The 
availability of fluorochromes, which emit light at different wave-
lengths following excitation, combined with flow cytometers that 
can perform multiple color analysis, provides a rapid and effec-
tive method of analyzing cell types.
39.4.1.3.8  Mechanistic Immunotoxicology Assays
From its beginnings, the discipline of immunotoxicology has 
constantly evolved and incorporated new techniques and para-
digms to understand the nature of immunomodulation. The 
assays described previously in this section allow one to make a 
first-pass evaluation of drugs and chemical agents for generalized 
immunotoxicity. That is, the assays may indicate whether or not 
the immune system has been perturbed, although the cellular and 
molecular mechanisms involved will not necessarily be obvious. 
These assays are valuable for quick and relatively accurate identi-
fication of toxic agents. On the other hand, the tools and concepts 
of immunotoxicology are increasingly utilized as research tools 
to understand the function of the immune system. For example, 
it may be useful to know not only whether or not an agent modu-
lates the immune response, but how and why. This is especially 
important in the discovery and development of pharmaceutical 
agents, where therapeutic manipulation of the immune system 
may be a desirable goal. In response to these novel applications 
of immunotoxicology, assays are needed that will allow for the 
determination of the precise mechanism of immunomodulation.

The methodology for Tier II-type assays such as T cell–
mediated immune function (CTL/DTH) and proliferation, 
MØ, and neutrophil function, and host resistance models have 
been reviewed in detail elsewhere and will not be reiterated

included in the immunotoxicology armamentarium.
Assessment of apoptosis: Apoptosis (programmed cell death) 
is increasingly recognized as a fundamental process in both 
health and disease states, including the response to toxic insult 
[130]. Apoptosis plays a vital role in the immune response, 
regulating the number and action of immune cells such as lym-
phocytes [131, 132]. Given the important role apoptosis plays in 
normal regulation of the immune system as well as its implica-
tion in immune-related disease, it is a logical and potentially 
valuable endpoint for mechanistic immunotoxicology evalua-
tion [133, 134]. Apoptosis has been found to play an important 
role in the immunotoxicity of a number of compounds, includ-
ing organotins, polychlorinated biphenyls, methylmercury, and 
2,3,7,8-tetrachlorodibenzo-p-dioxin [135–138].
Numerous techniques are available for assessing apopto-
sis, including analysis of DNA degradation, flow cytometry, 
morphological analysis, 3−-OH end labeling, and endonuclease 
analysis [139, 126]. Several ELISAs have also become avail-
able for assessing apoptosis; these ELISAs are based on the 
detection of Bcl-2 or histone-associated DNA fragments. The 
ELISA format offers several benefits over the other techniques 
including rapidity, simplicity, and cost-effectiveness.
Cytokine analysis: As described previously, cytokines repre-
sent an important mechanism not only for regulating immune 
system function but also for linking the immune system with 
other organ systems. As the intricacies of the cytokine/chemo-
kine network become better understood, cytokine assays allow 
for the elucidation and assessment of mechanisms responsible 
for underlying a variety of immunomodulatory effects and 
immunopathologies [140]. Cytokines play key roles in the ini-
tiation, amplification, and specificity of immune responses. 
Aberrant production of cytokines can lead to modulation of 
the immune response such that the host may be more suscep-
tible to infectious agents or development of allergic or autoim-
mune response to agents. Indeed, a variety of nonbiological 
agents have been described that are capable of specifically or 
nonspecifically altering cytokine production. These agents act 
via myriad mechanisms, including direct toxicity to cytokine-
producing cells (cyclophosphamide), inhibition of cytokine 
production (cyclosporin, FK506, pentoxifylline), inhibition of 
cytokine release (pentamidine), induction of immunosuppres-
sive factors (leflunomide), alterations in cellular homeostasis 
(tenidap), alterations in cellular activation or transcription 
mechanisms (thalidomide), alteration of cell cycle progres-
sion (Rapamune), and miscellaneous or undefined mecha-
nisms (glucocorticoids, phosphodiesterase isozyme inhibitors, 
metalloproteinase inhibitors, and p38 kinase inhibitors) [141].
Multiple platforms are available for cytokine assessments 
and include bioassays, immunoassays (ELISA and enzyme-
linked immunospot [ELISPOT]), mRNA gene expression 
analyses, protein microarrays, immunostaining of cells and 
tissues, and multiparametric and bead-based flow cytometry 
[142]. Each of these assay types has advantages and disadvan-
tages, as no one assay is best suited for all applications. The 
type of assay chosen is subjective and will depend on the capa-
bilities of the laboratory, as well as the type of information to 
be gained [143].
39.4.2  Allergy and Autoimmune 
Reactions to Xenobiotics
39.4.2.1  Introduction and Fundamental Concepts
Immune reactions to xenobiotics (e.g., industrial and natu-
rally occurring chemicals, metals, and drugs) can give rise to 
allergy and autoimmunity, and these reactions are frequent 
and can encompass a broad spectrum of diseases and organs. 
To decrease the health risks associated with exposure to these 
agents, it is important to understand the pathogenic mecha-
nisms involved and to identify human subjects at risk of devel-
oping these reactions prior to serious clinical manifestations.
Allergic or hypersensitivity reactions, which include aller-
gic asthma and rhinitis, food and environmental allergies, and 
allergic contact dermatitis, are one of the most common and 
costly health problems in the United States afflicting a large 
portion of the population. Individuals with potential occupa-
tional exposure (e.g., agricultural or manufacturing workers) 
are at a higher risk than the general public for the develop-
ment of respiratory and cutaneous hypersensitivity reactions 
to chemicals. Recent estimates place the financial burden of 
asthma alone at over 80 billion dollars annually, which include 
direct medical costs as well as indirect costs associated with 
absenteeism and wages lost due to asthma-related illness and 
mortality [144]. The nationwide burden of allergic disease in 
the United States estimates that 9.3%, 7.2%, and 12.2% of chil-
dren have respiratory, food, and skin allergies, respectively 
[145]. The burden of allergic diseases in adults in the United 
States are similar with approximately 7.6% reporting respira-
tory allergies like hay fever [146] and 10.8% reporting food 
allergies [147]. Industrial processes utilize many materials 
capable of inducing occupational immunological lung disease 
or contact hypersensitivity in workers and thus must be rigor-
ously controlled to ensure worker safety. Studies in the metal-
refining industry, for example, suggested that many workers 
regularly exposed to the complex salts of platinum develop 
disorders of the respiratory tract. Importantly, workplace 
exposures are estimated to be the cause of approximately 11% 
of all adult cases of asthma in the United States with occu-
pational chemical exposure increasing the risk of work-life 
asthma by 60% [148]. The proportion of adult asthma attribut-
able to workplace exposures had been declining in the past 
decades, however, likely owing to improved monitoring, engi-
neering controls, and adherence to workplace policies govern-
ing the use of personal protective equipment.
Drug allergy is also a significant problem and among the 
most common causes for new pharmaceuticals to be with-
drawn from the market following approval and release. This 
adverse immune reaction is not easy to predict utilizing the 
current battery of preclinical safety assessment methods [149]. 
A distinction should be made between immunologically medi-
ated adverse drug responses that represent true drug allergy

In drug allergy, the adverse reaction occurs as a consequence 
of an immune response directed against the drug. Examples of 
drug allergy include immediate anaphylactic reactions medi-

unique in that they can provoke allergic and autoimmune reac-
tions against blood cells, including erythrocytes and platelets, 
as well as a variety of other antigens, including haptenated drug. 
Usually, the reaction occurs to the drug or drug metabolites, in 
which case it is necessary for both the drug and the antibody 
to be present to produce the allergic or autoimmune reaction. 
In addition to producing allergic manifestations or pathology 
under certain conditions, drug-specific antibodies can also 
alter the pharmacokinetics and clearance of the drug in plasma. 
Thus, drug-induced allergic reactions can be present in many 
forms, producing either allergic or autoimmune phenomena.
39.4.2.2  Allergic Hypersensitivity in 
Response to Xenobiotic Exposure
Allergy (acute hypersensitivity) is a pathological state resulting 
from prior sensitization to a specific molecule or structurally 
related compound. Because of the clinical and pathological 
similarities between xenobiotic-induced allergy, autoimmu-
nity, and Graft-versus-host (GVH) reactions, there is consider-
able evidence that these reactions are initiated and maintained 
by sensitized T cells [151–153]. A primary difficulty in the 
elucidation of these immune reactions is that the neoantigen 
formed by the chemical or drug is often unknown. Based on 
current understanding, it is assumed that allergic reactions 
to most xenobiotics involve the formation of protein adducts 
(i.e., hapten–carrier conjugates). Metal ions react by oxidiz-
ing proteins or forming stable protein–metal chelates through 
multiple binding points with several amino acid side chains 
in the protein. A second mechanism for xenobiotics not reac-
tive enough to bind covalently to proteins is through the direct 
antigen recognition by ć–č and −–μ T cells. This hypothesis is 
supported by evidence of human gamma–delta T cells specific 
for the drug lidocaine and human CD8+ alpha–beta T cells 
that recognize pollen-derived carbohydrate antigen [154, 155].
Thus, there appear to be multiple mechanisms by which 
xenobiotics can become sensitizers. Highly reactive organic 
compounds most often act as electrophiles and react with pro-
tein nucleophilic groups such as thiol, amino, and hydroxyl 
groups to form hapten–protein conjugates [156]. Examples of 
reactive, haptenic chemicals that frequently lead to sensitiza-
tion after dermal or inhalation exposure are TDI, trimellitic 
anhydride, phthalic anhydride, benzoquinone, formaldehyde, 
hexyl cinnamic aldehyde, ethylene oxide, dinitrochloroben-
zene (DNCB), picryl chloride, penicillins, and d-penicil-
lamine. In contrast, most xenobiotics are unable to bind to 
proteins until they are converted to reactive metabolites and 
are thus considered prohaptens.
The liver is the main site for metabolic conversion of chemi-
cals and drugs, and despite this fact, immune reactions against 
the liver or its constituents are relatively rare. However, such 
reactions, although rare, have been observed in cases of auto-
immune hepatitis resulting from chronic treatment with the 
diuretic, tienilic acid; an outcome believed to be due to the 
production of autoantibodies against the cytochrome P450 iso-
zyme 2C9, the enzyme that converts this prohapten [157]. Two 
other examples of drug-induced autoimmune hepatitis involv-
ing a similar mechanism whereby autoantibodies are raised 
against the enzyme converting the prohapten to a reactive 
metabolite include the anesthetic halothane (anti-CYPE1) and 
dihydralazine (anti-CYP1A2) [158, 159].
Extrahepatic metabolism appears to play a more critical 
role in hypersensitivity reactions in prohapten conversion, 
and the skin serves as an example of an organ with metabolic 
conversion potential via dendritic Langerhans cells (isoen-
zyme CYP1A) or inflammatory leukocytes (myeloperoxidase 
[MPO], prostaglandin H synthase, and CYPs). Examples of 
prohaptens converted in the skin include the polyaromatic 
hydrocarbon dimethylbenz-[a]-anthracene that is converted to 
a potent haptenic sensitizer and urushiol, a mixture of aller-
genic 2-alkyl and 3-alkenyl catechols from poison ivy and 
poison oak that are oxidized to reactive quinones that elicit 
specific T cell responses [160, 161].
Exposure to any of a number of industrial chemicals and 
drugs has been associated with the development of allergic or 
hypersensitivity reactions (Table 39.5). In addition, in some 
cases, the reactive xenobiotic metabolite acts as a hapten, 
resulting in an adverse immune response in rodents, man, or 
both (Table 39.6).
It is not well understood why only a few of the hapten–pro-
tein conjugates formed in the body induce clinical allergy or 
autoimmunity. Exposure route, dose, and genetic background 
clearly all play important roles in the process. Cutaneous sen-
sitization of mice to the hapten dinitrofluorobenzene (DNFB) 
does not occur if animals are orally pretreated with DNFB 
[162]. This is believed to be due to the induction of both 
hapten-specific CD8+ T cells and CD4+ suppressor T cells. 
In the case of dose, subsensitizing doses of oxazolone induce 
tolerance, while higher doses sensitize. Genetic background 
further complicates our ability to accurately predict responses 
as observed with gold thiomalate, which induces a Th2-like 
response in brown Norway rats but not in Lewis rats [163].

39.4.2.3  Autoimmunity in Response 
to Xenobiotic Exposure
Autoimmunity is a multifactorial pathological response com-
prised of at least two processes. Initially, an immune response 
is initiated to “self” components of the host, and second, a 
pathological condition may ensue in which the response 
causes structural or functional damage (e.g., pathology). An 
autoimmune response does not necessarily reflect disease, 
although it is a prerequisite for disease to occur. The autoim-
mune response induced can be cellular in nature, mediated 
by CD4 and/or CD8 T cells, or more often, may arise from 
the antibody response, mediated by specific B cells. The most 
common autoimmune diseases are rheumatoid arthritis and 
those associated with the thyroid, such as Graves’ disease or 
Hashimoto’s thyroiditis. In total, they represent a significant 
and chronic morbidity with over 100 different disease mani-
festations that afflict 7–9% of the population, with women at 
2.7 times greater risk than men [164, 165].
What is the underlying problem in autoimmunity and auto-
immune disease? It is well understood that the immune system 
can recognize and produce a response against foreign mate-
rial while maintaining tolerance toward the body’s own con-
stituents. Unfortunately, immunological tolerance to self is not 
absolute, and autoantibodies can be produced experimentally 
and have been observed in a variety of human autoimmu-
nity diseases (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis, systemic lupus ery-
thematosus [SLE], and myasthenia gravis). The mechanisms 
responsible for conversion from an autoimmune response to 
autoimmune disease are not clear and may involve multiple 
pathways [166]. It is believed that the failure of any one of 
several immune processes can lead to the development of 
autoimmune disease. However, the key process involves the 
loss of self-tolerance, such as the missed deletion or activation 
of autoreactive lymphocyte precursors. This process may be 
exacerbated by altered immunoregulation, such as overexpres-
sion of the immunoregulatory cytokine IL-4 or C4 deficiency 
[167]. Autoimmunity may also occur in the absence of an aber-
ration in the immune system, such as when microbial agents 
express cryptic determinants [168]. Although autoimmunity is 
a disease of the immune system, nonimmunological genetic 
and epigenetic factors play major roles in disease develop-
ment. For example, autoimmunity is influenced strongly 
by infectious agents, stress, and diet (epigenetic), as well as 
polymorphisms in the T-cell receptor (TCR) and drug metabo-
lizing phenotypes (genetic) [169]. The association of autoim-
mune diseases with certain MHC haplotypes, such as human 
leukocyte antigen (HLA)-DR3 in systemic lupus, is striking.
In drug-induced autoimmunity, the adverse reaction results 
from an immune response directed against the body’s own tis-
sue, or subcellular or cellular components. A variety of drugs, 
with a molecular weight of less than 1,000 Da, from a vari-
ety of different chemical classes, have been shown to induce 
autoimmunity. These include aromatic amines (procain-
amide, practolol), hydrazines (hydralazine), hydantoins (phe-
nytoin, mephenytoin, ethotoin, nitrofurantoin), thioureylenes 
(methimazole, propylthiouracil), oxazolidinediones (trimetha-
dione, paramethadione), succinimides (ethosuximide, meth-
suximide, phensuximide), dibenzazepines (carbamazepine), 
phenothiazines (chlorpromazine), sulfonamides (sulfasala-
zine, sulfadiazine), pyrazolones (phenylbutazone), amino 
acids (D-penicillamine, captopril, methyldopa), allylamines 
(zimeldine, halothane), and certain metal salts (mercuric 
chloride, gold salts). It is important to note that drug-induced 
autoimmune disease is different than the classical sponta-
neous counterpart. For example, in drug-induced autoim-
mune disease, the disease is usually milder, there is minimal 
organ involvement, autoantibodies to native DNA are seldom 
observed in the circulation, and disease remission occurs fol-
lowing cessation of drug treatment. The latter phenomenon 
may be unique to compounds that induce autoimmunity via 
the haptenization of native proteins. Nonetheless, the health 
significance of this adverse effect can be significant.
The most common examples of drugs implicated in the 
emergence of autoimmune diseases are those that cause hema-
tological disorders such as neutropenia, thrombocytopenia, 
and immune hemolysis and include a variety of antibiotics 
as well as anticonvulsants such as phenytoin (Table 39.7). 
Approximately 10–20% of patients receiving procainamide 
and 5–20% receiving hydralazine develop drug-induced 
SLE. More recently, unexpected hematologic toxicity was 
related to treatment with a biotherapeutic treatment [170]. 
Many drugs that are implicated in autoimmunity are known 
to function through reactive metabolites that are capable of 
interacting and binding to host biomolecules. Recent efforts 
have used machine learning to predict the potential for auto-
immunity using structural alerts of reactive metabolites and 
daily dose metrics [171]. As a result, nitrogen-containing

Norway rat and B.10 mice suggested that the same genetic influ-
ences apply in animals as in humans [174].
39.4.2.4  Uncontrolled Cytokine Release in 
Response to Xenobiotic Exposure
Inappropriate regulation of the immune system in response 
to xenobiotic exposure, especially certain biologic drugs, 
can result in the inappropriate and/or uncontrolled release 
of cytokines from immune cells. This phenomenon has been 
coined as cytokine release syndrome and cytokine storm 
[175]. Essentially, uncontrolled activation of the immune cells 
results in release of copious amounts of cytokines, mostly 
pro-inflammatory, into organs and circulation leading to a sys-
temic inflammatory response reminiscent of anaphylaxis. The 
dysregulated sequelae of cytokines have pathologic conse-
quences characterized by systemic inflammation, multiorgan 
pathology, and failure and can be fatal. This immune response 
is more frequently a potential issue with immunomodulatory 
biologics, a relatively new class of drugs. Isolated examples 
include a case of cytokine storm observed following adminis-
tration of therapeutic chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T cells 
in a metastatic colon cancer patient [176]. Within 15 minutes 
of CAR+ T cell administration, the patient exhibited respira-
tory distress associated with marked pulmonary inflammatory 
infiltrate and serum samples showed marked increases in sev-
eral cytokines including pro-inflammatory cytokines, which 
was consistent with cytokine storm. The potential for tar-
geted immunomodulatory biologics to cause cytokine storm 
was highlighted in 2006 when cytokine release with multiple 
organ failure was observed in the initial Phase 1 clinical trial 
for TGN1412 [177]. TGN1412 was a novel humanized super-
agonist anti-CD28 monoclonal antibody that targeted and acti-
vated T cells. Out of eight trial participants, all six that received 
TGN1412 presented systemic inflammation and induction of 
pro-inflammatory cytokines within 2 hours of infusion. These 
changes were accompanied by gastrointestinal and cardiovas-
cular distress that progressed to life-threatening pulmonary 
damage, multiorgan failure, and disseminated intravascular 
coagulation. Owing to rapid medical attention including car-
diopulmonary and organ support and anti-inflammatory ther-
apy, all six individuals survived. Since this initial observation, 
several other immunomodulatory biologic drugs have been 
implicated in the initiation of cytokine storm (Table 39.8).
There are several multiplex platforms currently available that 
can measure a myriad of cytokines and chemokines in human

39.4.2.5  Techniques for Assessing Contact 
and Respiratory Allergy
Given the potential economic and medical importance of 
hypersensitivity, the importance of sensitive and reliable assays 
for the detection of sensitizing potential for drugs and chemi-
cals is obvious. For over 100 years, the guinea pig has served 
as the principal model for allergic reactions in humans since 
they demonstrate many similarities in their response to pulmo-
nary hypersensitivity (response to histamine, demonstration of 
immediate and delayed allergic reactions, etc.), as well as der-
mal hypersensitivity. In addition, the lightly pigmented skin 
of albino guinea pigs and their relatively small size and docile 
nature make them manageable model animals. Thus, they have 
traditionally been used for assessing the human safety of drugs, 
as well as other chemicals, for contact and respiratory sensitiza-
tion. Based on the specific experimental needs at hand, a variety 
of modifications have been described. In this section, we will 
discuss only two, the Buehler assay and the guinea pig maximi-
zation test, which are the two most widely used guinea pig tests 
for risk evaluation of contact sensitization.
The mouse has been developed as an alternative model to 
the guinea pig. The impetus for this development has been the 
mouse’s small size and reduced cost, more thoroughly under-
stood immune system, and the perceived need of a more quan-
titative endpoint than the subjective degree of erythema that is 
the hallmark of most guinea pig assays. Of the various mouse 
models, the murine local lymph node assay (LLNA) first 
described by Kimber and Weisenberger [180] has undergone 
extensive validation studies [181] and is the in vivo method 
of choice for identifying contact sensitizers, however, as with 
other in vivo testing approaches, is rapidly being replaced by 
alternative methods as discussed below.
Considerable efforts have been devoted to establishing rela-
tively simple tests to identify respiratory and food allergens. 
Guinea pig models have been used historically for the assess-
ment of potential respiratory sensitizers [182, 183]. One is 
directed to some excellent reviews on the use of these assays in 
risk assessment and drug development [184–186]. In addition, 
murine models for assessment of respiratory sensitizing poten-
tial, such as cytokine fingerprinting and the mouse IgE test, 
have been examined for their utility [187–189]. However, to 
date, there are no validated standard methods for hazard iden-
tification and characterization of respiratory sensitizers [190]. 
Likewise, several models are being developed to identify food 
allergens including the development of IgE antibody in brown 
Norway rats or BALB/c mice and a transgenic mouse strain 
engineered to produce class II HLA molecules [191, 192].
More recently, there has been mounting pressure and need 
to reduce the use of animals in science and medical research. 
In fact, in 2013, the European Union implemented a ban on 
the use of animals in toxicity testing for cosmetics [193] and 
non-animal testing methods are now part of the European 
Registration, Evaluation, Authorization and Restriction of 
Chemicals (REACH) regulations. More recently, the draft 
guidance “Nonclinical Safety Evaluation of the Immunotoxic 
Potential of Drugs and Biologics Guidance for Industry” [194] 
from the US Food and Drug Administration (US FDA) has 
forgone the LLNA in favor of alternative methods for the 
assessment of sensitization and hypersensitivity. In response 
to the changing approaches for hypersensitivity testing, the 
field of immunotoxicology has leveraged decades of discov-
ery from animal research investigating the pathobiology and 
mechanisms of allergy and hypersensitivity to better under-
stand opportunities, value, and risk associated with the devel-
opment of alternative methods. These efforts culminated in the 
definition of the Adverse Outcome Pathway (AOP) for dermal

the detection of respiratory sensitizers [201]. These assays will 
be valuable additions to the non-animal alternatives toolbox for 
prediction of chemical sensitizers.

39.4.2.5.1.1  Buehler Assay  The Buehler assay [202, 203] 
was originally developed to evaluate strong and moderate 
contact sensitizers, leaving only negative or weakly positive 
compounds for testing in humans. The hallmark of the Buehler 
assay was the use of an occlusive patch to enhance or exagger-
ate exposure to test materials. This method also has the advan-
tage of using an exposure method similar to that encountered 
in human exposure. The specific technique for performing this 
assay is involved and is only summarized below.
Materials and Reagents Required:
•	 Young adult albino guinea pigs (Dunkin–Hartley 
strain)
•	 Guinea pig restrainers
•	 Patch delivery system (e.g., Hilltop chambers, Webril 
patch, polymorphic membrane protein (PMP) patch)
•	 Dental dam
Procedure:
	
1.	On the day before induction exposure, clip the guinea 
pig’s fur. Expose the skin to the selected test dose 
using a patch delivery system and then restrain the 
animal using a combination of guinea pig restrainer 
and dental dam. Duration of exposure, number of 
inductions, and induction regimens may vary but are 
generally three 6 hours induction exposures with an 
interval of 5–9 days.
	
2.	Approximately 2 weeks after the last induction expo-
sure, the animals are exposed to the test material 
again but at a different skin site that has not been 
previously exposed. Again, the timing and duration 
of exposure may vary.
	
3.	If necessary, an animal may be rechallenged with 
test material between 6 and 14 days following the 
primary challenge.
	
4.	The day after the challenge or rechallenge depil-
ate the animals using a commercial depilatory. Two 
hours later, the animals are ready to be scored.
	
5.	Results are scored as 0 (no reaction), ± (slight patchy 
erythema), 1 (slight but confluent erythema), 2 (mod-
erate erythema), and 3 (severe erythema, with or 
without edema). Scoring should be performed at 24 
and 48 hours after the challenge or rechallenge.
	
6.	Scores of 1 or greater in the test group usually indi-
cate that the test material is a sensitizer, if the scores 
in control animals are less than 1. Results of the chal-
lenge and rechallenge should be expressed as both 
incidence and severity.
Positive Control:
DNCB has been the traditional positive control for this assay. 
Suggested test concentrations are 0.3% DNCB in ethanol for 
induction and two concentrations of DNCB in acetone (0.05% 
and 0.01%) for challenge. Inclusion of two different challenge 
doses provides for a range of response.
Notes:
	
1.	An irritation screen is performed prior to the actual 
test. This assay requires an induction concentration 
that will not produce severe irritation or toxicity. 
The concentration used for challenge should produce 
only a slight degree of irritation.
	
2.	Proper technique is essential to the success of this 
assay. In particular, animal restraint, test material 
occlusion, and consistency in scoring are all critical 
aspects. Whenever possible, it would be advisable 
to learn these techniques from a laboratory that has 
already demonstrated success with the assay.
	
3.	Due to the relatively small number of animals and the 
nature of the readout, statistical analysis has not gener-
ally been practical for this assay. Rather, hazard assess-
ment has been defined in terms of threshold levels.
	
4.	It is important to maintain occlusion for the entire 
duration of the exposure in this assay. Proper use of 
the restraining devices is considered to be critical

to obtaining consistent, meaningful results. Proper 
clipping and depilation of the animals are also 
important variables.
39.4.2.5.1.2  Guinea Pig Maximization Assay  The maxi-
mization assay was described by Magnusson and Kligman in 
1964 [204] and was developed to maximize the sensitivity of 
guinea pig tests. This was accomplished by intradermal injec-
tions of test material, inclusion of Freund’s complete adjuvant 
(FCA), and the use of a pretreatment to irritate the skin at 
the site of exposure. These treatments enhanced the chance 
of a test material to penetrate the skin and subsequently pro-
duce allergic contact dermatitis [205]. Perhaps even more so 
than the Buehler assay, the maximization assay is technically 
detailed, and only the basics of its performance are summa-
rized below.
Materials and Reagents Required:
•	 Young adult albino guinea pigs (Dunkin–Hartley 
strain)
•	 FCA
•	 Hilltop chambers or PMP patches
•	 Hypoallergenic tape and elastic wrap
•	 Dental dam
Procedure:
	
1.	Similar to the Buehler assay, a preliminary irrita-
tion/toxicity screen must be performed to determine 
the highest concentration of test material that can 
be tested. Both intradermal injection and occlusive 
patch tests are performed.
	
2.	For the induction step, the animal’s fur is clipped over 
the back on either side of the spine, and test material 
is injected intradermally in a volume of 0.1 mL. Each 
animal receives a total of six injections: two each of 
diluted adjuvant, two each of adjuvant containing 
test material, and two each of test material in vehicle. 
Control animals are treated similarly but do not receive 
test material. Let the animals rest for 6 days.
	
3.	If the test material being used is not an irritant, the 
injection sites should be treated with 10% sodium 
lauryl sulfate in petrolatum under an occluded patch 
for 24 hours. This step may be skipped if the test 
material is a known irritant.
	
4.	Place 0.8 mL of test material on a PMP patch (booster 
patch) and place this patch over the injection sites. 
Cover with dental dam and wrap the animal with 
elastic tape. Control animals should be treated with 
vehicle only. Remove the booster patch after 48 hours.
	
5.	Challenge the animals 10 days later by exposing them to 
test material (or vehicle) under an occlusive patch (PMP 
patch or Hilltop chamber). Wrap the animals with den-
tal dam and elastic tape. Note: Animals must be clipped 
prior to challenge, since the fur will have grown back.
	
6.	Remove the patches 24 hours later. Approximately 
21 hours later, remove any remaining fur with depila-
tory. Grade the reactions approximately 24 and 
48 hours, after the challenge patches are removed.
	
7.	Animals may be rechallenged with the same test 
material at the same or a different site within 2 weeks 
of the primary challenge. Naïve test sites should be 
used on the animals.
	
8.	The grading of this assay is like that of the Buehler 
assay.
Positive Control:
The positive control material generally used in the maxi-
mization test is 1-chloro-2,4-dinitrobenzene, at a concen-
tration of 0.1% in a vehicle of propylene glycol for the 
intradermal injections. The booster patch incorporates 
0.1% (w/v) of test material in an 80:20 ethanol/water (vol/
vol) vehicle.
39.4.2.5.1.3  Murine Local Lymph Node Assay  Guinea 
pig contact sensitization models have been used for assess-
ing the potential of compounds to induce hypersensitivity 
reactions for some time, but they have certain drawbacks. 
The endpoints are subjective and require skilled techni-
cians to evaluate the intensity of the reaction; in addition, 
this subjectivity precludes the use of statistical analysis. 
Moreover, the assays are relatively expensive and time-
consuming, and there are animal welfare issues regarding 
the use of an adjuvant. Although none of these issues alone 
are major detriments, together they led to the search for an 
alternative method.
In 1989, Kimber and Weisenberger [206] reported the 
development of an alternative approach to assess potential 
contact hypersensitivity using the mouse as a model system 
referred to as the LLNA. In contrast to the guinea pig tests 
described previously, sensitizing activity is assessed as a 
function of events occurring in the induction, rather than the 
elicitation phase of sensitization and, thus, does not utilize a 
secondary (challenge) exposure to the test material. The test 
involves quantifying cell proliferation in the draining lymph 
node following topical application of the test material. The 
LLNA exhibits several advantages over guinea pig assays 
including more quantitative and objective endpoints, insen-
sitivity to colored compounds, reduced turnaround time and 
cost, being independent of specialized reagents or materials 
(adjuvant, wrapping material), and benefits to animal wel-
fare. Currently, the LLNA has become the preferred method 
for assessing skin sensitization hazard by most regulatory 
authorities.
The biological basis of the LLNA is simple. Test materials 
are applied epicutaneously to the dorsal surface of the pin-
nae (Figure 39.8). The test agent is transported from the skin 
by Langerhans cells to the draining (i.e., local) lymph nodes, 
where it is presented to T lymphocytes. Contact sensitizers 
induce proliferation of these T cells. By radiolabeling these 
proliferating cells in situ using a radioactive tracer or a direct 
cell count, it is possible to determine the degree of proliferation 
induced. It is important to note that the LLNA does not utilize 
a secondary exposure (challenge) to the test material, as do 
the guinea pig assays. Thus, the LLNA differs fundamentally 
in that it evaluates only the induction phase of the hypersen-
sitivity response. A chemical is classified as a skin sensitizer 
if at one or more test concentrations it induces a threefold or

1975
Immunotoxicology
greater increase in draining lymph node cell proliferation 
compared with concurrent vehicle-treated controls (stimula-
tion index [SI] ∈ 3). In the original LLNA, four mice per treat-
ment group were used. The use of a reduced (r) LLNA has also 
been approved by the International Coordinating Committee 
on the Validation of Alternative Methods (ICCVAM) under 
certain circumstances. Since the rLLNA does not provide 
dose–response information, its utility for risk assessment pur-
poses is limited.
The LLNA has been the subject of numerous interna-
tional validation studies, validation data were evaluated by 
ICCVAM, and the assay was found to provide an equivalent 
prediction of the risk for human contact dermatitis when com-
pared to guinea pig assays [207, 208]; in addition, the method 
was robust, sensitive, and reproducible. Efforts have also been 
made to define ways in which the LLNA can be used to mea-
sure the relative potency of contact allergens including a two-
category system for the classification and labeling of sensitizers 
under the Globally Harmonized System of Classification and 
Labeling of Chemicals (high or low potency), which correlates 
with findings in humans [209, 210].
The following protocol is the standard procedure recom-
mended by the ICCVAM Working Group (IWG) [211]:
Materials and Reagents Required:
•	 Female CBA/J mice, 6–9 weeks old at initiation of 
assay
•	 Tritiated thymidine ([3H]TdR), specific activity 5–10 
Ci/mM
•	 PBS
•	 Nylon mesh (100 ōm opening size)
•	 15 mL conical-capped polypropylene centrifuge 
tubes
•	 5% (w/v) trichloroacetic acid (TCA)
•	 Scintillation vials and scintillation cocktail
Method:
	
1.	Apply vehicle, test compound, or positive control com-
pound to the dorsum of each pinna (25 ōL/ear), ensur-
ing that the vehicle is evenly distributed on the pinna. 
Dose the animals daily for three consecutive days.
	
2.	Rest the mice for 2 days, and then inject each mouse 
intravenously with 20 ōCi of [3H]TdR in saline.
	
3.	Five hours following [3H]TdR injection, euthanize 
the mice by CO2 and remove the lymph nodes drain-
ing the ear. Place the nodes from individual mice in 
culture tubes containing 4 mL of PBS.
	
4.	Transfer the nodes from the culture tubes to Petri 
dishes containing a 1 in.2 of nylon mesh. Gently rub 
the lymph node cells through the mesh, then transfer 
the cell suspension back to the tube, and allow it to 
settle for approximately 5 minutes.
	
5.	Transfer the cell suspension to a 15 mL centrifuge 
tube containing 6 mL of PBS, taking care not to 
transfer the sedimented debris. Centrifuge the tubes 
for 10 minutes at approximately 200 × g. Wash the 
cells a second time in PBS.
	
6.	After the second wash, suspend the cell pellet in 
3 mL of 5% TCA and incubate at approximately 4°C 
for approximately 18 hours.
	
7.	Centrifuge the cell suspensions at approximately 200 × g 
for 10 minutes, discard the supernatant, and suspend 
the pellet in 1 mL of 5% TCA. Transfer this suspension 
to a scintillation vial. Rinse the culture tubes with an 
additional 1 mL of TCA, and add this to the scintillation 
vial. Mix the contents of the vial thoroughly.
	
8.	Count the samples in a scintillation counter for 5–10 
minutes, and record the counts as disintegrations per 
minute (DPMs).
	
9.	Using the results obtained with the vehicle controls 
as baseline, calculate the SI (i.e., mean experimental 
results divided by mean control results). Test com-
pounds that induce an SI of 3 or greater at any con-
centration evaluated in this assay, and for which test 
DPM are statistically different from control DPM, 
are considered to be contact sensitizers.

39.4.2.5.2  Selected In Vitro Approaches to 
Classification of Chemical Sensitizers
The techniques detailed in this section are limited to the 
DPRA, KeratinoSens™, and h-CLAT assays as they are fully 
validated with OECD and are mostly used in the application 
of DA to classification of chemical sensitizers. Other assays 
are available and validated for the key events in the AOP as 
discussed above and the reader is referred to the references 
provided for additional details on those techniques.
39.4.2.5.2.1  Direct Peptide Reactivity Assay (DPRA)  The 
DPRA, as its name implies, is an in chemico method to 
determine the potential for reactive chemicals to bind to 
synthetic peptides as surrogates for endogenous proteins 
(key event 1) [213]. Test chemicals are evaluated for reactiv-
ity with synthetic peptides containing terminal cysteine or 
lysine residues. High-performance liquid chromatography 
(HPLC) analysis of peptide concentrations in the reaction 
solution are used to determine the percent peptide depletion 
caused by the chemical(s) being studied, thereby indicating 
the degree of peptide reactivity. DPRA testing is performed 
in accordance with OECD test guideline 442C: In Chemico 
Skin Sensitization [214]. Briefly, solubility of each chemical 
is determined prior to mixing with the peptides. Once the 
appropriate solvent is selected, the chemical is mixed with a 
cysteine-containing peptide and a lysine-containing peptide in 
two separate experiments and incubated for 24 hours to assess 
chemical reactivity. Binding of the chemical with the peptide 
can result in conformational alteration and/or precipitation 
of the peptide thereby reducing the peptide concentration in 
the reaction mixture. This depletion of peptide is measured 
by HPLC analysis and reported as percentage of cysteine and/
or lysine peptide by the chemical being tested. The percent 
depletion of the peptide peaks is subsequently used to clas-
sify each chemical as a non-sensitizer (0–6.38% depletion), 
or as a sensitizer with low (6.38–22.62% depletion), moder-
ate (22.62–42.27% depletion), or high (>42.47% depletion) 
reactivity. Cinnamic aldehyde, a strong sensitizer, is used as 
a positive control to gauge assay performance. Acceptance 
criteria for assay controls and test chemicals are described 
in the OECD 442C guideline. Detailed methodology for the 
DPRA that meets regulatory requirements can be found in the 
EURL ECVAM Database on Alternative Methods to Animal 
Experimentation (DB-ALM) protocol 154, version 31 January 
2019 [215].
The DPRA is well suited for determination of reactivity for 
direct electrophiles, however, since it is an in chemico method 
lacking metabolic capacity, it does not perform for chemi-
cals requiring metabolism such as pre- and prohaptens. A 
modification of the DPRA known as the Peroxidase Peptide 
Reactivity Assay (PPRA) incorporates horseradish peroxidase 
as a metabolic component to the reaction mixture and shows 
promise for detecting prohaptens [216].
39.4.2.5.2.2  KeratinoSens™ Assay  The KeratinoSens™ 
assay [217] evaluates keratinocyte activation (key event 2) via 
the Keap1-Nrf2-ARE-dependent pathway using the immortal-
ized, human-derived keratinocyte cell line KeratinoSens™ 
as described in OECD test guideline 442D: In Vitro Skin 
Sensitization Assays Addressing The AOP Key Event of 
Keratinocyte Activation [218]. The KeratinoSens™ cells used 
in this assay have been transfected with a plasmid containing 
the luciferase gene whose expression is under control of the 
AKR1C2 gene ARE sequence upstream of the SV40 promoter. 
Increased Keap1-Nrf2-ARE-dependent luciferase expression 
is indicative of keratinocyte activation and is used to classify 
test chemicals as non-sensitizers or sensitizers.
Briefly, chemical solubility is assessed in the assay solvents 
and the appropriate solvent is selected for definitive testing. The 
KeratinoSens™ cells are treated for 48 hours with each chemical 
at a range of concentrations starting at 2 mM final in-well con-
centration. Following chemical treatment, a luciferase substrate 
is added to the cells and the liberation of light is determined 
using a luminometer. The luminescence signal is proportional 
to activation of the Keap1-Nrf2-ARE-dependent pathway and is 
indicative of keratinocyte activation. To ensure that cytotoxicity 
is not a confounder in the interpretation of the results, cell viabil-
ity is measured in parallel using the MTT (3-[4,5-dimethylthi-
azol-2-yl]-2,5-diphenyltetrazolium bromide) assay. Cinnamic 
aldehyde, a strong sensitizer, is used as a positive control to 
gauge assay performance. Acceptance criteria for assay controls 
and test chemicals are described in the OECD 442D guideline. 
Detailed methodology can be found in the DB-ALM Protocol 
155: KeratinoSens, version March 09, 2018 [219].
39.4.2.5.2.3  h-CLAT Assay  The h-CLAT assay [220, 221] 
measures DC activation (key event 3) in response to chemical 
exposure using the immortalized human monocytic leukemia 
cell line, THP-1, as a DC surrogate, as described in OECD test 
guideline 442E: In Vitro Skin Sensitization Assays Addressing 
the Key Event on Activation of Dendritic Cells on the Adverse 
Outcome Pathway for Skin Sensitization [222].
Briefly, chemical solubility testing is performed to deter-
mine the appropriate solvent. THP-1 cells are treated with test 
chemicals at a range of concentrations for 24 hours. The highest 
concentration tested is determined from a dose-finding assays

Immunotoxicology
which optimizes the cytotoxicity of the chemical at 75% via-
bility (CV75). The CV75 concentration is used to calculate the 
CV75*1.2 starting concentration for the concentration range to 
be tested for each chemical. DC activation is determined by

39.4.2.6  Techniques for Assessing Autoimmunity
Several experimental animal and in vitro models are available 
to study the mechanisms of autoimmune disease (Table 39.9). 
However, immunotoxicology screening studies to identify 
autoimmune-inducing agents have historically used auto-
immune-prone rodents and monitored for the accelerated 
development of autoimmune disease as indicated by elevated 
levels of serum autoantibodies and/or Ig levels as well as his-
topathological changes in the target organ(s) or changes in 
serum chemistries. While a number of syndromes like those 
observed clinically in humans can be mimicked in animal 
models, the diversity of immune mechanisms and pathogen-
esis of autoimmune diseases limits the utility of any single 
model as a screening tool and validated tests do not exist. The 
primary reason for the lack of validated assays probably stems 
from the complexity of the disease. Autoimmune disease is not 
one disease but a group of over 25 diseases affecting distinct 
organs, often through different mechanisms. Unless a com-
mon early process is identified, a single test would be unlikely 
to provide an adequate degree of concordance to be useful for 
predictive risk assessment. Secondly, in humans, intrinsic fac-
tors (e.g., specific gene polymorphisms, sex-related hormones, 
and age) and extrinsic factors (e.g., lifestyle, infectious agents) 
are sometimes strongly associated with the induction, develop-
ment, and exacerbation of autoimmunity. Lastly, when using 
animal models, there is some uncertainty regarding what 
actually constitutes autoimmunity. This is also reflected by a 
lack of well-defined diagnostic tests for identifying autoim-
mune disease in humans. Despite these challenges, attempts 
to develop predictive screening assays for detecting xenobi-
otic-induced autoimmunity have been undertaken in several 
laboratories. While screening approaches have most often 
used spontaneous autoimmune-prone rodents [224], other 
approaches have included the following: identifying immuno-
globulin complexes or immunoglobulin deposits using immu-
nohistological procedures, monitoring for increased levels of 
serum autoantibodies, and the use of the popliteal lymph node 
assay (PLNA) with reporter antigens [225, 172].
The successful use of exacerbating disease by chemical 
exposure in autoimmune-prone rodent species has been illus-
trated by administering streptozotocin in diabetic mice [226, 
227]. The pristane-induced lupus murine model has been 
developed in BALB/c and C57BL/6 mice that do not develop 
spontaneous autoimmunity, therefore providing opportunities 
to investigate induction of disease rather than simply exacerba-
tion [228]. This model is useful in understanding the multiple 
mechanisms involved in systemic autoimmunity, including, 
but not limited to, the roles of inflammation and cytokine dys-
regulation, antibody production/specificity, as well as the path-
ological outcomes (e.g. joint involvement and arthritis) [229]. 
Less studied has been the monitoring of Ig deposits or autoan-
tibody production. In the PLNA model, the autoimmunoge-
nicity of a chemical, believed to mimic the GVH reaction, is 
determined by its ability to stimulate specific IgG responses to 
trinitrophenyl (TNP)–Ficoll and TNP–ovalbumin in the PLN. 
It is independent of the nature of the neoantigens and elimi-
nates many of the potential genetic confounders. In this assay, 
the test compound is coinjected with 10 ōg TNP–Ficoll or 10 
ōg TNP–ovalbumin subcutaneously into the right hind paw of 
BALB/c mice. The amount of test substance injected can be 
equimolar to a related compound or, if known, a concentra-
tion demonstrated to be stimulatory in the PLNA. Seven days 
following treatments, the thickness of the paw is measured 
using a micrometer and the draining PLN is isolated. Specific 
AFCs from the PLN are quantitated by any one of several 
methods such as ELISPOT. For memory responses, mice are 
similarly treated and then challenged in the right paw with 10 
ōg antigen, 4–5 weeks following the primary immunization, 
and antibody-producing cells from the PLN determined 6 days 
later. Serum samples can also be collected weekly following 
the primary immunization and serum antibodies determined 
using commercial procedures.

led to the development of a novel model of SLE [230]. This

39.5.1  Fundamental Concepts
Although infectious disease is the most obvious adverse con-
sequence of immunotoxicity (immunosuppression), the etiol-
ogy, progression, and/or severity of a much broader range of 
disorders, including certain cancers, allergy, and autoimmune 
disease, can result. Establishing the quantitative relationship 
between altered immune responses and frequency or severity 
of diseases in human populations is challenging, as many fac-
tors may contribute [233]. This can be summarized schemati-
cally in Figure 39.9, where the appearance, progression, and 
outcome of infectious disease are viewed as an interrelationship 
between the virulence of the organism, infectious dose (number 
of organisms required to produce illness), the integrity of the 
host’s anatomical and functional barriers, and the overall immu-
nocompetence of an individual. The latter, in turn, is affected by 
genetics as well as age, gender, use of certain medications, drug/
alcohol use, smoking history, stress, and nutritional status.
Infectious agents associated with immunodeficiency disor-
ders include community-acquired, opportunistic, and latent 
pathogens. Community-acquired pathogens, such as influenza 
virus, occur in the general population at frequencies associ-
ated with their infectious nature. The respiratory system is the 
most vulnerable target for common pathogens. Upper respira-
tory infections occur in all age groups but, due to their lack 
of immunocompetence, can be more severe in the very young 
and very old [234, 235]. While infections with common patho-
gens occur in the healthy population, opportunistic infections, 
such as Candida albicans, Mycobacterium avium complex, 
and Pneumocystis carinii, are most often seen in patients with 
profound forms of immunosuppression such as HIV/AIDS or 
primary immunodeficiency diseases. A third group of infec-
tions that are observed in immunosuppressed individuals is 
reactivation of latent infections. The most common agents 
are from the herpes virus family, which include cytomegalo-
virus, herpes simplex virus, and Epstein–Barr virus (EBV). 
Polyomaviruses, including John Cunningham (JC) virus, are 
another family of viruses where immune suppression can 
cause reactivation [236]. Infections generally occur early in 
life and these viruses remain in the tissue in a latent form 
for the duration of the host’s life. In healthy individuals, the 
immune system usually maintains viral latency. When the cel-
lular immune response is compromised, viral replication can 
ensue and clinical consequences ensue such as the appearance 
of cold sores although in some instances, severe complications 
can ensue. In some cases, modern immunomodulatory thera-
pies have resulted in reactivation of latent viral infection with 
severe outcomes, as was the case reactivation of JC virus and 
resulting progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy in some 
multiple sclerosis patients receiving natalizumab [237].
Immunodeficiency is also associated with an increased inci-
dence of certain virally induced tumors, such as non-Hodgkin’s 
lymphomas and tumors of the skin [238]. In contrast to cancers 
of internal organs, in particular those in the lung and liver that

39.5.2  General Testing Considerations  
and Approach
The design of human studies can range from controlled clinical 
trials to large, population-based observational studies. Clinical 
studies offer advantages in that exposure parameters of interest 
can often be controlled (e.g., clinical drug trials, chamber studies 
of inhaled toxicants, challenge studies of adenovirus infection) 
and outcomes can be prospectively monitored. With respect to 
drugs, the potential of a pharmaceutical to adversely affect the 
immune system can be difficult to determine in clinical trials due 
to limited endpoints. For suspected toxic chemicals, there are 
also disadvantages as ethical considerations provide little oppor-
tunity for exposure. Furthermore, studies with extensive biologi-
cal monitoring and functional immune tests can be expensive, 
and exposures as well as outcomes of interest may be difficult 
to study in the available time frame as study participants are not 
typically available for long-term exposures or extended follow-
up. Other types of human studies that have been employed in 
immunotoxicology are typically classified as observational or 
epidemiological. Observational studies can be of varying size 
and be cross-sectional (one point in time), retrospective, or pro-
spective in nature, each design having advantages and disadvan-
tages. The initial means of control in observational studies are 
introduced through the study design, and the quality and validity 
of results can be greatly affected by the methods used to select the 
study sample and the rigor with which exposures and outcomes 
are measured. In addition to high costs, observational studies are 
challenging for many reasons, including potential confounding 
by host (age, gender, and lifestyle) and environmental (frequency 
of exposure to chemicals and infectious agents) factors. A sec-
ondary measure of control in observational studies is through the 
use of multivariable analysis techniques (e.g., regression model-
ing), provided there is sufficient sample size and information on 
potential confounders. Overall, well-designed epidemiological 
studies (e.g., absence of selection bias, exposure or outcome mis-
classification, and control of confounding) can contribute valu-
able information to the assessment of risk due to immunotoxic 
exposures. Because of concern about individual variation, a con-
firmatory evaluation and a cross-sectional or longitudinal study 
design can be employed using randomized normal, nonexposed 
individuals. It is also imperative to obtain a careful medical his-
tory that covers the clinical features of immune dysfunction and 
accurate exposure assessments.
Initial screening tests include measurements of general 
parameters, such as CBCs, serum immunoglobulin levels, chest 
radiograph of the thymus (for adolescents), and delayed hypersen-
sitivity tests. In immunotoxicology, this screening paradigm may 
be appropriate for clinical studies or in individuals where signifi-
cant immunosuppression is suspected. However, this approach is 
unlikely to detect subtle immune changes. A systematic approach 
based on simple screening procedures followed by more special-
ized tests of immune function should provide the best overall 
assessment. This approach should include the functional evalua-
tion of cellular immunity (T cell), an antibody response (B cell), 
and nonspecific resistance (e.g., PMN function). It has also been 
suggested that immunization through vaccination, in order to 
elicit a primary immune response, may be an important crite-
rion to establish immunotoxicity [239]. Most investigations have 
traditionally included one or more of the following: CBCs and 
differential, immunophenotypic analyses, quantification of serum 
immunoglobulin levels, and subject’s recall of infectious disease. 
Other available tests or biomarkers may also be included based on 
information from preclinical studies or class of compounds, such 
evaluation of complement split products with phosphorothioate-
modified oligonucleotide therapies [240].

39.5.3.2  Immunoglobulin Concentrations
Serum concentration of the major immunoglobulin classes IgG, 
IgM, and IgA can be readily measured in epidemiological stud-
ies. There are several standardized laboratory methods and 
reagents available for measuring these major classes of immu-
noglobulin. These methods include single-radial diffusion, 
double diffusion in agar gel, immunoelectrodiffusion, radioim-
munoassay, enzyme-linked immunoassay, and automated laser 
nephelometry. Serum Ig concentrations vary with age, ethnicity, 
geographical location, gender, and environment. Thus, appropri-
ate norms must be used with any type of population assessment. 
Patients with primary immunodeficiency disease can manifest 
a profound decrease in all Ig classes or only in a single class 
or subclass and are associated with increases in infectious dis-
ease frequency. The adverse health effects associated with small 
changes in immunoglobulin levels are unclear.
39.5.3.2.1  Delayed-Type Skin Testing
Skin testing is a commonly used procedure to assess cellu-
lar immune competence since delayed cutaneous hypersensi-
tivity, a localized immunological skin response, depends on 
functional T cells and the production of inflammatory cyto-
kines. Antigens commonly employed to elicit a positive skin 
response include purified protein derivative, mumps, tricho-
phyton, candida, tetanus, or diphtheria. These antigens usually 
are employed in a panel and are administered by intradermal 
injection at the appropriate dilution. Skin responses are read at 
48 and 72 hours for maximal diameter of erythema and indu-
ration. The test is not considered very sensitive unless severe 
immunosuppression is suspected, which is unlikely to occur.
39.5.3.3  Specific Antibody Assessment
Efforts have also been made to quantify the response to vac-
cines, either by measurement of antibody titers (HI) or lym-
phocyte proliferation tests (CMI) to specific vaccine epitopes 
following vaccination as an indicator of immune function. 
While the short-term stress response may be beneficial chronic 
stress can adversely affect immunity [241], and a number of 
investigations have examined associations between chronic 
psychological stress and immune function in adults using 
hepatitis B, influenza virus, influenza, or pneumococcal vac-
cine responses [242, 243]. Sleijffers et al. [244] demonstrated 
a decreased response to hepatitis vaccination in a subgroup 
of students exposed to ultraviolet radiation in association with 
polymorphisms in genes that control inflammatory cytokines. 
A comprehensive review on the use of vaccination in immu-
notoxicity testing was published by van Louveren et al. [245].
39.5.3.4  Phenotypic Analysis by Flow Cytometry
Enumerating cell surface markers (CD) on lymphoid cells by 
flow cytometry has provided considerable information on the 
ontogeny and activation state of the human immune system in 
children and adults, as well as assisting in the clinical diag-
nosis for immunological and hematopoietic disorders [246]. 
Specific CD markers have been identified for almost all lym-
phoid cell populations and subpopulations, as well as for spe-
cific stages of cell differentiation and activation. In contrast 
to adults, children in the first few years of life have a much 
larger number of total lymphocytes, and both the percentage 
and numbers of leukocyte populations can vary significantly 
during critical periods of development.
Immunophenotyping is conducted using flow cytometers 
that have multiple photomultiplier tubes (four or more) and 
can measure multicolor fluorescence, 90° light scatter, and 
forward light scatter. When highly specific fluorochrome-
labeled monoclonal antibodies are used in these instruments, 
very quantitative measurements can be made of B and T cell 
subsets. The most commonly used procedure for processing 
peripheral blood samples for immunofluorescence is first to 
stain an aliquot of whole blood with fluorescent-conjugated 
monoclonal antibodies and then to lyse the erythrocytes. The 
proportion of circulating T cells is then determined by immu-
nofluorescence with Fluor-labeled CD2 or CD3 monoclonal 
antibodies in a flow cytometer. Normally, T cells constitute 
55–80% of peripheral blood lymphocytes. Normal values 
reported for absolute numbers of circulating T cells are 590–
3,090/mm3 for individuals greater than 18 months of age [247]. 
If an immune defect is suspected, the ratio of CD4 to CD8 
T cells can also be beneficial. This method is quite quantita-
tive, but the ability of this test method to detect subtle immune 
changes in populations of individuals was challenged [246]. 
However, flow cytometry is a key component of mechanistic 
studies, and with continued advances in the field and instru-
mentation, quantitative flow cytometry is also becoming an 
increasingly critical component of the clinical diagnostic tool-
box [248]. It should be noted that an immunotoxic effect may 
not always result in significant changes in lymphocyte subpop-
ulations nor reflected directly in functional effects. However, 
in situations where relevant effects are observed, immunophe-
notyping can provide a useful bridge between nonclinical and 
clinical studies.

39.5.3.5.2  Autoantibodies
It is often stated that the immune system is established on a 
principle of self-/nonself-recognition. In some cases, tolerance 
to self-antigens breaks down and autoantibodies are produced, 
which can be manifested as autoimmune disease. Antibodies 
to cellular components and nuclear antigens (ANA, DNA, 
mitochondria) and to rheumatoid factor (RA) and their fre-
quency in a population may reflect an immune alteration. 
Standardized diagnostic kits are available to detect the pres-
ence of these autoantibodies in sera. Finally, it is a good 
practice to establish a freezer bank of an aliquot of each test 
subject’s sera for later evaluation when new research questions 
or test methods are developed.
39.5.3.5.3  Acute-Phase Proteins and Complement
Acute-phase proteins were identified in the 1900s and are now 
recognized as an integral part of the acute-phase response 
and innate immunity. Acute-phase proteins can increase with 
infection, inflammation, and neoplasia and can serve as valu-
able biomarkers [255]. C-reactive protein (CRP) is an acute-
phase protein that can increase up to 1,000-fold as a result of 
infection or inflammation. CRP has been used as marker of 
inflammation but has also been shown to be involved in the 
inflammatory process itself [256]. Acute-phase reactant serum 
amyloid A (A-SAA) is another acute-phase protein with an 
important role in acute and chronic inflammation. CRP and 
A-SAA as well as other acute-phase proteins (e.g., procalcito-
nin) are used in clinical laboratories as markers of inflamma-
tion [257].
Complement can also be readily evaluated in the clinic 
on serum or plasma samples collected and stored at ′ 70°C. 
Proper handling of complement samples post-collection is 
very important since prolonged transport time at room tem-
perature or improper handling of the samples (i.e., excessive 
freeze-thaw cycles) can allow tick-over or result in activa-
tion and thereby affect the interpretation of these assays. 
Functional complement activity has been traditionally 
evaluated using a hemolytic assay, where serial dilutions 
of the sample are prepared and incubated in the presence 
of antibody-sensitized SRBCs. The complement activity 
is measured as the reciprocal of the dilution at which 50% 
hemolysis is observed (CH50). Quantitation of classical 
pathway activation can also be performed by evaluation of 
individual complement components or the combined com-
plement attack complex by nephelometric, turbidimetric, 
or ELISA, which all have excellent concordance with the 
hemolytic assay [258].
39.6  Risk Assessment and Data Interpretation
Immunotoxicology data used in risk assessment are derived 
primarily from animal toxicology studies. When adequate data 
are available, epidemiological or controlled clinical exposure 
studies take precedence. The results obtained from in vitro 
studies, quantitative structure–activity relationships (QSARs), 
or mechanistic investigations are used normally as supportive 
information. Mechanistic studies, however, are important, as 
they help determine plausibility and human relevance [259]. 
Interpretation of immunotoxicology data is dependent upon 
the immunopathology studied, that is, suppression/stimula-
tion, hypersensitivity, or autoimmunity.
39.6.1  Immunosuppression
Animal studies for testing immunosuppression or stimulation 
are used primarily for semiquantitative hazard identification. 
Since tests for immunosuppression use multiple and, to some 
extent, overlapping endpoints, the data can also be evaluated 
in terms of establishing an immune profile. This may help 
to identify the mode of action, such as direct cytotoxicity, 
hematopoietic toxicity, altered immune regulation, or altered 
regulatory circuits. At the least, the test panel can determine 
which arm of the immune system is the primary target. Hazard 
identification for immunosuppression can also be described as 
a weight-of-evidence approach as recommended by the guid-
ance documents prepared by the International Programme on 
Chemical Safety (IPCS)/World Health Organization (WHO) 
[259]. This approach suggests addressing specific questions 
in which weights are given for various endpoints including in 
vitro studies, QSAR, human studies, and other animal studies. 
The strength of association will also consider dose–response 
relationships, consistency of association, temporal association, 
biological plausibility, specificity, coherence, and analogy.
39.6.2  Hypersensitivity/Allergy
In cases where ethical considerations prevent the use of 
human patch testing to establish the potential of agents to 
induce allergic contact dermatitis, animal models, particularly 
the Buehler-occluded and Magnuson–Kligman maximiza-
tion tests in guinea pigs, have been used as predictive tests. 
However, it is expensive to purchase and maintain guinea pigs, 
there are few inbred strains, immunological reagents are not 
widely available, and there is some suggestion, although never 
fully substantiated that these models are overly sensitive when 
compared to the experience in humans.
The LLNA has been discussed earlier (see Section 4.2.5.1.3), 
and this assay has undergone a series of examinations to pro-
vide technical refinement and assess inter- and intralabora-
tory reproducibility [260–262]. LLNA data are used primarily 
for hazard identification. Since multiple doses are evaluated, 
the results can be interpreted in a semiquantitative manner, 
or at least in terms of relative potency, based upon the low-
est concentration that elicits a response above an EC3. Two-
category systems have been introduced for the classification 
and labeling of sensitizers under the Globally Harmonized

System of Classification and Labeling of Chemicals (high 
or low potency), which correlates well with humans. LLNA 
results should also be evaluated broadly in terms of a weight-
of-evidence approach as recommended by IPCS/WHO [259], 
using all available data, including QSAR, additional animal 
studies, and human data. It should be noted that the OECD 
has published a new guideline on DA for Skin Sensitization 
(OECD GL No. 497). This OECD Guideline issued in June of 
2021 uses several types of combined data to obtain chemical 
safety information, and to obviate the need for animal test-
ing. The DA included in this guideline use combinations of 
OECD validated in chemico, in vitro, and in silico data and 
have been shown to provide the same level of information or 
be more informative than the LLNA for hazard identification 
of a sensitizer. Two of the DA are also able to provide informa-
tion for categorization of sensitization potency equivalent to 
the potency information provided by the LLNA.
In contrast to predictive tests for allergic contact hypersen-
sitivity, the identification of proteins and chemicals capable of 
inducing respiratory hypersensitivity is still limited [263, 264]. 
As these tests are difficult to undertake, often involving respi-
ratory exposure and lung function tests, efforts to develop and 
validate new methods are limited. Although the guinea pig has 
significant immunological differences compared to humans 
(e.g., IgG1 vs. IgE reaginic antibodies), it appears to be a pre-
dictive model for humans given the limited comparative data 
available and has been used to test for high- and low-molecular-
weight sensitizers [265]. A modification of the LLNA has been 
used in mice to investigate respiratory sensitizers with agents 
being introduced into the airways by inhalation techniques or 
by intranasal installation. Cytokine profiling of the draining 
mandibular lymph nodes has been used to predict respiratory 
sensitization in this model [266]. While some new methods 
such as the use of the GARD assays for respiratory sensitizers 
show promise [267], validated assays are still needed.
39.6.3  Autoimmunity
General agreement exists among the regulatory and pharma-
ceutical communities that predictive tests for autoimmunity or 
systemic allergy are in most need of development in order to 
improve risk assessment in immunotoxicology [268]. While 
many models exist to study autoimmune processes, they do not 
readily lend themselves to use in risk assessment, as they do not 
consider the multifactorial nature of the disease. To improve the 
risk assessment process, screening models need to be developed 
and validated that not only incorporate mechanistic information 
into the assessment process but also allow for the consideration 
of the genetic, physiological, and environmental influences that 
lead to the loss of self-tolerance, autoimmune disease, or systemic 
allergy. Despite the challenges in developing such screening tests, 
the considerable amount of data generated by immunologists and 
pharmacologists pertaining to basic mechanisms of chemical-
induced autoimmune diseases has provided a conceptual frame-
work, which allows the establishment of potential QSARs. These 
QSARs are by no means definitive, and as the database increases, 
no doubt some will not be supported while others will be added. 
In all cases, a basic understanding of immunological and phar-
macological processes supports these relationships. For example, 
estrogens are known to be a major factor in classical autoimmune 
diseases presumably due to their ability to stimulate certain com-
ponents of the immune system [269], and as such, agents with 
estrogenic activity may be of concern.
Laboratory studies have also shown that thymolytic chemi-
cals, such as cyclophosphamide and cyclosporin A, can induce 
autoimmunity when given neonatally by altering normal pat-
terns of autoreactive T cell deletion [270]. In this respect, the 
thymus has been shown to be a target for many toxic chemicals. 
As in the case of halothane, chemicals that form protein adducts 
or damage tissue in such a way to allow expression of cryptic 
determinants would provide novel host antigens that could now 
be recognized by T cells. Agents that have adjuvant activity, 
or biologicals that stimulate certain cytokines, may deregulate 
immune activation, contribute to the initiation and/or amplifica-
tion of immunopathologies, or allow exacerbation of preexisting 
autoimmune disease [271–273]. Common features associated 
with many drugs that induce autoimmune diseases are that they 
serve as MPO substrates and/or cause changes in methylation 
[274]. The explanation for the latter association is less clear but 
may require identification of the specific antigenic epitopes 
responsible for the autoimmune response. In the case of the asso-
ciation with MPO substrates, it has been suggested that many of 
the chemicals require metabolism in proximity to immune cells 
in order to be antigenic and immune cells such as monocytes 
contain high levels of MPO. More recently, there has also been 
cumulating evidence that NETs play an important role in initia-
tion and resolution of inflammation and in autoimmunity [275].
In the interpretation of autoimmunity studies, any effect 
and/or dose–response relationship should be evaluated rela-
tive to generalized overt toxicity as well as for consistency, 
specificity, and coherence. The data should also be interpreted 
in context of all the tests that were examined for autoimmune 
potential whether supportive or not, as recommended in IPCS/
WHO [259]. Again, this implies a weight of evidence using all 
available data, including QSAR, in vitro, human, and labora-
tory animal data that are relevant to either induction or exacer-
bation of autoimmunity whether supportive or not.

Assessment of Immunotoxicity
39.7.1  Overview
Some of the earliest codified immunotoxicology test guide-
lines were developed to augment toxicological assessment of 
pesticides. In 1996, the Office of Prevention, Pesticides, and 
Toxic Substances (OPPTS) of the US Environmental Protection 
Agency (EPA) published guidelines entitled Biochemicals Test 
Guidelines: OPPTS 880.3550 Immunotoxicity [276], which 
described the preferred study design for evaluating potential 
immunotoxicity in biochemical pesticides. The panel of tests 
included in this guideline includes standard toxicology tests as 
well as immune function tests. A second document published 
concurrently (Biochemicals Test Guidelines: OPPTS 880.3800 
Immune Response) [277] provided rationale for testing, detailed 
explanations for testing strategies, and additional mechanistic 
tests including host resistance and bone marrow function.
Whereas immunotoxicity evaluation encompassed by the 
880 series of guidelines would arguably detect most types of

immunotoxicity, its breadth would probably render it tremen-
dously expensive and time-consuming. In 1998, EPA followed 
up with the Health Effects Test Guidelines: OPPTS 870.7800 
Immunotoxicity [278], which described immunotoxicology 
testing for nonbiochemical agents that would be regulated by 
EPA. This document provides descriptions of both why and 
how, with a much more abbreviated panel of testing to be per-
formed. Whereas the 880 series of immunotoxicology guide-
lines are excessive, the testing approach mandated by 870.7800 
has stood up well in intervening years and reflects the more lim-
ited, case-by-case approach currently favored. Most notably, the 
functional assessment is pared down to T-dependent antibody 
formation, NK cell function and quantitation of T and B cells.
In Europe, the OECD regulates testing of chemicals for toxic-
ity. OECD Guideline 407 entitled Repeated Dose 28-day Oral 
Toxicity Study I Rodents, while not specific for immunotoxi-
cology, includes a variety of toxicological endpoints that can 
provide early evidence of immune system alterations. Missing, 
however, are any functional assays to directly measure any 
immune deficit. Other OECD Guidelines specifically address 
immunotoxicity testing as part of the evaluations (Extended 
One-Generation Reproductive Toxicity Study, OECD TG No. 
443) or specific immunotoxicity endpoints (e.g., guideline on 
DA) for Skin Sensitization (OECD GL No. 497).
39.7.2  Pharmaceuticals
In the United States, safety testing of small-molecule phar-
maceuticals is the purview of the US FDA’s Center for Drug 
Evaluation and Research (FDA CDER). In October of 2002, 
CDER released the Guidance for Industry: Immunotoxicology 
Evaluation of Investigational New Drugs [279]. This docu-
ment is arguably the most comprehensive of any published 
guidance, describing a diversity of adverse events, including 
immunosuppression, immunogenicity, hypersensitivity, auto-
immunity, and adverse immunostimulation. The document 
describes each of these types of immunotoxicity (more accu-
rately, immunomodulation) in detail and not only provides 
approaches but also suggests methodology for evaluating each 
type. The FDA/CDER guidance advocates the use of infor-
mation derived from standard repeat-dose toxicity studies to 
provide early evidence of immunotoxicity, with subsequent 
evaluations to be rationally designed to use a minimum of ani-
mals and resources while deriving the maximum amount of 
information. Subsequent to the publication of the FDA/CDER 
document, the primary author of the guidance published a 
manuscript describing the implications of the guidance [280].
In Europe, safety testing of pharmaceuticals is regulated 
by the Committee for Medicinal Products for Human Use 
(CHMP). Prior to 2004 the CHMP was named Committee 
for Proprietary Medicinal Products (CPMP). In October of 
2000, CPMP published Note for Guidance on Repeated Dose 
Toxicity (CPMP/SWP/1042/99) [281]; although the primary 
purpose of this document was to describe an overall approach 
to safety testing of pharmaceuticals, it was important as the 
first guidance document mandating specific immunotoxicol-
ogy screening for pharmaceuticals.
As the first published document requiring immunotoxicol-
ogy evaluation, CPMP/SWP/1042/99 was predictably met with 
a combination of resistance and confusion. Much of this was 
allayed in a Drug Information Association-sponsored workshop 
held in Noordwijk, the Netherlands in November of 2001 [282]. 
Differences in how the US and EU guidance to immunotoxicol-
ogy assessment is interpreted led to intense discussion regarding 
the best approach for performing this assessment on a routine 
basis. Following the International Conference on Harmonization 
(ICH) process, at the ICH Steering Committee meeting on 
September 15, 2005, S8 ICH Guideline was recommended for 
adoption to the three regulatory bodies. This document was 
finalized in 2006 by FDA and is entitled Guidance for Industry: 
S8 Immunotoxicity Studies for Human Pharmaceuticals [283].
39.7.3  Biologicals
Biologicals (therapeutics derived by biotechnology) present a 
unique challenge for immunotoxicity assessment. Some of these 
agents, such as cytokines and other immunomodulatory mol-
ecules, are intended to therapeutically modulate the immune 
response; therefore, it can be difficult to differentiate between 
the agent’s efficacy and a truly adverse reaction. Second, because 
many of these agents are proteins or peptides, their introduction 
into a host often triggers an immune response directed against the 
molecule itself; this can lead to alterations in pharmacodynam-
ics or to other adverse reactions. Thus, development of appropri-
ate guidance on testing these agents is problematic. One approach 
is promulgated by the ICH, with the document Preclinical Safety 
Evaluation of Biotechnology-Derived Pharmaceuticals S6 [284]. 
This document includes sections on immunogenicity as well as a 
brief mention of immunotoxicity studies. The S6 document recog-
nizes the inappropriateness of a structured tier approach, opting 
instead for careful design of screening studies, followed by mecha-
nistic studies to clarify any potential evidence of immunotoxicity. 
Specific techniques and approaches are not described in ICH S6.
Safety evaluation of biological drugs is regulated in the United 
States by the FDA’s Center for Biologics Evaluation and Research 
(CBER). CBER has not promulgated any written guidance on 
immunotoxicology; the reason for this lack of written guidance 
is the extreme diversity of biological therapeutics, which makes 
it difficult to design a standardized testing approach. Rather, 
CBER’s approach to addressing potential immunotoxicity has 
been case by case and followed suggestions provided in the cur-
rent version of ICH S6 (R1) [285]. In February 2020, a draft 
guidance for Nonclinical Safety Evaluation of the Immunotoxic 
Potential of Drugs and Biologics was issued jointly by CDER 
and CBER for public comments. When finalized, this guidance 
will represent FDA’s current opinions on this topic.
39.7.4  Vaccines
Along with certain biologicals, vaccines present a challenge 
for immunotoxicological evaluation since they are specifically 
designed to induce an immune response, a situation deemed 
undesirable (or potentially so) for most of the other agents 
described in this review. Since methodology is well established 
to evaluate the desirable immunomodulation produced by vac-
cine, the concern of regulatory agencies is the potential of these 
agents to produce an undesired or deleterious effect on the 
immune system.

in Note for Guidance on Preclinical Pharmacological and

FDA/CBER is tasked with regulating vaccines in the 
United States. One of the primary documents describing vac-

Combination Vaccines for Preventable Diseases: Production, 
Testing and Clinical Studies [287]. Animal immunogenicity 
is covered in detail in the document, although immunotoxic-
ity is not specified as an area of concern. On the other hand, 
CBER’s Considerations for Reproductive Toxicity Studies for 
Preventive Vaccines for Infectious Disease Indications [288], 
although intended primarily to assess effects of vaccination on 
reproductive function (including generalized toxicity such as 
fetal malformations), acknowledges the potential immunologi-
cal reactions resulting from the vaccination process to exert 
unintended consequences. No specific guidance is provided on 
methods or approaches to be used in this evaluation.
39.7.5  Medical Devices and Radiological Agents
It has been recognized by the FDA that immunotoxicity may 
result not only from chemical or biological agents that dynami-
cally interact with humans’ physiology such as small-molecule 
drugs or biological agents but also from medical devices that 
contact the body externally (via skin or mucosa), internally 
(implantable devices), or by external communication to the blood 
or tissue. Thus, the FDA Center for Devices and Radiological 
Health published the guidance entitled Guidance for Industry 
and FDA Reviewers: Immunotoxicology Testing Guidance in 
May 1999 [289] that addresses testing for medical devices. 
This guidance is based on the General Program Memorandum 
G95-1, an FDA-modified version of International Standard 
ISO-10993, Biological Evaluation of Medical Devices Part 
1: Evaluation and Testing. The Immunotoxicology Testing 
Guidance provides detailed guidance for determining when 
immunotoxicity testing should be performed (including a flow-
chart and numerous tables) but does not provide details on 
which methods should be employed or for overall study design. 
Anderson and Langone [290] provided explanatory details on 
the use of this guidance.
39.8  Challenges and Frontiers 
in Immunotoxicology
39.8.1  Biotherapeutics
The biopharmaceutical industry is aware that biotherapeutics 
can be immunogenic, and the resulting production of antidrug 
antibodies can significantly alter their clearance, safety, and 
efficacy [291, 292]. Of particular concern, however, is the 
safety of biotherapeutic monoclonal antibodies, particularly 
those that are immunomodulatory. While generally proven 
to be safe, on occasion, they present exaggerated pharmacol-
ogy that was not predicted based on an understanding of the 
intended function or results from nonclinical studies [293]. 
This first became evident following the well-publicized 
adverse events observed with an immunomodulatory anti-
CD28 superagonist mAb (TGN-1412) during a clinical trial in 
the United Kingdom [294]. Therapeutic antibodies and nano-
therapeutic drugs (discussed in the next section) are widely 
used. These therapeutics are often used for targeted therapy. 
However, despite their targeted specificity, they may still result 
in unintended adverse effects on the immune system, including 
uncontrolled cytokine release as discussed in the Uncontrolled 
Cytokine Release in Response to Xenobiotic Exposure section 
above. Immunotoxicity with these molecules can be mediated 
by immunomodulation (either immunostimulation or immu-
nosuppression). Some of these adverse effects on the immune 
system were reviewed recently [295]. As the immunomodula-
tory biotherapeutics become increasingly complex and include 
a wide array of modalities with regard to their structure and 
function, including bispecific antibodies, CAR-modified T 
cells, bispecific T cell engager (BiTE®) combinations such as 
antibody-drug conjugates, gene and cell therapy modalities, 
and new constructs, the more standard testing paradigms are 
no longer applicable and evaluation of immunotoxicity more 
challenging [296].
The immunogenicity of biotherapeutics in humans is com-
plex. While in silico approaches and evaluation of T cell 
responses may identify the potential for immunogenicity; 
there are currently no truly predictive available animal models 
[297, 298]. The use of humanized mice has shown promise 
and great deal of progress has been made in leveraging infor-
mation obtained in these animal systems [299–302]; however, 
these models also have unique challenges and much remains to 
be done for their widespread acceptance and more routine use.
39.8.2  Nanotechnology
Another potential safety concern is the emergence of successful 
implementation of nanotechnology, the manufacture of materi-
als and devices at the molecular scale. A number of nanome-
dicinal products have been reported to accumulate in organs of 
the immune system and induce immunotoxic effects [303]. Some 
of the immunotoxicity endpoints that have been reported have 
included immunosuppression, complement activation-related 
pseudoallergy as well as activation of the adaptive immune 
response, inflammasome activation, and hypersensitivity reac-
tions [304], and immunotoxicology preclinical testing strategies 
need to be adapted to these materials [305].
39.8.3  Interface between Innate and 
Adaptive Immunity
Although the innate immune system is often described as 
primitive, it is increasingly recognized that the innate and 
adaptive systems represent a continuum of highly inter-
active and often complementary protective mechanisms. 
There are several effectors that form the bridge between 
these systems, including the TLRs [306], ć–č T cells [307], 
NK cells, DCs [308, 309], Tregs [310], and Fas signals [311], 
to name but a few. As a better understanding of these com-
plex regulatory circuits develops, it is becoming apparent 
that this system is responsible for immunomodulation of 
both normal and pathologic processes such as autoimmune 
disease [312–314].

39.8.4  Alternative Methods
Alternative methods to replace the use of animals are a req-
uisite for immunotoxicant screenings in the 21st century and 
the use of laboratory animals for such tests is generally pro-
hibited in Europe. As discussed in Section 4.2, alternative in 
chemico and in vitro methods have been developed, validated, 
and accepted by OECD for hazard identification for dermal 
sensitizers. Alternative methods for assessment of sensitiza-
tion are also being accepted by United States regulatory agen-
cies as part of safety evaluations. The first risk assessment to 
incorporate alternative methods was published by the US EPA 
for selected isothiazolinone pesticides [315]. These alternative 
methods have led to a marked reduction in animal use, espe-
cially for safety assessment in the cosmetics industry.
There is a need to develop additional alternative methods 
for the assessment of immune function and hazard identifica-
tion for immunomodulation, both suppression and activation. 
Several approaches have been developed using human cells 
including the basophil activation test [316] and the cytokine 
release assay [317]. Utilization of human whole blood or iso-
lated PBMC offers several advantages such as further reduc-
tion in the use of animals in research, “normal” complement of 
immune cells, and direct translatability to human health risk 
assessment. A natural extension of the cytokine release assay 
is to investigate immunomodulatory effects of xenobiotics by 
examining the cytokine profile following in vitro treatment. 
Human whole blood can also be used as the effector cell popu-
lation for studying neutrophil and NK cell function [318] for 
assessment of innate immune functions. Further research is 
needed to develop in vitro assays capable of assessing HI and 
CMI for non-animal alternatives to provide a comprehensive 
approach to immunotoxicity hazard identification.
39.8.5  Developmental Immunotoxicology
There is an increasing link between early-life exposure to 
chemicals, developmental immunotoxicity, and the develop-
ment of postnatal immune function. This concept introduces 
the need to identify chemical hazards to the developing 
immune system [319]. There is mounting evidence that per-
turbation of the immune developmental program can lead to 
diseases in other organ systems later in life. The concept of 
fetal origins of adult disease is not novel and was proposed 
in 1990 by David Barker, coined the Barker Hypothesis 
[320]. The premise of this hypothesis was that developmen-
tal issues, particularly poor nutrition, can lead to low birth 
weight and premature birth with causal links to coronary 
heart disease, hypertension, and diabetes. This concept was 
brought closer to developmental immunotoxicology with 
the emergency of the Hygiene Hypothesis first proposed in 
1989 [321]. This hypothesis was in response to rising preva-
lence of allergic and autoimmune diseases in industrialized 
nations and suggests that increasingly stringent hygiene 
practices are changing the way our developing immune 
system interacts with microorganisms in our environment. 
It is well documented that the microbiome plays a critical 
role in normal immune development and dysbiosis has been 
associated with susceptibility to multiple diseases [322]. 
Therefore, it is probable that to some extent, that a reduction 
in microbial exposure during immune development could 
influence the maturation of the immune system. The exact 
impact of microorganisms and xenobiotics on the develop-
ing immune system is highly dependent on timing and has 
led to the concept of windows of susceptibility in immune 
development, as illustrated in Figure 39.10.

Based on experimental animal studies, perturbations of the 
developing immune system may be manifested as a qualitative 
(i.e., affecting the developing immune system without affect-
ing the adult immune system) or a quantitative (i.e., affecting 
the developing immune system at lower doses than in adults) 
difference. A major concern for human health risk assessment 
is the duration of perturbed immune development. When the 
changes are permanent, there is a likelihood of influencing 
toxicity and disease processes in childhood and adulthood. 
Developmental impacts on the immune system could result 
in decreased host resistance (i.e., infections and cancer), 
increased susceptibility to immune activation (i.e., allergy 
and autoimmunity), and/or complications and disease in other 
organ systems (i.e., metabolic and cardiovascular diseases). 
For these reasons, it is essential to incorporate developmental 
immunotoxicity into safety assessment and human health risk 
assessment.
Developmental immunotoxicity testing is usually con-
ducted in rodent species and is driven by toxicity alerts 
from adult studies and exposure concerns during develop-
ment collectively referred to as “cause for concern” [324]. 
Since the immune system develops on a prescribed timeline 
(Figure 39.10), it can be difficult to determine the appropriate 
window of exposure to experimental treatment. One approach 
is to conduct the exposures using a life-course design which 
involves initiation of exposure early in gestation (i.e., gestation 
day 6 to ensure implantation is successful), through lactation 
and continuously into adulthood when the immune assess-
ments are conducted. If immunotoxicity is observed under 
this study design, the results are compared to existing adult 
studies for the chemical, or, if unavailable, an adult study 
is conducted. The adult study comparisons help to interpret 
the results of the developmental study so that changes spe-
cific to the developing immune system can be discriminated. 
Developmental immunotoxicity studies have very high costs 
associated with skill requirements, labor, funding, and ani-
mals, which prohibit the incorporation of testing into screen-
ing safety assessment. As such, relatively few chemicals 
have been tested for developmental toxicity. Developmental 
immunotoxicity testing was recently incorporated as a cohort 
into the OECD test guideline 443: Extended One-Generation 
Reproductive Toxicity Study [325]. However, this strategy 
only addresses chemicals that were nominated for reproduc-
tive/developmental testing, resulting in a potential to miss 
agents that specifically impact the immune system.
An international working group was recently formed to 
address these concerns and investigate opportunities for alter-
native approaches. The first workshop for the International 
Working Group around the Topic of Alternatives to in vivo 
Developmental Immunotoxicity Testing was held as a virtual 
meeting in May 2021 and proceedings are being prepared. It 
was clear from this meeting that development of alternative 
approaches will require a union of the disciplines of develop-
mental immunology with immunotoxicology. Experts in these 
areas need to collaborate for the identification of key events 
in the normal immune development to provide direction for 
design of alternative assays. Non-animal alternative assays 
will be essential for bringing routine screening to the develop-
ing immune system.
39.8.6  Immunotoxicology and the 
Aging Immune System
Several immune changes occur with aging, including a decline 
in various immune parameters referred to as immunosenes-
cence. While older individuals are not considered immunode-
ficient, immunosenescent immune responses are not as robust 
or efficient following exposure to previously encountered or 
novel antigens. Immunosenescence is characterized by an 
increased vulnerability to infection, an increased risk of latent 
virus reactivation, a higher incidence of cancer and autoim-
munity, and reduced protection conferred by vaccines due to 
poorer immune responses resulting in diminished efficacy 
[326, 327]. Increased vulnerability related to immunosenes-
cence is highlighted by the increased susceptibility to influ-
enza virus infection in individuals 70 years of age and older, 
and this susceptibility is compounded by a reduced response 
to vaccination in these individuals [328].
The effects of aging on the immune system are multifaceted 
and include a decreased rate of naïve T and B cell production in 
the thymus and bone marrow, altered composition and dimin-
ished quality of the mature lymphocyte pool, and reduced 
functionality of mature lymphocytes in secondary lymphoid 
tissues [328, 329]. The altered composition of the lymphocyte 
pool includes an imbalance of naïve and memory T cells, as the 
depletion of naïve T cells coupled with the clonal expansion of 
memory T cells results in a low CD4:CD8 T cell ratio. Indeed, 
such increases in relative percentages of CD8 T cells are a 
characteristic of the immune risk phenotype, which is associ-
ated with a higher risk of immune dysfunction and mortality 
[330]. Likewise, B cell production decreases with age, and the 
B cell repertoire is less diverse as class switch recombination 
is impaired with age [329]. Considering the critical role played 
by CD4 T cells in adaptive B cell responses, however, age-
related B cell deficiencies such as decreases in plasmablast 
production and antibody quality following antigenic challenge 
may be secondary to diminished T cell function [331]. These 
and other age-related alterations are influenced by a wide array 
of modifications, including changes in hematopoietic stem 
cells and progenitor cells; modulations in systemic and local 
tissue environments; reductions in cellular repair mechanisms; 
thymic atrophy; as well as inflamm-aging, an increased pro-
inflammatory state and general accumulation of inflammatory 
mediators [328, 332, 333].
Given the decline in overall immune function in the elderly, 
it would not be unreasonable to consider that this portion 
of the population may be more susceptible to immunotoxic 
insult. Older individuals have been shown to have more severe 
adverse effects following chemical exposures and are more 
vulnerable to immediate adverse effects following inhalation 
of fine particles, for example [334, 335]. Few studies, how-
ever, have examined the effect of immunosenescence on the 
exposure to known immunotoxicants with regard to immuno-
toxicology endpoints. The effects of aging on immune system 
function are an intricate and complex phenomenon, and these 
dynamic changes present special challenges for immunotoxic-
ity evaluations as the lack of available data constitute a signifi-
cant gap in the understanding of toxicological assessments in 
aged populations [333, 334].

39.9  Conclusions
The discipline of immunotoxicology has grown in impor-
tance in toxicology since its inception in the mid-1970s. It 
has progressed from the early identification of chemicals 
that may cause immunosuppression/modulation and aller-
gic contact dermatitis, through the validation of sensitive 
and quantitative assays that serve as biomarkers of immune 
system alterations in animals and man. More recently, aca-
demic, industrial, and government scientists have taken a 
more mechanistic approach to define how therapeutic and 
environmental agents alter immune function at the cellular 
and molecular levels and there are ongoing efforts to estab-
lish predictive tests to assess agents that may induce auto-
immune disease and chronic inflammation. Immunotoxicity 
data derived from experimental and human immunosuppres-
sion and hypersensitivity studies play an increasing role in 
establishing health standards and defining permissible levels 
of toxic chemical exposure in humans.
What is still needed is better correlation between animal 
data with known immunotoxicants and epidemiological or 
clinical studies in order to ascertain the predictive value of 
the immune evaluation methods for human populations that 
may be occupationally or environmentally exposed. Well-
controlled studies are still needed in human subjects exposed 
to environmental chemicals to establish concretely the rela-
tionship between documented exposure and immune-medi-
ated effects.
With pharmaceuticals where exposure is well documented, 
the correlations of the prediction value of animal studies for 
immune alterations (e.g., immunosuppression or allergy) in 
man are more clearly defined. However, as therapeutics con-
tinue to find new targets and new treatment modalities, and 
the field of immunology continues to expand, more predictive 
models and testing strategies are needed.
