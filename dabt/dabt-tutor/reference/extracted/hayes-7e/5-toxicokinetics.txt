5.1вАВ Introduction
Pharmacokinetics (PK) and pharmacodynamics (PD) refer to 
the study of the interaction of drugs within the body. PK relates 
to the movement of the drug into, within, and out of the body. 
PD describes the pharmacological effects (receptor binding 
and downstream biochemical and physiological effects) once 
the drug is delivered to its site of action.1
The principles of PK can apply to any xenobiotic, defined 
as any substance that is foreign to the body. The main pro-
cesses involved in PK are absorption, distribution, metabo-
lism (biotransformation), and excretion (ADME).2 Many of 
the biological processes involved in the ADME of xenobi-
otics are nonspecific and are shared by therapeutic drugs 
and other low-molecular-weight organic molecules such as 
food additives, pesticides, industrial chemicals, and envi-
ronmental contaminants. The field of toxicokinetics (TK) is 
an extension of PK. The term TK refers to the pharmacoki-
netic processes of toxicants, which do not have a therapeutic 
effect. In drug discovery and development, TK denotes the 
relationship of in vivo drug concentrations to observable toxi-
cological effects.
TK, analogous to PK, is defined as the movement of the 
toxicant into, within, and out of the body. Following dose 
administration, the toxicant is absorbed from the site of 
administration and distributed to the systemic circulation and 
into the target organ/tissues. The distribution, metabolism, 
and excretion properties of the toxicant determine the concen-
trations attained in the systemic circulation and at the target 
organ/tissues. The assessment of TK allows for the assess-
ment of systemic exposure, an integral part of risk assessment 
(Figure 5.1). Toxicodynamics defines the interaction of the tox-
icant with the biological target, which can lead to structural or 
functional alterations of the biological target. These alterations 
can lead to toxicity, which may be reversible or irreversible. 
The assessment of toxicodynamics allows for hazard identi-
fication and characterization, also a fundamental part of risk 
assessment (Figure 5.1).
The toxicant concentration in the systemic circulation is 
influenced by ADME processes, as shown in Figure 5.2. Upon 
oral administration, the toxicant is absorbed into the systemic 
circulation from the small intestinal lumen and enters the liver 
via the portal vein circulation. In the liver, the toxicant may 
be metabolized and/or eliminated as the parent compound in 
the bile, followed by excretion in feces. The fraction of the 
toxicant dose that is not metabolized in the intestine or the 
liver will then attain the systemic circulation. The first-pass 
effect is the phenomenon by which the fraction of the dose 
which reaches the systemic circulation is reduced by intestinal 
and/or hepatic metabolism. From the central compartment, the 
toxicant can then distribute into tissues and/or to the site of 
action. Upon intravenous (IV) dosing, the toxicant is delivered 
directly into the systemic circulation, bypassing the limita-
tions of intestinal first-pass. Upon intramuscular (IM) dosing, 
the toxicant is delivered directly into the peripheral tissues. 
Toxicants may also be administered by inhalation directly into 
the lungs. From the systemic circulation, circulating toxicants 
can be eliminated either by hepatic metabolism and/or biliary 
excretion or by urinary excretion.
The purpose of TK studies in the evaluation of nonclinical 
(animal) toxicity assessments is to demonstrate that the test 
compound is present in the groups of animals being tested 
and to understand the relationship between systemic exposure 
and the observed toxicological effects. An understanding of 
the TK properties (e.g., linear versus non-linear kinetics) is 
essential in the selection of dose levels for subsequent longer-
term chronic toxicity studies. TK data are important in the

5.3вАВ Mathematical Principles
As depicted in Figures 5.1 and 5.2, in theory, the exposure-
response relationship would ideally be established based on 
the toxicant concentrations at the site of toxicity/target organ. 
In practice, adequate serial sampling of target organs/tissues 
in animal toxicity studies to enable the calculation of TK 
parameters is prohibitive, both from cost and animal use per-
spectives. Furthermore, human exposure studies are based on 
toxicant concentrations measured in plasma or blood, which is 
the most accessible biofluid to collect serially over time to gain 
insight on toxicant disposition and ADME properties. One 
assumption for the interpretation of TK properties in plasma or 
whole blood is that the concentrations at the site of action and 
the concentrations in plasma/blood have attained equilibrium 
and that any changes in toxicant concentrations at the site of 
action will be reflected by a corresponding change in plasma/
blood concentrations.
The investigation of TK is a mathematical description of 
the toxicantвАЩs disposition in the body. To accurately describe 
the changes in toxicant concentrations in blood or plasma over 
time, a suitable mathematical model has to be assigned that 
accurately describes the shape of the concentration-time curve.

5.3.1.1вАВ Absorption
The administration of a toxicant by any route other than IV 
injection directly into the systemic circulation will require 
an absorption phase. Absorption describes the processes 
involved in the uptake of a toxicant from the site of adminis-
tration into the systemic circulation. Absorption may involve 
first-order or zero-order processes or a mixture of the two. 
The extent of absorption can be determined by the compari-
son of concentrations in plasma after oral and IV administra-
tion. The fraction absorbed may be low due to either poor 
uptake from the site of administration due to solubility- or 
permeability-limited absorption, or metabolism between the 
site of administration and the systemic circulation (first-pass 
metabolism).
5.3.1.2вАВ Tissue Extraction
The rate constant for tissue uptake may depend on either the 
tissue blood flow (perfusion-limited uptake), where k is related 
to the tissue flow rate, or the rate of diffusion through mem-
branes (diffusion-limited uptake), where k is related to the dif-
fusion rate constant and is not readily measurable. In general, 
diffusion rate limitation applies to highly water-soluble com-
pounds, whereas perfusion rate limitation applies to the entry 
of lipid-soluble compounds into slowly perfused tissues, such 
as adipose tissue. The extent of tissue uptake depends on the 
relative affinities of the toxicant for the blood and the different

5.4вАВ Practical Methods
5.4.1вАВ Dose Administration Techniques
Nonclinical toxicity studies in support of drug development 
programs are generally conducted using the intended route 
of administration in humans, and most frequently, this is oral 
(PO) administration for small molecules and parenteral routes 
of administration for biologics. Biologic drugs are protein- or 
peptide-based, and generally have poor oral bioavailability due 
to poor intestinal absorption or non-enzymatic degradation in 
the stomach. Parenteral routes of administration include: IV, 
IM, subcutaneous (SC), and intraperitoneal (IP) administra-
tion. All routes of administration that require absorption can 
result in reduced and variable systemic exposure compared to 
IV administration. Nonclinical toxicity studies in support of 
non-pharmaceutical products, such as pesticides, industrial 
chemicals, consumer products, food additives, and cosmetics, 
are generally conducted via the potential route of exposure in 
humans, which is most commonly via oral or dermal exposure.
For each route of administration, special considerations 
need to be taken into account for dose administration for a suc-
cessful toxicity and TK study, and these are outlined below. In 
experimental animals, many of the commonly used methods 
of dose delivery require restraint, sedation, and sometimes, 
general anesthesia, and the use of these manipulations should 
be considered when selecting the route of administration.
Dosing vehicles can exhibit adverse toxicological effects, 
which may also be species-specific. For nonclinical toxicity 
and TK studies, the ideal dose vehicle is one that23:
	
1.	can be administered via the intended route of admin-
istration or exposure in humans,
	
2.	does not adversely impact the stability or the bio-
availability of the test article, and
	
3.	is well tolerated and does not have biological effects 
on the test system.
In interpreting the results of toxicity studies, itвАЩs important 
to be able to distinguish between the toxicological effects of 
the test article under study and the vehicle alone. In general, 
it is good laboratory practice to choose dosing vehicles that 
are simple and for which there is adequate characterization of 
its toxicological effects based on historical data. Gad et al.24 
provide a comprehensive review on the tolerability of non-
clinical species to different dosing vehicles commonly used in 
toxicity studies and administered by multiple routes. Practical 
strategies to develop formulations appropriate for nonclinical 
toxicity studies are summarized in Li and Zhao.25 Strategies 
for oral formulation development to bridge early drug discov-
ery studies for development and the clinic are summarized in 
Thackaberry.23
5.4.1.1вАВ IV Injection
The IV route of administration is the most efficient method 
to deliver an accurate dose rapidly into the body, which is 
unattainable using other routes of administration. IV dose

5.4.1.1.1вАВ IV Bolus
When administering an IV bolus dose, the test article has to 
be administered as a solution, and the test article has to remain 
in solution and not precipitate in the bloodstream upon deliv-
ery, which can lead to shock (foreign body emboli) and animal 
death within minutes after dosing. In planning toxicity and 
TK studies, the solubility of test articles in the desired vehicle, 
as well as the tonicity of the solution, need to be taken into 
account. The recommended working range for the pH of solu-
tions for IV administration is 4.5вАУ8.0.24 The ideal dose vol-
ume for IV administration in the mouse is 5 mL/kg, whereas 
it is 1 mL/kg for other rodents and non-rodent species (see 
Table 5.2). In practice, the maximum dose volume to be used 
in nonclinical species is 5 mL/kg.26 Dose administration by IV 
should be delivered aseptically and the dosing solution should 
be sterile by filtration at 0.2 ¬µm.
The ideal dosing vehicle for IV administration is an iso-
tonic saline solution (0.9% sodium chloride); however, many 
test articles do not have adequate solubility to be administered 
in saline, and additional excipients and/or solubilizing agents 
are required. In practice, commonly used excipients in dos-
ing vehicles for IV administration include dimethylacetamide, 
polyethylene glycol, cyclodextrins, and the surfactant Tween 
80. It should be noted that Tween 80, while it is a commonly 
used and well-tolerated vehicle in rodents and monkeys, Tween 
80 is not tolerated in dogs, resulting in an anaphylactic reac-
tion and mortality.27
In rodent species, it is common practice to administer an IV 
bolus dose via the tail vein. In larger species (e.g., dogs, mon-
keys), it is common practice to administer an IV bolus via the 
saphenous or cephalic vein. Cannulation of a vein, such as the 
external jugular or femoral vein, under anesthesia, can also be 
used for dose administration.
One advantage of IV administration is the rapid and accu-
rate dosing into the systemic circulation with a bioavailability 
of 100% so that the test article can have a pharmacological (or 
toxicological) effect more quickly than if administered by any 
other route of administration. This also allows for the appro-
priate calculation of TK parameters such as CL, Vss, and t1/2. 
IV bolus administration also results in the highest Cmax that 
can be attained with any route of administration, which in the 
context of a toxicity study, allows for the characterization of 
Cmax-mediated toxicities.
5.4.1.1.2вАВ IV Infusion
IV infusion is the only mode of delivery for many therapeu-
tics, especially for small-molecule drugs indicated for the 
treatment of advanced cancer. In these cases, the nonclinical 
toxicity studies to support the safety assessment program must 
be conducted using IV infusion as the route of administration 
and using the same cyclical regimen, if applicable, as the pro-
posed clinical regimen in humans. For Cmax-mediated toxici-
ties, dose administration via infusion may be better tolerated 
than administration via bolus administration.

ambulatory animals using precision electronic infusion 
pumps. Continuous or intermittent infusion in rodents can be 
performed using a surgically implanted indwelling catheter 
and jacket-tether or a tail-cuff system, which allows for the 
free movement of animals and reducing stress. These types 
of systems also support blood sample collection for clinical 
pathology and TK assessments without interrupting or inter-
fering with continuous dose administration. For infusion stud-
ies in non-rodent species, both jacket-tether and ambulatory 
systems can be used. Programmable pumps can also be used 
to delay the initiation of the infusion, which can be particularly 
important when monitoring study parameters that are sensi-
tive to animal stress, such as cardiovascular function param-
eters. Typical infusion rates used in nonclinical species range 
from 2 mL/kg to 4 mL/kg per hour. It is reported that rates <2 
mL/kg hourly in rodents may result in patency issues with the 
catheter for dose administration.26 Green and Healing (editors) 
provide a comprehensive, two-volume text on the technologies 
and methodologies used for infusion studies in nonclinical 
species.28,29
5.4.1.2вАВ Oral Dosing
5.4.1.2.1вАВ Formulation Selection and 
Dose Administration
Oral dosage forms can be administered as solutions, sus-
pensions, or solid dosage forms, such as capsules or tablets. 
Currently, no regulatory guidelines are recommending which 
oral formulation should be used for nonclinical toxicity stud-
ies. In practice, oral dose solutions or suspensions are often 
prepared in methylcellulose/carboxymethylcellulose, cyclo-
dextrins, polyethylene glycols, Labrasol¬Ѓ, Solutol¬Ѓ HS15, 
Tween 20/80, and D-ќ±-tocopheryl polyethylene glycol 1000 
succinate (D-ќ±-tocopheryl polyethylene glycol succinate¬†[vita-
min E TPGS]). As mentioned above, the toxicological effects 
of the vehicle alone need to be considered when selecting a 
dosing vehicle for an oral toxicity study. Compared to rodents, 
dogs and monkeys are more prone to diarrhea, and dogs are 
particularly sensitive to emesis. Diarrhea and emesis may not 
be considered as marked toxicities, but they can have a dose-
limiting effect. Emesis can reduce exposure to the test article, 
especially if it occurs shortly following dose administration. 
Both diarrhea and emesis can reduce food intake and lead to 
weight loss, test article-related effects which are not compat-
ible with repeated dose toxicity studies.
Similar to IV administration, Tween 80 is not well toler-
ated in the dog when administered orally and can lead to 
analphylactoid reactions.27 Tween 80 at concentrations up to 
0.2% (w/v) is reported to be well tolerated upon repeated oral 
dosing for up to 90вАЙdays, but the tolerability to higher percent-
ages varies.24,30 Cyclodextrins, especially hydroxypropyl-ќ≤-
cyclodextrin, are commonly used as a complexation agent 
to enhance solubilization. Repeated, chronic oral dosing of 
hydroxypropyl-ќ≤-cyclodextrin in rats can result in an increase 
in adenocarcinomas in the large intestine and pancreas, 
which could confound the interpretation of carcinogenicity 
studies conducted in rats.31,32 Tumor formation induced by 
hydroxypropyl-ќ≤-cyclodextrin is largely believed to be rat-
specific. Administration of formulations containing 500 or 
1,000 mg/kg hydroxypropyl-ќ≤-cyclodextrin resulted in vehi-
cle-related toxicities in all species tested.30 In dogs and mon-
keys, the incidence of loose and/or soft stool was increased. In 
female rats, hydroxypropyl-ќ≤-cyclodextrin treatment resulted 
in time- and dose-dependent elevations in serum liver trans-
aminases that occurred without a histopathologic correlate. 
The etiology and significance of these findings in rats are 
uncertain; however, since these liver biomarkers are com-
monly assessed in toxicity studies, they could confound the 
assessment and interpretation of test article-related findings.
Certain surfactants used in dosing vehicles can result in 
inflammatory lung lesions due to reflux/aspiration or gavage 
error of the oral dose solution during administration. In the 
nonclinical safety assessment program of XTANDI¬Ѓ (enzalu-
tamide), a marketed anti-androgen drug used for the treatment 
of prostate cancer, aspiration of the dosing vehicle which con-
sisted of caprylocaproyl macroglycerides resulted in adverse 
macroscopic findings (mottled lung) and pulmonary inflam-
matory changes which occurred in both the vehicle- and 
enzalutamide-treated animals.33 These pulmonary lesions 
resulting from accidental aspiration of the formulation were 
thought to contribute to mortality in some animals on study. 
These nonclinical findings due to accidental aspiration are 
not relevant to human safety assessment since prostate cancer 
patients would take a capsule dose; however, these findings 
can complicate data interpretation and the reviewerвАЩs assess-
ment of drug-related nonclinical toxicity findings.
The most common method of oral administration of dosing 
solutions and suspensions in nonclinical species is via intra-
gastric gavage. Dosing solutions/suspensions are loaded into 
an appropriately sized syringe and administered directly into 
the stomach via a gavage needle for rodents or a catheter/intu-
bation tube for larger animals. It is also common to administer 
the dose by intranasal gavage in monkeys. To minimize the 
potential for reflux and aspiration, the dose administered is 
commonly followed by a saline, water, or dose vehicle flush 
to minimize dose deposition in the esophagus. The pH of oral 
dose solutions should be kept between 3 and 10.34 Ideal dose 
volumes for oral gavage in nonclinical species range from 5 to 
10 mL/kg24 (see Table 5.2).
Larger, non-rodent species can be administered a solid oral 
dosage form as a capsule or tablet. In nonclinical drug devel-
opment, capsule dosing is commonly used for chronic toxicity 
studies, especially in dogs. Dogs, compared to other nonclini-
cal species used for toxicity assessment, are particularly sensi-
tive to emesis, either induced by the dosing vehicle itself or 
the test article. If emesis is due to local irritation caused by 
high local concentrations of the test article in the stomach or 
GI tract when administered in solution or suspension, dose 
administration as a solid oral dosage form as a capsule or tab-
let can sometimes reduce the incidence of or avoid emesis. 
Solid oral dosage form studies in nonclinical species can also 
provide additional insight into oral absorption characteristics, 
such as ka and oral F, using a dosage form that is the same or

5.4.1.2.2вАВ Food Effect
Exploratory pharmacokinetic studies in support of drug dis-
covery are often conducted in fasted animals, whereas toxicity 
and TK studies are most often conducted in fed animals or in 
animals that have free (ad libitum) access to feed. Depending 
on the physicochemical properties of the test article, admin-
istration with feed can either improve or reduce oral absorp-
tion. Some test articles that are lipophilic may show enhanced 
absorption in fed animals, as feeding induces the secretion of 
bile acids into the GI tract that could help to solubilize the 
test article. In other cases, the test article may physically bind 
to the feed, reducing its oral absorption. Feeding status also 
impacts gastric pH, which can impact the solubility, dissolu-
tion, and oral absorption of test articles depending on physico-
chemical properties, such as pKa.
The anatomical and physiological processes of gastric 
motility and emptying which impact oral drug absorption are 
relatively similar in dogs and humans. The most notable dif-
ference between dogs and humans is gastric pH. The gastric 
pH in fasted dogs and humans is variable ranging from 1.5 
to 6.7 and 1 to 5, respectively. The gastric pH in fed humans 
ranges from 5 to 7, whereas fed dogs have a low gastric pH 
of ~2.35 Pre-treatment of dogs with pentagastrin, administered 
intramuscularly, reduces the gastric pH to ~1, similar to the 
fasted state in humans. Pre-treatment of dogs with pentagas-
trin predicted well the absence or presence of a formulation 
food effect in humans, although the magnitude of the feed 
effect observed in the dog model did not match that observed 
in humans.
5.4.1.2.3вАВ Dietary Studies
Dietary administration is most commonly used in chronic 
toxicity studies in rodents, in particular in carcinogenicity 
studies. One advantage of dietary studies is the elimination of 
animal handling and associated stress due to daily oral gavage 
procedures.23 The test article is blended homogeneously into 
the animal diet/feed and the desired daily dose is achieved 
based on the known feed consumption of the test animal spe-
cies. The remaining feed at the end of the dosing interval is 
then weighed to estimate the total feed intake based on mea-
sured body weights. Dietary administration can also reduce 
test article-related GI toxicity. Oral gavage typically produces 
an oscillating daily toxicokinetic profile characterized by a 
high transient Cmax followed by a low trough (Cmin) at the end 
of the dosing interval. Dietary administration provides a more 
constant and stable level of test article intake over the dosing 
interval, a result of the feeding habits of laboratory animals.
One disadvantage of dietary administration studies is that 
pilot studies are needed for a successful study design. Pilot 
studies are conducted to assess whether the test article has ade-
quate stability in animal feed and whether the test article-sup-
plemented animal feed remains palatable to the test species. If 
the test article itself reduces feed consumption or body weight 
gain, this can also lead to a reduction of the actual adminis-
tered dose and, consequently, reduce the systemic exposure to 
the test article.
For agrochemicals, dietary administration is a relevant 
route of oral exposure for regulatory toxicity studies and 
human risk assessment as it mimics potential human intake 
of residues in food commodities. A study by Hannas et al.36 
demonstrated the successful application of dietary adminis-
tration of two agrochemicals in a rabbit developmental toxic-
ity study. Dietary administration of sulfoxaflor or halauxifen 
resulted in stable 24-hour plasma concentrations, whereas oral 
gavage resulted in marked fluctuations between Cmax and Cmin 
over the dosing interval (see Figure 5.12). Oscillating toxi-
cokinetic profile versus constant plasma exposure may have 
implications in terms of detecting toxicities that may only 
be triggered by exposure during certain windows of suscep-
tibility of embryofetal development. Halauxifen, which has a 
short plasma t1/2, was detectable only up to 12вАЙhours postdose 
when administered by oral gavage over the dosing interval 
of 24вАЙhours (Figure 5.12, panel B). Dietary administration of 
sulfoxaflor actually allowed for a two-fold higher nominal and 
diurnal systemic dose to be administered by reducing dose-
limiting Cmax-associated maternal toxicity which occurred fol-
lowing oral gavage. The presence of sulfoxaflor or halauxifen 
was confirmed in fetal blood samples, confirming that dietary 
administration to rabbits results in constant and stable embryo/
fetal exposure throughout the gestational period.
5.4.1.3вАВ Inhalational Administration
The delivery of test articles via inhalation can pose some unique 
challenges when compared to other routes of administration. 
Rabbits and rodents are obligate nose-breathing animals, and it 
is not feasible to expose animals using the same delivery device 
that is intended for drug administration in humans. Different 
types of inhalation exposure chambers can be used for experi-
mental animals: nose-only, head-only, or whole-body cham-
bers, or inhalation/ventilation techniques in intubated animals. 
Dose administration techniques to enable inhalational toxicity 
studies have been described thoroughly in the previous edition 
of this text37 and are also discussed in Chapter 30 of this text. 
Methods of pulmonary exposure in nonclinical toxicity stud-
ies as it relates to the development and safety assessment of 
inhaled pharmaceuticals are reviewed by Owen.38
5.4.1.4вАВ SC Administration
SC injection is a rapid, inexpensive, and simple method of 
parenteral administration acceptable for non-irritating test 
articles. Aqueous or oily test articles may be administered sub-
cutaneously. This route of administration is most commonly 
chosen for exploratory studies in drug discovery and develop-
ment to increase systemic exposure to the test article when oral 
bioavailability is poor since the intestinal and hepatic first-pass 
effect is avoided. Absorption into the systemic circulation fol-
lowing SC administration occurs at a slower rate compared to 
other parenteral routes of administration. Dose vehicles often

5.4.1.5вАВ IP Injection
Injection into the IP space is a commonly used dosing route 
for single-dose exploratory pharmacokinetic, pharmacody-
namic, and toxicity studies in rodents and other small animals. 
IP injection is rarely used in larger animals and humans. This 
route is often chosen for small animals where IV administra-
tion is more technically challenging. Due to the risk of local 
irritation at the injection site, this route of administration is not 
commonly used for repeated dose studies. While IP admin-
istration avoids factors that may limit absorption across the 
intestinal barrier, the administered test article is still subject to 
the hepatic first-pass effect. The primary route of absorption is 
into the mesenteric vessels which drain into the portal vein.26
The IP space is devoid of an adequate amount of biological 
fluid to help in the dissolution of the administered test article, 
so the test article needs to be administered as a solution dose. 
Suspensions or precipitating solution doses can result in local 
irritation/ulceration of the tissues and organs of the peritoneal 
cavity. A solution near physiological pH is preferred for IP 
injections. Dosing solutions should be sterile (or sterile filtered 
at 0.2 ¬µm), isotonic and non-irritating. Saline, cyclodextrins, 
and polyethylene glycols are the most common vehicles used 
for formulating test articles for IP administration.24
Ideal dose volumes range from 5 to 10 mL/kg in mice and 
rats, 1 to 5 mL/kg in guinea pigs, and 3 mL/kg in rabbits, 
dogs, and monkeys. In rodents, dose administration is usually 
performed in the lower right abdominal quadrant in conscious 
animals using firm manual restraint with the head and body 
pointed downward. A common practice is to withdraw the 
syringe plunger prior to dose injection to assess the presence 
of urine, blood, or digesta, indicative that one of the organs of 
the peritoneal space has been erroneously punctured. While 
IP injection is thought to be less technically challenging 
than an IV injection, a common mistake is accidental dose 
delivery into the urinary bladder or cecum or accidental SC 
administration.

5.4.2вАВ Sampling Techniques
5.4.2.1вАВ Sampling of Blood or Plasma
In the planning of a TK study, it is important to first assess 
whether plasma or whole blood is the appropriate biofluid to 
collect to assess the systemic exposure of the test article on the 
study. Historically, drug/toxicant concentration assays have 
almost exclusively used plasma as a matrix for TK assess-
ment rather than whole blood, which has been based primar-
ily on unfounded theories about analytical assay sensitivity 
and matrix interference that it is simply вАЬbetterвАЭ to measure 
drug concentrations in plasma rather than whole blood. In 
fact, whether the TK of the test article should be measured 
in plasma or whole blood depends on its disposition in whole 
blood.
Blood is primarily composed of red blood cells (RBC), 
plasma, and a few white blood cells. For the discussion herein, 
the constituents of whole blood will be divided into two cat-
egories: RBC and plasma. The hematocrit is the proportion of 
blood that consists of RBC, by volume. Normal hematocrit in 
male humans and nonclinical species approximates 40вАУ50%,42 
meaning that whole blood is made up ofвАЙ~вАЙ50% RBC and ~50% 
plasma. The concentration of a test article in the blood (Cblood) 
is hence the sum of its concentration in RBC and plasma:

high blood-to-plasma partitioning ratios (вЙ•2.0), the interpreta-

and 37¬∞C for a given time. Measurement of concentrations in 
plasma and separated RBCs or whole blood is assessed follow-

blood-to-plasma ratios that is more amenable to high-through-
put screening to support drug discovery has been described.44 
Partitioning into RBCs will often be time- and concentration-
dependent, so complete characterization of a test articleвАЩs dis-
position in whole blood should include both the assessment of 
different incubation times and concentrations. In in vivo PK 
and TK studies, test article concentrations can be determined 
in both whole blood and plasma to compare and assess TK 
parameters. This approach can be done more readily in larger 
species often used for toxicity studies, such as dogs, non-
human primates, minipigs, and rabbits, where blood volumes 
collected are larger.
5.4.2.1.1вАВ Sampling Time Points
The number of time points that should be sampled to properly 
characterize the TK profile depends on the curve shape and 
the terminal t1/2. From a theoretical perspective, at least three 
sampling time points would be needed to characterize each 
phase of the TK profile (absorption, distribution, and elimina-
tion), for a total of nine time points. From a practical perspec-
tive though, the number of sampling time points is limited by 
the number of animals on the study and the volume of blood 
that can be withdrawn from the animals on study. It is com-
mon practice in TK studies that a minimum of five time points 
and up to seven time points be sampled over the dosing inter-
val. To characterize an oral TK profile, two to three sampling 
times should be collected to characterize the absorption phase, 
one time sampled at or near the anticipated Tmax followed by at 
least three times collected in the terminal phase (distribution/
elimination).

5.4.2.1.2вАВ Limitations of Blood Sampling
TK analysis requires multiple blood samples to be collected 
from animals over a short period of time, and there may be 
toxicological effects associated with acute blood loss, espe-
cially in smaller experimental animals like rodents. Acute 
blood loss can result in increased moribundity or mortality, 
as well as increased susceptibility to toxic effects induced by 
the test article under study. Blood sampling procedures may 
also interfere with the assessment of the toxicity endpoints, in 
particular hematology parameters that are routinely monitored 
in regulatory safety assessment studies.
In pivotal, regulated toxicity studies conducted in support of 
drug development, the assessment of exposure and TK is often 
conducted in a satellite group of animals from which only TK 
data will be derived, separate from the main study group that 
will be used for toxicity assessment. For rats, a common design 
is to use a dose group of nine animals per sex, and blood is 
sampled in groups of three animals at a given time point. A 
blood sample may be taken from one rat up to three times. 
Blood volumes of 200вАУ300 ¬µL are typically drawn from an 
animal at each time point. A single concentration-time curve 
is built as a composite of all nine rats in that dose group (see 
the below section; see Figure 5.14).
When the mouse is selected as the rodent species for a toxi-
cology study, due to the limitation in total blood volume avail-
able, a terminal procedure has to be used to collect a single 
blood sample. In mice, no more than 7.5% of circulatory blood 
volume can be removed from mice weighing 20вАУ40вАЙg within 
a week without inducing hypovolemia and other physiological 
stress responses.45
With multiple TK sampling times, if 7.5% total blood vol-
ume is removed within a 24-hour period, this would neces-
sitate a 7-day recovery period.46 In general, total blood draws, 
including sampling for clinical chemistry assessments, should 
target not to exceed ~10% of the total blood volume in an ani-
mal within a 7-day period. Approximate volumes of blood 
which represent 10% blood volume across different nonclini-
cal species are presented in Table 5.2.
5.4.2.1.3вАВ Microsampling
As described above, TK sampling in smaller animals like 
rodents usually involves the use of a satellite group of ani-
mals. If TK blood samples could be collected from the main 
study group, the satellite group used for TK could be elimi-
nated, which would reduce animal usage by 60% in explor-
atory studies and by 40% in regulated studies.47 This would 
also allow for blood concentrations, TK parameters, and 
toxicological findings to be assessed directly in the same ani-
mals, which improve the data quality and the interpretation of 
dose-exposure-toxicity relationships by removing inter-animal 
variability.48
Microsampling is defined as a method to collect a very 
small volume of blood (typically вЙ§50 ¬µL) for the pur-
poses of measuring test article (or metabolite) concentra-
tions in timed samples to calculate TK parameters. Recent 
advances in analytical chemistry have improved analytical 
method sensitivity substantially, allowing microsampling 
techniques to be used in TK assessment. The low sample 
volume collected using microsampling techniques allows 
for the entire TK profile to be generated from a single ani-
mal, and hence, there is no longer the need for a compos-
ite method to rebuild the concentration-time profile for TK 
parameter determination. As per International Conference 
on Harmonisation (ICH) S3A вАЬQuestions and AnswersвАЭ 
(Q&A),49 minimizing the volume of blood collection can 
also reduce pain and distress in study animals. The adop-
tion of microsampling techniques in drug safety assessment 
programs also reduces the amount of test article required 
to run the study, which can provide an economic advan-
tage for biopharmaceuticals and for small molecules with 
challenging chemical syntheses. The use of microsampling 
techniques in agrochemicals has also led to an increase in 
the number of studies where exposure data are generated 
early in product development, improving decision-making 
in agrochemical development programs.50
Blood microsamples in rodents are often collected from the 
tail vein or saphenous vein. Using capillary microsampling 
(CMS), typically 8вАУ25 ¬µL of whole blood or 32вАУ64 ¬µL of 
whole blood for subsequent processing to plasma is collected 
with a capillary tube.51 The whole blood collected can be 
processed to plasma and stored in a capillary for sample pro-
cessing and bioanalysis. Using dried blood spot technology, 
typically 15вАУ20 ¬µL of whole blood is collected and spotted 
onto the sampling absorbent filter paper and allowed to air dry 
at ambient temperature for a few hours. The dried blood spot 
samples are then processed by punching a small disc of the 
saturated filter paper, and the disc is then subjected to extrac-
tion with an organic solvent prior to bioanalysis of the super-
natant fraction (see Figure 5.13). One disadvantage of dried 
blood spot technology is that plasma cannot be obtained as a 
matrix from this sampling technique. The disposition of the 
test article in blood and plasma, such as the extent of blood cell 
partitioning and potential test article-induced effects on hema-
tocrit values, needs to be considered when selecting dried 
blood spots as a matrix for TK evaluation to allow for bridging 
and interpretation of TK data obtained using dried blood spot 
technology versus TK data obtained from other studies using 
plasma as a matrix.
Several studies have evaluated the feasibility of using mic-
rosampled bleeds in toxicology studies. The results of a study 
published by Wang et al.45 demonstrated that the application 
of CMS in regulated mouse toxicity studies was feasible. 
Serial blood sampling by CMS of a volume of 70 ¬µL was 
achieved from mice with minimal effects on hematology 
parameters. Samples collected by CMS from the tail vein 
and conventional TK sampling via cardiac puncture showed 
similar TK parameters for the drug candidate on the study. 
Bioanalytical analysis of CMS samples (15вАУ20 ¬µL of result-
ing plasma) conformed to the requirements of regulatory 
guidelines and was adequate for the quantification of two 
analytes (parent and metabolite with 5-¬µL sample volume 
per assay) and allowed for incurred sample reanalysis, an 
important validation parameter demonstrating the robustness 
of the bioanalytical method (see Table 5.3). Janssen Research 
& Development has reported that CMS has been successfully 
applied in 26 pivotal, Good Laboratory Practice (GLP) stud-
ies in 12 different drug development projects.52

5.4.3вАВ Sample Analysis
Whole blood, plasma, and bile samples collected for bioanal-
ysis are processed by protein precipitation by extraction in 
organic solvent (such as acetonitrile). Urine samples can often 
simply be diluted in an organic solvent before analysis. Feces 
samples require homogenization in an aqueous medium before 
processing by protein precipitation.
The analysis of whole blood, plasma, urine, and bile samples 
and fecal homogenates from exploratory studies using non-
radiolabeled test article is often achieved by high-performance 
liquid chromatography coupled with tandem mass spectrom-
etry (LC-MS/MS). For exploratory studies, a вАЬfit-for-purposeвАЭ 
or qualified method may be used, meaning that no validation 
or limited validation, respectively, has been accomplished for 
bioanalytical method parameters, such as specificity, accu-
racy, precision, matrix effects, and carry-over. To support 
pivotal, GLP-compliant toxicity studies, it is expected that a 
validated analytical method be employed, meaning that the 
method is robust, meeting pre-specified criteria for validation 
parameters prior to being used to determine concentrations of 
a test article in biological samples. The bioanalytical param-
eters that are commonly validated for regulatory bioanalysis55 
are listed in Table 5.3.
The analysis of biological samples collected in studies in 
which a radiolabeled test article has been administered is often 
achieved either by liquid scintillation counting or by LC-MS/
MS coupled with radiometric detection. LC-MS/MS coupled 
with radiometric detection allows for the structural elucida-
tion of metabolites using accurate mass spectrometry, and also 
allows for a quantitative assessment of parent and metabolite 
concentrations by radiometry, which can also be expressed as 
a percent of the dose administered.
5.4.4вАВ Data Handling
As described above, due to limitations in blood sampling vol-
umes and sparse sampling in rodents, a composite TK profile 
must be built from pooled concentration data which is aver-
aged across animals per time point. Composite TK profiles do 
not allow for an assessment of inter-individual variability in 
TK parameters. A single value for Cmax and AUC will be calcu-
lated for the dose group. Larger, non-rodent species will most 
often have a serial blood sampling design. With a serial blood 
sampling design, complete time course data are obtained in 
individual animals, allowing for the calculation of the TK 
parameters for each animal in every dose group, as well as the 
conduct of comparative statistics (i.e., group mean values ¬± 
standard deviation; see Figure 5.14).

5.4.5вАВ Interpretation of In Vivo Toxicokinetic Data
In interpreting TK data, it is not only important to assess the 
relationship of how each dose level and the duration of dosing 
relates to the computed TK parameters, but also how the TK 
parameters relate to the toxicity endpoints observed. As per 
regulatory guidance recommendations for toxicity testing of 
pharmaceutical products and chemicals, reporting and assess-
ment of the following TK parameters are expected in support 
of toxicity studies: Cmax, Tmax, AUC, and t1/2 (when feasible). 
Evaluation of TK data in terms of dose proportionality, sex 
differences, and accumulation with repeated administration 
are important aspects in terms of understanding the TK profile 
and disposition of the test article in relation to the observed 
toxicity endpoints.
5.4.5.1вАВ Dose Proportionality
Upon single and repeated administration, it is important 
to assess dose proportionality, meaning whether the fold-
increase in observed Cmax and AUC0вИТѕД values correlate with 
the fold-increase in dose levels, or whether there is a deviation 
in this relationship. An understanding of the dose proportion-
ality relationship is important in exploratory TK studies since 
this aids in the dose selection for the more pivotal and defini-
tive toxicity studies. As per established best practices, the low, 
mid-, and high dose levels should be selected not based on the 
nominal dose separation between dose groups, but based on 
the separation of the resulting exposures at given dose levels.
When the fold-increases in exposure are less than the 
fold-increases in dose, this can often be interpreted as вАЬdose

236
HayesвАЩ Principles and Methods of Toxicology
saturationвАЭ, which is common for orally administered test 
articles that show solubility- and/or permeability-limited oral 
absorption. When the fold-increases in exposure are greater 
than the fold-increases in dose (also called supra-proportion-
ality), this can often be interpreted as saturation of a clearance 
mechanism as dose levels increase, which then leads to expo-
sure levels that are higher than expected (see Figure 5.15).
5.4.5.2вАВ Sex Differences
Toxicity testing for pharmaceutical products and chemicals is 
often conducted in both male and female animals to address 
sex differences in toxicity. In addition to the potential for sex 
differences in the sensitivity to toxicity, it is also possible for 
a test article to demonstrate sex differences in TK parameters. 
Due to biological and inter-/intra-assay variability, only a dif-
ference of greater than two-fold in the Cmax and/or AUC0вИТѕД val-
ues between males and females is interpreted to constitute a 
meaningful sex difference in exposure.
Sex differences in TK parameters are often observed in 
rodents for xenobiotics and drugs that are metabolized by 
cytochrome P450s (CYP450) and when oxidative metabo-
lism constitutes the major route of elimination. Rats, and to 
a lesser extent, mice, demonstrate a sexual dimorphism in 
terms of expression of hepatic CYP450s, which can account 
for very notable differences in systemic exposure and can 
complicate toxicity assessments. Generally speaking, male 
rats have higher expression of CYP450s and higher metabolic 
capacity than female rats.60 The sexual dimorphism in hepatic 
CYP450s manifests as higher exposure to the test article in 
female rats, due to a reduced extent of metabolism, compared 
to males where the extent of metabolism is higher, and hence 
reducing the concentrations of the substrate drug/xenobiotic. 
Metabolism as a determinant of toxicity is discussed in Chapter 
4 of this text. Sex differences in TK properties in other species 
often used for toxicity assessments, such as dogs and monkeys, 
occur relatively infrequently, and a notable sex difference in 
hepatic enzyme expression has not been documented in these 
species like it has been for rats.
5.4.5.3вАВ Accumulation
The mathematical calculation of the accumulation ratio is 
described earlier in this chapter (Equations 5.68 and 5.69). 
The accumulation ratio essentially quantitates the extent of the 
increase of systemic exposure in terms of Cmax and AUC on 
the first day of dosing compared to the exposure on the last 
dosing day (Daylast) following repeated administration. Due to 
biological and inter-/intra-assay variability, an accumulation 
ratio of вЙ•two-fold when comparing Daylast Cmax and/or AUC0вИТѕД 
values to Day 1 values is interpreted to constitute a meaningful 
extent of accumulation during the course of the study.
When accumulation ratios exceed 2, this can be interpreted 
that repeated administration has resulted in the accumulation 
of the test article. The statement that a test article вАЬaccumu-
latesвАЭ is often misinterpreted as being an adverse observa-
tion in a study and is incorrectly associated with toxicity. The 
accumulation ratio simply describes the relationship between 
the frequency of dosing and the rate of elimination, and hence 
reducing the frequency of dosing will also result in a reduction 
of the accumulation ratio (see Figure 5.11). When interpret-
ing TK data in the context of a toxicity study, it is not that 
the extent of accumulation that should be considered to be the 
adverse finding, but the actual magnitude of the Daylast expo-
sure that results in toxicity.
It is also a common occurrence to observe an accumulation 
ratio of <1.0 following repeated administration. Due to bio-
logical and inter-intra-assay variability, an accumulation ratio 
must be вЙ§0.5 when comparing Daylast to Day 1 exposure val-
ues to be interpreted as a scientifically meaningful reduction 
in exposure. Reductions in exposure with repeated dosing are 
most often the result of вАЬauto-inductionвАЭ, a phenomenon where 
the test article is an inducer of the hepatic drug-metabolizing 
enzymes by which it is primarily metabolized. Enzyme induc-
tion is time-dependent, and hence following repeated admin-
istration, hepatic enzyme induction occurs which increases the 
metabolism of the test article and reducing its end-of-study 
exposure. Mechanisms of hepatic drug-metabolizing enzyme 
induction are described in Chapter 4 of this text.

5.4.5.5вАВ Control Sample Contamination 
with Test Article
From all pivotal, regulated toxicity studies, it is expected that 
TK samples be collected from the animals in the vehicle-con-
trol group to assess for contamination with the test article. In 
rodents, samples are typically collected and analyzed at the 
anticipated Tmax, as per the sparse sampling design (discussed 
earlier). In larger non-rodent species, all samples collected as 
per the serial sampling design are analyzed for the presence of 
the test article.
Sources of TK sample contamination include mis-dosing, 
sample handling/processing, and analyte carry-over during 
bioanalysis. The presence of the test article in control samples 
can complicate the interpretation of the toxicity study results 
by confounding the assessment of which observed toxicologi-
cal effects are truly test article-related. In some cases, inade-
quate interpretation of the toxicity study results can completely 
invalidate a study.
5.4.5.6вАВ Exposure-Based Safety Margin Estimations
The calculation of safety margins is an important aspect 
when interpreting nonclinical toxicology studies and transla-

only in drug development to evaluate the clinical safety of

assessment for chemicals, food additives, cosmetic ingre-

exposure-based safety margins are most often calculated using 
AUC as the relevant pharmacokinetic/toxicokinetic parameter; 
however, in certain circumstances, Cmax may actually be the 
more appropriate parameter, as can be the case when Cmax is 
the parameter that drives the pharmacodynamic and/or the 
toxicological effects.

5.5.2вАВ Pharmaceutical Product Development
In the development of pharmaceutical products intended for 
use in human subjects, the ICH of Technical Requirements for 
Registration of Pharmaceutical for Human Use has published 
a guidance document on how to integrate TK into toxicity 
studies (ICH S3A53). The ICH is an international non-profit 
association that brings together regulatory authorities and 
the pharmaceutical industry to discuss scientific and techni-
cal aspects of drug registration. ICHвАЩs mission is to achieve 
worldwide harmonization to ensure that safe, effective, and 
high-quality medicines are developed and registered in a 
resource-efficient manner.
ICH S3A53 (ICH Guidance on Toxicokinetics) was final-
ized and implemented in October 1994, which was followed 
by a Q&A document developed by the S3A Implementation 
Working Group in November 2017. The ICH S3A Q&A49 doc-
ument, which has reached Step 4 of the formal ICH procedure 
entailing its adoption as a harmonized guideline, is intended 
to address the benefit and use of microsampling techniques to 
support TK (discussed earlier in this chapter).
The guidance highlights the need to integrate PK into toxic-
ity testing to facilitate the interpretation of toxicology find-
ings and their relevance to clinical safety issues. The ICH S3A 
guideline dictates the study design, conduct, and interpreta-
tion of TK data conducted in support of regulated nonclini-
cal toxicity studies. As per ICH Safety guidelines, nonclinical 
toxicology studies to be supported by TK assessment include 
single- and repeat-dose general toxicology studies, reproduc-
tive and developmental, in vivo genotoxicity, and carcinoge-
nicity studies. For pivotal toxicity studies, the performance 
of which is subject to compliance with GLP regulations, the 
concomitant TK including analytical method development, 
method validation, bioanalysis, TK parameter calculation, and 
reporting must also conform to GLP regulations.
ICH S3A provides guidance in terms of dose selection for 
pivotal nonclinical toxicology studies. It is recommended 
that the low dose level in toxicity studies be a no-toxic-effect 
dose level and that the exposure in animals ideally be equal 
or slightly exceed the expected or known exposure in human 
patients at the pharmacologically active dose. The intermedi-
ate (mid) dose level would normally represent an appropriate 
multiple of the exposure attained at either the low or high dose 
levels. The high dose level will usually be chosen in terms of 
toxicological considerations. It is expected that the route of 
administration is the intended route for humans. Although it is 
not strictly specified in the ICH S3 guidance, it is established 
industry best practice to determine TK parameters using non-
compartmental methods.
As per ICH S3A Q&A,49 bioanalytical methods support-
ing microsampling techniques are expected to be developed, 
qualified, and/or validated (for GLP studies) to ensure the reli-
ability of analytical results, similarly to conventional methods.

In the development of non-pharmaceutical products (e.g., pes-
ticides, industrial chemicals, consumer products, food addi-
tives, and cosmetics), regulatory authorities have different 
requirements regarding toxicokinetic endpoints and param-
eters for different classes of chemicals. Data requirements 
under non-pharmaceutical regulatory frameworks do not con-
sistently include TK assessment.
The OECD has issued three Test Guidelines (TG) outlining

particular regulatory needs or situations, not all of the studies

5.7вАВ Concluding Remarks
While the fundamental mathematical principles of TK have 
long been established, the study design, dose administration, 
sampling, and bioanalytical techniques are constantly evolving 
to reduce the number of animals required to execute success-
ful in vivo toxicokinetic studies. A strong regulatory frame-
work has been established to regulate toxicokinetic study 
conduct and data interpretation, to enable extrapolation from 
animal to human, and to support scientifically sound- and 
evidence-based human risk assessment. This framework has 
allowed for investigational new drugs to be tested in clinical 
trials at doses that are safe for the enrolled human subjects 
and that are likely to provide therapeutic benefits to patients. 
Similarly, this framework has also allowed for the appropriate 
hazard identification of non-pharmaceutical products (chemi-
cals, pesticides, etc.) that we use routinely in our daily lives so 
that these consumer products may be used safely.
