37.1  Introduction
Male reproduction is a complex process with the simple goal of 
producing and delivering sperm to form offspring. Our current 
state of knowledge about the physiological, biochemical, and 
molecular orchestration of reproduction is captured in many excel-
lent reference volumes.1–3 Male reproductive toxicology is the study 
of the potential effects of xenobiotics on the structure and func-
tion of the male reproductive system. Xenobiotics can disrupt the 
male reproductive process at numerous sites, including hormonal 
interactions in the hypothalamic–pituitary–testicular (HPT) axis, 
spermatogenesis, various cell types within the testes, the post-tes-
ticular ductal system (efferent ducts, epididymis, and vas deferens), 
the accessory sex glands, or mating behavior. Therefore, assess-
ing chemicals and drugs for the potential to cause male reproduc-
tive toxicity involves multiple disciplines and includes data from 
experimental, epidemiological, and mechanistic studies.
Testicular toxicity is a relatively infrequent but persistent 
problem in pharmaceutical development.4,5 In some indica-
tions, such as oncology, toxicity to male fertility is an unde-

Ascertaining the relationships between environmental 
exposures and male reproductive risks for an informed risk 
assessment process requires not only a sufficient amount and 
type of experimental data but also a systematic approach to 
evaluating those data. The approach utilized by the U.S. 
Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) and Organisation for 
Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) for male 
reproductive toxicity risk assessment will be contrasted with 
International Council for Harmonisation (ICH) S5(R3) stud-

emphasis on a critical analysis of the strategies and endpoints 
that are available for male reproductive risk assessment. The 
ultimate safety assessment for testicular toxicity comes from 
human observations. It has been reported that approximately 
17% (235 compounds) of the single-ingredient, Food and Drug 
Administration (FDA)-approved drugs had an adverse effect 
on spermatogenesis in at least one animal model, of these 
235 compounds 93 also evaluated human sperm. Positive 
sperm effects were observed for 60 of the 93 drugs, result-
ing in a false positive rate of 35%.19 This translation is similar 
to another study that estimated the translation of reproductive 
toxicity from animal tests to humans to be around 60%.20
The primary goal of this chapter is to summarize how infor-
mation on the potential for chemicals to interfere with male 
reproductive capability is obtained and used by regulatory 
agencies, industry, and others to protect human and environ-
mental health. To achieve this goal, the chapter will summarize 
the biology of reproduction, compare and contrast reproduc-
tive testing paradigms used across industries, highlight tools 
and endpoints to evaluate male reproductive toxicity, illustrate 
how the Endocrine Disruption Screening Program (EDSP) 
Tier assays can be used to understand endocrine modes of 
action and discuss the future of reproductive toxicology.
37.2  Biology and Toxicology of the 
Male Reproductive Tract
The following sections describe the biology of the male repro-
ductive system as it develops, matures, and ages, as well as the 
various endpoints that can reflect male reproductive toxicity 
at these different life stages. A comprehensive assessment of 
male reproductive toxicity requires information on multiple 
endpoints that can detect the range of potential adverse effects.
Alterations in these reproductive endpoints may be the 
result of direct or indirect toxicity to the male reproductive 
system. In either case, when exposure has caused a reproduc-
tive effect, there may be cause for concern. Careful evaluation 
of the dose–response curves for the various target organs and 
effects may help determine the most sensitive target organ or 
tissue. Once identified, this most sensitive endpoint would be 
the basis for setting the No Observed Adverse Effect Level 
(NOAEL), Lowest Observed Adverse Effect Level (LOAEL), 
or calculation of the benchmark dose for estimating human 
risk. Statistical analysis can be important, but the biological 
significance of the data is most important. When many end-
points are evaluated, statistically significant differences may 
occur by chance. Conversely, apparent trends with dose may 
be relevant biologically even if statistical significance is not 
achieved. Additionally, species differences need to be consid-
ered in determining whether observed effects would be rel-
evant for human risk. In the following discussion, endpoints 
are identified in which significant changes may be considered 
adverse, but concordance is needed between statistically sig-
nificant results and known biological relevance of the results.
37.2.1  Male Reproductive Tract Development 
and Developmental Toxicity
Development of the male reproductive system is both genetically 
and hormonally regulated. Sexual differentiation is primarily con-
trolled by the sex-determining region Y gene (SRY), which initi-
ates the activation of molecular and cellular signaling pathways 
leading to testis formation and regression of the Mullerian duct.21–
23 As the gonad differentiates into the testis, the Sertoli cells are the 
first to form and contribute to further differentiation of the male 
reproductive tract through the production of Mullerian Inhibiting 
Substance (also known as Anti-Mullerian Hormone). Fetal Leydig 
cells differentiate as a result of the paracrine action of Sertoli 
cells and contribute to the development of the male accessory sex 
glands (vas deferens, epididymis, seminal vesicles, and ejaculatory 
duct) through the secretion of androgens and insulin-like factor 3 
(Insl3). The production of these hormones is partially regulated by 
the presence of human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG) and lutein-
izing hormone (LH).21,24 Once differentiated, the male accessory 
sex glands are androgen dependent.21 The conversion of the testos-
terone produced by the Leydig cells to dihydrotestosterone (DHT) 
by 5-alpha reductase results in the further differentiation of the

allows for normal spermatogenesis to occur in the mature testis 
and is mediated by Insl3 in two major phases.21,24–27 The first phase 
occurs during gestational week 25 in humans and is hormonally 
mediated21,24,25,27; the second phase occurs about 10 weeks follow-
ing completion of the first phase and the final process of testicular

In humans, development of the prostate initiates with the 
growth of solid ducts from the urogenital sinus between 11 and 
12 weeks of gestation.29 Growth is further promoted by testos-
terone and other androgens binding to the androgen receptors 
(ARs) in the urogenital sinus mesenchyme. Reciprocal signal-
ing between the mesenchyme and epithelial tissues is critical 
for development, and dysregulation of this process has been 
implicated in the development of disease states.30 The timing 
of prostate development differs between species. In humans, 
initial development is completed by birth and resumes in the 
presence of increased androgens during puberty. Whereas in 
rodents, prostate development is continuous from the end of 
gestation to the beginning of adulthood.31
The male reproductive tract is particularly vulnerable to tox-
icants during development, especially to those with endocrine-
disrupting mechanisms, due to the complexity of intertwined 
developmental pathways and hormonal feedback loops. This 
is exemplified by the different responses of the developing 
seminal vesicles (testosterone-dependent) and prostate (DHT-
dependent) to hormone stimulation.32 As such, these organs 
should be examined separately in any developmental study. 
Developmental susceptibility of the male reproductive tract 
has also been demonstrated through several studies investi-
gating the developmental effects of mixtures of plasticizers 
and antiandrogens, including di-(n-butyl) phthalate (DBP), 
di-(2-ethylhexyl) phthalate (DEHP), vinclozolin, linuron, and 
prochloraz.33,34 Effects on several reproductive endpoints were 
observed in these mixture studies, including morphologic mal-
formations in male reproductive tract differentiation and dis-
ruption of fetal hormone production.
37.2.2  Pubertal Male Reproductive Tract
Sexual maturation is dependent on the hypothalamus–pitu-
itary–gonadal (HPG) axis not only during fetal development 
but also during puberty. Puberty is initiated with the secretion 
of gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) by the hypothala-
mus, which triggers nocturnal pulsatile secretions of the gonad-
otropins (Luteinizing hormone [LH] and Follicle Stimulating 
Hormone [FSH]) from the pituitary gland.35 Development 
of secondary sexual characteristics, which define the onset 
of phenotypic puberty, occurs when the gonadotropin pulse 
frequency reaches a critical level.35 Secretion of LH induces 
Leydig cell hyperplasia leading to testosterone release, while 
FSH plays a role later during sperm maturation.36
During puberty, the testis cords form into seminiferous 
tubules through the development of lumens and the migration 
of germ cells toward the periphery of the tubule where they 
begin to differentiate into sperm in the process of spermato-
genesis, as described briefly below.23 As the spermatogonia 
proliferate to primary spermatocytes, the blood–testis barrier 
(BTB) develops around postnatal day (PND) 15–20 in rats, as 
an important immunological and xenobiotic barrier to protect 
the post-meiotic spermatocytes from systemic circulation.37,38 
The BTB is formed through tight junctions (TJ) between 
adjacent Sertoli cells lining the seminiferous epithelium. 
Histologically, the peripubertal testis has lower cell numbers, 
fewer elongating spermatids, and smaller tubular diameters in 
comparison to the adult rat.39 Additionally, apoptotic cells are 
a common feature of the peripubertal testis, especially during 
the formation of the BTB barrier and during the first wave 
of spermatogenesis.37,38,40 Spermatozoa can be observed in the 
epididymis around PND 52 in the rat.39,41 The end of puberty 
is marked by the point in which the reproductive organs and 
accessory sex glands have reached an adult state, typically 
around PND 70 (10 weeks old).39,41
The pubertal state is assumed to be particularly susceptible 
to perturbations, mostly as a result of research on endocrine-
disrupting chemicals, due to the complex hormonal events 
required to initiate puberty. This has been demonstrated 
through studies where antiandrogenic fungicides (i.e., vinclo-
zolin, iprodione) administered at the time of puberty results 
in decreases in accessory sex organ weights, Leydig cell 
hypertrophy, and sloughed germ cells in the epididymis (indi-
cating altered spermatogenesis).42,43 Distinguishing potential 
exposure-related histologic lesions in peripubertal animals is a 
challenge, as the greater incidence of abnormal and degenerat-
ing cells in the sexually immature testis can be mistaken as 
a toxicological response. Careful evaluation of the cell types 
affected and use of age-matched controls can aid in the differ-
entiation of these effects, especially in animals that die early 
in subchronic toxicity studies.44
37.2.3  Adult Male Reproductive Tract
The adult testis has two major functions, spermatogenesis 
and the production of androgens, which are highly depen-
dent on the synchronized regulation of interdependent cell 
types (Leydig cells, peritubular myoid cells, Sertoli cells, and 
germ cells). The testis is structurally composed of seminifer-
ous tubules and an interstitial compartment. The seminifer-
ous tubules are tightly coiled tubes encased in peritubular 
myoid cells that are organized into loops that converge at the 
rete testis connecting them to the excurrent duct system. The 
peritubular myoid cells play a role in the movement of fluid 
and sperm through the seminiferous tubule lumen.45 Directly 
adjacent to the seminiferous tubules is the interstitial space, 
which contains testosterone-secreting Leydig cells as well as 
blood and lymphatic vessels. Within the seminiferous tubules 
are the Sertoli cells that form the BTB and provide the nutri-
ents and environment necessary to support spermatogenesis. 
In addition to their supportive role, Sertoli cells phagocytose 
the germ cells undergoing apoptosis as a normal function of 
spermatogenesis and the residual cytoplasm shed during sper-
miogenesis.45 Sertoli cells also secrete the fluid that fills the 
tubular lumen allowing for transport of released sperm to 
the epididymis. Once fully differentiated, Sertoli cells do not 
typically proliferate and upon initiation of spermatogenesis 
the number of Sertoli cells is fixed.45 Despite being fixed in 
number, Sertoli cells are extremely resistant to cell death.26

Structurally, spermatogonia (sperm progenitor cells) line the 
basement membrane of the seminiferous tubule and maintain 
close contact with the Sertoli cells as they move inward toward 
the lumen, while proliferating and differentiating.
As mentioned above, testes are the end-organ component of 
the HPG axis, the endocrine feedback system responsible for 
testosterone synthesis. This highly regulated pathway begins 
with the secretion of GnRH by the hypothalamus, which then 
stimulates the anterior pituitary to secrete LH and FSH.46,47 
Leydig cells have LH receptors that are sensitive to FSH-
induced up-regulation, making them more responsive to LH. 
Upon LH stimulation, the Leydig cells enhance cholesterol 
desmolase activity, which in turn converts cholesterol to preg-
nenolone and ultimately leads to testosterone synthesis and 
secretion.46,47 The production of testosterone by Leydig cells is 
critical for normal spermatogenesis by activating pathways in 
Sertoli cells that result in spermatogonial differentiation.
Spermatogenesis is the synchronized development of the 
germ cells from spermatogonial stem cells into spermatozoa 
that occurs over three phases: the proliferative phase, the mei-
otic phase, and the differentiation phase.45 Germ cells in the 
proliferative stage include undifferentiated and differentiated 
spermatogonia. The undifferentiated spermatogonia either 
divide to renew their populations or become different classes of 
spermatogonia mainly through mitotic divisions. The differen-
tiated spermatogonia (Type B) then divide to become prelep-
totene spermatocytes, which move across the BTB, complete 
S-phase, and enter meiosis.47,48 The spermatocytes then transi-
tion through the leptotene, zygotene, pachytene, and diplotene 
phases of meiosis. By the end of meiosis I, haploid second-
ary spermatocytes have been formed. Meiosis II quickly fol-
lows meiosis I to separate sister chromatids to produce haploid 
round spermatids, which undergo terminal differentiation.48 
The differentiation phase is also known as spermiogenesis. In 
this phase, the spermatids develop a flagellum and an acro-
some, undergo nuclear shaping and condensation, and elimi-
nate the majority of their cytoplasm to become spermatozoa.45 
Spermiation is an active process where the spermatozoa are 
released into the seminiferous tubule lumen following the deg-
radation of specialized cellular junctions.26
Spermatogenesis demonstrates a histologically apparent 
synchrony that can be divided into stages based on the pro-
gression of changes observed in the seminiferous epithelium 
(Table 37.1). These changes are described by cell associations, 
which include the spermatogonia, spermatocytes, and sperma-
tids, but the most readily apparent cell association is the devel-
opment of the acrosome in the spermatids.45 These stages are 
sequentially ordered (in roman numerals) along the length of 
the seminiferous tubule and occur in a wave-like pattern within 
the seminiferous tubule, forming a loop that converges at the 
rete testis. The spermatogenic cycle is the temporal progres-
sion of the stages through development and unlike the number 
of stages that can vary across species, this cycle remains con-
stant within species and strains.47–51 The minimum duration 
of exposure to assess a male reproductive toxicant is listed in 
Table 37.1. It is noted that ICH S5 only requires 14 days of 
exposure for fertility assessment when subchronic studies have 
demonstrated no microscopic testicular effects. When micro-
scopic effects are seen, a 10-week exposure is recommended 
for the fertility assessment.
Following spermiation, the immature spermatozoa are 
then transported by peristaltic contraction through the effer-
ent ducts to the caput epididymis, where they acquire motil-
ity and become capable of fertilization.48,52,53 The epididymis 
is a coiled tube that is divided into three sections (the caput, 
corpus, and cauda) and connects the testis to the vas deferens. 
The prostate gland surrounds the urethra below the bladder 
and functions to store and secrete a white and slightly alka-
line fluid (pH 7.3) to combine with the spermatozoa and other 
secretions during ejaculation. The prostatic secretions com-
prise 25–30% of the ejaculate volume and combine with semi-
nal vesicle secretions to neutralize the acidity of the vaginal 
tract to increase sperm survival and facilitate fertilization.
37.2.4  Aging Adult Male Reproductive Tract
Once sexual maturity is achieved, a male will retain fertility 
with limited changes until testicular injury occurs or there is 
reduced reproductive system function due to age. Unlike with 
fetal development, reproductive system changes as a result of 
age are not genetically programed and encompass both hor-
monal and morphological/cellular changes.54 Characteristic 
morphological changes associated with age include decreased 
Leydig cell numbers (leads to a decrease in testosterone pro-
duction), arteriosclerotic lesions, and thickening of the outer 
layer of the testis (tunica albuginea).55 Additional decreases in 
testosterone are observed in the aging male as a result of a 
gradual decrease in HPG axis function.56 These age-related 
changes are also observed in rodents, with the rat (spe-
cifically the Brown Norway rat) being the preferred model 
organism.57–59 There are a number of reasons why the Brown 
Norway model is preferred for male aging studies, one of 
which is that decreased testosterone production (lower serum 
and intratesticular levels) occurs over a longer period of time 
(from 18 to 30 months old) compared to the Sprague Dawley 
strain (from 21 to 24 months old).57 In the Brown Norway

increase in the postejaculatory interval when compared to 
younger rats.60 As a result of these age-related changes, breeder 
males in fertility studies are typically retired by 10 months of 
age to maintain optimal reproductive performance.
There is evidence that sensitivity to toxicants can be influ-
enced by age. This is exemplified by data showing differ-
ences due to age-related decreases in the number of receptors, 
including the steroid receptor.61 Another factor that plays a role 
in increasing susceptibility are changes in the aged individual 
that impacts the distribution of the chemical, thus influenc-
ing relative toxicity. Decreased plasma albumin is common 
with aging and can lead to an increase in the amount of free 
compound available for tissue distribution. Additionally, the 
elderly have decreased lean body mass (increased percentage 
of adipose tissue and decreased body water) that results in a 
change in distribution kinetics, where lipophilic compounds 
would have a greater volume of distribution.61 Further, metab-
olism and excretion can vary with age, and lower renal func-
tion in older individuals can lead to higher body burdens of 
chemicals over longer periods of time.61,62

37.3.1  Reproductive Organ Weights
The male reproductive organs for which weights may be use-
ful for reproductive risk assessment in adults include the tes-
tes, epididymides, seminal vesicles (with coagulating glands), 
and prostate. Pituitary gland weights can also be included as 
an endpoint; however, the pituitary gland contains cell types 
that are responsible for the regulation of a variety of physi-
ologic functions, including some that are separate from repro-
duction, so changes in pituitary weight may not necessarily 
reflect reproductive impairment. Organ weight data may be 
presented as both absolute weights and as relative weights (i.e., 
organ weight-to-body weight ratios). Organ weight data may 
also be reported relative-to-brain weight because, subsequent 
to development, the weight of the brain usually remains quite 
stable.63 Evaluation of data on absolute organ weights is impor-
tant because a decrease in a reproductive organ weight may be 
quite independent of body weight. Typically, at the lower effec-
tive dose levels of most potent reproductive toxicants, there is 
no change in body weight. Less than severe reductions in adult 
body weight induced by restricted nutrition have shown little 
effect on the male reproductive organs or on male reproductive 
function.64,65 In the presence of severe body weight depression 
(i.e., >10% relative to control), altered reproductive endpoint 
may result from a generalized toxic effect that is not typically 
considered a specific, adverse reproductive outcome.
Normal testis weight varies only modestly within most test 
species, such as the rat.66 This relatively low inter-animal vari-
ability suggests that absolute testis weight should be a precise 
indicator of gonadal injury; however, damage to the testes 
may be detected as a weight change only at doses higher than 
those required to produce significant effects in other measures 
of gonadal status.67–69 This can be a result of several factors, 
including a delay before cell deaths are reflected in a weight 
decrease. The simultaneous occurrence of germ cell death and 
Leydig cell hyperplasia may offset any observed change in tes-
tis weight, and blockage of the efferent ducts by cells sloughed 
from the germinal epithelium or the efferent ducts themselves 
can lead to an increase in testis weight due to fluid accumula-
tion,70,71 again, an effect that could offset the effect of germ cell 
depletion on testis weight. Nonetheless, a significant increase 
or decrease in testis weight in the absence of a comparable 
body weight change is indicative of an adverse effect.
The weights of the epididymis, the prostate, and the semi-
nal vesicles are androgen dependent and may reflect changes 
in the animal’s endocrine status or testicular function when 
exposure to a variety of endocrine-active compounds occurs 
(e.g., antiandrogens, xenoestrogens). Separation of the seminal 
vesicles and coagulating gland is difficult in rodents; however, 
the seminal vesicle and prostate can be separated, and results 
may be reported for these glands separately or together, with 
or without their secretory fluids.
Significant and biologically meaningful changes in absolute 
male reproductive organ weights may constitute an adverse 
reproductive effect, although the entire spectrum of endpoints 
should be considered when making this assessment.
37.3.2  Histopathological Evaluation
Histopathologic evaluations of test animal tissues have a 
prominent role in male reproductive risk assessment.72 With 
proper tissue preparation and analysis, data from histopatho-
logic evaluations provide a relatively sensitive tool that is use-
ful for the detection of low-dose effects. Organs that are often 
evaluated include the testes, epididymides, prostate, and semi-
nal vesicles (often including coagulating glands). Tissues from 
lower dose exposures are often not examined histologically if 
the high dose produced no difference from controls. Changes 
in testis histology provide insights regarding the sites and 
mechanisms of action for the agent on that reproductive organ. 
When similar targets or mechanisms exist in humans, the 
basis for interspecies extrapolation is strengthened. Depending 
on the experimental design, information can also be obtained 
that may allow prediction of the eventual extent of injury and 
degree of recovery in that species and humans.73

preparation, examination, and interpretation of observations 
for normal and high-resolution histology of the germinal epi-
thelium of rats, mice, and dogs is available.47 Also, a decision-
tree scheme for staging rat spermatogenic cycle has been 
published.74 Cell staging is based on the examination of cross 
sections of the seminiferous tubules, where the temporal and 
spatial relationships of the various spermatogenic cell types

excurrent ducts and accessory sex glands, histologic evaluation

secretory epithelium. Evaluation should include information 
on the efferent ducts and caput, corpus, and cauda segments 
of the epididymis. The presence of debris and sloughed cells 
in the epididymal lumen is a valuable indicator of damage to 
the germinal epithelium or the excurrent ducts. The presence 
of lesions such as sperm granulomas, leukocyte infiltration 
(inflammation), or the absence of clear cells in the cauda epi-

Significant and biologically meaningful histopathologic 
damage in excess of the level seen in control tissue of any of 
the male reproductive organs should be considered an adverse 
reproductive effect.
37.3.3  Sperm Evaluations and Mechanistic Endpoints
Data on epididymal (or vas deferens) sperm number, sperm 
morphology, and sperm motility can provide an estimation 
of both sperm quantity and quality and are therefore more 
informative than sperm numbers alone. Decreased sperm pro-
duction will be reflected by reductions in both the number of 
homogenization-resistant spermatids in the testis and the num-
ber of sperm stored in the cauda epididymis. Likewise, altera-
tions in sperm morphology or motility can reflect a testicular 
insult as well as altered maturation in the epididymis. Data on 
sperm quantity and quality can be obtained noninvasively by 
collecting serially timed ejaculates from species such as mon-
keys, dogs, and rabbits, thus enhancing the ability to confirm 
effects seen in rodents and establishing the feasibility to detect 
such effects in humans. Note that epididymal sperm mea-
sures in rodents are relatively consistent among animals of the 
same strain, whereas comparable measures in human semen 
are highly variable both between and within men.82 Brief 
descriptions of these measures in rats are provided below, fol-
lowed by a discussion of various additional mechanistic sperm 
measures that can be useful in a comprehensive male repro-
ductive risk assessment.
37.3.3.1  Sperm Number
In toxicity testing using rats, cauda epididymal sperm are 
enumerated to determine the epididymal sperm reserves. 
Automated counting has improved the efficiency of this end-
point.83 It is not recommended to express the epididymal sperm 
count relative to the weight of the cauda epididymis because 
sperm contribute very little to epididymal weight and ratio 
data can mask declines in sperm number. Thus, absolute sperm 
counts can improve resolution for detecting an effect. As is 
true for ejaculated sperm counts, epididymal sperm counts are 
influenced directly by the level of sexual activity.84 Intra- and 
interindividual variation is often high but is reduced somewhat 
if ejaculates are collected at regular intervals from the same 
male, as can be done with some large animal models.85
An estimate of sperm production in the testis may be derived 
from counts of the distinctive elongated spermatid nuclei that 
remain intact after homogenization of testes in a detergent-
containing medium.84,86,87 These elongated spermatid counts 
are a measure of sperm produced from spermatogonial stem 
cells.88 However, spermatid counts may be misleading if the 
duration of exposure (e.g., 14 days) is shorter than the time 
required for a spermatogonial stem cell to develop into an 
elongated spermatid (59 days in the rat).
37.3.3.2  Sperm Morphology
Sperm morphology refers to structural aspects of sperm. A 
thorough morphologic evaluation identifies abnormalities in 
the sperm head and flagellum. An increase in abnormal sperm 
morphology has been considered evidence that the agent has 
gained access to the germ cells.89 There is evidence that sper-
matogonia are targeted, since the occurrence of some unique 
sperm abnormalities were found to perpetuate long after ces-
sation of exposure to a test chemical (lasting beyond more than 
at least one duration of spermatogenesis).90
The traditional approach to characterizing morphology in 
toxicology testing has relied on subjective categorization of 
sperm head, midpiece, and tail defects in either stained prepa-
rations by bright field microscopy91 or fixed, unstained prepa-
rations by phase contrast microscopy.92 Such approaches may 
be adequate for mice and rats with their distinctly angular head 
shapes. Because human sperm exhibit considerable heteroge-
neity of structure and categorizing normal sperm involves sub-
jectivity, the World Health Organization (WHO) has provided 
consensus guidance on classification of sperm morphology93 
and emphasizes that reference values relating this outcome to 
fertility depend largely on the strictness with which a normal 
sperm is defined. Data that categorize the types of abnormali-
ties observed and quantify the frequencies of their occurrences 
may provide more information in a toxicology or epidemiol-
ogy study than simply quantifying the percentage of normal 
sperm.

37.3.3.3  Sperm Motility
The biochemical environments in the testes and epididymides 
are highly regulated to ensure proper development and matu-
ration of the sperm and the acquisition of critical functional 
characteristics such as progressive motility and the potential 
to fertilize.94 With chemical exposures, perturbation of this 
balance may occur, producing alterations in sperm proper-
ties such as motility. Chemicals (e.g., epichlorohydrin) have 
been identified that selectively affect epididymal sperm motil-
ity and reduce fertility.95 Motility measurements are typically 
obtained on epididymal rat spermatozoa, but sperm recovered 
from the vas deferens can be similarly analyzed.96
Standardized methods are necessary because motility is 
influenced by a number of experimental variables, includ-
ing abstinence interval, method of sample collection and 
handling, elapsed time between sampling and observation, 
the temperature at which the sample is stored and ana-
lyzed, the extent of sperm dilution, the nature of the dilu-
tion medium, and the microscopic chamber employed for the 
observations.92,97–101
Sperm motility can be evaluated in real time under phase 
contrast microscopy, or sperm images can be recorded and 
stored in video or digital format and analyzed later, either man-
ually or by computer-assisted sperm analysis.102–106 For manual 
assessments, the percentage of motile and progressively motile 
sperm can be estimated and a simple scale used to describe the 
vigor of the sperm motion. The application of video and digital 
technology to sperm analysis allows a more detailed and more 
objective evaluation of sperm motion, including information 
about the individual sperm tracks. Using this technology, 
chemically induced alterations in sperm motion have been 
detected,107–109 and such changes have been related to the fer-
tility of the exposed animals.110–112
37.3.3.4  Sperm Mechanistic Endpoints
Exclusion of vital dye remains a standard method for evaluat-
ing sperm viability by assessing sperm membrane integrity. A 
combination of the SYBR-14 dye and propidium iodide (PI) 
seems to be particularly effective.113 PI is impermeant and can 
bind to sperm DNA only if the cell membrane is compromised. 
SYBR-14 is a permeant dye that can bind DNA when the sperm 
membrane is intact. For simultaneous evaluation of both via-
bility and a specific membrane protein, a fixable impermeant 
live/dead stain can be used. These dyes will result in intracel-
lular staining wherever the membrane is compromised.
The ability of sperm to fertilize an egg does not guarantee 
fertility. The sperm genome must also be intact for embry-
onic/fetal development to proceed normally. A variety of 
assays for DNA and chromosome damage in sperm have been 
used in human epidemiology and rodent toxicology studies. 
Of these, the sperm chromatin structure assay has been most 
widely used.114,115 This assay is based on the properties of the 
fluorescent, metachromatic dye acridine orange, which fluo-
resces green when intercalated into normal double-stranded 
DNA but red when bound to denatured DNA (or RNA). Other 
tests for detecting DNA damage in human and rodent sperm, 
including the comet assay, the Terminal deoxynucleotidyl 
transferase (TdT) dUTP Nick-End Labeling (TUNEL) assay, 
and measurement of oxidative DNA adduct sites, may be use-
ful mechanistic tools to evaluate the effects of environmental 
contaminants on the genetic integrity of sperm.116
Another direct indicator of sperm function is the ability to 
undergo the acrosome reaction after in vitro incubation under 
conditions that support sperm capacitation. Fluorescent probes 
can be used to label the acrosome and permit determination of 
acrosomal status; for example, the plant lectin, Pisum sativum 
agglutinin (peanut agglutinin, PNA), can be used to identify 
acrosome-intact sperm.117 In combination with viability stains, 
live acrosome-reacted sperm can be distinguished from live 
acrosome-intact sperm and from dead sperm that have shed 
their acrosomes and the percentages of each type quantified 
using flow cytometry.
37.3.4  Functional Fertility Assessments
Mating trials have traditionally been a central endpoint for 
determining adverse effects of chemicals and drugs on fertil-
ity in animal experiments, either in single or multiple genera-
tion studies. In fertility testing, pairs of animals are cohabited 
for a period of time sufficient to ensure conception, generally 
2 weeks for rats. Females are evaluated daily for evidence of 
mating as determined by presence of a vaginal plug or sperm 
in a vaginal lavage sample, after which the animals are sepa-
rated. The ability to sire a litter is then determined by allow-
ing the females to deliver or via intrauterine examination. 
Endpoints typically measured are time to mating, number of 
males mated, number of males producing a litter, and number 
of offspring, implantation sites and/or viable embryos pro-
duced, depending on the female terminal endpoint.
While mating trials are a routine part of a fertility assess-
ment, it is questionable whether they provide meaningful 
information to the risk assessment as far as identifying adverse 
effects of chemicals and drugs on male fertility. One of the 
primary concerns with mating trials is the relative insensitivity 
of mating outcomes relative to changes in sperm parameters. 
Studies in rats and mice have shown that reductions of up to 
90% in number of normal sperm may occur without a statis-
tically significant effect on fertility.88,118,119 Histopathology is 
generally considered the most sensitive indicator of toxicity 
to male reproduction and therefore is the most likely endpoint 
to define the NOAEL.120 Consistent with this, fertility, indi-
cated by the number of implantations and pregnancies, less 
frequently showed a response at the lowest dose and was less 
frequently affected across all dose levels than histopathology, 
organ weights, or sperm endpoints.121
Considering the higher sensitivity of other endpoints in male 
reproduction studies, the usefulness of including mating end-
points is uncertain. One argument for the inclusion of mating 
endpoints is that it provides for an assessment of effects to sex-
ual function and behavior. There is some evidence that com-
pounds which disrupt sexual behavior in animals have similar 
effects in humans.122 However, unlike mating rats in a labora-
tory setting where the physiology of the sexual response can 
be tightly controlled and monitored, human sexual behavior is 
a complex mix of physiological and social interaction such that 
predicting impacts to human sexual behavior from rat fertility

male fertility assessments for both drugs and chemicals, it is 
unlikely that mating endpoints will provide meaningful infor-

tion available from other endpoints such as reproductive organ 
weights, histopathology, and sperm parameters.
37.4  Testing Protocols
Along with the guidelines published by the relevant regula-
tory authorities for chemicals and pharmaceuticals, detailed 
overviews of the regulatory history and study designs have 
been published.124,125 Therefore, the purpose of this section is 
to provide an overview of the study designs to illustrate the 
differences in approaches to male reproductive toxicity testing 
for pharmaceuticals, industrial chemicals, and pesticides.
37.4.1  Pharmaceuticals
The primary ICH guidelines for potential effects of new drug 
candidates on male fertility is the ICH S5(R3) guideline on 
reproductive toxicology.126 Since adoption of the initial ICH 
S5 guideline, there has not been a high-profile human tragedy 
where nonclinical male fertility testing of a pharmaceutical 
was proven deficient. This is likely due to the stepwise pro-
cess of pharmaceutical development that involves evaluation 
of both animal and human efficacy and safety rather than 
any unique attributes of the male fertility study. Required 
studies for pharmaceutical development usually include gen-
eral toxicity studies in two species (one rodent and one non-
rodent), reproduction toxicity studies, genotoxicity studies, 
and studies for assessment of carcinogenic potential. Other 
studies conducted on a case-by-case basis include phototox-
icity, immunotoxicity, juvenile animal toxicity, and abuse 
liability.127 Detection of toxicity to reproduction for human 
pharmaceuticals begins early in the safety testing paradigm 
during the conduct of repeat-dose (subchronic) toxicity stud-
ies. These studies, conducted in two species, involve detailed 
histopathologic evaluation of the male reproductive organs 
and accessory sex glands. According to the ICH M3 guid-
ance, human male subjects may be included in Phase 1 and 
Phase 2 clinical trials without having conducted male fer-
tility studies in animals, but an assessment of male fertility 
should be included prior to large scale or long duration clini-
cal trials (e.g., Phase 3). The rationale for this approach is 
that the most sensitive endpoint for detecting effects on male 
fertility is generally considered to be histopathology of the 
male reproductive and hormone responsive tissues, while fer-
tility is a rather insensitive endpoint.119,128,129 Supporting this 
concept was an analysis conducted by Ulbrich and Palmer 
showing that after ∈2 weeks of dosing the combination of 
organ weights and histopathology detected 94–100% of male 
reproductive toxicants compared to 83–91% for mating trials 
only.129
The ICH S5 male fertility study design has been described 
in detail,130 with only minor changes in the recent R3 revision. 
Provided there is no evidence of an effect on male reproduc-
tive organs in general toxicity studies, the guidance requires 
that males are dosed for at least 2 weeks before being paired 
with female animals, based on studies showing that this dura-
tion of treatment is sufficient to detect male reproductive 
toxicity.121,131,132 In general, the majority of male reproductive 
toxicants affect post-meiotic germ cells, and thus direct effects 
on spermatogenesis, mating behavior, and fertility are likely 
to be detected following 2–4 weeks of treatment.133 If effects 
on male reproductive organs and/or histopathology were noted 
in general toxicology studies, a 10-week pre-mating dosing 
period prior to mating is required.
Following the pre-mating dosing period, male and female 
animals are cohabited (1:1) typically for 2–3 weeks and the 
number of days to mating is recorded. The female animals are 
euthanized at mid-gestation and the ovaries and uterine con-
tents are examined. Fertility and implantation indices are cal-
culated and compared between the treated and control groups. 
Males are necropsied and subjected to a gross examination. 
Optional endpoints include male reproductive organ weights 
and microscopic examination of the reproductive organs, but 
as these endpoints have generally been evaluated in longer 
duration general toxicity studies, the utility of collecting this 
information in male fertility studies is limited. Sperm evalua-
tion is also optional and may provide meaningful information, 
especially in cases where organ weights or histopathological 
changes were noted in general toxicity studies.
The potential for adverse effects on development of the 
male reproductive system is evaluated in the pre- and post-
natal development (PPND) study (ICH S5(R3)).126 In the 
PPND study, pregnant females are dosed from implantation 
(~gestation day 6 in rats) and dosing continues until weaning 
(~PND 21 in rats). The dams are allowed to deliver and rear 
their offspring to weaning. A subset of offspring is selected 
for evaluation of sexual maturation (determined by age of 
balanopreputial separation) and for reproductive competency 
as adults by determining their ability to mate and produce 
offspring.
As determined by the context of clinical use, the safety 
profile based on existing toxicology data, and the intended 
pharmacology, studies in juvenile animals are conducted on 
a case-by-case basis in accordance with ICH S11 guidance.134 
A juvenile animal study, when needed, consists of a stan-
dard battery of core endpoints that, in regard to male fertility 
assessments, include sexual development and histopathology 
of the testis. When the male reproductive system is identified 
as a specific concern, suggested additional endpoints include 
expansion of histopathology to include reproductive and endo-
crine tissues, and sperm analysis. Mating assessments are gen-
erally not recommended due to their low sensitivity.
Additional considerations are provided for development of 
drugs for advanced cancer indications135 and for biotechnol-
ogy-derived pharmaceuticals.136 The exception allowed for 
advanced cancer indications is that an understanding of the 
potential impact of the pharmacology on testicular function, 
along with histopathology from repeat-dose toxicity studies, 
should provide sufficient information to allow for identifica-
tion of risk to male reproduction. As mating studies are not 
practical for biotechnology-derived drugs where nonhuman

37.4.2  Industrial Chemicals and Pesticides
In the early 1980s, the two-generation reproduction toxic-
ity study (USEPA 870.3800; OECD Test Guideline 416) was 
introduced as the globally agreed standard test protocol for 
assessing potential adverse effects of industrial substances 
and pesticides on fertility, reproduction, and postnatal devel-
opment. In general, multigenerational studies are designed to 
assess the effects of long-term exposure to ambient low levels 
of industrial chemicals in the workplace, or to low-level pesti-
cides or food additives in the diet.125 Specifically, these studies 
encompass the reproductive life cycle from preconception, in 
utero, pre- and postnatal development, puberty, sexual matu-
rity, gestation, and onward to the next generation.
Briefly, in a two-generation reproduction study, parental (P) 
animals are exposed to the test substance for 10 weeks prior to 
mating and continuing through lactation until weaning of the 
first-generation offspring (F1). After weaning, F1 offspring are 
directly exposed to the test substance until adulthood (at least 
10 weeks) at which time F1 offspring are mated. Exposure of 
F1 females continues through gestation and lactation, such that 
second-generation offspring (F2) are potentially exposed in 
utero and via milk until PND 21. Thus, males in the P genera-
tion are exposed from puberty through adulthood; F1 males 
are potentially exposed in utero and via milk, and then directly 
exposed from weaning (typically PND 21) until adulthood; 
and F2 males are potentially exposed in utero and via milk. A 
unique attribute of this study is that it encompasses the entire 
reproductive cycle in the F1 generation thereby providing the 
potential to detect effects on male reproduction at any time in 
the reproductive cycle.
Subsequent retrospective analysis of two-generation 
studies indicated that the F1 mating and production of a 
F2 generation provided little value to the reproductive risk 
assessment.139,140 This retrospective evaluation led to the 
issuance of OECD 443,141 an extended one-generation repro-
ductive toxicity study (EOGRTS) that is now the required 
study for reproductive toxicity assessment under REACH 
(Registration, Evaluation, Authorisation and Restriction of 
Chemicals).142 The EOGRTS was first proposed in 2006143 
and the study design underwent several modifications before 
being adopted by the OECD as Guideline 443 in July 2011 
(OECD 443) and this study has generally been accepted as 
the standard approach to assessing male reproductive toxicity 
for industrial chemicals and pesticides.144 Compared to the 
two-generation reproductive toxicity study, which requires 
F1 mating to produce a F2 generation, in the EOGRTS 
design, the F1 generation is followed until adulthood, but the 
subsequent mating of F1 offspring to produce a F2 generation 
is triggered based on existing information or data collected 
within the study. With this modification, the EOGRTS has 
the potential to reduce the number of rats required by nearly 
1,200 animals (Σ40% of animals) per study.140 In addition to 
evaluating reproductive endpoints in F1 offspring (Cohort 1), 
in the EOGRTS study, additional cohorts of F1 offspring can 
be assigned for evaluation of neuropathological and behav-
ioral endpoints (Cohort 2; developmental neurotoxicity), and 
immune function (Cohort 3; developmental immunotoxic-
ity). The EOGRTS study design often varies depending on 
the requirements set by different regulatory authorities and 
can include alterations beyond the OECD TG 443 such as 
a 10-week pre-mating exposure period for the parental ani-
mals, or addition of mating of a second generation indepen-
dent of within study triggers.
Male reproductive endpoints are similar in both the 
EOGRTS and two-generation studies. Parental animals are 
dosed for 2 weeks prior to cohabitation. In the EOGRTS and 
two-generation studies, time to mating and mating success are 
recorded. The females are allowed to deliver and rear their 
offspring. Dosing of parental males should continue until F1 
offspring are weaned (total dosing duration of ~8 weeks). At 
weaning, the parental males are euthanized and clinical bio-
chemistry and hematology samples are evaluated in a ran-
domly selected subset of animals. Sperm counts, motility, 
and morphology are measured. Male reproductive tract organ 
weights (testes, epididymides, prostate, and seminal vesicles 
with coagulating glands) are collected along with other major 
organ (e.g., brain, liver, heart, thymus). Other hormone respon-
sive tissues, including the vas deferens and mammary glands, 
are retained. Full histopathology of the organs weighed and/
or collected is performed. While most testicular toxicity can 
occur with no change in hormone levels, one possible mecha-
nism could be a hormonal change. However, given the inher-
ent variability in hormone levels, hormonal imbalances are 
often identified by morphologic changes in hormone-sensitive 
tissues.138 One often overlooked such tissue included in the 
EOGRTS is the male mammary gland, which is sensitive to a 
number of disruptions in hormone physiology in rats but not in 
other laboratory species.138,145
At weaning (PND 21), 20 animals/sex/group are assigned 
to Cohort 1A, and an additional 20 animals/sex/group are 
assigned to Cohort 1B. Cohort 1A is intended as the primary 
group of animals used for assessment of effects upon repro-
ductive system. Cohort 1B is the “triggered” group intended 
for follow-up assessment of reproduction by mating of F1 ani-
mals and to obtain additional histopathology assessments if 
necessary. Direct dosing of the F1 offspring commences at 
weaning and continues until scheduled necropsy. Anogenital 
distance is measured at least once between PND 0 and 4, 
nipple retention in male pups, an indicator of antiandrogenic 
activity, is evaluated on PND 12 or 13, and the age at bala-
nopreputial separation, a pubertal landmark associated with 
normal development, is evaluated. Evaluation of clinical bio-
chemistry/hematology, organ weights, sperm parameters, and 
histopathology for F1 males are the same as described above 
for the parental males.

37.4.3  Other Nonclinical Studies
As mentioned previously, subchronic toxicity studies may be 
of special value to the design and interpretation of male fertil-
ity studies. The endpoints evaluated for the male reproductive 
system include visual examination of the reproductive organs 
at necropsy, plus weights and histopathology for the testes, 
epididymides, and accessory sex glands. The feasibility and 
value of incorporating some basic measures of male reproduc-
tive toxicity into 13-week studies has been discussed for both 
industrial chemicals and pharmaceuticals, the latter included 
a functional fertility assessment.118,146 The authors concluded 
that while it may be useful to identify an agent as a potential 
reproductive hazard, without adding the mating component, 
the subchronic study lacks information about the integrated 
function of the reproductive system (sexual behavior, fertility, 
and pregnancy outcomes); and, in the case of industrial chemi-
cals and pesticides, it does not include effects of the test article 
on immature animals.
The OECD 421 reproductive toxicity screen uses less ani-
mals than the definitive male fertility study designs described 
earlier, but it does allow for mating assessment and evaluation 
of offspring parameters such as anogenital distance and male 
nipple retention.147 Although limited in sample size, histopa-
thology of reproductive organs is included as an endpoint, but 
not assessments of spermatogenesis. The OECD 422 study is 
the OECD 421 study combined with a 28-day repeat-dose tox-
icity study.148,149 The value of these study designs is to provide 
reproduction data with a relatively limited number of animals; 
however, the assessment is not as robust as that achieved with 
the larger studies.
The dominant lethal test is intended to detect mutagenic 
effects in the sperm cells, for which the most likely outcome 
is lethality to the embryo or fetus.150 Males are exposed to the 
test article for a short duration of 1–5 consecutive days fol-
lowed by sequential mating trials that correspond to various 
stages of spermatogenesis, with the number of mating periods 
scheduled corresponding to at least one spermatogenesis cycle. 
Females are monitored for evidence of mating, sacrificed at 
approximately mid-gestation, and examined for incidence of 
pre- and/or post-implantation loss. The acute exposure pro-
tocol of the standard dominant lethal test, combined with 
serial mating, may allow identification of the spermatogenic 
cell types that are affected. Use of the dominant lethal assay 
has seen a decline, primarily due to attempts to reduce animal 
usage, but also due to acceptance of the suitability of genotox-
icity screening batteries to detect germ cell mutagenesis.
Another protocol that provides a more sensitive evaluation 
of subfertility is the Reproductive Assessment by Continuous 
Breeding protocol conducted in mice or rats.151 In this study 
design, mating pairs are cohabitated for 98 days with continu-
ous exposure. Litters are examined and removed shortly after 
birth. With this approach, each pair may deliver up to five lit-
ters in the time period designated, and the average number 
of litters per pair provides an index of fertility or subfertility. 
While more sensitive than single mating trials used in defini-
tive male fertility assessments, given that histopathology is the 
most sensitive endpoint, the value of the continuous breeding 
study design is limited.
37.4.4  Study Design Considerations
The rat is generally appropriate for male reproductive toxicity 
testing and is the most commonly used species for reasons of 
practicality and availability of background data. For pharma-
ceuticals, there also should be a general knowledge of phar-
macology in the selected species. For instance, when the drug 
is not pharmacologically active in the rat but is pharmacologi-
cally active in the mouse, then the mouse would be selected for 
reproductive toxicity testing.
Both the EOGRTS and ICH S5(R3) male reproduction stud-
ies should be conducted in accordance with Good Laboratory 
Practice (GLP) regulations. Both studies typically include a 
control and three test article dose groups. Group sizes should 
contain a sufficient number of mating pairs to yield at least 20 
pregnant females per dose group for an EOGRTS and 16–20 
liters per dose group for ICH fertility studies.
Dose level selection in both situations is most commonly 
based on toxic effects. The maximum dose should induce 
some systemic toxicity, but not death or severe toxicity. For 
industrial chemicals and pesticides, a descending sequence of 
dose levels should be selected with the objective of identify-
ing a NOAEL that will allow for derivation of a benchmark 
dose. Two- or four-fold intervals are considered optimal for an 
EOGRTS, and it is recommended that rather than using a large 
multiple (e.g., >10-fold) adding a 4th dose group is preferable. 
For pharmaceuticals, in addition to setting the maximum dose 
based on toxicity, doses producing >25-fold the exposure at 
the human maximum recommend high dose (MRHD) are 
generally considered acceptable as a maximum dose. Both 
guidelines provide for a limit dose of 1,000 mg/kg/day if a 
dose-limiting toxicity is not achieved.
Toxicokinetic (TK) data are not required in an ICH S5(R3) 
male fertility study as this data is generated in repeat-dose tox-
icity studies. TK data also are not required in the EOGRTS 
study; however, the OECD 443 guideline does stress that TK 
data from previously completed studies are extremely useful 
in planning the EOGRTS, selection of dose levels, and inter-
pretation. The OECD 443 guideline allows for collection of 
TK data within the EOGRTS if this does not interfere with the 
collection of the main study endpoints.
Selection of the route of administration should be the most 
relevant for human exposure. For pharmaceuticals, this is the 
route of administration planned for the marketed drug prod-
uct, and the most common route of administration in nonclini-
cal studies is therefore oral gavage. For industrial chemicals 
and pesticides, the most common route of administration is 
through the diet, although in certain situations, other routes of 
administration (drinking water, gavage, inhalation, and der-
mal) may be more appropriate depending on the characteris-
tics of the compound and the information required.
37.5  Tools for Follow-Up Mechanistic Assessments
The following discussion highlights in vitro and in vivo meth-
ods used in male reproductive toxicology. In vitro methods 
have been proposed with two goals in mind: either as screens 
that would assist in prioritizing chemicals for in vivo testing or

1880
Hayes’ Principles and Methods of Toxicology
as specific tests for use in elucidating modes of toxicant action. 
For the latter purpose, it is important to consider linkage to a 
specific tissue or cell type that has been demonstrated to be 
altered by in vivo toxicant exposure. Even if appropriate pre-
liminary in vivo data are obtained, one may fail to demonstrate 
an effect in vitro, or vice versa. This may reflect the need for 
a toxicant to interact in vivo with another tissue or cell type 
or to form an active metabolite; thus, it is advisable to ascer-
tain both the metabolic fate and dosimetry of a toxicant and 
metabolites within the putative target tissue.
Today, given the rising practical and ethical concerns asso-
ciated with the use of large numbers of animals for research 
and the growing number of environmental chemicals requir-
ing risk assessment, there is an increased emphasis on the use 
of cell lines for screening potential reproductive toxicants. 
Progress in this direction has been limited because cell lines 
frequently fail to retain specific aspects of normal differenti-
ated function as discussed below for cell lines used to screen 
for steroidogenic compromise. It is also important to recog-
nize that a chemical that tests negative in a cell line screen 
may actually test positive in a primary cell culture or in vivo 
system and vice versa, a chemical could test positive in an in 
vitro assay, but have no response in vivo.
37.5.1  Seminiferous Tubule Culture
While significant progress has been made in the development 
of an in vitro model of spermatogenesis, the majority of the 
methods do not have the capacity to support more than just a 
few steps of spermatogenesis. Most models allow for the dif-
ferentiation of spermatogonia into spermatozoa, but few can 
progress through meiosis, and even fewer models can pro-
duce spermatozoa. Additionally, many of these models lack 
the consistency necessary to use as a screen for reproductive 
toxicity in a regulatory study. However, there is still a consid-
erable need for in vitro models with which to explore potential 
mechanisms of toxicity and there are several promising mod-
els under development.
The cell–cell interactions that occur normally within the 
seminiferous tubule, particularly the interactions between 
Sertoli cells and germ cells,152 justify the use of in vitro 
models in which the whole seminiferous tubule is studied. 
Seminiferous tubule cultures from rats have been used to 
study the in vitro effects of 1,3-dinitrobenzene and methoxy-
acetic acid (MAA). These agents are toxic to Sertoli cells 
and pachytene spermatocytes, respectively.153 However, the 
amount of inhibin, a Sertoli cell product, secreted by the sem-
iniferous tubules in vitro was significantly increased by both 
compounds. More importantly, the stimulation of inhibin 
secretion was seen in rats exposed in vivo to doses result-
ing in intratesticular toxicant concentrations that approxi-
mated the effective in vitro concentrations. The seminiferous 
tubule culture reported by Allenby et al.153 formed the basis 
of a series of elegant studies designed to determine the role 
germ cells play in the expression of androgen-dependent 
protein secretion.154,155 Seminiferous tubules were isolated 4, 
18, and 30 days following exposure to MAA when pachytene 
spermatocytes, round spermatids, and elongating spermatids, 
respectively, would be selectively depleted. Specific proteins 
were secreted selectively by these different germ cells. The 
effects of androgen depletion were then assessed 4 days fol-
lowing exposure to ethane dimethanesulfonate (EDS), with 
or without exogenous androgen supplementation. Together, 
these studies present evidence that specific androgen-depen-
dent proteins are synthesized by seminiferous tubules when 
different germ cell types are present. This may explain why it 
has been impossible to demonstrate androgen-dependent pro-
teins in isolated Sertoli cell cultures. It is clear from this work 
that isolated seminiferous tubules can be used to identify in 
vitro effects on the seminiferous epithelium if a toxicant dis-
rupts spermatogenesis in vivo.
In the study by Allenby153 mentioned previously, a com-
parison was made between the response of optimized cul-
tures of isolated seminiferous tubules and cultures of isolated 
Sertoli cells. The results clearly showed that inhibin produc-
tion by isolated Sertoli cells was smaller and more variable 
in response to both toxicants. This may be attributed to the 
fact that the isolated Sertoli cells were derived from immature 
Sertoli cells. It would be useful to know whether the response 
of Sertoli cells from adult rats would better approximate the 
response of the isolated seminiferous tubules. Immature cells 
have been used in most of the Sertoli cell culture/toxicologi-
cal studies to date, although the effects of various phthalate 
esters have been compared in cultures of Sertoli cells obtained 
from both immature and young adult rats.156 Because differ-
entiated function (i.e., transferrin, androgen-binding protein, 
inhibin secretion) of the Sertoli cell changes with sexual 
maturity,157,158 an in vitro study should use Sertoli cells that 
are at the same ontogenic stage as those affected in the in vivo 
studies. This is important if in vitro data are to be useful in 
risk assessment. Advances in culturing adult Sertoli cells may 
provide promising in vitro models for male reproductive toxi-
cology research.159,160 For this and any system using testicular 
cells161 or combinations of cells in vitro, it will be important to 
demonstrate the extent to which differentiated endpoints are 
maintained and can be evaluated following both in vivo and 
in vitro exposures.
The use of cell lines representing cells in a less differenti-
ated state holds more promise for screening, particularly as 
it applies to perturbation in development of the male repro-
ductive system. Testicular cells from neonatal rats and novel 
approaches for culturing cells on matrices have been used 
recently to generate Sertoli cell–germ cell cocultures.162 In 
theory, these undifferentiated cells should be maintained well 
in culture and be capable of responding to developmental male 
reproductive toxicants in a manner similar to that observed 
in vivo. Spermatogenesis has been reportedly maintained in 
an in vitro organ culture system.163 In this system, immature 
testis fragments were used and spermatogenesis proceeded 
through meiosis to round spermatid development. After mul-
tiple refinements, the same laboratory was able to demonstrate 
that functional sperm could be produced by these cultures.164 
This approach is valuable because it offers a primary endpoint 
(histopathology of spermatogenesis) that is directly translat-
able to nonclinical safety studies. However, despite efforts to 
optimize this model as a screen for spermatogenic toxicity, 
the model could not consistently produce enough germ cells 
in control explants.165 Aside from the difficulty in maintaining

consistency, further challenges will be to demonstrate whether 
functional (i.e., cell–cell interactions) and molecular (i.e., 
transcriptional and proteomic) alterations observed following 
chemical exposure in vitro can be evaluated in neonatal Sertoli 
cells and spermatogonia following in vivo insult.
Advances in stem cell biology may lead to other approaches, 
including the use of pluripotent spermatogonial stem cells166 
at different stages of differentiation. Progress in this area has 
been made, with the ability to produce and detect DNA dam-
age, reactive oxygen species formation, and apoptosis in mouse 
spermatogonial stem cells exposed in vitro to hydroxyurea.167 
These findings recapitulated the reproductive toxicity observed 
in concurrent in vivo mice studies. Likewise, advances in bio-
engineering may produce novel systems wherein testis devel-
opment can be reenacted and studied with respect to toxicant 
response. For example, dissociated immature testis cells from 
rats were recently reported to develop into structures resem-
bling immature seminiferous tubules when they were grown 
in an extracellular matrix gel and then xenografted into a 
rat. Encouragingly, the xenografts became vascularized and 
the tubular structures exhibited lumena and a few putative 
spermatogonia, as well as an interstitium-containing putative 
Leydig cells.168 Further development of the xenograft model 
has involved the transplantation of human fetal testis from 
pregnancy losses into the renal subcapsular space of athymic 
castrated nude mice to directly study the effects of antiandro-
genic agents (di-butyl phthalate [DBP] and abiraterone) on 
human testis development and function.169 Use of this model 
generated additional evidence that the human fetal testis is 
not as sensitive to DBP exposure as rodents exposed via the 
same model, but antiandrogenic effects are observed with abi-
raterone exposure in the form of reduced testosterone levels. 
Although there are limitations with the use of this model given 
the use of human tissue and the amount of time and effort 
involved, it does provide a unique mechanistic opportunity to 
directly study the effects of chemicals on human fetal testis 
development and function.
Many groups have attempted to model the testis in vitro 
through culturing cells and small fragments of tissue in sup-
portive 3D systems. These methods include the hanging-drop 
method, where a small piece of testis is suspended in a small 
drop of media placed on the lid of a petri dish170–172 as well as 
attempts to culture testicular cells within a 3D support matrix 
to allow for cell-to-cell interactions.173 Unfortunately, little 
or no germ cell differentiation has been reported from these 
3D organoid models. Another potential model that has been 
gaining traction is the application of organ-on-a-chip tech-
nology to generate a microfluidic testis-on-a-chip that allows 
for the evaluation of chemical effects on human testis cells.173 
Although in the early stages of development, if successful, 
testis-on-a-chip models would be a useful screening tool for 
male reproductive toxicity.
37.5.2  Epididymal Cells
Because the epididymis is the organ that confers fertilizing 
ability on maturing sperm, it is important to develop meth-
ods that identify epididymal toxicity following in vivo expo-
sure and confirm any direct toxicant action on the epididymis 
following in vitro exposure. To establish direct toxicant action, 
a culture system must be used that is capable of maintaining 
facets of normal epididymal sperm maturation. This means 
that the culture system must (1) preserve the morphological 
integrity of the epididymal epithelial cells, that is, TJ between 
adjacent cells and polarized ultrastructure and (2) synthesize 
and secrete proteins in an androgen-dependent manner. Few 
in vitro models meet these criteria despite several epididy-
mal cell lines having been developed. Immortalized epithe-
lial cells from the caput epididymides of the mouse achieved 
polarized features and, based on expression of specific genes, 
retained some degree of differentiation but failed to express 
AR and other key markers of epididymal epithelial cell func-
tion.174 Another immortalized epididymal epithelial cell line 
was shown to exhibit polarity, form TJ between adjacent 
cells, and express the AR but was not androgen responsive.175 
Epididymal epithelial cells from both prepubertal and adult 
rats have been cultured and maintained normal in vivo expres-
sion of both AR and ERs but these cells were not contiguous 
and displayed no polarity.176
One in vitro model that could meet the culture system crite-
ria is the development of a 3D organoid with epididymal cells. 
Less progress has been made on the epididymis models than 
compared to the testis. However, one group has developed 3D 
organoids using primary human caput epididymis epithelial 
cells to study the role of Cystic Fibrosis (CF) transmembrane 
conductance regulator (CFTR) in tight-junction maintenance in 
the human epididymis.177 Although this model was developed 
to study CFTR protein function, it shows promise for applica-
tion as a toxicity screening assay for epididymal toxicity.
37.5.3  Assays to Identify Endocrine-
Disrupting Compounds (EDCs)
The U.S. EPA’s Endocrine Disruption Screening Program 
(EDSP) has developed a mode-of-action tiered screening 
approach to assess endocrine-active compounds for commod-
ity chemicals which have less safety testing than agricultural 
products and drugs.6,178–181 These EDSP Tier 1/2 assays can 
be used to characterize adverse outcome pathways (AOPs) for 
endocrine-active compounds,179 as well as to identify/confirm 
potential endocrine mechanisms of action. The first tier of 
assays screens chemicals for potential activity in estrogen, 
androgen, and thyroid pathways (Table 37.2). The battery of 11 
complementary assays includes five in vitro assays and six in 
vivo assays. For chemicals determined to have potential endo-
crine activity, four longer-term, Tier 2 tests were proposed to 
characterize dose–response relationships and adverse effects. 
These tests were envisioned to be used to screen and test for 
potential EDCs but due to their cost, time, and animal use, a 
shift toward exposure assessment, computational approaches, 
and high-throughput screening (HTS) is being assessed under 
a program called Endocrine Disruption Screening Program for 
the 21st Century (EDSP21).178,181 While the Tier 1 and 2 tests 
may not be used widely for screening, they provide valuable 
tools for identifying possible endocrine mode-of-action when 
testis toxicity is observed and an endocrine mechanism is sus-
pected. These points will be highlighted below in understand-
ing effects on the testis (e.g., fertility, Leydig cells).

37.5.3.1  Tier 1 In Vivo and In Vitro Screens
The Tier 1 in vivo tests to screen for alterations in male 
reproductive development in the rat are the Hershberger and 
pubertal assays.182 The Hershberger assay attempts to identify 
androgenic or antiandrogen activity of a compound. Young 
castrated (removal of the testis and epididymis at 6 weeks 
old – Table 37.2, 890–1400) male rats are used to increase the 
sensitivity for detecting a change in androgen status in sex 
accessory organs with the rationale that castration establishes 
low endogenous androgen levels. One week after castration, 
treatment groups are assigned on the 49th day and animals 
are necropsied between 60 and 70 days of age. Thus, there is 
a 10-day exposure period. Androgen-dependent sex accessory 
organs (glans penis, the levator ani-bulbocavernosus muscle, 
ventral prostate, seminal vesicles with coagulating glands, 
and Cowper’s glands) are weighed following exposure to the 
putative endocrine-active compound in groups of animals 
without or with concurrent exogenous androgen supplementa-
tion. Testosterone propionate is given daily during exposure 
when antiandrogenic activity is tested.183 It should be noted 
that this assay could be performed equally well if castration 
involved removal of only the testis. In this case, the epididymis 
would also be weighed. The AR and ER assays described in 
Table 37.2 can provide additional insight as to the mode-of-
action if a signal is seen in the Hershberger assay.
The pubertal male assay is similar in concept, except dos-
ing begins just after weaning, on PND 23, and continues until 
PND 53.184 Two dose groups are required in addition to the 
vehicle control group. This assay is capable of detecting altera-
tions induced by a variety of toxicants including estrogenic 
chemicals, chemicals that affect steroid hormone biosynthesis 
directly, and chemicals that alter the hormonal status of the 
hypothalamic/pituitary axis.185–189 In addition to sex acces-
sory organs weights (e.g., prostate, epididymis), various serum 
hormones (e.g., FSH, LH, testosterone) are measured, and 
histology of the testis and epididymis is evaluated. Onset of 
puberty as determined by balanopreputial separation is also 
evaluated in this assay.
Nonmammalian assays are also used to screen for endo-
crine-active chemicals and focus on agonistic/antagonistic 
activities of androgenic and estrogenic pathways. The fish 
short-term assay190 (Table 37.2, 890–1350) measures many 
endpoints common to mammalian reproduction, evaluates 
both sexes simultaneously, and assesses fertility. Briefly, four 
female and two male fish, typically fathead minnows, are 
housed in a flow-through water tank containing one of three 
test concentrations or a vehicle control. Exposures continue 
for 21 days at which time fish are evaluated for alterations in 
gonadal histology, hormones, and secondary sex characteris-
tics; fertility is assessed throughout the exposure period.
There are five primary assays to screen for chemicals that 
bind or bind and activate ER, bind the AR, or affect steroido-
genesis (STR) by altering the steroidogenic enzymes, includ-
ing aromatase (Table 37.2). Competitive binding assays are 
used to ascertain whether a test chemical has significant affin-
ity for either the androgen or ER in vitro (Table A, 890–1150, 
890–1250). A positive receptor binding test can be confirmed 
in transcriptional assays for that gene. Cells are transfected 
with a plasmid containing a hormone response element and 
coupled to a reporter gene such as luciferase. Often, the cells 
used in these assays have no endogenous steroid receptor, so a 
vector coding for the steroid receptor is also transfected. Both 
androgen-191 and estrogen-192,193 based assays have been devel-
oped. The transcriptional activation assays, both mammalian 
cell and yeast based, have proven less standardized than recep-
tor binding assays,194 and neither assay is particularly well 
suited for testing chemicals requiring metabolic activation.

37.5.3.2  Hypothesis-based Testing
Most of the EDC research published today represents hypothe-
sis-based testing that is presented within AOPs to characterize 
and organize data sets.179 For example, if a chemical is known 
to alter STR in an adult animal or in an in vitro screening 
assay, one might consider a test to evaluate the potential for 
this chemical to alter testosterone throughout reproductive 
development. In this case, exposures would continue in utero, 
through puberty, and into adulthood. At the end of gestation, 
during the pubertal period, and as adults, the exposed males 
would be evaluated. This approach led to dozens of studies 
with phthalates. The early work established that phthalates 
were capable of significantly reducing testosterone production 
by the fetal testis.204 This decrease in testosterone production 
was associated with aggregation of fetal Leydig cells and sub-
sequently linked to postnatal phenotypic alterations such as 
cryptorchidism (as a result of decreased INSL-3 production 
leading to agenesis of the gubernaculum), agenesis of the 
epididymis, and hypospadias.205,206 Gene expression studies 
later established that at higher exposures, key steroidogenic 
molecules were compromised.207 It has also been shown that 
Leydig cell aggregation occurs at much lower dose levels and 
this dysgenesis precedes the decreased ability of the fetal 
Leydig cell to produce testosterone.208 This is one example of 
how sophisticated studies with a more targeted approach can 
be utilized to gain a better understanding of the underlying 
toxicity resulting from a chemical exposure to facilitate a bet-
ter human risk assessment.
37.5.4  Leydig Cells
Leydig cells are found in the interstitial space within the testis 
and their primary physiologic role is testosterone production. 
During the conduct of safety studies (general toxicology and 
Developmental and Reproductive Toxicity [DART] studies), 
the most common lesions seen with Leydig cells are hyperpla-
sia and/or adenomas. Adenomas are increasingly referred to as 
Leydig cell tumors (LCTs). These findings are a hallmark of 
HPT axis disruption (commonly referred to as endocrine disrup-
tion) and are driven by increased LH levels. Leydig cell hyper-
plasia can be either focal or diffuse, but it is more commonly a 
focal response. In the rat, the distinction between Leydig cell 
hyperplasia and adenoma is based on size of the lesion. Leydig 
cell adenoma is commonly defined as a focus of hyperplasia 
larger than the diameter of three seminiferous tubules, although 
some criteria have used a one tubule diameter.46
Understanding the regulation of the HPT axis is key to 
understanding how chemicals can produce Leydig cell hyper-
plasia/adenomas (Figure 37.2). LH maintains testosterone lev-
els in homeostasis and chemicals that disrupt this homeostasis 
produce Leydig cell hyperplasia/adenomas. Neurons arising 
from the arcuate nucleus in the hypothalamus secrete GnRH 
into the hypophyseal portal circulation. GnRH binds to its 
receptors on the plasma membrane of gonadotrophs, which are 
found in the adenohypophysis (anterior pituitary). This bind-
ing stimulates the secretion of LH into the bloodstream where 
it travels to the testis. LH binds to its receptors on the plasma 
membrane of Leydig cells and activates adenylate cyclase to 
increase cAMP levels. Increased cAMP and calcium levels 
stimulate testosterone biosynthesis causing testosterone lev-
els to rise in the bloodstream and inhibit further release of 
GnRH and LH from the hypothalamus and adenohypophysis, 
respectively. An additional feedback control mechanism of 
LH involves the steroid estradiol.46 Testosterone is converted 
to estradiol via the enzyme aromatase, a process which is com-
monly referred to as aromatization.46

to humans of the remaining five pathways of LCT induction 
cannot be ruled out. Therefore, the central issue becomes what 
is the relative sensitivity between rat and human LCs in their

A variety of in vitro and in vivo methods are available to

cells are the preferred approach and can be performed either

elegantly illustrated with tofacitinib.210

morphological integrity following in vitro EDS exposure.201

purified Leydig cells were incubated with increasing con-

Leydig cells were used to study the interaction between Sertoli

37.6  Paternally Mediated Effects on Offspring
Potential mechanisms by which paternal drug and chemical 
exposure could result in effects in offspring include transfer of 
genetically damaged sperm, epigenetic transmission via sperm 
and transfer of xenobiotics in seminal fluid.
Heritable mutations due to alterations in sperm DNA are 
recognized as potentially detrimental to the offspring and 
transmission of genetic changes to the offspring have been 
demonstrated in laboratory animals, although reports of 
malformed offspring in animals following genetic damage 
of the paternal sperm cells are sparse.225,226 In rats, genetic 
damage induced in sperm cells by chronic cyclophospha-
mide treatment was imparted to the fertilized embryo.227–229 
Understanding the risk for alterations in sperm DNA is based 
on assessing the potential of the drug or chemical to produce 
alterations in genomic DNA (i.e., genotoxicity). There is cur-
rently no evidence for a unique germ cell mutagen and histori-
cal and experimental data support that somatic cell mutation 
assays predict putative mutagenic effects in the germline.230–232 
Therefore, assessment of genotoxicity based on somatic cell 
mutation assays protects the germline by default226,233 and the 
risk for transfer of heritable mutations is addressed by a stan-
dard genotoxicity testing battery. Genotoxic medicines, espe-
cially those intended for cancer treatment, have the potential to 
induce genetic changes in germ cells, even though no increase 
in birth defects has been detected among the progeny of male 
cancer patients treated with these drugs.234,235 The risk man-
agement approach for genotoxic drugs should consider that 
the genetic damage to the sperm cells can occur at any stage 
of spermatogenesis, and therefore, men should be advised to 
avoid unprotected intercourse for the duration of a complete 
spermatogenic cycle plus epididymal transfer time, which in 
humans is approximately 90 days.227,228
While genomic DNA contributes to the majority of inheri-
tance, it is recognized that non-DNA sequence-based infor-
mation is also passed on to future generations.236,237 Multiple 
mechanisms have been proposed to underlie non-DNA 
sequence-based inheritance, specifically DNA methylation, 
histone modifications, and inherited RNA, that are commonly 
termed epigenetic mechanisms of inheritance. However, an 
experimentally-verified mechanism to account for this mode 
of inheritance in mammalian species has yet to be conclu-
sively established.238 Conceptually exposure to the paternal 
animal to drugs or environmental chemicals could result in 
intergenerational effects (that could be due to direct effects on 
offspring via exposure to the parental organisms) and/or trans-
generational effects (phenomena that cannot be ascribed to 
direct effects of a particular trigger on the affected organism) 
without causing permanent changes to the DNA sequence.236 
In the case of female inheritance, an environmental stimulus 
can directly affect a gestating embryo and the already-formed 
oocytes within the female embryo so only effects in the third 
generation can be attributed to transgenerational epigenetic 
inheritance. For males, altered phenotypes occurring in the 
second generation can be described as transgenerational.239 
Therefore, for pragmatic reasons, research on transgenera-
tional epigenetic inheritance has focused on males. Although 
evidence of transgenerational inheritance in mammals is 
debated, intergenerational paternal epigenetic inheritance 
in nonhuman mammals is well established.240 The most cel-
ebrated example of epigenetic inheritance was the demon-
stration that exposure to either vinclozolin or methoxychlor 
was associated with decreased sperm capacity and infertil-
ity in male offspring out to four generations.241 The finding 
by Anway and coworkers triggered an explosion of research 
on epigenetic inheritance following exposure to other EDCs, 
which mostly confirmed intergenerational epigenetic effects, 
if not transgenerational effects.242 In addition to EDCs, stud-
ies in mammals have provided evidence that paternal expo-
sure to environmental stressors, such as diet, psychological 
stress, and toxicants, can influence offspring phenotypes, and 
sperm epigenome may be responsible for transmission of such 
phenotypes.243
While many important questions remain unanswered, stud-
ies showing epigenetic transmission have important implica-
tions for male reproductive health. One such question is in 
respect to the potential adverse impact of epigenetic heredity. 
It is possible to measure epigenetic alterations in germ cells, 
yet it is unclear to what extent modifications of an individual’s 
epigenetic profile impact the phenotype of their offspring.244 
Owing to the long duration of a single human generation, 
adaptive transgenerational epigenetic inheritance seems 
unlikely, although intergenerational inheritance of environ-
mental insult-triggered disorders, as demonstrated in rodents, 
could have a medically relevant effect on individual physi-
ology. While the research focus has been on environmental 
exposures, development of novel therapeutic approaches spe-
cifically targeting epigenetic mechanisms to combat diseases 
such as cancer have the potential to elicit off-target effects on

Embryo-fetal exposure to drugs following vaginal admin-
istration has been demonstrated,247–249 and therefore, drugs in 
semen are a potential route of exposure for pregnant partners 
of male patients. This concern is focused on drug in semi-
nal fluid, rather than related to drug-related effects on sper-
matozoa. Due to the physiochemical properties, the type of 
drug (small molecule, monoclonal antibody, or other type of 
large molecule) will greatly influence the potential risk. For 
example, the transfer to the seminal compartment for mAb 
therapeutics and subsequent vaginal uptake is essentially zero 
as demonstrated in rabbits, monkeys, and humans.249–251 The 
risk of seminal transfer for small molecule drugs can be deter-
mined using a series of theoretical estimates, based on worst-
case assumptions, to model potential exposure in the female 
partner. A safety margin to the NOAEL from developmental 
toxicity studies can then be calculated from the theoretical 
exposure.252 There is typically a large margin of safety estab-
lished (1,000-fold) due to the small volume of semen and large 
volume of distribution in the female partner. Accordingly, 
even when the developmental NOAEL is an order of magni-
tude less than clinical exposure, exposure via semen would 
not achieve systemic exposures of concern in female part-
ners. Therefore, only the most potent teratogens would be a 
potential concern, and these are likely limited to compounds 
for which the developmental liabilities are associated with the 
intended mode-of-action.
37.7  Evaluating Male Reproductive 
Safety in Humans
Once testicular toxicity has been identified in nonclinical 
studies, a human study is often required to determine the 
presence, extent, and reversibility of the testicular toxic-
ity observed. Human studies typically rely on measurement 
of semen parameters and serum hormone concentrations to 
detect potential toxicity to the male reproductive system as a 
surrogate for fertility, since measuring pregnancy rates is nei-
ther feasible nor practical. For pharmaceuticals, this informa-
tion can be derived from measurements in clinical trials while 
human male reproductive data for industrial chemicals typi-
cally rely on epidemiology studies.
Epidemiological studies of male reproduction attempt to 
relate between-subject differences in sperm measures with 
various risk factors for reduced reproduction. Therefore, it is 
the between-subject variability that is important in establish-
ing epidemiological risk factors. However, semen endpoints 
(sperm volume, sperm count, sperm forward motility, and mor-
phology) are highly variable even when measured according 
to WHO guidelines.253 As sperm endpoints show much higher 
between-subject variability than within-subject variability, the 
sensitivity to detecting a change in sperm parameters follow-
ing a single collection, as is common with epidemiology stud-
ies, is limited.254
Unlike in epidemiology studies where the ability to collect 
repeated samples is limited, semen analysis may be useful in 
clinical and research settings where repeated measurements on 
individual subjects are common, allowing for a within-subject 
comparison for samples collected during the treatment period 
to the individual pre-treatment data.255 An example of the 
effectiveness of assessing semen in clinical trials was pro-
vided in a study of the time course to onset and recovery from 
reduced sperm counts after initiation of various cancer treat-
ments.256 The typical clinical semen study uses a noninferi-
ority design based on a responder analysis against placebo. 
The semen parameter and cutoff point to define a responder 
(i.e., toxicity), as well as the inferiority margin from placebo, 
are arbitrarily defined.257 In an analysis of semen data from 
five clinical trials, men receiving placebo showed that using a 
cutoff of a 50% reduction in sperm concentration to define a 
responder would capture more than 5% of a population on pla-
cebo.257 Clinical studies to evaluate possible effects on sper-
matogenesis require adequate duration of exposure of at least 
two to three spermatogenic cycles and adequate sampling to 
compensate for individual variation in semen endpoints, for 
example, three samples over 2 weeks at each study assessment 
time point.258
However, as reduced sperm counts are a trailing indica-
tor (i.e., damage to the testes has already occurred before 
changes in sperm endpoints can be detected), a more sensi-
tive approach to allow for assessing translation of findings in 
preclinical species to humans at an early, reversible stage of 
injury is needed. Toxicogenomics, proteomics, and epigenetic 
approaches are currently being explored for the potential to 
identify new and improved biomarkers for testicular toxicity 
that may confer greater sensitivity and earlier predictivity than 
sperm assessment.5
37.8  Future of Male Reproductive Toxicology
The field of male reproductive toxicology and risk assessment 
has been evolving rapidly since reports of adverse effects of 
pesticides such as dibromochloropropane became evident in 
the 1970s.259 Our understanding of modes of action of chemi-
cals on male (and female) reproductive health has expanded 
with the application of cell and molecular approaches, includ-
ing genomics and proteomics. Despite this progress, the trans-
lation of reproductive toxicity from animal tests to humans is 
estimated to be around 60%.20 In addition, when decreases in 
sperm count are seen in nonclinical species, the variability 
in human sperm counts limits its interpretation and utility in 
pharmaceutical risk assessment. To improve human transla-
tion, moving from bulk RNAseq to single-cell RNAseq will 
improve the ability to understand effects on specific cell types 
within the testis. The ability to conduct RNAseq on formalin-
fixed paraffin-embedded tissues allows interrogation of sub-
chronic, chronic, and reproductive studies without conducting 
additional studies, consistent with 3R’s initiative. Coupling 
proteomics with transcriptomics will allow identification and 
verification of key mechanisms of toxicity and thereby increase 
translation fidelity. The field of male reproductive toxicology 
is also hampered by the lack of physiologic in vitro systems 
that can replicate testis functions such as sperm production 
across species (rat, dog, monkey, and human). The develop-
ment of microphysiological systems provides an opportunity 
to close this gap.260,261 Another area to improve translation is 
the development of biomarkers which has alluded scientific

biomarkers that may provide breakthroughs for the future. 
Regarding risk assessment, the application of AOPs coupled 
with point-of-departure analyses provide another advance-
ment to improve protection of human health.179 While there 
are many challenges left to understand male reproductive toxi-
cology, the advancements in scientific tools and risk assess-
ment methodologies offer promise for future advancements to 
improve human health protection.
