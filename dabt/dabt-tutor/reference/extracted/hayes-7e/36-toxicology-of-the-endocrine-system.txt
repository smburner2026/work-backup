36.1  Anatomy and Functions of 
the Endocrine System
The glands and organs of the endocrine system make hor-
mones and release them into the systemic circulation for travel 
to, and action on, a variety of organs and tissues.1
The endocrine system is complex and is comprised of 
major endocrine glands and organs including hypothalamus, 
pituitary gland, pineal gland, adrenal glands, thyroid gland, 
parathyroid glands, ovaries, and testes as well as organs with 
hormone-secreting cells including the thymus, pancreas, and 
gut.2 In the sections to follow, each of the components of the 
endocrine system and the hormones secreted by the gland or 
organ are described. Figure 36.1 illustrates most of the organs 
and glands of the endocrine system. In addition, the regula-
tory cross talk between the endocrine gland or organ and other 
members of its axis are summarized.
36.1.1  Hypothalamus
The hypothalamus is a small, cone-shaped collection of nuclei 
linking the central nervous system (CNS) to the endocrine sys-
tem via a downward projection from the brain ending in the 
infundibular stalk which connects to the pituitary gland.2 This 
brain region lies below the thalamus and comprises the floor 
of the third cerebral ventricle.3,4 Located on either side of the 
third ventricle, the thalamus, hypothalamus, epithalamus, and 
subthalamus comprise the diencephalon.3,4 See Figure 36.2. 
The hypothalamus controls many functions of the autonomic 
nervous system as well as communicating with the endocrine 
system.5 In addition to interactive innervation, the hypothala-
mus and pituitary gland are also connected by chemical path-
ways.2,6 The nerve endings of many neurosecretory cells are 
found in the posterior hypothalamus (median eminence) and 
pass through the infundibular stalk into the pituitary gland.2
The hypothalamus produces and secretes neurotransmit-
ters, neuropeptides, and the neurohormones vasopressin 
(antidiuretic hormone) and oxytocin.7–11 These two neurohor-
mones are produced by neurons, packaged into secretory gran-
ules, carried down axons, and then stored in nerve terminals 
in the median eminence.8 Neural stimuli extrude the granular 
contents stored in the nerve terminals into capillaries.2 Via 
the hypophyseal-portal vasculature, the neurohormones are 
transported into the anterior pituitary.12 The hypothalamus 
influences caloric intake and weight regulation, establishes 
a metabolic set point for weight gain, regulates body heat as 
related to ambient temperature, influences wakefulness and 
sleep, and regulates fluid intake and feelings of thirst.2
36.1.2  Pituitary Gland
The pituitary gland is also called the hypophysis from the 
Greek word for “lying under”.13,14 The hypophysis is descrip-
tively named as it is positioned on the underside of the brain.13 
The pituitary is housed within the bony sella turcica, located 
behind the nose and beneath the hypothalamus (Figure 36.3). 
Via a stalk of neuronal axons termed the infundibulum and 
hypophyseal-portal veins, the pituitary is attached to the hypo-
thalamus. The pituitary gland is quite small as it only weighs 
500–900 mg and is approximately the diameter of a pea in 
an adult human.13,14 Human pituitary glands are composed 
of functionally, anatomically, and embryologically distinct 
anterior (adenohypophysis) and posterior (neurohypophysis) 
lobes.13
The embryology of the anterior pituitary is complex. The 
homogeneous appearance of the epithelial cells of the anterior 
pituitary under light microscopy is deceptive as there are at 
least five different constituent cell types that secrete differ-
ent hormones.2 These epithelial anterior pituitary cells derive 
embryologically from Rathke’s pouch, an outcropping of the 
roof of the pharynx.15 The five epithelial cell types and their 
secretory products comprising the anterior pituitary are as fol-
lows: thyrotrophs – thyrotropin (thyroid-stimulating hormone 
[TSH]); gonadotrophs – gonadotropins including both lutein-
izing hormone (LH) and follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH); 
corticotrophs – adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH, cortico-
tropin); somatotrophs – growth hormone (GH, somatotropin); 
and lactotrophs – prolactin.16 Gonadotroph cells constitute 
about 10% of the pituitary, while somatotrophs are more 
plentiful comprising about 40% of the anterior pituitary and 
secrete between 1 and 2 mg of GH daily.17
The proteinaceous anterior pituitary hormones consist of 
one or two long polypeptide chains. TSH, LH, and FSH are 
glycosylated and composed of two glycopeptide chains. The 
alpha chain is identical in all three hormones. The beta chain 
differs in TSH, LH, and FSH which is believed to underlie 
their heterogeneous activities.18 All proteinaceous hormones, 
including those of the anterior pituitary, are synthesized in the 
cytoplasm as large inactive prohormones and then stored in 
cytoplasmic granules. As is the case for all protein hormones, 
the hormones of the anterior pituitary are synthesized in the 
cytoplasm of the cells as large inactive molecules called pro-
hormones. Within the storage granules, the prohormones are 
cleaved into active hormones and then secreted into the sys-
temic circulation.19

The hypothalamus and the posterior pituitary work collab-
oratively. The hypothalamus contains two clusters of nerve 
cell bodies termed nuclei, i.e., supraoptic and paraventricular 
nuclei. The cell bodies comprising the two nuclei, the axons 
projecting from the cell bodies, and the nerve endings termi-
nating in the posterior pituitary gland, form the neurohypoph-
yseal system.20 The supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei not 
only project to the posterior pituitary, but also project to other 
brain regions including areas that sense solute concentrations 
and regulate thirst.21 The microanatomy of the posterior pitu-
itary differs from that of the anterior pituitary. The posterior 
pituitary is composed largely of unmyelinated secretory neu-
rons.21 The axonal projections from the hypothalamic nuclei 
transport hormones from the hypothalamus directly to the 
posterior pituitary lobe, which then leads to pituitary release21 
into the systemic circulation.
Vasopressin (antidiuretic hormone) and oxytocin are the 
major neurohypophyseal hormones.22 Both of these hormones 
are synthesized in the cell bodies of the supraoptic and para-
ventricular nuclei, and then incorporated into neurosecretory 
granules. Vasopressin and oxytocin are made as part of a pre-
cursor protein complex comprised of either vasopressin or 
oxytocin, and a second protein called neurophysin. Following 
synthesis of the precursor protein complex, the precursor pro-
tein undergoes cleavage into separate neurophysin, and either 
vasopressin or oxytocin molecules. Neurophysin remains 
loosely attached to either vasopressin or oxytocin while 
remaining inside the granules. These neurophysin-hormone 
laden granules are transported down the axonic projections 
from the hypothalamus to the posterior lobe of the pituitary 
gland for storage.22
External or internal stimuli can activate the hypothalamic 
nuclei. For example, infant breast suckling stimulates oxy-
tocin-secreting neurons.23 Following neural stimulation, the 
granules stored in the posterior pituitary fuse with the cell 
wall of the terminal nerve endings, thereby dissociating oxy-
tocin from neurophysin. Both oxytocin and neurophysin are 
released into the systemic circulation.24 The oxytocin travels to 
the uterus and stimulates uterine contraction which plays a role 
in both labor and parturition.25 Similarly, during dehydration, 
the serum osmolality of the blood increases, which stimulates 
the secretion of vasopressin which increases reabsorption of 
water from the kidneys.26
36.1.3  Pineal Gland
The pineal gland is also called the conarium, epiphysis cerebri, 
pineal organ or pineal body.27 In embryologic development, 
the pineal gland derives from the roof of the diencephalon.28 
In humans, the pineal gland is located near the center of 
the brain. Lying between the two cerebral hemispheres, it is 
located behind the third cerebral ventricle in the midline.29 It 
is named for its shape as pineal derives from the Latin word 
for pinecone, i.e., pinea. The reddish-gray organ is quite small, 
measuring only about 0.8 cm (0.3 in.) long and weighing only 
100–200 mg.2,29,30 Pia mater encases the pineal gland.31 The 
gland is divided into lobules via connective tissue septae that 
project into the glandular tissue.31 See Figure 36.4. Growth of 
the pineal gland slows or stops after the first year of life with 
serum melatonin concomitantly declining during childhood.32 
In adults, the pineal gland becomes increasingly calcified from 
the presence of corpora arenacea bodies (“brain sand”) which 
appear as radiographic opacities on plain film x-rays.2,33,34
Ninety-five percent of the cellular content of the pineal gland 
is composed of pinealocytes. The remaining 5% of the cells 
are neuroglial cells that resemble astrocytes.35 The primary 
function of the pinealocyte is the photo-regulated production 
of melatonin (5-methoxy-N-acetyltryptamine).36 Melatonin 
interacts with the brain’s circadian rhythm to regulate the 
sleep and wakefulness cycle. Some data suggest that melato-
nin also plays a role in sexual development, thermoregulation, 
and cellular metabolism.30,37 Fortunately, the pineal gland does 
not tend to develop disorders of pathologic melatonin excess or 
deficit. However, the normality of its function has somewhat 
obscured the study of its possible physiologic roles.38
In addition to the pineal gland, melatonin is also syn-
thesized in the vertebrate retina, with minor contribu-
tions from the gastrointestinal (GI) tract and skin.36 The 
rate limiting step of melatonin biosynthesis is the con-
version of serotonin to N-acetylserotonin by serotonin 
N-acetyltransferase (AANAT).39 Subsequently, acetylsero-
tonin O-methyltransferase converts N-acetylserotonin to 
melatonin (ASMT). AANAT is activated by darkness, thereby 
leading to increased circulating melatonin.40 Melatonin levels 
are higher in the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) of the third ventri-
cle as compared with the CSF levels of the fourth ventricle, or 
in the systemic circulation. This finding suggests direct secre-
tion of melatonin into the CSF.41
36.1.4  Thymus
The thymus gland has two lobes and is constructed of soft tis-
sue encapsulated in connective tissue.42,43 It is situated near the 
pericardium, i.e., anterior to the great vessels of the heart. An 
isthmus connects the two lobes of the thymus.44 Each lobule of 
the thymus is comprised of a peripheral dark zone termed the 
cortex, and a lighter middle zone termed the medulla.45 The 
organ is a pinkish-gray color and achieves a maximum weight 
of about 1 oz. during puberty.46 See Figure 36.5. Lymphoid 
cells termed thymocytes, and reticular epithelial cells of the 
thymus, participate in growth and development of the thymus 
until puberty.47 After puberty, the thymus gradually decreases 
in size until involution begins around age 60.48 By age 75, it is 
eventually replaced by fatty tissue.46 Some researchers believe 
that involution of the thymus gland may represent programmed 
aging and result in deterioration of the immune response.49
Two embryonic processes form the thymus gland. During 
week six of gestation, the epithelium of the thymus (along 
with thyroid and parathyroid epithelia) forms from the ventral 
diverticular epithelium of the third pharyngeal pouch.50 The 
thymic epithelium extends posterolaterally as two flask-shaped 
structures into the surrounding mesoderm. The epithelial cells 
lining the flask-shaped structures proliferate and become sur-
rounded by invasive mesoderm.47,51,52 During week eight of 
gestation, the thymus descends to the anterosuperior medi-
astinum and the two flask-shaped structures fuse. Bone mar-
row precursor cells of mesenchymal origin migrate into the 
thymus during late fetal development. This migratory event

produces small amounts of melatonin and insulin.55,65 In addi-

receptors to which other hormones can affect function.66,67
36.1.5  Adrenal Gland
Humans have two yellowish, small, triangle-shaped adrenal 
glands (about 1 × 2 in., or 2.54 × 5.08 cm) each located atop 
a kidney, i.e., suprarenal.68,69 Each adrenal weighs approxi-
mately 4–6 g in an adult.70 The adrenal glands are highly vas-
cularized with up to 60 small arteries per each gland.71,72 See 
Figure 36.6. The adrenal glands affect metabolism, blood pres-
sure, immune function, sex hormones, and responses to physi-
ological stress. Each adrenal gland is enveloped in a protective 
adipose and is comprised of two main parts – the larger outer 
cortex and the smaller inner medulla. The cortex is further 
divided into three separate zones: the outermost zona glomer-
ulosa, the middle zona fasciculata, and the inner zona reticu-
laris.73 The three adrenal cortical zones combined can produce 
all classes of steroid hormones.73 Under normal physiological 
conditions, the major products produced by the adrenal cortex 
are 11-hydroxylated C21 steroids (corticosteroids).73 There are 
two different types of corticosteroids. Mineralocorticoids reg-
ulate sodium and potassium levels. Glucocorticoids regulate 
carbohydrate metabolism.73
The major mineralocorticoid is aldosterone, i.e., 4-pregnen-
11μ, 21-diol-3,18,20-trione. This hormone is produced only by 
the zona glomerulosa, which contains the unique mitochon-
drial enzyme aldosterone synthase (CYP11B2).73 Aldosterone 
functions in blood pressure, sodium and potassium regulation. 
Aldosterone regulates blood pH by signaling the kidneys to 
absorb more sodium into the bloodstream while releasing 
potassium into the urine.74 In humans, the major glucocorticoid 
is cortisol (or hydrocortisone), i.e., 4-pregnen-11μ, 17−, 21-triol-
3,20-dione.73 Cortisol is primarily produced by the zona fas-
ciculata with a smaller contribution from the zona reticularis. 
These two inner cortical zones contain 17−-hydroxylase 
(CYP17) but lack enzymes to convert corticosterone to aldo-
sterone. Cortisol plays a role in fat, protein, and carbohydrate 
metabolism, blood pressure, and blood sugar regulation. 
Cortisol secretion suppresses inflammation, decreases bone 
formation, and controls the sleep-wake cycle.68,69 Cortisol lev-
els are controlled via a negative feedback loop. The hypothala-
mus produces corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH), which 
stimulates the pituitary gland to secrete ACTH. The release 
of ACTH then stimulates the adrenal glands to synthesize and 
secrete cortisol into the bloodstream. The blood levels of cor-
tisol are controlled by the relative synthesis and secretion of

to the gonads, where they are converted into testosterone or 
estrogen.73
Cholesterol is the common precursor of all corticosteroid 
hormones.75 Cellular acquisition of cholesterol for steroid 
hormone synthesis can occur via two different pathways.75,76 
Dietary cholesterol is transported in blood as cholesterol esters 
imbedded in low-density lipoprotein (LDL) complexes that 
enter cells via LDL-receptor-mediated endocytosis.77 When 
LDL levels are unusually low, cholesterol from the cell’s own 
endoplasmic reticulum can be recruited for steroid synthesis.78
Catecholamines are water-soluble compounds composed of 
a catechol group (benzene core carrying two hydroxy substitu-
ents ortho to each other), and an amine group.79 The adrenal 
glands secrete the majority of systemic epinephrine, but only a 
minor percentage of circulating norepinephrine.80–82 The main 
catecholamine hormones secreted by the adrenal medulla are 
epinephrine (adrenaline) and norepinephrine (noradrenaline).70 
These hormones function in the “fight or flight response” 
of the sympathetic nervous system by increasing heart rate, 
blood pressure via vasoconstriction, myocardial contractile 
force, and blood flow to the muscles and brain; mobilization of 
glucose metabolism to increase short-burst energy; and relax-
ation of smooth muscles in airways.83 Chromaffin cells in the 
adrenal medulla produce the catecholamines from tyrosine via 
hydroxylation catalyzed by tyrosine hydroxylase to L-DOPA 
(levodopa or l-3,4-dihydroxyphenylalanine).84 L-DOPA is the 
precursor to the catecholamine neurotransmitters dopamine, 
norepinephrine (noradrenaline), and epinephrine (adrenaline). 
L-DOPA is first converted to dopamine, which can then be 
converted to norepinephrine. In the cytosol of the chromaffin 
cells, norepinephrine is converted to epinephrine by phenyle-
thanolamine N-methyltransferase (PNMT) and stored in gran-
ules.85 Splanchnic nerves of the sympathetic nervous system 
innervate the adrenal medulla and can stimulate the release of 
catecholamines from the storage granules by opening calcium 
channels in the chromaffin cell membrane.
Genomic analysis of the human adult adrenal glands demon-
strates the importance of the enzymatic activities occurring in 
the organ. Approximately 20,000 protein-coding genes com-
prise the human genome and 70% of these genes are expressed 
in the normal adult adrenal glands.86,87 However, only about 
250 genes display a particular level of gene activation in the 
adrenals with the highest level of expression found in the cyto-
chrome P450 superfamily of enzymes.88
36.1.6  Thyroid Gland
The two lobes of the thyroid gland give it a butterfly appear-
ance at its location at the front of the neck (Figure 36.7).89,90 
Two functional cell types populate the thyroid gland, i.e., 
follicular and parafollicular cells.89,91 The follicular cells of 
the thyroid produce two thyroid hormones – triiodothyro-
nine (T3)92 and thyroxine (T4).92 T4 is exclusively produced in 
the thyroid gland.93 In contrast, only 20% of T3 is produced 
directly from the thyroid gland.93 Extra-thyroidal deiodination 
of T4 produces the remaining 80% of T3. This deiodination 
occurs mainly in the liver or kidneys.94
Similar to other endocrine glands, the thyroid gland is con-
trolled via feedback and cross talk among the hypothalamus, 
pituitary, and target gland, i.e., thyroid. The communication 
network comprised of the hypothalamus, pituitary, and thyroid 
is termed the HPT axis.95 The hypothalamus produces thyro-
tropin-releasing hormone (TRH). This tripeptide is secreted 
into the venous circulation that drains to the pituitary.96 In the 
pituitary gland, TRH binds to thyrotroph cells.96 Following the 
impetus provided by this receptor binding, TSH, a.k.a. thyro-
tropin, is produced and secreted by the thyrotroph cells. TSH 
binds to specific receptors in the follicular cells of the thy-
roid and stimulates the production of T3 and T4.92 Although 
the thyroid utilizes both negative and positive feedback loops, 
the negative feedback system predominates. When the thy-
roid hormone level is sensed, the thyroid sends signals back 
to the hypothalamus and pituitary informing that further pro-
duction is unnecessary.97 This negative feedback loop system 
maintains a relatively constant level of circulating thyroid 
hormones.
The dimeric 660 kDa glycoprotein thyroglobulin is also 
notable as it accounts for about half of the protein content of the 
thyroid gland.98 This protein is secreted and accumulated at a 
concentration of hundreds of grams per liter in the extracellu-
lar compartment of the thyroid follicles.98 Three-dimensional 
orientation of the follicular cells creates the colloid space 
where thyroglobulin is produced.99,100 In all vertebrates, thy-
roglobulin is the main precursor to the thyroid hormones T3 
and T4, which are produced via cleavage of the thyroglobu-
lin’s tyrosine residues.101 Each thyroglobulin molecule forms 
approximately ten thyroid hormones, i.e., T3 or T4 hormones.98
36.1.7  Parathyroid Glands
The parathyroid glands are small endocrine glands about 
the size of a grain of rice, located behind the thyroid in the 
neck (Figure 36.8).102 Most humans have four individual para-
thyroids, although extra parathyroids are seen at autopsy in 
approximately 13% of the population.103 Two of the parathy-
roids are usually found superiorly and two inferiorly, but up 
to 16% of parathyroid glands are seen ectopically, i.e., in the 
mediastinum, retro-esophagus, or lateral neck.103 Two differ-
ent cell types populate the parathyroid glands, i.e., chief cells 
and oxyphil cells.104 Chief cells are smaller but more numer-
ous than oxyphil cells. However, the number of oxyphil cells 
increases with age.104 Chief cells are characterized by well-
developed endoplasmic reticula and distinct Golgi organelles 
that assist in the secretion of parathyroid hormone (PTH). In 
contrast, the function of oxyphil cells is not well understood.104 
However, the parathyroid oxyphil cell proportion is known 
to increase dramatically in patients with chronic kidney dis-
ease.105 The parathyroids play an important role in regulating 
blood levels of calcium.
When calcium levels fall, the parathyroids initiate a series 
of events eventually leading to the secretion of PTH. Initially, 
the parathyroids secrete the 115 amino acid hormone termed

36.1.8  Pancreas
The exocrine function of the pancreas aids in digestion, while 
the endocrine function regulates blood sugar level.108 The pan-
creas is located in the upper left abdomen behind the stom-
ach.109 It sits adjacent to the small intestine, liver, and spleen. 
Spongy and about 6–10 in. long, it resembles a flattened pear 
extending horizontally across the abdomen.110 The sections 
of the pancreas going from near the midline leftward are the 
relatively wider head, and then the neck, body, and tail.110 
The head of the pancreas is connected to the duodenum via 
the pancreatic duct (Figure 36.9).110
Approximately 95% of the pancreas is constructed from 
exocrine tissue.111 The exocrine secretions of the pancreas 
are complex and include digestive enzymes secreted by exo-
crine acinar cells, and bicarbonate secreted from epithelial 
cells lining small pancreatic ducts.112 The pancreatic diges-
tive enzymes fall into three categories: proteases; lipases; 
and amylase.113 Protein digestion starts in the stomach via the 
action of pepsin,114 but most protein digestion is facilitated by 
pancreatic proteases.115

where they are converted into their active forms, i.e., trypsin 
and chymotrypsin. The intestinal mucosal enzyme enterokinase

36.1.9  Ovaries
In infants and prepubescent girls, the ovaries are a mass of 
elongated tissues located in the pelvic girdle,145 i.e., the ring-
shaped ilium, ischium, and pubis bones of the hips.146 The 
ovaries grow and change shape as females approach puberty147 
eventually achieving their adult almond shape and size of 
about 4 cm (1.6 in.) in length, 2 cm (0.8 in.) width, and 1.5 cm 
(0.6 in.) thickness.145 The combined weight of the two ovaries 
is usually between 4 and 8 g (0.14 and 0.3 oz.).145
Ovaries are constructed of an outer cortex and an inner 
medulla.148,149 The cortex contains the follicles, oocytes, and 
interstitial cells (Figure 36.10). About 150,000–500,000 fol-
licles are present at birth.145 The follicles are hollow balls of 
cells containing immature eggs. By menarche, the number of 
immature follicles has already fallen to about 34,000, with 
further numerical decline over time eventuating at menopause 
where the few remaining follicles degenerate.145 Throughout 
the fertile period between ages 13 and 50, only about 300–400 
of the follicles undergo the maturation process.145
During the early follicular phase of the menstrual cycle, sev-
eral follicles expand and migrate toward the outer surface of 
the ovary. Follicle growth encompasses oocyte enlargement, 
follicular cell replication, and formation and expansion of a 
central follicular antrum, i.e., cavity.150 The cells lining the fol-
licle proliferate into a stratified epithelium termed the zona 
granulosa.145 An enclosing sheath called the theca forms from 
stromal and interstitial cells and surrounds the zona granu-
losa. A single or sometimes more follicles progress to further 
growth and maturation, i.e., Graafian follicles, achieving a 
diameter of about 30 mm (1.2 in.) prior to rupture.145,151
Several hormones interact to initiate and regulate follicular 
processes. Interstitial cells found in the theca produce andro-
gens.152 These androgens are converted into the major ovar-
ian hormones, estradiol, and estrone, within the cells of the 
zona granulosa.153 The oocytes are bathed in the antrum by 
fluid containing high concentrations of estrogens, progester-
one, androgens, enzymes, and bioactive proteins.151,154 The fol-
licular development phase lasts approximately 2 weeks.155 The 
follicular phase ends with the rupture at the ovarian surface of 
usually one, but sometimes two or even more mature follicles 
with attendant release of the egg(s).156 Following follicular rup-
ture, cells of the granulosa and theca fill the follicular lumen 
thereby forming the corpus luteum (Latin for yellow body). 
The corpus luteum is a temporary structure that produces high 
levels of progesterone for about 2 weeks, and moderate levels 
of estradiol and inhibin A, a glycoprotein hormone whose level 
can be measured clinically as a prenatal test for Down’s syn-
drome.157 The egg enters a fallopian tube for transport to the 
uterus.158 If after 2 weeks, the egg is not fertilized then the cor-
pus luteum undergoes involution resulting in whitish scar mass 
term the corpus albicans. Concomitant with the disappear-
ance of the corpus luteum, progesterone levels decline with 
the uterine lining and unfertilized egg shedding.159 In contrast, 
if the egg is fertilized, the corpus luteum maintains production 
of progesterone for several months and remains in the ovary 
until the end of pregnancy.160 Progesterone plays several roles 
including assisting the fertilized egg to attach to the uterine 
wall, and in embryonic development.161

36.1.10  Testes

36.1.11  Gut
The gut is the largest endocrine organ in the body.180–182 The 
supremacy of the gut regarding its contribution to the endo-
crine system is due to the wide distribution throughout the 
GI tract, stomach and pancreas (Figure 36.12) of specialized 
epithelial cells derived from the embryonic endoderm layer 
termed enteroendocrine cells (EECs).183 These cells constitute 
approximately 1% of the total GI intestinal epithelium.184 The 
function of EECs in the upper GI tract is better understood 
than the function of EECs in the lower GI tract.181
Acting as classic hormones on distant receptor targets, EECs 
sense luminal content and release signaling molecules into the 
systemic circulation. The hormonal secretions of EECs can 
also affect neighboring cells.185 The gut and brain also com-
municate bi-directionally.186 EECs directly communicate with 
neurons innervating the GI tract thereby initiating determin-
istic responses. Axon-like processes form neuroepithelial cir-
cuits between EECs and the nervous system.183,184
There are at least 15 different types of EECs.187,188 The 
diversity of EECs has rendered definitive assignment of exact 
biological roles and hormones secreted as problematic. The 
GI tract produces at least 30 different hormones.181 In addi-
tion, the functions of a particular hormone can overlap with 
other gastric hormones.189 The hormones secreted by EECs

Chemicals into Perspective
36.2.1  Detection Biases
The late, great pathologist William A. Gardner, M.D. was fond 
of this parable illustrating the difficulties inherent in endocri-
nology investigation. A policeman was walking down the side-
walk after dark and saw a man on his hands and knees under a 
nearby streetlamp frantically searching for his car keys. After 
20 minutes of assisting the somewhat inebriated man in his 
futile search, the policeman asked the man as to the approxi-
mate location where he had lost his keys. The man pointed to 
a spot in the dark. The moral of the story being that we tend to 
search where we can see (and measure) but not necessarily in 
the most relevant places.
The traditional approach to determining whether an agent is 
“toxic” usually entails employing an in vitro or in vivo assay 
capable of displaying a dose-response relationship over a pre-
viously agreed upon, and sufficiently wide, range of doses. 
Frequently, the test agent is certified as pure down to a rela-
tively low percentage of impurities. The highest test doses are 
usually selected so as to elicit some type of adverse effects 
or pathology.203 The pathology or adverse effects seen at the 
higher dose levels are assumed to also represent some level 
of risk to humans.204 However, many researchers have chal-
lenged this assumption on the grounds that pathology artifacts 
are frequently produced at high toxic doses that would not 
be expected at lower doses more representative of real-world 
exposure levels.204–214
Since the inception of animal bioassays for the testing of 
the toxicity of chemicals,215 investigators have understood 
that species differences between humans and experimental 
animals,216 sensitivity differences between various rodent 
strains,217 test animal diet218 and body weight,219 acute versus 
sub-chronic or chronic dosing regimen,215 and even circadian 
rhythm220 are possible confounders in extrapolating the results 
to humans. Despite these challenges, many animal models 
have been developed which can provide toxicological or path-
ological information relevant to humans. In some cases, the 
degree of modeling can be quite good, e.g., the modeling of 
dietary atherosclerosis in baboons221 or rhesus macaques.222
36.2.2  Signal-to-Noise Ratio Problem in 
Detecting Health Effects of Endocrine-
Disrupting (ED) Chemicals (EDCs)
A different type of experimental challenge is presented when 
the individual, worker cohort, or population is exposed to a 
synthetic biologically active agent that binds to the same 
receptor (or receptors) as a larger concentration of naturally 
occurring chemicals. The general scientific community was 
first alerted to the potential magnitude of this problem in 1990. 
That year, Ames, Profet, and Gold reported a quite surprising 
result, i.e., that 99.99% (by weight) of the pesticides ingested 
in the American diet are chemicals produced by plants to pre-
vent being eaten by insects or higher animals.213 Also, in 1990, 
Duke at the United States (US) Department of Agriculture 
Research Service in Oxford, MS223 provided strong support 
for the contention made by Ames, Profet, and Gold.213 Duke223 
reported that tens of thousands of chemicals have been iden-
tified in plants with most functioning as pesticides, and that 
experts estimated the existence of hundreds of thousands of 
natural plant products.224
The realization that humans were ingesting about 1.5 grams 
per day of a huge variety of natural plant pesticides213 raised 
the question as to whether these chemicals were toxic in stan-
dard toxicology tests. The 1990 paper by Ames, Profet, and 
Gold reported that 52 natural plant pesticides had been tested 
in chronic rodent bioassays with 27/52 (52%) displaying car-
cinogenicity. This carcinogenic percentage is comparable to 
that seen for synthetic chemicals.213 Based on the earlier work 
by Ames and his colleagues,213 we recently used standard US 
Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) programs to estimate 
the toxicity (T.E.S.T. 4.2) and persistence (EPI Suite 4.1) of 
a series of high-volume synthetic and natural pesticides. On 
average, synthetic pesticides were more persistent in the envi-
ronment than were natural pesticides. This result is consistent 
with cost, time, and logistical constraints under which farmers 
apply a limited number of applications of pesticides during a 
crop cycle. Synthetic and natural pesticides are predicted to

man-made production levels, from 1945 to 2015, an estimated 
5,000-fold more organic compounds were produced by a vari-

est litter-degrading fungi; microorganisms in temperate and 
boreal forest soils; bacteria in marine sponges; marine macro-
algae; volcanoes; and forest fires. Exposure to these naturally 
produced organic compounds occurs via inhalation of ambient 
air, ingestion of food and water, and contact with soil, fresh-
water, and sea water. Contact with low levels of several thou-
sand different endogenous or exogenous chemicals per day is 
unavoidable.227 Of these naturally occurring daily chemical 
exposures, the most relevant to the endocrine system is the 
problem of excess adipose tissue.
36.2.3  Studying EDCs in Populations 
with High Rates of Obesity
The US Centers for Disease Control and Prevention provides 
a standard definition of body mass index (BMI), and based on 
BMI, definitions of underweight, normal weight, overweight, 
and obese are assigned.229 BMI is defined as weight in kilo-
grams divided by square of height in meters.229 Although body 
types vary, with some stocky or muscular individuals incor-
rectly classified as obese, a high BMI is used as an indicator or 
screening tool indicative of a high body fat percentage.
Obesity has risen rapidly both worldwide and in the US over 
the last several decades. In 2014, it was estimated that more 
than 1.9 billion adults were overweight with over 600 million 
classifiable as obese.230 Approximately two-thirds of US adults 
over 20 years of age are currently classified as overweight with 
about 35% classified as obese.231 The obesity rate in the USA 
is predicted to reach 42% by 2030 in people over 18 years of 
age.232
Adipose tissue is composed of a number of different types 
of cells that produce secretions including adipocytes, endothe-
lial cells, macrophages/foam cells, neutrophils, lymphocytes, 
and fibroblasts, and also contains various cell precursors.233 
The range of biological activities, structural diversity, and 
sheer number of different molecules secreted by adipose tis-
sue is enormous. Molecules secreted by adipose tissue include 
cytokines and cytokine-like proteins; proteins of the fibrino-
lytic system; complement and complement-related proteins; 
enzymes; lipid transport molecules; endocannabinoids and 
other lipids; and proteins of the renin-angiotensin system.233
The class of molecules secreted by fat cells, termed adi-
pokines, is sufficiently complex and important that it merits 
separate discussion. Approximately 500 adipokines have been 
discovered to date with the following examples demonstrat-
ing the extremely wide range of biochemical and physiological 
reactions reported in the literature: leptin regulates appetite; 
adiponectin enhances insulin sensitivity and lessens inflam-
mation; ADAMTS1 affects fat stem cell differentiation, blood 
vessel formation, and ovulation; chemerin increases inflamma-
tion and blood pressure; resistin mediates insulin resistance; 
retinol-binding protein 4 affects insulin resistance; lipocalin-2 
increases insulin resistance and inflammation; isthmin-1 
improves fat metabolism in the liver, mediates immune func-
tion, and influences embryonic developmental patterning; 
asprosin modulates glucose release from the liver; Slit2-C 
stimulates glucose metabolism; and lipocalin-5 improves skel-
etal muscle respiration.233,234
Adipose tissue is hormonally active, possessing a large num-
ber of different receptors for classical endocrine hormones.235 
Insulin and glucagon receptors are present as are receptors for 
GH, TSH, gastrin/CCK-B, glucagon-like peptide-1, and angio-
tensin II receptors types 1 and 2. Adipose tissue also possesses 
enzymes capable of activating, interconverting, and inactivat-
ing steroid hormones.236,237
A large mass of adipose tissue leads to significant hormonal 
release. Adipose tissue contributes up to 100% of circulating 
estrogen in postmenopausal women and 50% of circulating 
testosterone in premenopausal women.236,237 Stromal cells and 
preadipocytes in adipose tissue express cytochrome P450-
dependent aromatase and 17b-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenases 
(17HSD), important in the conversion of androgens to estro-
gens. This aromatase regulates the conversion of the weakly 
androgenic rostenedione to the more powerful androgen tes-
tosterone and concomitantly the conversion of the weakly 
estrogenic estrone to the strong estrogen estradiol. In subcu-
taneous adipose tissue, the expression of 17HSD is decreased 
relative to the cytochrome P450-dependent aromatase. In con-
trast, in visceral adipose tissue, the expression of 17HSD is 
increased relative to the cytochrome P450-dependent aroma-
tase.236,237 Deposition of body fat to the abdominal region in 
the pattern typically observed in males and postmenopausal 
females is associated with this increase in 17HSD expression 
relative to aromatase expression.236,237

Estradiol is of particular importance due to its role in 
increasing risk for development of breast, endometrial, and 
ovarian cancers.238 It is clear that locally produced, rather than 
circulating, estrogens play an important role in breast cancer 
development, bone mineral maintenance, and preservation of 
cognition.239
In addition to systemic inflammation, obesity is associated 
with inflammatory changes in the brain.240 Obesity-associated 
inflammation was first reported in the hypothalamus.241 Miller 
and Spencer242 have reported that circulating cytokines, free 
fatty acids, and immune cells cross the blood brain barrier into 
the hypothalamus. Once there, these systemic factors induce 
local inflammation, including the proliferation of microglia. 
These authors242 hypothesize that the localized inflamma-
tion remodels synapses and causes neurodegeneration thereby 
disrupting the hippocampus and amygdala. Obesity-related 
inflammation may also affect the hippocampus, brainstem, 
amygdala, and cortical structures.243
The National Cancer Institute has identified 11 different 
human cancers that show a positive association with obesity 
as reported in epidemiology studies.238 These are endome-
trial, esophageal, gastric, liver, kidney, pancreatic, colorectal, 
gallbladder, breast, ovarian, and thyroid cancers. Multiple 
myeloma and meningioma are also increased in incidence in 
the obese. A variety of mechanisms are thought to be involved. 
Estrogen secreted by adipose tissue contributes to the gene-
sis of breast, endometrial, and ovarian cancers.238 Obesity is 
associated with a chronic low-grade inflammatory state and 
inflammation can contribute to cancer risk in at least two 
ways – by exposing cells to potential mutation by activated 
oxygen and nitrogen species244 and by inducing cell prolifera-
tion rate as part of a repair response.245 Acid reflux from gas-
troesophageal reflux can lead to Barrett’s esophagus, strongly 
associated with esophageal adenocarcinoma. Gallbladder 
inflammation from stones is a risk factor for gallbladder can-
cer238 and chronic ulcerative colitis is a risk factor for colon 
cancer. Hepatitis increases a number of forms of liver can-
cer.238 Hyperinsulinemia or insulin resistance may promote 
the growth of colon, kidney, prostate, and endometrial can-
cers.238 Additional suggested mechanisms include direct and 
indirect effects on cell growth regulators, alterations in the 
mechanical properties of connective tissue matrix scaffold-
ing that supports breast cells, altered immune responses, and 
effects on the nuclear factor kappa beta system.238
The adverse health effects of obesity are likely to be exten-
sive and include many factors apart from the widely under-
stood type-2 diabetes risk. A significant body of literature 
reports that exposure to EDCs can adversely affect the endo-
crine glands, with an increased tendency toward developing 
obesity among the notable effects.246 Any study on the poten-
tial adverse effects of exposures to relatively low concentra-
tions of chemicals that might alter the endocrine system must 
take into account the very high percentages of societal obe-
sity and the clear biological activity of the many molecules 
released by adipose tissue.247
Obesity as determined by BMI is a confounder in clinical 
evaluations of the effects of EDCs.247 Validated regulatory 
tests are used to determine whether a chemical acts via a mode 
of action (MOA) that affects estrogen, androgen, thyroid, or 
steroidogenic pathways.248 Test batteries for evaluating EDCs 
include quantitative structure activity relationship,249 in vitro 
assays,249 and animal testing.249 Studies suggest that EDCs 
pose the greatest risk during prenatal and early infant devel-
opment when organ systems are developing.250 Health effects 
include lowered fertility, endometriosis, and cancers associated 
with estrogenic activity.251 Epidemiology studies on adverse 
effects of EDCs in the general population are difficult to con-
duct due to very low exposures of EDCs in non-occupational 
cohorts, and lack of exposure measurements between cases 
and controls. In contrast with very low levels of hormonal 
perturbation from nano-molar to micro-molar exposures to 
EDCs, adipose tissue in obesity alters estrogen, testosterone, 
TSH, and inflammation levels.247 Obesity in pregnancy and 
gestational diabetes are associated with adverse outcomes in 
infants and children including autism, poor motor skills, low-
ered IQ, and altered birth weight.252 Neonatal effects of obesity 
are confounded by average lower socio-economic status. The 
already perturbed endocrine balance in overweight or obese 
persons renders them particularly worthy subjects for clinical 
epidemiology investigations on the possible effects of EDCs. 
However, inclusion of subjects with obesity requires account-
ing for potentially confounding effects of the hormonal influ-
ences arising from excess adiposity. If subjects with obesity 
are to be included in clinical epidemiological evaluations 
related to hormonal effects, the subjects should be classified 
by body fat percentage rather than by the much less exact mea-
sure of BMI.247
36.2.4  Commentary on the Signal-to-
Noise Problem of EDC Detection
In 2020, a group of 19 distinguished senior scientists orga-
nized to address the problem of detecting the low levels of 
EDCs found in the environment. Toward that end, they co-
published a manuscript in a variety of journals almost simul-
taneously253 with the title, “Human exposure to synthetic 
endocrine-disrupting chemicals (S-EDCs) is generally neg-
ligible as compared to natural compounds with higher or 
comparable endocrine activity. How to evaluate the risk of 
the S-EDCs?” It would be difficult to conduct a more insight-
ful analysis of this perplexing problem than that provided by 
these 19 co-authors.
These authors noted that on theoretical grounds, “both syn-
thetic endocrine-disrupting chemicals (S-EDCs) and natural 
(exogenous and endogenous) endocrine-disrupting chemicals 
(N-EDCs) can interact with endocrine receptors and disturb 
hormonal balance”. However, this statement was then cave-
ated by noting that as compared to endogenous hormones, 
S-EDCs are only weak partial agonists with receptor affini-
ties several orders of magnitude lower than N-EDCs. The 
very low receptor affinities of S-EDCs necessitate very high 
concentrations to displace the more robustly binding natural 
hormones or other endogenous ligands to elicit an observ-
able effect. Eating significant amounts of soy, green tea, 
sweet mustard, and other N-EDCs can result in biologically 
significant exposures despite possessing potencies lower 
than natural endogenous hormones. While less potent than 
natural hormones, these N-EDCs are usually more active

1841

than S-EDCs. In contrast with the observed effects of some 
N-EDCs in humans, epidemiology studies on potential asso-
ciations between S-EDC exposure and adverse human health 
effects have yielded consistently negative results, consistent 
with the lack of biological plausibility of such low concen-
trations and receptor affinities. These senior scientists have 
recommended using in vitro tests to quantitatively compare 
the potencies of candidate S-EDCs against those of reference 
N-EDCs. They conclude, “When the potency of the S-EDC 
is similar or smaller than that of the N-EDC, further testing 
in laboratory animals and regulatory consequences are not 
warranted”.253
36.3  Endocrine-Disrupting Chemicals
36.3.1  Definitions
Certain chemicals (drugs, industrial chemicals, pesticides, 
etc.) and biological agents (bacteria, viruses, etc.) are known 
to affect the endocrine system.246,254,255 Perturbation of endo-
crine homeostasis often produces consequences that can lead 
to metabolic derangements, developmental abnormalities, and 
sexual or reproductive dysfunction. The US EPA has defined 
an endocrine disruptor as “an exogenous agent that interferes 
with the production, release, transport, metabolism, binding, 
action, or elimination of natural hormones in the body respon-
sible for the maintenance of homeostasis and the regulation 
of developmental processes”.256–258 The US EPA has generated 
several lists of suspected chemicals and chemical contami-
nants that they believe need to be tested as possible EDCs.
The World Health Organization (WHO) defines an EDC 
as an “exogenous substance or mixture that alters function(s) 
of the endocrine system and consequently causes adverse 
health effects in an intact organism, or its progeny, or (sub) 
populations”.259 Under the auspices of the United Nations 
(UN) Environment Programme, the International Panel on 
Chemical Pollution has published a list of chemicals classi-
fied as EDCs or potential EDCs.260 This list has been adopted 
by the European Commission. Additional chemicals are under 
consideration by the European Chemicals Agency (ECHA) for 
classification as EDCs.261
EDCs are under intense scientific study, at least in part due 
to the influential 1996 publication of Our Stolen Future.262 
The book’s contention of adverse estrogenic effects of certain 
environmental contaminants received widespread public-
ity. The original concern regarding estrogenic chemicals was 
subsequently extended to other potential hormonal effects. 
Exposures to EDCs have been hypothesized to result in ill-
timed maturation, development, growth, or regulation. The 
EPA has proposed at least four categories of adverse effects 
linked to EDCs. The putative adverse effects include cancer, 
immunological effects, neurological effects, and reproductive 
and developmental effects involving the adrenals, thyroid, 
pituitary, and gonads.263
In experimental animals, several chemicals have displayed 
perturbations in the endocrine system. Following relatively 
high environmental exposures to EDCs, adverse developmen-
tal and reproductive effects have been observed in birds,264 
dolphins,265 alligators,266 and other wildlife.267–270 While 
similar effects could be hypothesized to occur in humans 
under analogous exposure conditions, the lower exposures 
to EDCs experienced by human populations make it dif-
ficult to disentangle potential effects of confounders like 
obesity.228,257,263,271,272

36.4.2.1  Tier 2 Testing
The three goals of Tier 2 assays include the following:
	
1.	Determining whether a substance may cause endo-
crine-mediated effects through or involving estro-
gen, androgen, or thyroid hormone systems.
	
2.	Determining the consequences to the organism of 
the activities observed in Tier 1.
	
3.	Establishing the relationship between doses of an 
endocrine-active substance administered in the test 
and the effects observed.
The Tier 2 tests are longer in duration than Tier 1 tests, are 
designed to encompass critical life stages and processes as 
well as a broad range of doses, and are intended to be admin-
istrated by a relevant route of exposure. Effects associated 
with endocrine disruption may not be expressed until later in 
the test subject’s life or may not appear until the reproductive 
period is reached. Therefore, Tier 2 tests usually encompass 
two generations and include effects on fertility and mating, 
embryonic development, sensitive neonatal growth and devel-
opment, and transformation from the juvenile life stage to 
sexual maturity. The two-generation reproduction study or 
the extended one-generation reproduction test is the mam-
malian multigeneration test that will enable EPA to obtain a 
more comprehensive profile of the biological consequences of 
a chemical exposure and identify the dose or exposure that 
caused the consequences. The Tier 2 non-mammalian eco-
toxicology tests include the LAGDA, Avian Two-Generation 
Toxicity Test in the Japanese Quail, and Medaka Extended 
One-Generation Reproduction Test.

36.6  Diethylstilbestrol (DES)
The field of endocrine toxicology has moved from a focus on 
the endocrine effects of high-dose exposures like pharmaceu-
ticals, to low-dose exposures to EDCs. The case of DES is a 
classic and tragic example of the effects of exposure to high-
dose synthetic hormones. DES was first synthesized in 1938 
for use as a synthetic estrogen. From 1938 to 1971, DES was 
prescribed to 5–10 million pregnant women for the prevention 
of miscarriages. A study reporting that DES did not prevent 
miscarriages or premature births was published in 1953, but 
as established medical procedures (and practitioners) can err 
on the side of resistance to change, DES was prescribed until 
1971 when the FDA issued a Drug Bulletin. This action by the 
FDA was based on the observation of a rare vaginal cancer in 
young women exposed prenatally to DES. It has since been 
established that prenatal DES exposure can cause clear cell 
adenocarcinoma of the vagina and cervix.319
36.7  Performance-Enhancing Drugs
A number of different drugs have been used to enhance ath-
letic performance, the best known class being anabolic-andro-
genic steroids, i.e., synthetic derivatives of testosterone.320 
Testosterone itself is subject to rapid degradation and was thus 
modified chemically to slow degradation and androgenic side 
effects.321 Body builders in California were already experi-
menting with testosterone-like compounds by the late 1940s 
and early 1950s.322 Anabolic steroid abuse was first noted in 
athletic competition at the 1954 world weightlifting champi-
onships, but rapidly spread to other Olympic sports during 
the 1960s.322 Soon thereafter, steroids were being used in the 
National Football League and by the 2000s were also being 
used in Major League Baseball.322 The major appeal to ath-
letes was muscle building and enhanced recovery following 
vigorous workouts.323 The frequently high doses of anabolic 
steroids can have a number of serious side effects in men 
including gynecomastia, prostate enlargement, testicular atro-
phy and infertility.323 The biochemical and physiological mis-
match presented by synthetic male hormones in females leads 
to even more severe side effects including reversible hirsutism 
and amenorrhea, and potentially irreversible deepening of the 
voice, baldness and clitoral hypertrophy.324 In an attempt to 
beat the widespread testing protocols in place for detection 
of anabolic steroid derivatives, black market chemists have 
developed so-called designer steroids for which analytical 
standards are lacking.325 The side effects of designer anabolic 
steroids are unknown to the potential user and thus represent 
an even higher risk than abuse of traditional anabolic steroid 
derivatives.
Widespread testing for anabolic steroids by all of the major 
sporting organizations, and the severe side effects of anabolic 
steroid abuse, led to the selection of human GH for enhanced 
sports performance.326 First isolated from human pituitary 
glands in the 1950s, by the 1980s athletes had begun to abuse 
GH.326 The depth of the desire for improved athletic perfor-
mance is illustrated by GH abuse predating therapeutic use 
in adults by 10 years.327 The increased supply of human GH 
ushered in by recombinant DNA technology probably further 
incentivized GH abuse in the 1980s.326,327
The anabolic activity of human GH is mediated by Insulin 
Growth Factor-1 (IGF-1).328 There are two analytical methods 
for the detection of “doping” with human GH. The isoform 
method distinguishes pure recombinant 22 kDa GH from the 
heterogeneous isoforms secreted in a pulsatile manner from 
the pituitary.329 The marker method measures blood levels of 
GH responsive proteins, i.e., IGF-1 and the N-terminal pro-
peptide of type III collagen.329 Sustained use of human GH 
can cause a number of serious side effects including joint 
pain, muscle weakness, fluid retention, diabetes, visual dis-
turbances, carpal tunnel syndrome, defective glucose regu-
lation and cardiomegaly.330 (One of the more infamous side 
effect profiles regarding long-term use of human GH was pro-
vided in testimony given in 2011 at the American baseball 
player Barry Bond’s federal perjury trial wherein witnesses 
alleged that the player’s hat and shoe sizes increased while in 
his mid-30s.331)
While testosterone-mimic anabolic steroids and human GH 
have been surreptitiously used in athletic competitions with an 
emphasis on explosive muscle strength, erythropoietin (EPO) 
has been used in sports dependent on cardiovascular fitness.332 
EPO is a hormone produced by the kidneys that facilitates the 
production of red blood cells (RBCs).332 In response to hypox-
emia, EPO is released by the kidneys after which it travels to 
and stimulates the bone marrow to make RBCs.332 EPO has a 
short half-life and is then excreted in urine.333 In severe kid-
ney disease, EPO production can be insufficient leading to the 
development of anemia.334
In 1983, Amgen Incorporated developed the recombinant 
165-amino acid EPO drug called Epoetin alfa (Epoge), fol-
lowed by additional alfa and beta formulations as a treat-
ment for anemia.335 Epoge is structurally identical to natural 
EPO and thus possesses the same biological activity.336,337 
Professional cyclists injected EPO during the 1990s and 
2000s in an attempt to improve performance,332 even though 
some recent studies question its effectiveness.332 EPO use has 
been associated with 18 deaths among competitive cyclists 
via stroke, myocardial infarction, and pulmonary embo-
lism.338 Seven-time Tour de France champion (1999–2005) 
Lance Armstrong brought EPO abuse to the public con-
sciousness when he admitted his use of EPO, diuretics, and 
human GH.339

36.8  Conclusions
There are several potential sources of hormones or hor-
mone-like substances: therapeutics like birth control pills,340 
estrogen replacement therapies341; and androgen-blocking 
agents for prostate cancer patients342; natural plant estrogens 
like soybean-derived genistein and daidzein343; and EDCs. 
The obesity epidemic is exposing hundreds of millions of 
people to elevated levels of estrogens, inflammatory medi-
ators, and adipokines to which humans were not exposed 
over the long course of evolution.344,345 In addition to being 
relatively lean until recent times, humans have historically 
expended significant amounts of energy in day-to-day liv-
ing.346 Exercise levels can have significant effects on a vari-
ety of hormones including testosterone.347 Also, nulliparity 
was relatively rare during human evolution and is increas-
ingly common in Western and other highly industrialized 
societies, e.g., the low birth rates in Western Europe and 
Japan.348 The statistically significant increase in lifetime 
risks for estrogen-sensitive breast and ovarian cancers in 
nulliparous women illustrates the long-term impact of preg-
nancy on hormones.349
Recently, a great deal of research emphasis has centered 
on the effects of EDCs.350,351 In order for a synthetic chemi-
cal to exert an adverse biological effect, it must outcompete 
natural hormones with much higher receptor affinities,253 and 
usually occurring at much higher exposures,253 for the same 
protein receptor site.253 While this condition is readily achiev-
able by pharmaceuticals, e.g., DES or artificial contraceptives, 
the receptor binding hurdle is higher for synthetic chemicals 
inadvertently released into the environment at relatively low 
concentrations as pollutants.253 However, these data regard-
ing human health should not be misconstrued to argue that 
regulation prohibiting release of EDCs into the environment is 
unwarranted, as the literature is replete with reports of adverse 
environmental impacts ranging from feminization of frogs 
exposed to atrazine352 to harm to fish populations from birth 
control pills entering the sewer system.353 Given the adverse 
impacts on endocrine-related health effects from obesity and 
a sedentary lifestyle, much of the responsibility lies with 
changes in behavior, and public health and educational strate-
gies toward achieving that end.354
