22.1  Introduction
Toxicological test methods are necessary to assess the hazard 
and safety of various substances such as medicines, agrochem-
icals, consumer products, and industrial chemicals. Many of 
these methods have traditionally used laboratory animals as 
the test system. However, there has been increasing interest in 
the development of new approach methodologies (NAMs) that 
incorporate advances in science and technology and leverage 
more human-relevant systems to provide improved accuracy 
and efficiency compared to existing animal-based methods. 
NAMs are broadly defined as any technology, methodology, 
approach, or combination thereof that can be used to provide 
information on chemical hazard and risk assessment to avoid 
the use of animal testing [1]. NAMs also have the potential to 
provide more detailed insight into the mechanisms by which 
chemicals may cause toxicity. There are continuing national 
and international interests and legislative mandates to develop 
alternative test methods that can replace animal use, reduce 
the number of animals required for a test procedure, and where 
animals are still required, refine testing procedures to lessen 
or eliminate unrelieved pain and distress.
Prior to regulatory use, any new method or approach must 
first be demonstrated as scientifically valid. Historically, this 
validation process involved lengthy multilaboratory ring tri-
als to test large numbers of chemicals to demonstrate test 
method accuracy, reproducibility, and reliability as compared 
to the reference test method (typically the in vivo test method). 
However, new approaches developed with 21st-century science 
require new approaches for establishing confidence in NAMs 
that incorporate the overarching principles of validation more 
flexibly and efficiently. This chapter provides a historical 
overview and contemporary perspective of the fundamental 
concepts underlying alternatives development, the ongoing 
modernization of toxicology testing, and the principles of test 
method validation by highlighting available alternatives to 
animal testing and key components in the process for estab-
lishing confidence in and facilitating regulatory acceptance of 
NAMs. Specific examples are provided of toxicity endpoints 
for which NAMs are available as well as those for which more 
work is yet to be done.
22.1.1  The 3Rs in Toxicology Testing
The concept of animal use alternatives was first described in 
1959 by Rex Burch and William Russell in their book The 
Principles of Humane Experimental Technique [2]. Commonly 
referred to as the 3Rs of alternatives, this concept involves 
replacing animals with nonanimal systems and approaches, 
reducing the number of animals needed for a specific study, 
and refining animal use to lessen or avoid unrelieved pain 
and distress. Public concern and increased awareness about 
animal use contributed to the passage of laws requiring con-
sideration of alternative methods before the use of animals in 
the United States in 1985 [3] and in Europe in 1986 and 2010 
[4, 5]. Additional laws in 1993 and 2000 directed the National 
Institutes of Health (NIH) to conduct research on alterna-
tive methods, to develop and validate alternative methods for 
testing, and to establish a formal process for consideration of 
proposed alternative testing methods [6, 7].
22.1.1.1  Regulatory Requirements for 
Consideration of Alternative Methods
In the United States, Animal Welfare Act regulations imple-
mented in 1989 require investigators to consider alternative 
methods prior to the use of animals for research or testing 
whenever proposed procedures involve more than slight or 
momentary pain or distress [8]. Before animals can be used, 
the investigator must provide evidence of the sources used to 
determine if alternative methods to procedures that cause more 
than slight or momentary pain or distress are available. The 
investigator must document this search, and both the search 
for alternatives and the proposed animal use must be reviewed 
and approved by an institutional animal care and use com-
mittee (IACUC). Institutions using animals for research and 
testing must register with the U.S. Department of Agriculture 
(USDA) and are subject to periodic compliance inspections by 
the Animal Care Unit of the USDA Animal and Plant Health 
Inspection Service.
Investigators subject to the provisions of the Public Health 
Service (PHS) policy on the Humane Care and Use of 
Laboratory Animals must also consider replacement, reduc-
tion, and refinement alternatives prior to the use of animals [9]. 
These include organizations that receive funding from PHS 
agencies (e.g., NIH, Food and Drug Administration [FDA], 
Center for Disease Control and Prevention [CDC], Agency for 
Toxic Substances and Disease Registry [ATSDR]), as well as 
organizations that participate in the voluntary animal facil-
ity accreditation program of the Association for Assessment 
and Accreditation of Laboratory Animal Care International. 
The PHS policy implements relevant provisions of the Health 
Research Extension Act of 1985 and requires that studies using 
animals comply with the U.S. Government Principles for the 
Utilization and Care of Vertebrate Animals Used in Testing, 
Research, and Training [9]. These principles effectively require 
the incorporation of replacement, reduction, and refinement 
alternatives into animal studies to the extent that they are con-
sistent with obtaining testing and research objectives.
22.1.1.2  Replacement Alternatives
Replacement alternatives are methods or approaches that 
do not require the use of animals (e.g., cell, tissue, or organ 
cultures; microphysiological systems [MPS]; computational 
approaches) or that replace a traditional laboratory animal 
model with a nonsentient phylogenetically lower species (e.g., 
roundworms, insects) [2]. A complete replacement alternative 
represents the “holy grail” in regulatory toxicology testing and 
such approaches have been achieved for only a small number 
of endpoints. For example, nonanimal test methods for assess-
ing skin corrosivity have been adopted internationally (as 
harmonized test guidelines [TGs] under the Organization for 
Economic Cooperation and Development [OECD]) such that 
no animal or other further testing is required when the sub-
stance is identified as a corrosive.

However, recognizing that a single approach is unlikely to 
successfully address the mechanisms associated with most 
toxicity endpoints, defined approaches (DAs) that combine 
outputs from multiple nonanimal alternatives are being devel-
oped to overcome some of the limitations of the individual 
test methods and replace animal use for toxicity testing. DAs 
consist of a fixed data interpretation procedure applied to data 
generated using a defined set of information sources to derive 
a prediction without the need for expert judgment. Their devel-
opment has been stimulated by advances in new technologies 
and an enhanced understanding of the molecular and cellular 
mechanisms of toxicity. Such approaches potentially provide 
more human-relevant information than traditional laboratory 
animal-based toxicology test methods.
In silico approaches that leverage the computational power 
of artificial intelligence applications such as machine learn-
ing are ever increasing in number as new opportunities are 
created through the identification of reference datasets that 
are sufficiently large to use in such modeling approaches 
(e.g., high-throughput screening [HTS] data). These model-
ing approaches are particularly exciting given their speed and 
efficiency in evaluating the potential toxicities associated with 
thousands of chemicals in just a few seconds. The number and 
diversity of in vitro and in silico approaches will undoubtedly 
only continue to expand greatly in the coming years.
22.1.1.3  Reduction Alternatives
As the name implies, reduction alternatives are alternative 
approaches that result in fewer animals being used in an in 
vivo method while still attaining the same study objective. For 
example, minimizing one or more experimental variables can 
often improve statistical power, allowing for fewer animals 
per group. Optimal statistical designs of studies will also con-
tribute to ensuring the use of the most appropriate number of 
animals.
One approach to reduction is to periodically conduct a ret-
rospective review of testing results to determine if the num-
ber of animals can be reduced without significantly affecting 
the outcome of the study. For example, six rabbits were rou-
tinely used to conduct an ocular irritation assay; however, a 
retrospective statistical evaluation determined that the number 
could be reduced to a maximum of three in most situations [10, 
11]. The latest guideline for this endpoint reflects opportunities 
for further animal reduction by testing one animal at a time 
sequentially and stopping if evidence indicates severe irrita-
tion or ocular corrosion in one animal (TG 405).
Additional examples of such an approach are described in 
guidance documents published by the U.S. Environmental 
Protection Agency (EPA). Based on a retrospective of fish 
bioconcentration factor studies for pesticide registration, the 
EPA concluded that a single treatment level concentration 
was adequate for most studies with the potential to reduce 
fish used in these tests by 30% [12]. Additional retrospective 
evaluations were conducted by the EPA on acute oral and der-
mal systemic toxicity studies to determine that the oral LD50 
would suffice for hazard classification and labeling of agro-
chemicals, both active ingredients (AIs) and final formulations 
[13, 14]. Accordingly, testing waivers can be granted for the 
acute dermal toxicity endpoint thereby substantially reducing 
the number of animals required for hazard classification and 
labeling of agrochemicals.
22.1.1.4  Refinement Alternatives
The goals of refinement alternatives are to minimize or elimi-
nate unrelieved pain and distress and to enhance the well-being 
of animals used in testing and research when no alternative 
nonanimal approaches exist. Refinements not only provide 
for improved animal welfare but also enhance the quality of 
experiments by reducing or eliminating pain and distress as an 
experimental variable [15].
Death has been used historically as an experimental endpoint 
in toxicity testing; however, considerable pain and distress may 
precede death, and therefore, the occurrence of spontaneous 
deaths should be avoided during toxicity studies [16]. National 
and international testing guidelines for acute toxicity were 
updated in the 2000s to reflect that death is no longer a required 
endpoint. Many of these guidelines now allow for humane 
euthanasia of moribund animals, as well as animals that show 
evidence of severe pain and distress (OECD TG 420, 423, 425). 
International guidance has also been developed for selecting 
appropriate endpoints for toxicity studies (OECD GD).
Refinement can be achieved in toxicity studies by identify-
ing earlier, more humane endpoints that are predictive of tra-
ditional study endpoints involving pain and distress [15, 16]. 
Clinical signs, physiologic parameters, biochemical measure-
ments, and other parameters can serve as potential earlier 
biomarkers of humane endpoints. Detailed data should be col-
lected to confirm the validity of the earlier biomarker. When 
it has been determined that the earlier biomarker provides the 
same or better accuracy as the traditional biomarker, it can be 
proposed for acceptance by regulatory authorities.
22.1.2  Interagency Coordinating Committee 
on the Validation of Alternative 
Methods (ICCVAM): Harmonizing 
Efforts to Advance Alternatives
ICCVAM is a permanent committee of the National Institute of 
Environmental Health Sciences (NIEHS). ICCVAM was for-
mally established in 2000 by the ICCVAM Authorization Act 
[7], “To establish, wherever feasible, guidelines, recommen-
dations, and regulations that promote the regulatory accep-
tance of new or revised scientifically valid toxicological tests 
that protect human and animal health and the environment 
while reducing, refining, or replacing animal tests and ensur-
ing human safety and product effectiveness” [7]. The original 
ICCVAM chartered in the Act was composed of representa-
tives from 15 U.S. federal regulatory and research agencies. 
In 2017, the National Institute of Standards and Technology 
(NIST) became the 16th U.S. agency, followed shortly there-
after in 2020 by the Department of Veteran’s Affairs Office of 
Research and Development as the 17th and most recent addi-
tion to the committee. The full list of ICCVAM agencies is 
listed in Table 22.1. These regulatory and research agencies 
require, use, generate, or disseminate toxicological and safety

22.1.2.1  Re-envisioning ICCVAM
For much of the first 12 years of ICCVAM, activities were 
focused on formal evaluations of test methods through peer 
reviews of validation studies designed to demonstrate the 
usefulness and limitations of alternative approaches. This 
involved an extensive process of test method nomination, 
validation, and evaluation through NICEATM and ICCVAM 
that ultimately culminated in an ICCVAM test method evalu-
ation report (TMER). Each TMER conveyed ICCVAM rec-
ommendations on (1) test method usefulness and limitations; 
(2) a standardized test method protocol; (3) future studies to 
improve the usefulness and limitations of the test method; 
and (4) performance standards that can be used to assess the 
performance of functionally and mechanistically similar test 
methods. The TMER was then routed through each ICCVAM 
agency for clearance through their agency head such that test 
method recommendations could be published and made pub-
licly available for the stakeholder community. These were 
productive years for ICCVAM, resulting in recommendations 
for alternative approaches applicable to eye and skin irritation/
corrosion, skin sensitization, acute toxicity, endocrine disrup-
tors, and pyrogen testing. Many of these recommendations 
were used as seminal publications supporting internally har-
monized TGs.
However, this was a lengthy process that required years of 
effort to develop recommendations that in some cases were 
met with limited stakeholder uptake and implementation. A 
primary challenge was the reality that regulatory agencies 
are required by statute to use toxicology test information for 
a variety of purposes, including labeling and registration, and 
these requirements are not uniform. In reality, some of the

Another driver for reconsidering ICCVAM operations was 
the National Research Council report entitled, Toxicity Testing

the disconnect between their test method evaluation process 
and its inability to keep pace with rapid scientific advance-
ments that were occurring in biomedical research.
These events culminated in 2013 when a new approach to 
ICCVAM operations was announced [19]. This new approach

22.1.2.2  Strategic Roadmap for Establishing 
New Approaches to Evaluate the Safety 
of Chemicals and Medical Products
The next few years of ICCVAM activities were driven by the 
principles described above. The concept of developing a stra-
tegic roadmap to establish new approaches for toxicity test-
ing in the United States was proposed at the 2015 SACATM 
meeting and further developed at the 2016 SACATM meet-
ing. ICCVAM subsequently developed the key elements of the 
roadmap over the next year, which included multiple opportu-
nities for public stakeholder input and feedback. In 2018, the 
ICCVAM report, A Strategic Roadmap for Establishing New 
Approaches to Evaluate the Safety of Chemicals and Medical 
Products in the United States was published to serve as the 
directional principles under which ICCVAM will operate going 
forward. The Roadmap describes three key processes that must 
be established and integrated: (1) connecting end-users with the 
developers of NAMs; (2) fostering the use of efficient, flexible, 
and robust practices to establish confidence in new methods; 
and (3) encouraging the adoption and use of new methods and 
approaches by federal agencies and regulated industries.
Ultimately this approach should expedite the development 
and use of NAMs that provide information more relevant to 
human health than existing animal-based methods. It pro-
vides a conceptual framework to support the development, 
evaluation, and use of NAMs and facilitate communication 
and collaboration within and between governmental agencies, 
stakeholders, and international partners. It is anticipated that 
this will be a living document that is updated as appropriate to 
remain current with modern approaches.
22.1.3  Validation of NAMs
Before NAMs may be used for regulatory decision making, or 
even as part of research and development pipelines or chemi-
cal prioritization steps, they must be validated. The traditional 
validation process is outlined in the OECD guidance docu-
ment on the validation and international acceptance of new 
or updated test methods for hazard assessment (OECD GD34) 
[21], which outlines the process methods must undergo to 
be accepted as an OECD TG and by OECD member coun-
tries under Mutual Acceptance of Data (MAD). Validation is 
defined in OECD GD34 as “the process by which the reliabil-
ity and relevance of a particular approach, method, process, 
or assessment is established for a defined purpose” [21]. The 
reliability of a method covers both its intra-laboratory repeat-
ability and its interlaboratory reproducibility, while relevance 
refers to “the extent to which the test correctly measures or 
predicts the biological effect of interest” and also considers 
“accuracy”, i.e., the concordance of the results from the new 
test with results from the reference standard. Traditionally, 
this has entailed a “ring trial”, where the method is transferred 
between different laboratories which all test the same sets 
of blinded chemicals to assess the method. This approach to 
validation is also described in the “Validation and Regulatory 
Acceptance of Toxicological Test Methods”, published by 
ICCVAM in 1997 [22].
Comparison to a reference standard is fundamental to the 
concept of validation. Therefore, understanding the level of 
variability of a reference standard, and reasons for the observed 
variance, allows for a better characterization of the capabilities 
of the standard and creates appropriate expectations about the 
performance of NAMs that are compared against them. Many 
recent publications have curated and analyzed large datas-
ets of existing regulatory standard in vivo reference tests for 
exactly this purpose and have demonstrated a high degree of 
variability particularly with respect to regulatory classification 
schemas. Examples include eye irritation [23], skin irritation 
[24], endocrine disruption [25, 26], and acute systemic toxicity 
[27, 28] endpoints. Efforts to understand and quantify this 
degree of inherent variance in the reference standards have 
resulted in proposed confidence intervals that could be applied 
to NAM evaluation [29].
While the OECD documents and the original ICCVAM 
1997 guidance have provided an important foundation for 
validating NAMs, the concept of validation has evolved sub-
stantially over the last decade to acknowledge that one stan-
dardized process may not fit every scenario. New ideas have 
emerged related to the development of NAMs and the evalua-
tion of their utility, namely the concept of “qualifying” a NAM 
for a particular “context of use” (also known as fit-for-purpose 
validation). This has evolved substantially from the traditional

validation approach and necessitates moving beyond reliance

qualify a NAM ultimately depends on the detailed descrip-
tion of the purpose. Furthermore, validation by evolving con-
fidence is an important aspect, as agencies may adopt assays 
at various stages, for various purposes, and contexts of use can 
broaden as assays are used, experience is gained via case stud-
ies and applications, and more data are generated.
The ICCVAM strategic roadmap provides a high-level guide 
for establishing scientific and regulatory confidence in NAMs. 
It also encourages agencies to investigate new approaches to 
evaluate the robustness of NAMs without relying solely upon 
in vivo animal reference data to define performance, focus-
ing upon human mechanistic understanding [30]. In 2020, the 
U.S. EPA published its work plan for the adoption of NAMs, 
which includes developing a scientific confidence framework 
to evaluate the relevance and reliability of NAMs by the third 
quarter of 2022 [1]. The European Commission Joint Research 
Centre (JRC) published a commentary on the different avail-
able approaches to establishing scientific confidence and has 
curated a list of credibility criteria to allow for comparison 
between different predictive toxicology approaches [31]. One 
commonality between many recently proposed frameworks is 
the need to move toward assessing the validity of a NAM based 
on the relevance to human biology [32–34]. This approach was 
demonstrated in a recent publication that evaluated the abil-
ity of in vitro, ex vivo, and rabbit test methods to assess eye 
irritation/corrosion by focusing on the human anatomical eye 
structure and the mechanisms that occur in the development of 
eye irritation in humans [35].
22.1.4  Global Efforts to Advance Alternatives
There are many international organizations created to foster 
communication among countries around the topic of alter-
natives to animal testing and to encourage cooperation in 
areas such as validation studies, independent peer review, 
and development of harmonized guidelines and recommen-
dations. The International Cooperation on Alternative Test 
Methods (ICATM) [36] was originally established in 2009 
among ICCVAM, the European Union Reference Laboratory 
for Alternatives to Animal Testing (EURL-ECVAM); [37], 
the Japanese Center for the Validation of Alternative Methods 
(JaCAVM) [38], and Health Canada in partnership with 
the Canadian Centre for Alternatives to Animal Methods 
(CCAAM) [39]. The Korean Center for the Validation of 
Alternative Methods (KoCVAM) [40]; formally joined 
ICATM in 2011, and other countries such as China, Brazil, 
Singapore, and Taiwan have participated in recent workshops 
and activities. ICATM promotes voluntary international coop-
eration, collaboration, and communication among national 
validation organizations to ensure the optimal design and con-
duct of validation studies to support national and international 
regulatory use of alternative methods, to ensure high-quality 
independent scientific peer reviews of NAMs, to support the 
development of harmonized recommendations by national 
validation organizations, to achieve greater efficiency and 
effectiveness by avoiding duplication of effort and leverag-
ing limited resources, and to support the timely international 
adoption of alternatives.
Representatives from ICCVAM agencies, ICATM partners, 
and in total 37 member countries, participate in the devel-
opment and national review of chemical testing guidelines 
issued by the TGs Programme of the OECD [41]. Networks 
of national coordinators and national experts provide input 
from scientists in government, academia, and industry to con-
tinuously update OECD TGs which are used in a regulatory 
safety testing context and accepted by the 37 OECD member 
countries under the MAD system. This legally binding frame-
work states that tests that have been conducted according to an 
OECD TG, under good laboratory practices, must be accepted 
by all OECD member countries, avoiding duplicate testing and 
creating a common basis for international cooperation. The 
MAD system is estimated to save governments and industry 
around 309 million Euros (EUR) each year, and substantially 
reduces the number of animals used for chemical safety test-
ing [42]. There are targeted OECD expert groups formed for 
topic areas such as skin sensitization, ecotoxicology, eye irrita-
tion, nanomaterials testing, and quantitative structure–activity 
relationship modeling.
There are many comprehensive efforts on developing and 
applying alternatives within the European Union under the EU 
Framework Programme for Research and Innovation, Horizon 
2020 funding umbrella. EU-ToxRisk, an “Integrated European 
‘Flagship’ Programme Driving Mechanism-based Toxicity 
Testing and Risk Assessment for the 21st century”, began in 
2016 with funding for 6 years, covering a wide range of case 
studies applying alternative methods and next-generation risk 
assessment practices [43]. Three additional multi-partner 
research projects were funded in 2021 for 5-year durations. 
PrecisionTox aims to identify molecular key event (KE) bio-
markers that predict chemically induced adverse health effects 
in humans using nonsentient animal species to replace mam-
malian models, determine safety factors based on genetic 
variability, and facilitate their uptake into regulatory and 
industry practice. The ONTOX (ontology-driven and artificial 
intelligence-based repeated dose toxicity testing of chemicals 
for next-generation risk assessment) consortium is focused on 
developing a generic strategy to predict systemic repeated dose 
toxicity effects using NAMs addressing adverse outcomes in 
the liver (steatosis and cholestasis), kidneys (tubular necrosis 
and crystalopathy), and developing brain (neural tube clo-
sure and cognitive function defects). The vision of the RISK-
HUNT3R (RISK assessment of chemicals integrating Human 
centric next generation testing strategies promoting the 3Rs) 
consortium, coordinated by Leiden University, is to provide 
a fully human-centric risk assessment strategy encompassing 
exposure estimation, information on chemical distribution in 
the body, hazard characterization, adverse outcome prediction, 
and human (disease) genetics and exposome data.

22.2  Key Approaches
22.2.1  In Vivo Alternatives
In addition to the obvious ethical concerns often associated 
with in vivo mammalian toxicology studies, these studies 
can also be very costly and time consuming. While high-
throughput in vitro and computational approaches offer the 
possibility of evaluating large numbers of compounds much 
more efficiently, these methods still suffer from the potential 
shortcoming of not fully recapitulating the developmental, 
physiological, and disease processes observed in the whole 
animal. For this reason, significant focus has been directed 
toward alternative animal models using phylogenetically lower 
species that could be considered replacement alternatives for 
mammalian systems.
22.2.1.1  Invertebrates
For decades, a common tool for environmental biomonitor-
ing and toxicity screening has been invertebrate models, 
ranging from shellfish to sponges to earthworms. They pro-
vide an opportunity to survey potential bioaccumulation and 
toxicities of environmental chemicals using a wide variety 
of cellular, physiological, behavioral, and morphological 
endpoints [48–50]. However, they are also a useful predictive 
tool in human health safety assessments that offer an in vivo 
model that offers higher throughput and causes fewer ethical 
concerns from an animal welfare perspective. The nematode 
Caenorhabditis elegans is often used as an alternative model 
organism in high-throughput toxicological screening [51].
22.2.1.2  Aquatic Vertebrate Models
Perhaps some of the most promising approaches to evaluat-
ing potential toxicities without using mammalian species are 
those involving fish models, particularly small fish models like 
zebrafish (Danio rerio). Such methods offer many advantages 
that are both practical and scientific. They generally have 
low maintenance and husbandry costs, high fecundity, and 
provide substantial genetic diversity [52]. Additionally, fish 
models used in the embryonic stages have even further ben-
efits associated with animal welfare stages. The NIH Office 
of Laboratory Animal Welfare (NIH OLAW) [53]; considers 
aquatic models “live, vertebrate animals at hatching”. Test 
methods based on embryonic fish are not required for inclu-
sion in the Animal Requirements section of an Animal Study 
Protocol. Furthermore, NIH OLAW considers zebrafish larvae 
younger than 8 days post-fertilization to be incapable of expe-
riencing pain or distress, thereby supporting their use in lon-
ger term studies without significant animal welfare concerns 
[54]. This is particularly important when considering their use 
beyond short-term exposures, which is one reason they are 
popular choices for developmental toxicology research and 
testing approaches. Zebrafish also have external fertilization 
that can result in hundreds of offspring produced weekly, and 
because of their small size, they can be plated in microplates 
for higher throughput assay formats [55]. Internal organs and 
structural effects can be visualized stereoscopically because 
of the translucent chorion and embryo [56]. Zebrafish are 
amenable to transgenic manipulation to visualize developing 
structures and their susceptibility to perturbations from chem-
ical exposures [57]. And perhaps most importantly, zebrafish 
screening assays show good correlation to mammalian models, 
particularly in the area of developmental neurotoxicity [58].
22.2.2  In Vitro Alternatives
The Oxford English dictionary defines in vitro as “performed 
or taking place in a test tube, culture dish, or elsewhere out-
side a living organism”. Clearly, the phrase “in vitro alterna-
tive” encompasses a huge variety of test method types and 
approaches. Such assay systems can be relatively simple 
approaches comprised of a single monolayer of adherent cells 
in a culture dish. These cells may be primary cells obtained 
directly from a tissue of interest (e.g., primary keratinocytes 
harvested from a dermatomed skin sample) with a limited life 
span and passage number, or they could be transformed by 
viral vectors to yield an immortalized cell line with a theoreti-
cally infinite life span. However, such modified cell types can 
be prone to genetic instability and thus drift toward altered 
phenotypes relative to their original form. Cells can be co-
cultured with other cell types to leverage interactions that

produce a more physiologically relevant environment than 
when cultured alone.
Popularized initially by the pharmaceutical industry for 
lead compound identification, HTS approaches have been 
increasingly applied to the identification of potential toxicants 
in other sectors, particularly where there is so little toxic-
ity information available (e.g., industrial chemicals). These 
assays offer the promise of providing high-throughput deci-
sion support tools for screening and assessing not only hazard 
but also exposure and risk. They employ robotics to greatly 
increase the volume and speed of testing. As detailed in 
Section 1.5, these efforts have been led by the federal govern-
ment, specifically in a multiagency collaboration among the 
National Institute of Environmental Health Sciences-National 
Toxicology Program (NIEHS-NTP), the NIH’s National 
Center for Advancing Translational Sciences, the EPA’s 
National Center for Computational Toxicology, and the FDA, 
collectively referred to as Tox21 [59, 60]. The Tox21 program 
provides an efficient approach to prioritizing substances for 
more extensive toxicological evaluation and identifies poten-
tial toxicity-associated and disease-associated pathways based 
on mechanistic information. Most of the assays are based on 
human cells intended to better predict how substances will 
affect biological responses in humans [47].
Increasing complexity can be realized through production 
of three-dimensional (3D) models that more closely mimic 
the tissue of interest and even recapitulate conditions that are 
similar to an in vivo microenvironment [61]. Some of the sim-
plest examples of such approaches are epithelial cell systems 
cultured on a submerged monolayer on a synthetic membrane 
and then raised to the air–liquid interface to create a strati-
fied, differentiated epithelial surface. Such models have been 
produced for both skin and lung epithelia, the latter of which 
even possess beating cilia just like cells in the human airway.
Stem cells have often been used as an in vitro model and 
have become increasingly popular due to their intrinsic prop-
erties of self-renewal, infinite proliferation, and the capacity 
for differentiation into multiple tissue cell types [62]. While 
more than 100 human embryonic human cell lines have been 
derived, their use has largely been focused on studies associ-
ated with understanding the pathophysiology of genetic dis-
ease, regenerative medicine, and a variety of other clinical 
applications [63]. However, they have been a very powerful 
tool for identifying potential developmental toxicants because 
the formation of the embryoid body is considered to represent 
the key aspects of gastrulation [64]. The ethical controversies 
associated with the use of embryonic stem cells have driven 
the continued development and evaluation of induced pluripo-
tent stem cells for screening and testing approaches relevant to 
toxicology. Their use has been particularly widespread in the 
areas of developmental and cardiac toxicity [65].
Three-dimensional cell culture systems also construct cell–
cell and cell–extracellular matrix interaction networks that 
play a vital role in maintenance of normal cellular properties 
and mechanisms [61]. Cells cultured in suspension can aggre-
gate and assemble into spheroid cultures. These cultures facili-
tate the aforementioned cell–cell and cell–extracellular matrix 
interaction networks and more closely replicate the in vivo 
microenvironment, thereby improving viability and overall 
performance [66]. For example, the development of new and 
improved liver toxicity screening assays has largely focused 
on hepatocellular spheroids that show improved hepatocyte 
differentiation, longevity, and functionality compared with 2D 
cultures [67].
More recently, advances in bioengineering have resulted 
in the design and development of physiologically relevant 
MPS that are microsystems capable of recreating aspects of 
human organ physiology and function. While not yet at the 
stage of practical application in routine toxicology testing, 
MPS (also known as “tissue chips” or “organs-on-a-chip”) 
can be designed to recapitulate human (or other animal) tis-
sues as a multicellular environment applicable to a variety of 
toxicology endpoints [68]. Initially, these systems were largely 
organ-specific cell types co-cultured in a microenvironment 
that fostered the cell–cell interactions noted above, particu-
larly with regards to facilitating metabolic competence and 
understanding the importance of mechanical stress on the nor-
mal physiology of certain types [69, 70]. But the fruits of col-
laborations among bioengineers, toxicologists, and physicians 
were quickly realized in more sophisticated MPS that mimic 
organ systems that can more fully interrogate potential toxici-
ties of the cardiovascular, pulmonary, excretory, dermal, and 
nervous systems [71–74]. As technology continues to advance 
and practical challenges are addressed to make MPS more 
approachable for routine testing, one can envision a pivotal 
role of MPS in the identification of potential toxicities without 
using animals.
22.2.3  In Chemico Alternatives
Methods that are not derived from living organisms and mea-
sure chemical reactivity are known as “in chemico” methods. 
These abiotic approaches can be applied in toxicology testing 
based on the assumption that chemical reactivity and toxic-
ity are directly proportional as covalent interactions between 
a chemical and a biological molecule result in toxicity [75]. 
Reference to such methods and their application to sensiti-
zation reactions can be found as far back as the 1930s [76, 
77]. However, they have seen a significant uptick in interest 
since the start of the 21st century as our understanding of the 
molecular initiating event associated with the adverse outcome 
pathway (AOP) for allergic contact dermatitis has been solidi-
fied. These methods and their application are more thoroughly 
detailed in Section 3.4.
22.2.4  In Silico Alternatives
An emerging class of alternatives are those which typically 
do not require experimental testing and instead rely upon 
computer models to make predictions about chemical toxicity 
based on inherent features of the chemical, such as molecu-
lar structure or physicochemical properties, called “in silico” 
methods. As computational power increases and publicly avail-
able databases are becoming better annotated and accessible, 
approaches such as machine learning and artificial intelligence 
are increasingly playing a larger role in synthesizing large 
datasets to build predictive models. For example, Quantitative

Structure–Activity Relationship (QSAR) models are trained 
on large numbers of chemicals with existing information, to 
identify complex, nonlinear relationships between chemi-
cal structure and toxicity, and then used to make predictions 
about untested chemicals. There are many types of molecu-
lar descriptor libraries and machine learning algorithms that 
can be applied to building such models, but one particularly 
effective approach has been demonstrated to be using multiple 
methods and combining them in a collaborative fashion. This 
consensus modeling framework has been successfully applied 
to endocrine disruption [78, 79] and acute systemic toxicity 
endpoints [80]. More information on QSAR and in silico pre-

22.3  Regulatory Testing and 
Available Alternatives
Recently, a great deal of effort has been focused on finding 
animal replacement or reduction alternatives for the “six-
pack” of acute toxicity test methods required for pesticide 
products, and often provided for other types of chemicals [81], 
and used by a range of regulatory agencies for risk assessment 
and decision-making purposes [82]. This package of in vivo 
studies encompasses the evaluation of acute systemic toxic-
ity by three exposure routes: oral, dermal, and inhalation, and 
three acute topical toxicity endpoints: eye irritation/corrosion, 
skin irritation/corrosion, and skin sensitization. In 2016, the 
U.S. EPA Office of Pesticide Programs released a Letter to 
Stakeholders [83] discussing EPA’s development of a process 
for identifying and adopting alternatives to the traditional in 
vivo acute toxicity studies. Substantial progress has been made 
toward the replacement of animal tests for each of these end-
points, as described in the following sections.
22.3.1  Acute Systemic Toxicity
Acute systemic toxicity tests are used to assess the potential 
lethality of chemicals and formulations following oral, der-
mal, or inhalation routes of exposure. The traditional test 
method uses rats or mice to estimate the chemical dose or 
concentration that would be lethal to 50% of animals tested 
(i.e., the LD50 or LC50) [84–86]. Data generated in these tests 
are used for a wide range of regulatory purposes including 
to determine if substances need protective packaging, warn-
ing labels, environment release guides, and require protective 
equipment. Given the multiple mechanisms associated with 
chemically induced lethality, a complete replacement for the 
in vivo test method has yet to be developed. Initial 3Rs efforts 
for this endpoint focused on reduction and refinement alterna-
tives that resulted in new and updated TGs that required much 
smaller numbers of animals and looked for ways to end the test 
early to reduce significant pain and distress. More recently, 
significant research efforts have focused on the development of 
mechanistically based nonanimal testing approaches that may 
provide more human-relevant data than in vivo rodent models, 
as well as enhancing understanding of toxicity mechanisms.
22.3.1.1  Acute Oral Toxicity
As noted above, initial 3Rs efforts were principally focused 
on refinement and reduction alternatives. For acute oral toxic-
ity, new guidelines that minimized the number of dose groups 
and the number of animals used overall were implemented in 
harmonized TGs [87–89]. Multiple guidance documents were 
developed by international expert groups to harmonize termi-
nology associated with humane endpoints and provided guid-
ing principles that would minimize animal use and limit pain 
and distress [90, 91]. In fact, these alternatives were imple-
mented globally such that, in 2002, the original TG adopted 
in 1987 (OECD TG 401) was deleted by the OECD Council. 
In vitro cytotoxicity methods were also explored as screening 
approaches that would define in vivo starting doses, thereby 
obviating the need for in vivo dose range finding studies. An 
international interlaboratory validation study resulted in an 
ICCVAM recommendation to use these methods in a weight-
of-evidence approach to determine starting doses for acute 
oral systemic toxicity test methods. However, they could not 
be used on their own to determine the hazard classification 
of chemicals, and thus were not a complete replacement. 
The European Union Reference Laboratory for Alternatives 
to Animal Testing-European Centre for the Validation of 
Alternative Methods (EURL-ECVAM) further recommended 
it in a weight-of-evidence approach to support identifying 
substances that would not be classified for acute oral toxic-
ity under CLP (Classification, Labeling and Packaging of sub-
stances and mixtures) and REACH (Registration, Evaluation, 
Authorisation and Restriction of Chemicals) regulations [92]. 
This approach is also referenced in REACH guidance for use 
in a weight-of-evidence approach to satisfy acute oral toxic-
ity requirements [93]. An OECD guidance document is also 
available with the intent of harmonizing approaches to be used 
[94]. However, despite these recommendations and guidances, 
in practice, it appears that these methods have gained little 
traction.
Over the past decade, efforts have increased toward identi-
fying practical and predictive approaches for acute systemic 
toxicity. Non-testing approaches, including waiving tests and 
calculating the acute lethality of mixtures based on the LD50 
values of mixture components (i.e., the Globally Harmonized 
System of Classification and Labeling of Chemicals [GHS] 
mixtures equation), are also being used, especially at the 
extreme ends of the lethality spectrum (in the very toxic or 
non-toxic classification ranges). A recent study conducted in 
conjunction with the U.S. EPA demonstrated that the GHS 
mixtures equation is promising for identifying substances 
expected to induce minimal toxicity [95]. Additional efforts 
have focused on international collaborations aimed at iden-
tifying key areas to explore to better understand the mecha-
nisms of acute lethality and in turn to develop and implement 
approaches that target such mechanisms [96–98].
Perhaps most promising in this area are newly developed in 
silico approaches. Leveraging large, curated datasets of legacy 
in vivo acute oral toxicity data, computational approaches pro-
vide the promise of building high performing predictive mod-
els based on chemical structural similarities. Computational 
approaches such as QSARs are increasingly recognized

22.3.1.2  Acute Inhalation Toxicity
Acute inhalation toxicity tests identify substances that could 
cause illness or death after a single inhaled exposure. This 
endpoint includes challenges associated with dosimetry and 
the potential impact of airway exposures to aerosols, vapors, 
dusts, or mists [99]. Like acute oral toxicity, multiple OECD 
TGs are available that describe in vivo approaches to assess-
ing acute inhalation toxicity [85, 100, 101]. The original 
study design was harmonized in OECD TG 403, which now 
includes recommendations for minimizing animal usage. 
For example, when the test article is known or expected to 
be minimally toxic, a single-dose group can be used that is 
exposed to the limit concentration (or maximum attainable 
concentration if a limit concentration cannot be attained), 
generally for 4 hours [85]. There are also reduction and refine-
ment alternatives to the traditional study design that use fewer 
animals by following a stepwise procedure to fixed concentra-
tions or incorporates evident clinical signs of toxicity prior to 
lethality [100, 101].
Multiple in vitro approaches have been suggested to predict 
acute inhalation toxicity, but there still is no single harmonized 
test system considered adequate to fully replace inhalation 
exposure studies [102]. Additionally, anatomical and physi-
ological differences exist between rodent and human respira-
tory tracts (e.g., breathing mode and ventilation rates, airway 
architecture and branching pattern, cell types, and composi-
tion within the regions of the respiratory tract). As a result, 
differences in metabolism and the deposition pattern of an 
inhaled substance can vary significantly, which impacts not 
only the initial dose in the portal of entry but also the specific 
pathways by which inhaled material is cleared and redistrib-
uted [103, 104].
Mathematical models have been developed to address the 
challenges associated with inhalation dosimetry. For estimat-
ing particle deposition in humans and laboratory species, mul-
tiple-path particle dosimetry models allow predictions of the 
inhaled dose based on the specific exposure scenarios [105, 
106]. Computational kinetic models also allow the determina-
tion of in vitro doses delivered to cells in culture [107, 108] or 
to predict air-particle dynamics in vivo [109].
While monolayer culture systems exist, a more human-
relevant approach that allows direct exposure of the cultured 
cells of the respiratory tract at the air–liquid interface to cre-
ate reconstructed human airway models is needed. A com-
prehensive review of the state of the science of nonanimal 
approaches for acute inhalation toxicity testing details many 
of these approaches and provides recommendations on the 
next steps toward the development and implementation of 
such approaches [110]. This review describes new approaches 
under development to target specific mechanisms of toxicity 
and incorporate relevant dosimetry depending on the physi-
cal form of the material being tested. Importantly, the authors 
summarize the AOPs available for nonanimal methods that 
can be used to assess the KEs leading to lethality following 
inhalation exposure. Such AOPs are critical to identifying 
and addressing knowledge gaps and optimizing nonanimal 
approaches that can be used to investigate KEs of toxicity 
[104].
22.3.1.3  Acute Dermal Toxicity
Another common route of exposure is through the skin. 
Chemicals considered to be relevant to this route (e.g., agro-
chemicals) are evaluated in acute dermal toxicity tests for their 
potential to cause lethality following topical exposure. Hazard 
classification systems based on dermal LD50 values are associ-
ated with specific signal words and hazard statements. These 
categories are also associated with specific recommendations 
for personal protective equipment to mitigate skin exposures.
Much like the acute oral toxicity test, 3Rs approaches have 
largely focused on protocol modifications to achieve reduc-
tion and refinement of animal use, which is reflected in the 
most recent version of the OECD TG, adopted in 2017 [86]. 
In achieving these modifications, multiple study designs were 
considered and a biometrical analysis was conducted to assess 
and compare their relative performance [111]. This included 
alterations of the study designs described for acute oral toxic-
ity including a Fixed Dose Procedure (FDP), a modified Acute 
Toxic Class design, and the Up-and-Down Procedure [88, 89, 
112]. The dermal FDP was the highest performing design and 
used the fewest number of animals, so a stepwise procedure 
was added to OECD TG 402, with the use of up to three ani-
mals of a single sex per step [86].
While nonanimal methods have yet to be developed to 
identify potential toxicants by the dermal route, significant 
progress has been made to identify opportunities for waiving 
the requirement for testing at all. Physicochemical properties 
can certainly be used to establish whether or not a substance 
is likely to traverse the stratum corneum and reach the sys-
temic circulation, thereby presenting a systemic toxicity risk. 
Where such a determination cannot be made, or where der-
mal hazard needs to be assessed, data from acute oral toxicity 
studies could potentially be used to assign relevant categories 
without conducting additional in vivo studies via the dermal 
route. A retrospective evaluation based on several hundred 
agrochemicals (representing both AIs and final formula-
tions) tested in both acute oral and dermal toxicity studies 
was conducted to determine whether acute oral toxicity data 
from pesticide formulations and AIs can be used in lieu of 
acute dermal toxicity data for the purposes of dermal hazard 
classification and labeling. With very few exceptions, these 
evaluations demonstrated that acute oral hazard categories 
are sufficiently protective as replacements for acute dermal 
hazard classifications of pesticides [113]. These results were 
used as the basis for waiver guidance published by EPA for 
agrochemical registrants [13, 14].

22.3.2  Acute Eye Irritation and Corrosion
For more than 75 years, the in vivo rabbit eye test has been used 
to identify the potential for eye irritation and corrosion asso-
ciated with topical exposure to chemicals and other products 
[114]. In the past three decades, numerous in vitro methods 
have been brought forward as potential nonanimal alternatives, 
several of which are available as internationally harmonized 
TGs for regulatory use (OECD TGs). Many of these methods 
have been comprehensively evaluated in international, multi-
laboratory validation studies to demonstrate their usefulness 
and limitations. However, there has yet to be a single method 
or approach considered sufficiently predictive of human ocular 
injury to be used as a complete replacement for the traditional 
rabbit test [115]. More recently, efforts have focused on bet-
ter characterizing the limitations of the rabbit test as a refer-
ence comparator for new approach methods. These include the 
inherent variability of the rabbit test, differences between rab-
bits and humans in ocular anatomy, and differences in topical 
exposures in the rabbit test versus what might be experience 
in human exposure. Ultimately, it is expected that a combined 
approach that leverages the strengths of multiple methods 
described below will be necessary to achieve full replacement 
given the multiple mechanisms of the eye irritation response. 
For example, in 2015, the EPA published an accepted alterna-
tive testing approach for evaluating the eye irritation potential 
of certain pesticide products using a decision tree structure 
based only on in vitro and ex vivo assays [116]. A compre-
hensive summary of the available methods for eye irritation 
testing, and their relevance to the mechanisms of chemically 
induced eye irritation in humans is detailed [35].
Monolayer cultures of human and animal cell types provide 
a relatively simple approach to eye irritation testing, albeit 
with the obvious limitation of requiring aqueous solubility 
of the test substance (or inclusion of a vehicle that augments 
solubility) to be used in such a system. These methods are 
typically based on induced cytotoxicity or other alteration of 
cell function following exposure to a test substance at a spe-
cific time interval. Depending on the endpoint, they are scal-
able to a higher throughput format to facilitate more efficient 
screening. Many are based on monolayers of corneal cells, but 
several other mammalian cell types have also been used as 
predictive models. Those that have been adopted as interna-
tional TGs are approved for identifying severe eye irritants/
corrosives and substances that do not require hazard classi-
fication because they are considered nonirritating to mini-
mally irritating, but not for substances predicted to be mildly 
or moderately irritating. The Short Time Exposure test mea-
sures cytotoxicity in a confluent monolayer of rabbit corneal 
cells (Statens Seruminstitut Rabbit Cornea cells) based on 
changes in cell viability as a correlate to eye irritation poten-
tial [117]. Decreased cell viability corresponds to increased 
severity of eye irritation potential. Cell viability is assessed 
quantitatively based on the blue formazan salt produced by the 
living cells following enzymatic conversion of the vital dye 
MTT (2,5-diphenyl-2H-tetrazolium bromide), also known as 
Thiazolyl Blue Tetrazolium Bromide. The neutral red release 
assay uses the extent of membrane damage in normal human 
epidermal keratinocytes (NHEK) as a cytotoxic endpoint to 
predict the severity of eye irritation potential. The vital dye 
neutral red accumulates within lysosomes of cells upon expo-
sure in culture medium. Cell membrane disruption follows 
exposure to cytotoxicants which in turn results in neutral red 
release [118]. Similarly, the fluorescein leakage assay detects 
changes in membrane permeability to sodium fluorescein in 
Madin-Darby Canine Kidney CB997 tubular epithelial cells. 
Increased cellular uptake of sodium fluorescein corresponds to 
increased severity of eye irritation potential [119].
Cellular metabolism is tightly coupled to ATP usage and 
the extracellular release of acidic byproducts. Cytotoxicants 
disturb the metabolism of cells, thereby changing the rate at 
which these metabolic byproducts are released by the cell 
which can be measured by changes in the pH of the culture 
medium surrounding the cells. Accordingly, using a pH meter 
to detect changes in acidity, the Cytosensor Microphysiometer 
test method quantifies the concentration of test substance that 
reduces by 50% the acidification rate (MRD50) in mouse L929 
fibroblasts. MRD50 is considered to be inversely proportional 
to the severity of eye irritation potential of a test substance 
where a lower MRD50 corresponds to more severe eye irrita-
tion potential [120, 121].
Reconstructed human corneal models are histologically, 
morphologically, biochemically, and physiologically similar 
to the human corneal epithelium. Since these tissues are cul-
tured at the air–liquid interface, they avoid the requirements 
for aqueous solubility in monolayer cultures and therefore pro-
vide an assay system that can evaluate both mono-constituent 
substances and final formulations. Several functionally and 
mechanistically similar methods are now available and are 
part of an internationally adopted TG [122]. The validated 
reference methods are comprised of either normal human 
corneal epithelial cells, transformed human corneal epithelial 
cells, or NHEK. These test methods are all based on damage 
to the epithelial cell barrier function and cell death indicated 
by changes in cellular metabolic rate (e.g., as indicated in the 
MTT assay described above). Data reported using the interna-
tionally harmonized methods are the reduction in viability rel-
ative to the negative control group and the extent of potential 
ocular damage is inversely proportional to the potential sever-
ity of eye irritant response. Additional study designs allow for 
a time-to-toxicity to be established to further delineate relative 
potency based on the time required to reach a certain thresh-
old of viability reduction, often set at 50% (i.e., resulting in an 
ET-50) [123].
Ex vivo models are also commonly used to evaluate poten-
tial eye irritants using ocular tissues obtained from slaughter-
house animals such as chickens, cows, and pigs. The bovine 
corneal opacity and permeability (BCOP) assay uses bovine 
corneas dissected from whole eyes that are then mounted 
in enclosed holders and maintained in culture medium. The 
extent of corneal opacity is measured using an opacitometer 
and fluorescein penetration across the corneal surface is mea-
sured spectrofluorometrically. These quantitative measure-
ments of corneal opacity and permeability are combined into 
an In Vitro Irritancy Score, which is directly proportional to 
the irritancy level of the test chemical [124]. Histopathology 
can also be used to identify potential irritants that were not 
detected using the BCOP quantitative scoring. This has been

As its name suggests, the isolated chicken eye (ICE) test 
method uses ex vivo chicken eyes that are mounted on spe-
cialized whole globe eye holders to provide short-term main-
tenance of normal conditions [125]. The ICE test method 
measures chemically induced corneal swelling, corneal opac-
ity, and corneal permeability, but unlike the BCOP, which is a 
quantitative assay, two of the three ICE test method endpoints 
are qualitative. Test and control eyes are evaluated using a slit-
lamp microscope. Corneal opacity and fluorescein retention 
are scored with a subjective scoring system, whereas corneal 
swelling is measured quantitatively with an optical pachym-
eter and is expressed as a percentage. The resulting scores are 
combined to assign an overall prediction [126]. Although not 
included in a standardized TG, a similar method has also been 
developed that uses rabbit eyes [127]. Just as in the BCOP, his-
topathology of the ocular tissues has been shown to improve 
test method performance.
The porcine corneal opacity/reversibility assay (PorCORA) 
uses ex vivo porcine corneas that are maintained at the air 
interface on a soft agar:culture medium matrix. This allows 
the porcine corneal cells to be sustained for 21 days and dam-
age to the epithelial barrier function is detected using a sodium 
fluorescein stain. Accordingly, similar to the study design of 
the in vivo rabbit test, PorCORA provides the opportunity to 
evaluate reversibility of any adverse effects over an extended 
period post-exposure [128, 129].
Assays developed based on protein denaturation have also 
been successfully demonstrated as useful approaches to pre-
dict the eye irritation potential of certain types of chemicals. 
These tests (e.g., the Ocular Irritection and Optisafe test meth-
ods) measure the extent of denaturation within a molecular 
matrix exposed to a test substance [87, 130]. The matrix is 
comprised of lipids, proteins, glycoproteins, and low molecu-
lar weight substances that are considered to be a biochemi-
cal model of the cellular components of the cornea. The test 
system is not a “live biological system” and therefore can be 
performed in any laboratory and does not require cell culture 
facilities. The tests also offer the practical advantages of an 
objective measurement (via optical density readings) that can 
be used as the decision criteria to identify eye irritation poten-
tial (and potency).
A fundamental challenge to the adoption and implementa-
tion of alternatives for eye irritation and corrosion testing is 
the reference data against which new approaches are com-
pared (i.e., the rabbit eye test). While internationally harmo-
nized TGs are available for methods that can identify either 
end of the eye irritation spectrum (ocular corrosives or mini-
mally irritating/nonirritants), there have yet to be any complete 
replacements adopted due to their apparent inability to iden-
tify mild and moderate eye irritants. However, recent efforts to 
characterize the variability of the rabbit eye test, which is less 
than 50% reproducible for mild and moderate eye irritation 
categories [23], have brought to light consideration for chang-
ing the paradigm for evaluating these new approaches. Efforts 
are underway to better define a metric for establishing confi-
dence in new approaches that considers both the variability of 
the reference test method, along with the human relevance and 
mechanistic basis in determining the usefulness and limita-
tions of proposed alternatives [35].
22.3.3  Acute Skin Irritation and Corrosion
Skin irritation describes chemically induced reversible dam-
age to the skin, whereas chemically induced skin corrosion 
is irreversible damage characterized by necrosis and scar-
ring. Just as is the case for eye irritation testing, skin irrita-
tion testing has included an in vivo rabbit skin test since the 
early 1940s [114]. And similar to the situation for eye irritation 
testing, there are no nonanimal methods that are considered a 
complete replacement for the existing animal test for all regu-
latory applications. However, unlike testing for eye irritation, 
alternative methods for skin corrosion are typically different 
than those intended for identifying less severe skin irritants. 
Many of these methods are internationally adopted TGs that 
have been used for many years for a variety of internal and 
regulatory decision scenarios.
Several test methods are currently included in the OECD 
health effects TGs program. These include methods that uti-
lize biochemical membrane barrier systems, ex vivo rat skin, 
and reconstructed human epidermal constructs [131–133].
The membrane barrier systems have been developed for 
predicting skin corrosion based on the time it takes for a test 
substance to break through the membrane, which is inversely 
proportional to the severity of the response (i.e., the most cor-
rosive chemicals break through the membrane in the shortest 
amount of time). Similar to the ocular models mentioned above, 
the membranes are comprised of lipids, proteins, glycopro-
teins, and low molecular weight substances that are intended 
to model the stratum corneum barrier of the skin. As they are 
artificial systems, these methods can also be performed in any 
laboratory without the need for cell culture facilities because 
as artificial systems, they do not require sterility. And, like 
the ocular methods, they use an objective measurement that 
can be used as the decision criteria to identify skin corrosion 
potential (and potency) [132]. Importantly, these assays can be 
used to identify corrosive chemicals as well as subcategorize 
corrosive test chemicals based on potency according to the UN 
GHS and U.S. Department of Transportation (DOT) classifica-
tion systems.
This transcutaneous electrical resistance (TER) assay relies 
on skin discs collected from harvested rat skin [131]. The assay 
is based on the premise that skin corrosives produce a loss 
of normal stratum corneum integrity and barrier function that 
can be measured by decreases in TER below a defined thresh-
old. As many as 15 skin discs can be obtained from one rat, 
thereby significantly reducing the number of animals required 
for this endpoint, and eliminating pain and stress associated 
with in vivo testing. While the rat skin TER can discriminate 
between corrosive and non-corrosive substances, it cannot fur-
ther subcategorize corrosive substances according to national 
and international regulatory requirements.
The 3D reconstructed human epidermal models use human 
cells cultured at the air interface to recapitulate the viable epi-
dermis, complete with an intact stratum corneum. They typi-
cally include nontransformed human keratinocytes that grow

into a stratified epithelium. These models closely mimic the 
histological, morphological, biochemical, and physiological 
properties of the upper parts of the human epidermis. Their 
utility is based on the principle that corrosive chemicals pene-
trate the stratum corneum by diffusion or erosion and are cyto-
toxic to the cells in the epidermal cell layers. These methods 
measure changes in cellular metabolic rate after a fixed expo-
sure (e.g., MTT assay) to discriminate between non-corrosives, 
and subcategories of corrosive substances. Chemicals that do 
not reduce tissue viability below a certain threshold can be 
considered non-corrosive, but additional testing is required to 
determine if there is any irritation potential. At least five meth-
ods have been developed and validated, which are all included 
in the OECD TG [133].
Chemically induced skin irritation is marked by damaged 
cells that either release inflammatory mediators or induce 
an inflammatory cascade. The same Reconstructed Human 
Epidermis (RhE) methods used for skin corrosion have been 
adapted for use in identifying skin irritation potential of chem-
icals and formulations [134]. While the RhE models them-
selves are identical histologically to those used for identifying 
skin corrosivity potential, modified protocols are used to mea-
sure the initiating events in the inflammatory cascade (i.e., cell 
damage), again using cell viability as readout. The principal 
difference between the corrosivity and irritation protocols is 
the chemical exposure time and the length of post-exposure 
incubation before cell viability is measured. At least six com-
mercially available RhE test methods have been developed for 
identifying potential skin irritants.
Similar to the situation with eye irritation testing, skin 
irritation testing has yet to benefit from a complete replace-
ment in some jurisdictions (e.g., U.S. EPA) that avoids alto-
gether the use of animals. Again, the challenge to establishing 
such a replacement has been the mild and moderate irritants. 
Therefore, similar analyses to those with in vivo rabbit eye test 
data have been conducted on in vivo rabbit skin test data, and 
again, the rabbit test falls short of reproducing itself in these 
categories more than 50% of the time [24]. Efforts to fully 
characterize the human relevance and mechanistic basis of 
available and future skin models will be needed to best deter-
mine the usefulness and limitations of proposed alternatives 
without using in vivo data.
22.3.4  Skin Sensitization
Allergic contact dermatitis, or skin sensitization, is a prevalent 
occupational and public health concern, affecting an estimated 
20% of the population [135]. In 2013, OECD published an AOP 
for skin sensitization linking molecular initiating events and 
cellular and tissue effects in the sensitization process [136]. 
Since then, several in vitro (cell-based) and in chemico (cell-
free) testing methods for assessment of dermal hypersensitivity 
mapped to KEs in the AOP have been validated in interna-
tional interlaboratory ring trials. Currently, there are OECD 
TGs based on four KEs of the AOP: (1) covalent binding with 
skin proteins (haptenation) measured by in chemico assays, 
e.g., the direct peptide reactivity assay (DPRA) [137]; (2) acti-
vation of the Keap1/Nrf2 pathway by electrophilic substances, 
as monitored by the antioxidant response element assay, e.g., 
KeratinoSensTM [138]; (3) dendritic cell activation, which mod-
els presentation of new antigens (hapten/carrier complexes), 
e.g., via the human cell line activation test (hCLAT) [139]; 
and (4) lymphocyte proliferation, as measured by the in vivo 
murine local lymph node assay (LLNA) [140]. The LLNA is 
currently the regulatory standard for assessing skin sensitiza-
tion potential, although the guinea pig test method [141] is still 
requested under specific circumstances (e.g., for certain medi-
cal device products).
At this stage, none of the individual in vitro or in chemico 
methods are accepted as stand-alone replacements for the 
LLNA, but combinations of these methods (so-called DAs) 
have been developed and were submitted to the OECD as case 
studies. These DAs providing chemical hazard and potency 
prediction range from simple rule-based decision trees to 
more complex machine learning algorithms that represent 
nonlinear combinations of data from the in vitro/in chemico 
approaches, chemical structural features, and physicochemi-
cal properties. Work performed in collaboration with the 
Cosmetics Europe industry consortium demonstrated that the 
DAs provide superior performance to the existing animal tests 
when compared to human data [142]. While no individual in 
vitro test can recapitulate the hypersensitivity response in its 
entirety, these analyses demonstrated that integrated strategies 
using varying combinations of in vitro, in chemico, and/or in 
silico methods (predictions from computer programs) could be 
highly accurate in identifying potential skin sensitizers. Based 
on this work, the U.S. EPA released a draft science policy in 
2018 announcing the acceptance of multiple skin sensitiza-
tion DAs as replacements for laboratory animal testing [143]. 
The two DAs included in the EPA science policy (Figure 22.1) 
are the “2 out of 3” approach, which provides a prediction for 
hazard identification based on majority concordant results of 
assays covering the first three KEs of the AOP, e.g., the DPRA, 
KeratinoSensTM, and hCLAT, and the sequential testing strat-
egy “STS”, which predicts hazard based on assays mapping to 
KE 3 and KE 1.
Beginning in 2017, an OECD project led by the United 
States, Canada, and the European Union (EU) was established 
to curate high-quality reference datasets of unprecedented size 
and to develop a novel internationally harmonized DA guide-
line. The ICATM used the DAs for skin sensitization as a case 
study to develop an assessment framework for establishing 
confidence in DAs, which can be applied to many additional 
endpoints [144]. The OECD guideline on DAs for skin sensiti-
zation, the first phase of which covers simple rule-based DAs, 
was adopted in 2021 as the first of its kind and provides full 
replacement alternatives to the existing in vivo methods [145]. 
The validated DAs contained in the OECD guideline (Figure 
22.1) were extensively characterized and shown to either pro-
vide the same level of information or be more informative than 
the murine LLNA (OECD TG 429) for hazard identification 
(i.e., sensitizer versus non-sensitizer) and for GHS potency cat-
egorization. The “2 out of 3” DA is equivalent to that found in 
the EPA science policy, described above, and the integrated 
testing strategy “ITS” DA predicts hazard and GHS potency 
categorization based on the DPRA, hCLAT, and an in silico 
computer model prediction. Other DAs for future consider-
ation provide quantitative point of departure estimates, e.g.,

22.4  Repeat Dose Chronic Endpoints
While substantial progress has been made toward the full 
replacement of animal methods for acute toxicity endpoints, 
the situation is more complicated for endpoints that repre-
sent chemical contributions toward chronic health condi-

manifest over specific life stages following repeat exposures, 
like developmental or reproductive toxicity. These diseases 
involve multiple mechanisms and pathways, both adverse and 
compensatory, interacting in complex ways over longer time 
scales. This necessitates more complicated models to repre-
sent this level of biological complexity, which has tradition-
ally only been possible using animal studies. However, some 
approaches break down these multifaceted disease endpoints

22.4.1  Developmental and Reproductive Toxicity
As detailed in Sections 1.5 and 2.2, publicly available HTS 
datasets are now available for widespread in vitro profiling 
of bioactivities across large inventories of chemicals, and 
substantial work has gone into mapping these HTS assays to 
mechanistic targets and developmental modes of action [150]. 
Coupling this tremendous amount of mechanistic data with a 
more in-depth understanding of molecular embryology and 
developmental signaling pathways facilitates using NAMs to 
evaluate chemical toxicity, drug efficacy, and safety assessment 
for embryo-fetal development. Platforms such as embryonic 
or induced pluripotent stem cell assays [151], in silico virtual 
tissues models [152], mechanistically informed testing batter-
ies for endpoints like developmental neurotoxicity [153], small 
model organisms like zebrafish [57], and developmentally 
targeted MPS [154], just to name a few examples, are being 
developed and applied to address a wide range of research 
and regulatory needs and decision contexts. A comprehensive 
review of the state of the science on NAMs in developmental 
and reproductive toxicity may be found in [155].
One example where NAMs have been successfully imple-
mented in regulatory decision processes relevant to devel-
opmental and reproductive toxicity testing is in the area of 
endocrine disruption. The U.S. EPA Endocrine Disruptor 
Screening Program (EDSP) was established in the late 1990s 
to address both potential human and ecological effects by 
examining chemical perturbations on estrogen, androgen, and 
thyroid hormone-related processes, all of which are important 
for normal development and reproduction. The validated bat-
tery of assays for Tier 1 (screening) and Tier 2 (establishing 
dose-response and adversity) consists of five in vitro and six in 
vivo tests for Tier 1, and five additional in vivo tests for Tier 2. 
Recognizing that it was not logistically feasible to screen thou-
sands of chemicals in such a resource intensive battery, the 
EPA began to investigate other NAM-based approaches to 
fulfill the EDSP mandate. The ToxCast ER Pathway model, 
part of the Tox21 research program, is a computational model 
combining multiple in vitro assays along the estrogen signal-
ing pathway that provides a weight-of-evidence assessment of 
whether a chemical is estrogenic or not [156]. Using a per-
formance-based approach, this model was validated against 
the existing regulatory standard in vivo method for screening 
chemicals for estrogenic activity, the rodent uterotrophic assay 
[46]. This model is now accepted by the EPA as a replacement 
for three of the Tier 1 assays, including the uterotrophic, and 
is also accepted by the European Chemical Agency (ECHA) 
and European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) as the pre-
ferred data source for indicating an estrogenic modality for a 
chemical. Following a similar approach, multiple assays were 
integrated into a computational model for androgen receptor 
pathway activity [157] and compared to data from the rodent 
Hershberger test [158], and this work is under consideration for 
its utility in the EDSP and elsewhere.
There are substantial international activities in the area of 
NAM development and evaluation under the auspices of the 
OECD for developmental and reproductive toxicity, particu-
larly for endocrine disruption [25]. Another example is the 
development of integrated approaches for testing and assess-
ment (IATA) of developmental neurotoxicity that rely on a 
combination of multiple data sources (e.g., in vitro, in silico, 
and non-mammalian in vivo models) that are supported by 
mechanistic knowledge organized according to the AOP 
framework [159]. Disruption of the retinoid signaling path-
way at various stages leads to developmental effects on most 
tissues, including lineage determination of pluripotent stem 
cells; morphogenesis of the heart and lungs; segmentation of 
the neural tube and vertebral column; growth of the limb-buds 
and genital tubercle; orofacial and palate morphogenesis; mat-
uration of neurosensory systems (eye, ear); and gametogenesis 
in adults; hence, this has been an area of active focus in alterna-
tives as well [160]. In the field of pharmaceutical development, 
a recent guideline on reproductive toxicology: “Detection of 
toxicity to reproduction for human pharmaceuticals – step 5 
from the International Council for Harmonisation of Technical 
Requirements for Pharmaceuticals for Human Use (ICH S5 
R3)” describes qualification of alternative assays, including 
recommended reference substances found in Annex 2 [161], 
potential scenarios of use and provides options for deferral of 
developmental toxicity studies as part of an ITS to support the 
inclusion of women of childbearing potential prior to Phase 3 
clinical trials [162].
22.4.2  Cardiovascular Toxicity
Cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of death for people 
of most ethnicities in the United States and represents one of the 
most pressing public health concerns. While some contribut-
ing factors have been relatively well-characterized and include 
lifestyle choices, genetic factors, and off-target effects from 
various pharmaceuticals, there is accumulating evidence for 
the contribution of environmental chemicals to cardiovascular 
disease burden. Many cardiotoxic effects of pharmaceuticals, 
including heart arrhythmia (QT prolongation), change in blood 
pressure, altered cardiac contractility, or cardiomyocyte/myo-
cardial injury, are identified in nonclinical animal models, but 
others such as stroke, myocardial infarction, and heart fail-
ure are more challenging to detect, underscoring the need for 
better in vitro and in silico screening and testing programs. 
Recently, the U.S. FDA committed to a major departure from 
the status quo by initiating and utilizing the Comprehensive 
In Vitro Proarrhythmia Assay program for evaluating adverse 
cardiac events with in vitro data from engineered human cells 
and stem cell-derived cardiomyocytes together with in silico 
reconstruction of human cardiac action potential [163]. A divi-
sion of the NTP has recently initiated a health effects inno-
vation program centered around detecting and understanding 
environmental chemical contributions to cardiotoxicity, with a 
strong focus on alternative in vitro and in silico approaches as 
key components of a translational toxicology pipeline. Recent 
work applied the Tox21 HTS data, mapped by molecular and 
cellular targets to six cardiovascular failure modes, to iden-
tify cardio-relevant bioactivity signatures and screen over a

thousand chemicals using a “CardioToxPi” approach [164]. 
This approach identified drugs with known cardiotoxic effects

cardiovascular targets.
22.4.3  Carcinogenicity
Perhaps the most complex chronic endpoint with evidence 
for environmental chemical contributions in the field of toxi-
cology is cancer, and while 2-year rodent bioassays remain 
the regulatory standard at this stage, there are robust efforts 
to develop NAMs that target mechanistic pathways and pro-
vide the ability to screen chemicals for carcinogenic poten-
tial. Recent research on the ability of chemicals to act via 
multiple biological pathways, including immunosuppres-
sion, inflammation, endocrine disruption, and mutagenesis, 
provides strong support for the potential for chemicals and 
chemical mixtures to influence carcinogenesis [165, 166]. 
The Key Characteristics (KCs) of Carcinogens is a systematic 
method for evaluating mechanistic data to support conclu-
sions regarding human hazards from exposure to carcinogens 
that was developed by the International Agency for Research 
on Cancer (IARC) during a series of workshops in which an 
international expert group identified ten KCs, one or more of 
which are commonly exhibited by established human car-
cinogens [167]. These KCs can be informed upon by in vitro 
mechanistic assays and contribute to cancer hazard assess-
ments being performed by IARC [168]. Similar approaches 
are also being implemented by the U.S. EPA’s Integrated 
Risk Information System Program and the U.S. National 
Toxicology Program. More recently, this approach was com-
bined and contrasted with the hallmarks of cancer, biologi-
cal pathway perturbations, and phenotypes exhibited across 
cancers, to map available assays and biomarkers and identify 
data gaps, particularly those that would be ripe for alternative 
method development [169]. In 2016, the OECD established an 
expert group to develop an integrated approach to the testing 
and assessment of non-genotoxic carcinogens [170]. Various 
cancer models were developed using the AOP concept, over-
arching mechanisms and modes of action were identified, and 
a call for assays to help build out such an IATA is currently 
open.
22.5  Computational Resources
Fundamental to the development of reliable alternative meth-
ods that can eventually serve as replacements for animal 
tests are robust datasets that are readily accessible by both 
humans and machines. To support the development of robust, 
integrated testing strategies and chemical assessments, these 
datasets contain information ranging from chemical structural 
features, to physicochemical properties, to in vitro screening, 
to reference data from legacy in vivo animal studies, and much 
more. To best support effective toxicological research and 
decision making, data should adhere to the “FAIR” principles 
(Box 1), meaning that datasets and data objects (e.g., analysis 
code, computational models) should be Findable, Accessible, 
Interoperable, and Reusable [171].

22.5.1  FAIR Data Repositories
There are many databases under active development for stor-
ing and accessing FAIR data resources, and a few examples are 
detailed below. NICEATM has built the Integrated Chemical 
Environment (ICE) [172], which is designed to provide intui-
tive access to high-quality (curated) data and computational 
workflows to support chemical evaluations, data integration, 
informatics analyses, and model development [150, 173]. ICE 
contains data and/or model predictions on ~800,000 chemi-
cals, and a limited subset of chemical mixtures, derived from 
validation studies, systematic literature reviews, the Tox21 
HTS data, and many other sources. Users can query by user-
provided chemical IDs or established reference chemical 
lists, and results are provided in a flexible, exportable com-
puter-friendly format as well as a human-friendly summary 
format for quickly combining datasets and comparing data 
between chemicals. The data in ICE are curated and anno-
tated, particularly the HTS data, which has been mapped by 
mechanistic target and mode of action to provide biological 
context to those unfamiliar with the assay platforms. Using 
standardized chemical identifiers, connections are provided 
to other data resources such as the NTP’s Chemical Effects 
in Biological Systems (CEBS) [174] database. The U.S. EPA’s 
CompTox Chemicals Dashboard [175] provides a wealth of 
information in a high-quality, structure-curated, easy-to-use, 
open-data format to meet the various needs of the toxicology 
and environmental sciences communities, with a focus on sup-
porting alternatives assessments [176]. The EPA Dashboard is 
built upon a database of curated substances linked to chemi-
cal structures and uses this cheminformatics layer to integrate 
diverse data types, including physicochemical, environmen-
tal fate and transport, exposure, usage, in vivo toxicity, and 
in vitro bioassay data. The Comparative Toxicogenomics 
Database [177] is a robust, publicly available database that 
provides manually curated information about chemical–gene/
protein interactions, chemical–disease and gene–disease rela-
tionships, with over 45 million toxicogenomic relationships for 
over 16,000 chemicals in the 2021 version [178]. These data 
are integrated with functional and pathway data with the aim 
to understand how environmental exposures affect human 
health and develop hypotheses about the mechanisms under-
lying environmentally influenced diseases. In the EU, the 
eChemPortal [179] is a collaborative effort between the OECD 
and the European Chemicals Agency, with contribution from 
governments and other stakeholders. The eChemPortal pro-
vides free public access to information on chemical properties, 
exposure and use information, and toxicity data on existing 
chemicals, new industrial chemicals, pesticides, and biocides, 
including national/regional classification results according to, 
for example, the GHS.
These important data resources are intended to democra-
tize access for non-experts, further education, and training in 
NAMs, and enable the wider scientific community to engage 
in the use of alternative and computational approaches for 
assessing chemical safety. The use of controlled vocabular-
ies and ontologies (e.g., OBO Foundry [180], National Cancer 
Institute [NCI] Metathesaurus [181], Gene Ontology [182], 
Monarch Initiative [183], and standardized reporting formats 
(e.g., OECD Harmonized Templates [184], Clinical Data 
Interchange Standards Consortium [185]) ensures that multi-
ple datasets from different species, technology platforms, and 
study types can be optimally joined and leveraged to better 
develop testing strategies that provide mechanistic insight into 
chemical perturbations of health and disease pathways.
22.5.2  Computational Models and Workflows
Many of the resources detailed above include tools and work-
flows designed to support the application of alternatives in 
computational toxicology analyses for chemical hazard and 
risk assessment. In the ICE tools section [186], the in vitro to 
in vivo extrapolation workflow allows the user to translate in 
vitro activity concentrations to predicted external exposures 
and can be run with ICE data or user data via the template pro-
vided online. The Physiologically Based Pharmacokinetics 
tool predicts internal tissue-level concentrations resulting 
from in vivo doses, allowing users to simulate experiments 
or real-world human exposures. The chemical characteriza-
tion tool allows for comparison between user-defined lists, 
or for comparing user-selected chemicals with large chemi-
cal libraries, by displaying distributions of physicochemical 
properties, bioactivity- and structure-based projections, and 
consumer product use case information derived from the U.S. 
EPA’s Chemicals and Products Database [187]. Other tools 
provided by the NTP include Tox21BodyMap [188], which 
maps in vitro assay targets to the tissues in the body where 
those genes are preferentially expressed, allowing the user 
to develop hypotheses about target tissues where a chemi-
cal might cause biological activity based on their patterns of 
effects in almost 1,000 HTS assays [189]. ChemMaps [190] 
provides an interactive visualization environment for project-
ing chemical space and investigating properties of similar 
chemical neighborhoods [191]. The EPA CompTox Chemicals 
Dashboard [192] hosts a suite of tools as well, including the 
ability to generate exposure predictions for different demo-
graphic populations, the Generalized Read-Across function 
[193] which allows for identification and evaluation of simi-
lar compounds, and the PubMed Abstract Sifter [194] litera-
ture searching tool where results from a PubMed search are 
imported into an Excel sheet where the end-user can then use 
a novel “sifter” methodology for quick, agile relevance rank-
ing of articles and tagging functionality.
As briefly described in Section 2.4, QSAR models rep-
resent a powerful approach to link chemical structural and 
molecular features with toxicity predictions. The OECD has 
developed a guidance document with a validation framework 
for QSAR models [195], and based on these principles, the 
EURL-ECVAM JRC established a harmonized QSAR model 
reporting format (QMRF) to ensure standardized information 
is available across different modeling approaches. The JRC 
QSAR Model Database [196] is a historical archive provid-
ing information on submitted QSAR models and associated 
QMRFs that can be used to identify valid QSARs, e.g., for 
regulatory purposes. There are many examples of QSAR 
modeling suites, both open-source and commercially avail-
able, that are used to support chemical assessments. The 
OECD and ECHA have led the development of the QSAR

1232
Hayes’ Principles and Methods of Toxicology
Toolbox [197], a free software application that supports 
reproducible and transparent chemical hazard assessment, 
offers functionalities for retrieving experimental data, simu-
lating metabolism, and profiling properties of chemicals. 
NICEATM and the U.S. EPA have collaborated to develop 
the Open Structure–Activity/Property Relationship App 
(OPERA). OPERA is a free and open-source/open-data suite 
of QSAR models providing predictions for physicochemi-
cal properties, environmental fate parameters; absorption, 
distribution, metabolism, and excretion (ADME) properties, 
and toxicity endpoints [198]. All OPERA models are built on 
curated data and QSAR-ready chemical structures standard-
ized using an open-source workflow, and technical and per-
formance details are described in OECD-compliant QMRF 
reports. OPERA follows the five OECD principles for QSAR 
modeling to provide scientifically valid, high-accuracy mod-
els with minimal complexity that support mechanistic inter-
pretation when possible. Predictive models yield prediction 
accuracy estimates and applicability domain assessments, 
and OPERA also provides a tool for standardizing chemical 
structures and experimental values when available. OPERA 
predictions are available through ICE and the EPA CompTox 
Chemicals Dashboard, and the OPERA application can be 
downloaded from the NIEHS GitHub repository [199] as a 
stand-alone command-line or graphical user interface for 
Windows and Linux operating systems.
22.6  Future Perspectives
This chapter provides an overview of many of the recent 
developments in the field of alternatives and NAMs, but it is 
by no means an exhaustive tome of the tremendous number 
of efforts ongoing globally in this continuously expanding 
field. Looking toward the future, the AOP conceptual frame-
work, linking molecular to cellular, to tissue, to organism 
level effects, has the potential to fundamentally transform 
the way we address toxicology by emphasizing mechanis-
tic understanding of chemical effects within the context of 
human biology and toxicity pathways. Within the OECD 
“AOP-Wiki”, there are over 340 proposed AOPs in various 
stages of development or review, covering a huge range of 
biological endpoints, many relying upon alternative methods 
to provide the data for chemical assessments. Many of these 
will go on to form the basis for IATA, which may then evolve 
into regulatory guidelines that can replace animal testing 
for endpoints with sufficient mechanistic understanding and 
scientific confidence. Novel technologies such as next-gen-
eration sequencing, high-content imaging, MPS, artificial 
intelligence/machine learning, and multi-scale computa-
tional models are rapidly evolving. While it is challenging 
for regulatory policies to keep pace with cutting-edge sci-
entific developments, a coordinated effort among all stake-
holders will ensure that the field of alternatives continues to 
grow and meets the needs of protecting human health and the 
environment while upholding the 3Rs principles and mov-
ing away from a reliance on animal models toward a human 
biology-based approach.
