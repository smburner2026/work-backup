7.1  Introduction to Systems Toxicology
Systems toxicology is a subdiscipline of systems biology that 
aims to provide a holistic view of biological processes by using 
systems-wide molecular measurements, which are commonly 
termed “omics”, such as genomics, proteomics, lipidomics, 
and metabolomics to cite a few. Computing omics data from 
different biological systems and experimental conditions has 
led to better characterization of molecular interactions and 
networks and their roles in cellular processes. Furthermore, 
the availability of a large number of omics datasets that are 
freely accessible from public repositories has resulted in new 
data modeling frameworks, including prediction algorithms, 
data-driven network models, and pharmacokinetics modeling, 
among others.
Systems toxicology aims to leverage systems biology tools 
to better characterize the physiological and molecular pertur-
bations associated with biologically active substances or their 
metabolites [1].
Systems toxicology approaches combine various omics 
data, computational methods, functional measurements, and 
prior knowledge to gain mechanistic, quantitative, and predic-
tive insights into the effects of test substances. In contrast to 
standard toxicology test batteries, which often result in binary 
decisions, systems toxicology approaches aim to provide 
probabilistic results with more comprehensive and integrative 
outcomes.
Omics technologies generate a large amount of data, giv-
ing rise to a new need for data management, traceability, and 
sharing. Management of data generation and transformation 
processes is particularly important in systems toxicology stud-
ies conducted in the context of safety assessment.
From a regulatory perspective, systems toxicology 
approaches need to be standardized and validated in order 
to be considered reliable tools for quantifying the probability 
of harmful effects. To this end, several initiatives have been 
undertaken at various levels. For example, several United 
Stated (US) federal agencies, including the Food and Drug 
Administration (FDA) and National Center for Computational 
Toxicology, have initiated the Tox21 program, which aims to 
develop and translate innovative methods for better character-
izing substances that may affect humans and the environment. 
This program mainly focuses on evaluating mechanisms of 
action, developing predictive models, and increasing the usage 
of in vitro systems [2]. Similar frameworks have been recom-
mended in the recent update of the U.S. Toxic Substances 
Control Act [3] and by the European Commission Directorate 
General for Health and Consumer Safety [4].
This chapter will discuss the general considerations for 
designing systems toxicology studies and recent advances in 
experimental systems and exposure methods and describe the 
main omics technologies as well as current trends in computa-
tional methods used for exploiting omics data in the context of 
toxicity testing and health monitoring.
7.2  Design of a Systems Toxicology Experiment
The success of a systems toxicology study mainly depends on 
the robustness of its design. An optimal study design needs to 
take into consideration several factors. First, clear scientific 
questions must be formulated, and the key assumptions that 
are made should be enunciated. These should drive the selec-
tion of the most adequate exposure modalities and biological 
test systems for addressing the scientific questions. Second, it 
is crucial to select the most adequate measurement methods 
and ensure an optimal combination of dose- and time-depen-
dent sampling schedules. Finally, a well-planned experiment 
is also statistically powered to enable proper interpretation of 
the results.
Ideally, the results from a well-designed experiment should 
achieve at least one of the following goals: (1) determine the 
reactivity (molecular bioactivity) profile of stimuli in order 
to associate them with potential molecular targets in a cell-
type– and tissue-specific context; these profiles can, in turn, be 
linked to possible modes of action; (2) capture specific modes 
of action to discriminate stimuli that are likely or unlikely to 
induce toxicity in humans; (3) identify stimuli that are associ-
ated with a specific mode of action (MOA) and rank them with 
respect to their toxicokinetic potency; and (4) identify stimuli 
associated with a specific MOA and estimate threshold values 
for lethality that can be used to derive an in vivo effect level, as 
a function of dose dynamics.
An optimal experimental design relies on three equally 
important dimensions: (1) the experimental system, (2) expo-
sure regimen, and (3) relevant biological measurements, which 
will be indicative of potential adverse effects (Figure 7.1).
The experimental system is subjected to a standardized 
time- and dose-dependent exposure regimen (Figure 7.1a). A 
zero dose is used to represent control conditions. The treat-
ment design consists of multiple biological replicates to ensure 
optimal statistical power. Additionally, the statistical design 
associated with the execution of the experiment is a crucial 
component for assessing experimental error by handling varia-
tions, eliminating systematic bias, and accounting for extra-
neous confounding variables, including those linked to the 
technology and extraction/isolation steps employed.

7.2.1  Experimental Systems
Data collected from longitudinal clinical studies are the most 
relevant for assessing potential human health risks associated 
with exposure to active substances. However, the potential 
adverse effects related to a stimulus may manifest only after 
decades of exposure, and it is often difficult to conduct such 
longitudinal human studies. Toxicity testing thus relies largely 
on animal models as well as models based on cellular and 
organotypic three-dimensional (3D) in vitro cultures. While 
in vitro and in vivo models may have many shortcomings, 
they can provide robust mechanistic insights when appropri-
ately used. By taking a systematic approach, the gaps between 
in vivo/in vitro systems and human biology can be reduced, 
potentially resulting in the identification of specific biomark-
ers suitable for human studies. Eventually, these mechanisms 
provide a link from the initial exposure to the downstream 
onset of a disease. The recently developed multiorgans-on-
a-chip technology—which combines one or several in vitro 
cultures within a single chip and thus mimics the human 
body microenvironment(s)—is very promising and may help 
improve the reliability and relevance of in vitro data.
7.2.1.1  In Vivo Systems
Traditional toxicological assessment approaches follow 
the OECD (Organisation for Economic Co-operation and 
Development) Guidelines for the Testing of Chemicals. 
However, the combination of omics technologies and conven-
tional toxicology has been shown to be a useful approach for 
improving mechanistic investigations and identifying putative 
biomarkers [5,6].
In relation to chemical hazard/risk assessment, omics tech-
nologies provide tools for improving our understanding of 
mechanisms of toxicity, reducing uncertainty in the grouping 
of chemicals, and developing alternative methods for screen-
ing chemicals. The OECD assists international collaborative 
efforts on molecular screening and toxicogenomics with the 
aim of defining needs and possibilities for their application in

7.2.1.2  In Vitro Systems
Animal models are usually used to assess the toxicologi-
cal impact of certain stimuli. They provide a comprehensive 
view of how a substance interacts with a whole organism. 
Nevertheless, toxicity testing in animals presents considerable 
challenges. It is very expensive, time consuming, and ethically 
controversial. The actual effort to “Replace, Reduce, Refine” 
animal experiments (as stated by Russell and Burch [8]) is 
based on the use of more appropriate cell-based assays that 
could provide relevant data on the effects of short- and long-
term exposures to chemicals. Figure 7.1c represents different 
experimental systems and how they are connected in accor-
dance with the principle of translational systems toxicology 
[9]. One example of in vitro, in vivo, and interspecies extrapo-
lation has been illustrated by Kienhuis et al. [10], who com-
paratively assessed acetaminophen-induced hepatotoxicity in 
rats and in human in vitro cellular systems and, additionally, in 
a rat in vitro and in vivo systems at the level of gene expression.
To increase the relevance of in vitro assays and to further 
mimic physiologically relevant cellular functions, primary 
culture of target-organ–specific cells obtained from human 
donors has been further investigated as a potential surrogate 
to human/animal testing. Primary cells show more preserved 
cell-type–specific functions than immortalized cell lines [11–
13]. In addition, human primary cells also offer the advan-
tage of the possibility to test the response to an exposure in 
cells from different donors, allowing to evaluate the impact of 
genetic variability. To reproduce tissue-specific architecture, 
biochemical and mechanical cues, and cell-cell interactions 
in the tissue microenvironment, a number of in vitro mod-
els have been further developed as 3D tissue reconstruction 
models. The development of new in vitro technologies (such 
as 3D printers) and new composite materials that allow us to 
create biocompatible scaffolds that mimic the organ structure 
or simply the advent of new culture methods (hanging-drop 
method, air–liquid interface (ALI) culture, and ultra-low 
adhesion surfaces) has made it possible to create a 3D in vitro 
equivalents for almost every organ of the human body (see 
Table 7.1).
The key advantage of some 3D models over their two-
dimension (2D) counterparts is that, under physiological con-
ditions, 3D cultures will be composed of multiple cell types 
that are also observed in vivo. Indeed, when normal human 
bronchial epithelial (NHBE) basal cells are cultured at the 
ALI, they differentiate into polarized ciliated and goblet cells 
that will form a pseudostratified epithelium with all the mor-
phological features that define the bronchial epithelium in vivo 
[14]. In addition, cells that form the 3D airway cellular model 
have a comparable gene expression profile to tracheal and 
bronchial epithelial cells that have been collected in vivo by 
brushing human airways [15]. Furthermore, the gene expres-
sion profiles of a human 3D airway model (obtained from 
MatTek™; Ashland, MA, US) exposed to whole cigarette 
smoke or air have been compared by computational methods 
to the in vivo smoking gene signature of bronchial epithelial 
cells obtained from a large group of smokers and nonsmokers 
[16]. The results of this study show that the human organo-
typic bronchial epithelium-like tissue culture exposed to 
cigarette smoke at the ALI presents similar biological pertur-
bations as those observed in the smokers’ airway epithelium. 
Similarly, a study on drug efficacy has shown that 3D cultures 
of endometrial cancer cells better reflect the effect of some 
drugs tested in patients than cultures propagated in 2D [17]. 
Experimental evidence that demonstrates the relevance of 3D 
human organotypic cultures is accumulating, and more work 
will be required to further validate the use of such experimen-
tal models for toxicological assessment. Figure 7.1d illustrates 
the organotypic culture systems that may be used for testing 
tobacco products or other inhalable substances.
A new technology is currently revolutionizing the field of 
in vitro research—the multiorgans-on-a-chip [18]. These small 
devices combine an in vitro model—in general, a 3D culture—
with an engineered microenvironment to recreate the condi-
tions present in the organs (e.g., the stretching in the lungs [19] 
and blood flow in the liver [20]). These devices have allowed 
in vitro models to reach an unprecedented level of resemblance 
to their in vivo counterparts. For example, human epithelial 
duodenal cells seeded within a chip composed of a stretchable 
membrane to recreate the peristaltism observed in the organ 
start forming microvilli as observed in vivo [21].
Another benefit of these devices is the possibility to connect 
multiple cultures that mimic different organ functions. Our 
bodies are composed of multiple interconnected organs, each 
having specific roles and expressing specific proteins. In the 
context of drug safety assessment, it is crucial to consider all 
organs that are potentially impacted by a given drug, as each 
organ may express specific xenobiotic enzymes, resulting in 
the formation of organ-specific metabolites. A high number of 
multiorgans-on-a-chip devices have been developed over the 
last years. Most of them recreate the connection of the liver 
with another organ such as the lungs [22], kidney [23], skin, 
or intestine [24]. Recently, multiorgans-on-a-chip connecting 
three [25] or four [26] organs have been developed, offering 
new tools for studying drug toxicity.

7.2.2  Exposures
An exposure, in the context of predictive mechanistic toxicol-
ogy, is a controlled process during which mass transfer of a 
test substance or a mixture of test substances from an expo-
sure matrix into a biological test system occurs. The exposure 
matrix can, for instance, be a solution, an extract, an aerosol, 
a gas, or a solid that is inhaled, ingested by, or applied to the 
biological test system, which may be a human, an animal, an 
organ, a tissue sample, or a cell culture.
Ideally, the exposure matrix should be well characterized 
chemically, even in the case of a complex mixture. The goal is 
to recreate an exposure regimen—i.e., the dose, frequency, tim-
ing, and overall duration of exposure—that most realistically 
mimics the human situation while being technically feasible. 
It is, therefore, imperative to define a set of standard exposure 
regimens to be applied systematically to the selected experi-
mental systems. Such exposure regimens should be validated 
and verified, at least in part, during the experiment to ensure 
that the expected exposure is actually taking place (e.g., by 
using biomarkers of exposure) and the stimuli are indeed 
delivered to the target site. Furthermore, appropriate biological 
assays and measurements should be used to obtain time- and 
dose-dependent data to capture both early and late events and 
ensure that a representative dose range is covered. If exposure 
information is available or can be estimated for the substance 
to be tested, it should be considered in the study design. This 
step will minimize the chance of unrealistically high doses 
being used that could overwhelm the homeostatic mechanism 
of the experimental system and thus fail to reflect the pathways 
that might be functional at more realistic doses [41].

In the context of in vitro studies, it is crucial to select the 
biological test systems that are representative of the target tis-

systems earlier in this chapter and will now take a look at the 
relevant aspects to be taken into consideration when these sys-
tems are to be exposed to a test substance.
Irrespective of the test substance and biological test sys-
tem, the overall context in which the exposure takes place will 
have profound effects on the phenotypic, transcriptional, and 
secretory properties of cells in vivo and in vitro [43–48]. A 
well-designed in vitro exposure experiment will, therefore, 
aim at simulating the microenvironment in which the expo-
sure takes place in the living organism to the greatest extent 
possible.
The first aspect to take into consideration is whether, in the 
living organism, exposure occurs by mass transfer from a liq-
uid phase (for instance, blood or intestinal juices) into the tis-
sue or at the interface between the tissue and a surrounding 
gas phase (for instance, from inhaled air into lung tissues or 
from an externally applied cream into the skin). Accordingly, 
we can differentiate two modes of in vitro exposures: expo-
sure in the submersed state and exposures at the ALI, which, 
as a rule, should be applied according to the in vivo situation 
to be modeled [49,50]. In the submersed mode of exposure, 
the test substance is dissolved in an appropriate liquid, which 
is then brought into contact with the test system, in the sim-
plest case, by pipetting it into the cell culture vessel. In the 
ALI mode of exposure, one side of the test system is in con-
tact with the cell culture medium (or another nutritive and/or 
supporting matrix), whereas the other side is in contact with 
the surrounding air. Exposure relies on the direct applica-
tion of the test substance to the air-exposed side of the test 
system.
Because application of a test substance to a cell culture sur-
face at the ALI in a uniform and controlled way is challeng-
ing, such exposures are more common of higher experimental 
complexity than exposures in the submersed mode, and, in 
many instances, they rely on more or less complex exposure 
systems [50,51]. In addition, cultivating biological test systems 
suitable for ALI exposure is commonly more time- and cost-
intensive than cultivating submersed cultures. Therefore, sub-
mersed modeling of an exposure that takes place at the ALI in 
vivo may be considered to optimize the cost and throughput 
of a study or if no adequate cell culture system is available. 
However, interpretation of the results will have to take into 
account that: (1) dissolving the test substance in an exposure 
vehicle may have affected its composition and bioactivity and 
(2) the non-physiological exposure mode may have interfered 
with the biological responses and, hence, rendered them less 
representative of the in vivo situation [50,52,53].

Other aspects of the physiological (micro)environment to be 
simulated in vitro include the temperature; oxygen and carbon 
dioxide saturation of the surroundings; chemical composition, 
pH, and osmolarity of the culture medium; relative humid-
ity of the surrounding air (in case of ALI exposure); surface 
properties of the cell culture vessels; and mechanical forces 
acting on the cells, such as shear, tension, and compression 
[44–48,54–56].
In the broader sense, the tested dose range (including the 
dosing frequency and duration) is a part of the (micro)envi-
ronment too and should, hence, be kept in a physiologically 
relevant range. In this context, it is important to note that a 
dose defined as the mass of a test substance applied per unit 
of the test system is not necessarily sufficient. In highly com-
plex exposure scenarios, such as aerosol exposure at the ALI, 
several factors may contribute to the bioactivity of the aerosol 
and, therefore, need to be aligned with the in vivo conditions. 
These include not only the absolute and relative deposition of 
aerosol mass (particle mass deposition on as well as diffusion 
of gases into the test system) but also the deposition surface 
and particle, number, size, and shape.[57,58].
Another example is of test substances that are metabo-
lized in the living organism, leaving behind catabolic inter-
mediates or functionalized molecules that may possess very 
different bioactivity than the test substance itself. In vivo sys-
temic responses may not or may only partly be attributed to 
the original test substance; that is, the absence of biological 
responses in vitro will be a poor indicator of the low bioactiv-
ity of the original substance if metabolism is not taken into 
account.
Parameters such as exposure temperature or the pH and 
osmolarity of the exposure matrix can be controlled with 
relative ease. Simulating the biomechanical and systemic 
complexity of an organism is considerably more challenging 
and cannot be achieved by conventional exposure methodol-
ogies. Accordingly, there have been huge efforts to develop 
new approaches, and the progress made in recent years is 
impressive. Steps have been taken toward developing exposure 
systems that simulate the functional and structural complex-
ity of the respiratory tract, and such systems will ultimately 
allow us to simulate physiological aerosol and gas exposures 
in vitro [59]. Furthermore, new cell culture systems are being 
developed, most notably the multiorgan-on-a-chip systems 
described earlier in this chapter. These provide conditions 
under which cells are able to differentiate and organize them-
selves into complex systems, 3D structures that are highly 
similar to the ones found in the living organism [56,60,61] and 
simultaneously create the physiological microenvironment for 
exposure.
Thus, with the recent progress in the field, the boundar-
ies between cell culture and in vitro exposure are becoming 
increasingly blurry; exposure conditions are created not by the 
act of exposure or by an exposure system, but rather by the 
cell culture system itself; that is, the exposure system becomes 
a part of the cell culture, and the exposure evolves from the 
action of a test substance on a test system toward the inter-
action between the two—both properties being characteristic 
for how living organisms are exposed to external chemical 
stimuli.
7.3  Omics Measurements in Toxicity Testing
7.3.1  Genomics and Transcriptomics
Genomics is the field of research that focuses on studying vari-
ous biological processes associated with the genetic material 
of a given organism. In addition to analysis of DNA sequences 
(e.g., genome assembly, phylogenetics, and metagenomics), 
genomics includes the study of gene transcription, DNA rep-
lication, epigenetic mechanisms, and DNA damage/repair, 
among many other processes. Genomics processes are crucial 
to organism function, and their perturbation by external expo-
sures is associated with many human disorders. For example, 
DNA damage caused by mutagens can induce mutations and 
alter DNA replication, leading to cancer or other disorders 
[71]. In addition to genetic perturbations, a number of diseases 
are associated with epigenomic alterations, including enzy-
matic dysfunctions that may alter chromatin structure and, 
subsequently, genome stability and regulation [72].
The rapid advances in high-throughput sequencing (HTseq) 
technologies and the decrease in sequencing costs make 
genomics applications accessible for large-scale toxicological 
and clinical studies. In this section, we will discuss the main 
genomics endpoints in the context of toxicological studies.
7.3.1.1  Transcriptomics
Transcriptomics, or gene expression profiling, is used to study 
the changes in gene expression in a population of cells or at a 
single-cell level. Currently, transcriptomic analysis is the most 
well-established approach for identifying perturbed pathways 
and gaining mechanistic insights on the response to a stimu-
lus. At present, transcriptomic changes are assessed by two 
main techniques, microarray analysis and RNA sequencing 
(RNA-seq).
The microarray technology is based on the inherent prop-
erty of a DNA molecule to form a stable duplex (hybridization) 
from two complementary strands. In a typical experiment, 
each RNA sample is individually hybridized to a microar-
ray, and comparisons are made between samples at the data 
analysis stage. Microarray-based transcriptomics is now a 
well-standardized approach for both experimental and compu-
tational aspects and is still largely used in clinical and toxico-
logical studies, for which method validation is often required. 
Since 2008, RNA-seq has emerged as an alternative method 
for measuring gene expression levels [73]. RNA-seq involves a 
reverse-transcription reaction to convert RNA to complemen-
tary DNA, followed by adapter ligation, PCR amplification, 
and sequencing (Figure 7.2a). RNA-seq provides information 
about the abundance and sequence of RNA molecules by using 
HTseq technologies. The main advantage of RNA-seq is its 
ability to interrogate different families of RNA (e.g., messen-
ger RNA, non-coding RNA, and transfer RNA) at a genome-
wide level, including annotated and unknown transcripts, 
while microarrays are limited by design to a predefined subset 
of known transcripts. This limitation constitutes an obstacle 
for comparing data generated with different microarray plat-
forms or generations. By contrast, RNA-seq output is simply a

296

read count and, therefore, platform-independent regarding the 
computational aspect. Moreover, RNA-seq data can be reana-
lyzed when new versions of genomes and gene annotations 
are available. RNA-seq can also identify single-nucleotide 
variants, gene fusions, and mutations. As with other sequenc-
ing technologies, RNA-seq is a versatile technique, and new 
variations are being developed continually, paving the way for 
reduced costs and starting amount of RNA as well as adapta-
tion of the technology to large toxicological studies through 
robotic platforms.
Similar to all sequencing data analyses, RNA-seq analy-
sis starts by aligning sequencing reads against the appropri-
ate genome, followed by reading quantification at regions 
of interest (transcripts, exons, introns, etc.). The elementary 
unit of gene expression measured by RNA-seq is simply the 
number of reads overlapping the genomic region of interest. 
These values can be further normalized for sequencing depth 
(total number of reads in a library or at genomic targets) and 
transcript length to calculate the RPKM, FPKM, or TPM val-
ues (reads, fragments, or transcripts per kilobase per million). 
Gene length normalization is important for comparing expres-
sion levels between different genes within the same sample, 
while sequencing depth normalization is required for compar-
ing the expression of a given gene between different samples.
Differential expression between experimental groups can 
be computed by various methods. A number of user-friendly 
packages and pipelines are available for this purpose. For 
example, the DESeq2 [74] and edgeR [75] R-packages, respec-
tively, based on the negative binomial distribution and likeli-
hood ratio tests, are largely used for RNA-seq analysis.

of cells or organisms. Such changes are permanent and will be

large-scale changes that affect the chromosomal structure. 
Deletions and duplications in a chromosome, referred to as 
copy number variations (CNV), are a significant determinant 
of individual differences as well as contributors to many dis-
eases. Recurrent CNVs are the result of homologous recombi-
nation between repeated sequences, and non-recurrent CNVs 
can occur anywhere in the genome through non-homologous 
recombination events [76]. Genetic changes may also confer 
survival and growth advantages to the cell carrying them, as is 
frequently observed during malignant transformations. Even 
though evaluation of DNA damage is often included in toxicity 
assessment, it is also important to further identify the affected 
genes when DNA damage leads to genomic changes.
Genomic changes at the nucleotide level have become 
of increasing interest in recent times. Until now, mutational 
spectra—i.e., the frequency of certain base substitutions to 
occur under given conditions—were limited to small regions 
amenable to targeted sequencing. These included marker 
genes from mutagenesis experiments performed in vitro as 
well as genes known to be involved in cancer, such as p53, 
which could be sequenced from tissue samples [77]. With the 
increase in HTseq capacity in the last decade, mutation finding 
studies have been extended first to whole-exome sequencing 
of mammalian tissue and then to full-genome (re)sequencing, 
thus allowing researchers to resolve almost the entire comple-
ment of mutations in a genome at the nucleotide level [78]. The 
characteristic mutational patterns of mutagens have thus been 
described for key mutagenic compounds such as aristolochic 
acid [79]. Researchers have sequenced the genomes of various 
cancerous tissues and used computational methods to extract 
characteristic mutation signatures from the ensemble of muta-
tions found; some of these can be attributed to the action of 
specific mutagenic substances [80]. HTseq can be applied not 
only to tissue samples but also to the products of in vitro assays 
that produce clonal cell populations, such as clonal colonies of 
mouse embryonic fibroblasts treated with different mutagens 
[81]. Such strategies have been used to identify the mutagenic 
signature of suspected mutagenic compounds on a large scale 
[82]. Within the context of toxicology, the application of muta-
tional signatures may lie with disentangling the molecular eti-
ology of mutational patterns in cancer cells and identifying the 
key mutational processes and associated causative chemical 
agents.
7.3.1.3  Epigenetics
Epigenetics can be defined as the study of genomic mecha-
nisms that regulate genome properties and function without 
involving changes in the DNA sequence. The main epigen-
etic mechanisms include covalent modification of DNA (e.g., 
DNA methylation and hydroxymethylation), posttranslational 
modification of histone proteins, chromatin remodeling, and 
the activity of non-coding RNAs. All these mechanisms can 
be altered by environmental exposures [83] and potentially 
lead to changes in gene expression, genome instability [84], 
or alterations in DNA repair mechanisms [85], which, in turn, 
can trigger the onset of various diseases. Epigenetic features 
are highly dynamic and can undergo rapid and reversible 
changes in response to external stimuli [86]; on the other hand, 
some epigenetic alterations can persist for decades [87], and 
some may even be heritable [88]. Hence, epigenetic changes 
represent promising metrics for evaluating the short- and long-
term impacts of environmental factors.
HTseq technologies have enabled the interrogation of vari-
ous epigenetic marks at a genome-wide level. The use of these 
approaches in the context of toxicological studies has raised 
hopes of developing accurate detection methods and reliable 
biomarkers. In this section, we review the main epigenomic 
features and their application in the context of systems toxicol-
ogy. We describe the experimental and computational work-
flows used to generate, process, and interpret epigenetic data.
7.3.1.3.1  DNA Methylation and Hydroxymethylation
In mammals, DNA methylation and hydroxymethylation 
mainly occur at the 5′-carbon position of cytosine in the con-
text of CpG dinucleotides and are termed 5mC and 5hmC, 
respectively. The addition of a methyl group to cytosine is 
catalyzed by DNA methyltransferases; this reaction is revers-
ible via the activity of the ten-eleven translocation enzymes, 
which convert 5mC to 5hmC and, subsequently, to unmethyl-
ated cytosine. The 5mC is the most frequent DNA modifica-
tion in mammals and is associated with the silencing of DNA 
cis-regulatory elements (RE). However, gene bodies exhibit 
high levels of 5mC regardless of their expression status, 
although, in some cell types, gene body methylation positively 
correlates with gene expression [89,90]. DNA methylation is a 
crucial epigenetic mechanism involved in gene regulation, and 
impairment of DNA methylation patterns is associated with 
various environmental exposure effects [91] (e.g., cigarette 
smoke) and diseases, including cancer [92].
The genomic levels of 5mC and 5hmC can be measured 
by two main sequencing approaches; whole-genome bisul-
fite sequencing (WGBS) [89], which interrogates every 
cytosine in the genome, and targeted bisulfite sequencing 
(BS-seq) that captures a predefined set of loci [93]. Both the 
approaches require bisulfite conversion. During this reaction, 
unmethylated cytosines (C) are converted into uracil and read 
as thymine upon sequencing, while 5mC and 5hmC remain 
unconverted. Bisulfite-based approaches cannot distinguish 
5mC from 5hmC; therefore, the levels measured in standard 
methylome analyses indicate the sum of both modifications. 
Recent methods based on oxidative BS-seq allow research-
ers to distinguish between these two modifications [94,95]. 
The contribution of each modification to the measured signal 
depends on its relative abundance; in the majority of tissues, 
the levels of 5mC largely exceed those of 5hmC. The major-
ity of methylome investigations are based on simple bisulfite 
conversion, with the assumption that 5mC is the major cyto-
sine modification. Here, we describe an analysis workflow 
for BS-seq data, and the term “DNA methylation” will refer 
to 5mC + 5hmC as measured by BS-seq, unless otherwise 
stated.
DNA methylation analysis requires four main steps: (1) read 
alignment to the reference genome; (2) read count at target 
loci; (3) calculation of methylation levels; and (4) computation 
of differential methylation between experimental conditions.

BS-seq data alignment needs to take into consideration the 
C-to-T conversion that results in loss of complementarity with 
the reference genome. After read alignment and counting, the 
methylation level of cytosine is reported as the ratio of the 
number of methylated reads over the total number of reads 
and, therefore, ranges from 0 to 1.
Identification of differentially methylated regions between 
experimental conditions mainly relies on the statistical model 
employed. It is recommended to apply regression models for 
comparing methylation profiles in studies with several bio-
logical replicates. For example, beta binomial distribution is 
largely used in this context, as it can correct for technical sam-
pling and intragroup variability [96,97]. It is also important to 
take into consideration the read coverage at the investigated 
positions, and particular attention should be paid to low-count 
regions.
7.3.1.3.2  Chromatin Organization and 
Histone Modifications
DNA is organized in nucleosomes, which consist of 147 base 
pairs wrapped around histone octamers. Nucleosomes are the 
fundamental unit of chromatin and can be seen as an instruc-
tive scaffold that regulated the usage of DNA molecules. 
Chromatin properties mainly depend on histone posttransla-
tional modifications (hPTM), including acetylation, methyla-
tion, and phosphorylation, among others [98]. The hPTMs play 
an important role in gene regulation, DNA damage/repair, and 
DNA replication. The function of hPTMs depends on their 
chemical nature and location in histone proteins. For example, 
monomethylation at lysine 4 of histone 3 (H3K4me1) is a hall-
mark of enhancers, while trimethylation of the same residue 
(H3K4me3) is enriched in transcribed promoters. Acetylation 
of lysine 9 and 27 (H3K9ac/H3K37ac) is associated with 
active RE. Other hPTMs play central roles in gene silencing, 
such as methylation of lysine 9 (H3K9me2/H3K9me3), which 
is associated with heterochromatin.
The genomic distribution of hPTMs is usually assessed by 
chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) followed by HTseq 
(ChIP-seq) [99]. ChIP-seq is also used to map transcription fac-
tors and enzymes involved in epigenetic modifications. ChIP-
seq is based on immunoprecipitation with antibodies directed 
against the target protein to isolate DNA–protein complexes. 
Upon sequencing of the purified DNA, the resulting reads are 
mapped to the reference genome and used to identify genomic 
loci that are enriched for target proteins. Identification of dif-
ferentially enriched regions between experimental groups is 
mainly based on the difference in read counts. Statistical sig-
nificance is usually computed by using the negative binomial 
distribution (e.g., DEseq2 package [100]).
ATAC-seq (assay for transposase-accessible chromatin 
using sequencing) is an antibody-free method that allows the 
identification of accessible chromatin regions [101]. It is based 
on transposase activity via the insertion of sequencing adapt-
ers in open chromatin regions. Reads generated from these 
adapters are mapped back to the reference genome and used 
to identify enriched regions, in a process similar to that in 
ChIP-seq analysis. ATAC-seq requires only 50,000 cells and 
is, therefore, more suitable for toxicological or clinical studies 
with limited amounts of material.
7.3.1.4  Microbiome and Metagenomics
The human body contains numerous niches in which microor-
ganisms, mostly bacteria and fungi, can thrive. The sum of all 
microbes present in a niche is referred to as the microbiome, 
and its characterization can often help shed light on the health 
status of the human host [102]. It is estimated that the total 
gene set of the human microbiome is at least 150 times larger 
than the set of human genes [103]. In a healthy host, micro-
bial flora will generally consist of commensal species form-
ing a stable community, largely held in check by host defenses 
such as generic bactericidal defense compounds or by the 
immune system [104]. The microbiome can be perturbed, for 
example, by a disease that alters host defenses or in response 
to an environmental insult that leads to oxidative stress. This 
state, termed dysbiosis [105], is characterized either by the 
overgrowth of preexisting, opportunistic pathogens or by the 
appearance of exogenous species and is reflected in the relative 
abundance of the various taxonomic groups within the micro-
biome. While, quantitatively speaking, the gastrointestinal 
tract microbiome is the most important and arguably the most 
studied niche, most parts of the human body that are exposed 
to the environment harbor some form of microbiome, particu-
larly the mucosal surfaces. Determining the composition of 
the microbiome has long been of interest to the scientific com-
munity [106]. While early attempts employed standard micro-
biological methods involving culturing techniques to dissect 
the microbial makeup of microbiomes [107], these methods 
are limited to species that can be grown in vitro. Likewise, 
techniques such as targeted nucleic acid amplification meth-
ods, i.e., quantitative PCR, can detect and quantify individual 
species [108], but they are limited to known species.
Modern sequencing technologies have opened up new 
possibilities in characterizing the microbiome. Sequencing 
approaches can be categorized into two general classes: ampli-
con and shotgun sequencing. Amplicon sequencing requires 
PCR amplification of a target gene or gene region, usually 
the universally present 16S ribosomal subunit [109], while 
shotgun sequencing involves random fragmentation of DNA 
through chemical or physical means. The prepared DNA is 
then sequenced, and the read sequences are processed to infer 
taxonomic or functional information. This assignment uses 
the similarity of the sequences to those in a reference data-
base of known microbial species to assign this information to 
experimental sequencing reads from a given sample [110]. By 
counting the number of reads emanating from taxon, one can 
derive a proxy of its abundance, usually expressed as a scaled 
abundance measure [111], which is very often simply the per-
centage of the total.
The abundances measured by sequencing can be sensitive to 
the methods involved in the analysis [112]. The steps involved 
in sequencing can be subdivided into sample taking, sample 
processing for isolating microbial DNA, optional DNA ampli-
fication, sequencing, and bioinformatics processing, each of 
which can introduce bias in the taxonomic composition mea-
sured [113]: sample storage, preparation, and processing can 
all introduce bias which will influence downstream analysis 
and thus have to be carefully controlled. For amplifying the 
16S ribosomal subunit, the choice of primers influences the

taxonomic range that is discernible during downstream analy-
sis, as certain regions of the gene may be variable among some 
taxonomic subdivisions—and thus contain information for 
resolving subtaxa—but not among others [114]. Finally, bio-
informatics analysis often relies upon knowledge bases, such 
as known gene or genome sequences, which are biased toward 
species that have been easier to identify, cultivate, and inves-
tigate; however, de novo assembly methods of genome recon-

Comparing the abundances of taxonomic groups, or taxa, in 
healthy and diseased states can help identify the characteris-
tic species of a given condition. The gut microbiome displays 
changes upon changes in diet, exercise, and disease status. The 
changes in gut microbiome composition as a consequence of 
chemical insult have been reviewed in detail [116]. Typical 
disease models for inflammatory bowel disease rely on the 
induction of a disease state by subjecting animals to dextran 
(dextran sulfate sodium) treatment, which is related to substan-
tial changes in microbiome composition.
7.3.2  Proteomics
The proteome represents the full complement of the proteins 
produced by an organism [117], and proteomics is a system-
atic approach for characterizing and quantifying all proteins 
in a cell population. The data produced by proteomics tech-
nologies complement the genome-centric view of biological 
networks with protein-specific data and, therefore, hold signif-
icant potential for drug discovery, diagnostics, and molecular 
medicine. Proteomic data can be equally valuable in toxicity 
testing, because the proteome responds readily to various 
stimuli, including those that can lead to adverse health effects 
in humans. Toxicoproteomics sets out to develop tools that 
can identify potentially damaging changes in the proteome, 
thereby further increasing our understanding of a stimulus 
on mechanisms of toxicity. Proteomic technologies can be 
divided into untargeted approaches that provide a global over-
view of the changes in the proteome and targeted approaches 
that precisely measure changes in a preselected panel of target 
proteins.
7.3.2.1  Untargeted Protein Profiling Approaches
Global quantitative protein profiling is commonly supported 
by liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry (LC–MS) 
approaches. In the standard bottom-up workflow, protein sam-
ples are denatured by using a lysis buffer, digested into pep-
tides by using enzymes like trypsin and finally desalted before 
MS analysis. Peptides are separated in LC columns connected 
online to the MS system. To increase the depth of the pro-
teome, specific enrichment strategies that use off-line fraction-
ation can be applied at the protein or peptide level before the 
LC–MS analysis. Figure 7.2b illustrates a standard LC–MS/
MS workflow for global protein profiling.
LC–MS has brought many advances to toxicoproteomics, 
including unsurpassed sensitivity, improved speed, and the 
ability to produce high-throughput datasets. Because of its 
high accuracy, MS can detect peptides in the femtomolar to 
attomolar range in tissues and biological matrices with an 
accuracy level of less than 10 parts per million. This is greatly 
beneficial in comparative analysis, where simultaneous quan-
titative comparisons between control and treated samples are 
key to increasing our understanding of how stimuli affect the 
proteome and can support subsequent identification of signa-
tures [118].
Protein identification and quantification can be performed 
with or without chemical labeling of the different samples. A 
common label-based approach requires peptides to be chemi-
cally labeled with isotopic tags after digestion (for example, 
isobaric Tag for Relative Quantification or Tandem Mass Tag 
[TMT]), after which the samples are pooled before MS anal-
ysis. The main advantages of this label-based approach are: 
(1) simultaneous comparison of up to 16 samples [27,119]; (2) 
reduction in sample number, as samples are pooled before MS 
analysis; and (3) accurate relative quantification between the 
samples on the basis of specific reporter ion fragments gener-
ated by each labeled sample. The limitations of this technique 
are its limited dynamic range, the fact that the protein profiles 
have to be similar, and the lengthy sample preparation process.
In contrast, label-free approaches analyze peptides directly 
by LC–MS. The original methods are based on the data-
dependent acquisition (DDA) mode in the MS, where the MS 
selects the most intense ions (typically the top 10) to be further 
fragmented in MS/MS scans. The main challenges of DDA are 
related to the dynamic range of the instrument and difficulties 
in reliably quantifying the peptides and performing the run-
to-run comparison. To overcome these limitations, the data-
independent acquisition (DIA) mode has been developed as an 
alternative. In this variant of the direct analysis approach, one 
MS scan is followed by wide-window MS/MS scans that sys-
tematically examine a large m/z range. Subsequently, the MS/
MS spectra are matched to spectral libraries for identification 
and quantification. The main advantages of this approach are 
that it: (1) allows comparison of multiple samples; (2) allows a 
broad dynamic range of concentrations; and (3) requires no fur-
ther sample treatment [120,121]. A recent study demonstrated 
the similar performance of the DIA and TMT approaches in 
quantification by using a spiked-in experiment with a known 
amount of standard [122]. Overall, these advantages will likely 
favor the use of DIA over the chemical labeling techniques for 
future systems toxicology assessment studies.

Quantification Approaches
7.3.2.2.1  MS-Based Approaches
As systems biology requires accurate quantification of a 
specified set of proteins across multiple samples, targeted 
approaches such as multiple reaction monitoring (MRM) and 
parallel reaction monitoring (PRM) have been developed for 
biomarker quantification. Samples analyzed by MRM/PRM 
are processed through a standard bottom-up workflow to gen-
erate peptides. Then, the mix of peptides is spiked with known 
amounts of standard isotope-labeled peptides and analyzed 
together with the samples. The ratio between the light (endog-
enous) and the heavy, labeled (exogenous) signals allows accu-
rate quantification of each peptide representing the proteins.

300
Hayes’ Principles and Methods of Toxicology
For MRM, samples are usually analyzed by using a triple-
quadrupole instrument, and each transition (coming from the 
peptide fragmented in the second quadrupole) is analyzed one 
after the other. For PRM, samples are usually analyzed on 
high-resolution MS systems, which allows one to follow all 
the transitions coming from the same peptide at the same time.
An MRM/PRM-based proteomic experiment starts with 
the selection of a target list of proteins on the basis of previ-
ous experiments, scientific literature, or prior knowledge. This 
step is followed by the (1) selection of target peptides that opti-
mally represent the protein; (2) selection of a set of suitable 
transitions for each target peptide; (3) detection of the selected 
peptide in a sample; (4) optimization of other assay parameters 
if some of the peptides cannot be detected; and (5) applica-
tion of the assays for detecting and quantifying the proteins/
peptides.
The major advantages of the MRM/PRM technique are (1) 
multiplexed analysis of 10–100 of protein monitored during 
the same run; (2) absolute quantification of the targets; (3) 
high quantitative reproducibility; and (4) especially for PRM, 
high molecular specificity. The limitations of the technique are 
that (1) only a limited number of measurable proteins can be 
included in the same run (the system is not able to monitor 
thousands of proteins per run or analysis), and (2) its sensitiv-
ity cannot reach the entire proteome space of all organisms 
(limit of detection is at the attomolar level) [123].
The PRM approach has been successfully applied for the 
assessment of e-liquid aerosols [124,125], where the e-liquid 
aerosols showed no significant alteration in the markers for 
xenobiotic metabolism response, oxidative stress, or autoph-
agy, whereas the positive control (3R4F cigarette smoke) did. 
The same approach also revealed fewer alterations in protein 
abundance in several cultures of the aerodigestive tract (buc-
cal, bronchial, and nasal epithelia) after treatment with heated 
tobacco aerosol than after treatment with 3R4F cigarette 
smoke [22,126–128].
7.3.2.2.2  Antibody-Based Approaches
Antibodies are still the most common affinity reagents that 
support the targeted quantification of specific proteins. 
Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assays (ELISA) are one of the 
oldest antibody-based measurement platforms used to detect 
and quantify target proteins in a sample. An ELISA involves 
at least one antibody with specificity for a particular antigen. 
A sample with an unknown amount of antigen is immobilized 
on a solid support either non-specifically or specifically (via 
capture by another antibody specific to the same antigen, in 
a “sandwich” ELISA). After the antigen is immobilized, the 
detection antibody is added, and it forms a complex with the 
antigen. The detection antibody can be covalently linked to an 
enzyme or can itself be detected by a secondary antibody that 
is linked to an enzyme through bioconjugation. Between each 
step, the plate is typically washed with a mild detergent solu-
tion to remove any proteins or antibodies that are not specifi-
cally bound. After the final wash step, the plate is developed 
by adding an enzymatic substrate to produce a visible signal, 
which indicates the quantity of antigen in the sample [129].
Luminex is an antibody-based measurement platform 
that allows multiplexing and exhibits high sensitivity and 
specificity. The principle behind this technology is the ability 
to measure multiple analytes simultaneously in a single-reac-
tion well. With the Luminex technology, molecular reactions 
take place on the surface of microsphere sets that have been 
color-coded by using a blend of two dyes with different fluo-
rescence intensities. The advanced optics capture the color 
signals, which are processed digitally in real time to quantify 
each reaction [130]. With this approach, cytokines have been 
successfully quantified in many systems toxicology studies for 
the assessment of heated tobacco products and e-liquids [125].
Other technologies are also commercially available, like 
Mesoscale Discovery (MSD, Maryland, USA), which is based 
on SULFO-Tag™ labels. Sulfo-Tag labels emit light upon elec-
trochemical stimulation, which allows one to detect the bio-
markers of interest in a single or multiplex format. The main 
advantages of this technology are its very low detection limit 
and very large dynamic range (up to 5 log of the linear dynamic 
range) [131]. Finally, the ultra-sensitive fluorescence-based 
approach developed by SIMOA (Quanterix, Massachusetts, 
USA) allows the detection of fg/mL of target proteins.
7.3.3  Lipidomics
Among eukaryotic cellular metabolites, lipids stand out by 
their sheer number of distinct molecular species. Among the 
thousands of identified and characterized lipid structures, more 
than 2000 lipid species are linked to the homeostasis between 
healthy and diseased states. Lipids are integral structural com-
ponents of cell membranes, function as signaling molecules 
in various cellular cascades, and support energy homeostasis. 
The vital equilibrium of the lipidome—the set of all lipids 
in an organism—is strongly affected by the cellular supply 
of each lipid class. Changes in the composition and organi-
zation of lipids can have profound effects on cellular func-
tions, including signal transduction, membrane plasticity, and 
membrane trafficking [132,133]. Defects in lipid homeostasis 
contribute to several pathophysiological conditions, ranging 
from metabolic diseases to cancer. For example, cholesterol 
is one of the most important regulators of lipid organization, 
and tightly controlled mechanisms maintain the cellular cho-
lesterol levels in membranes that regulate cholesterol traffick-
ing [134]. Dysregulation of this balance can lead to diseases, 
such as Niemann–Pick type C [135] and atherosclerosis [136]. 
Furthermore, in the case of phospholipids—known to be the 
major components of all cellular membranes—significant 
modification of their cellular levels can lead to pathogenic pro-
cesses such as Alzheimer’s disease [137]. Imbalances in major 
lipid signaling pathways, such as those involving eicosanoids 
(prostaglandins, thromboxanes, and leukotrienes), phospha-
tidylinositol 3,4,5-trisphosphate, sphingosine-1-phosphate, 
ceramides, and oxysterols, contribute to disease progression 
in chronic inflammation, autoimmunity, allergy, cancer, ath-
erosclerosis, hypertension, heart hypertrophy, metabolic, and 
degenerative diseases [132].
Current MS-based lipidomic techniques are capable of 
detecting and quantifying hundreds of molecular lipid spe-
cies in numerous sample types such as cell cultures, tissues, 
and biofluids (plasma, urine, and cerebrospinal fluid) in a very 
selective, quantitative, sensitive, and high-throughput manner.

Toxicopanomics
A number of extraction protocols have been specifically devel-
oped to offer more efficient isolation of highly non-polar lipids 
than classical metabolomics methods. Most of these extrac-
tion techniques use organic solvent liquid–liquid extraction, 
including the Bligh and Dyer [138], methyl tert-butyl ether, 
and butanol–methanol methods [139]. Automated sample 
preparation and lipid extraction in a 96-well format provide 
reproducible and precise results from large sample sets [140], 
allowing us to implement lipidomics analyses in large-scale 
clinical and toxicological studies. Following sample prepara-
tion, the total lipid extracts can be analyzed by different MS 
techniques, depending on the purpose of the analysis.
In the shotgun lipidomics approach [141], the total extract, 
containing labeled internal standards, is directly injected 
without further separation into a high-resolution MS system. 
Simultaneously, the spectra of all intact molecules (scans in 
MS1 mode) and their collision-induced fragments (scans in 
MS2 mode) are acquired. Data interpretation is performed 
by specialized software, which deconvolutes the spectra and 
assigns the signals to particular lipid molecules on the basis 
of their exact molecular mass and fragmentation pattern. 
Absolute lipid quantification is performed by comparing the 
signal intensity of the target lipid with that of an internal stan-
dard of known concentration.
LC–MS is another widely used MS technique for detecting 
low-abundance lipid species [142]. In this approach, the total 
lipid extract is separated on an LC column and then injected 
into the MS device. This helps generate three unique param-
eters—retention time (RT), mass-to-charge ratio, and specific 
fragmentation pattern—as selective criteria for specific lipid 
identification. LC–MS lipidomics analyses can be performed 
in the targeted or untargeted mode, which aims either at a few 
specific molecules or fishing for unknown compounds in an 
unbiased way, respectively [143].
Finally, matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization is an 
MS approach that is widely used for tissue lipidomics analysis 
[144]. It provides molecular information in the form of a 2D 
distribution map of various lipid species directly from the sur-
face of biological samples.
Optimally, by using a combination of current lipidomics 
approaches, hundreds of molecular lipids from dozens of 
lipid classes of different abundance ranges can be relatively 
quickly identified and accurately quantified from small sample 
volumes.
Profound changes occur in the cellular/tissue lipid compo-
sition in response to intracellular and environmental factors. 
Lipidomics is thus a powerful tool for monitoring the over-
all status of cells and tissues and, because of its sensitivity, 
has great potential for identifying and detecting potential bio-
markers that indicate toxicity at an early stage. As an example, 
oxidized lipids, including eicosanoids, have been suggested to 
be major mediators of toxicity [145] and can be readily mea-
sured in biofluids. For example, 8-iso-prostaglandin F2α and 
11-dehydrothromboxane B2 are recognized as clinical mark-
ers for oxidative stress linked to respiratory conditions [146–
148] such as those caused by tobacco smoke exposure, asthma, 
allergy, hypoxia, and cardiovascular conditions [149]. Urinary 
leukotrienes have been associated with certain forms of can-
cer, including prostate and kidney cancer [150,151]. Lipids 
from necrotic tissues have also been demonstrated to be pres-
ent in urine [152]. Moreover, urinary leukotriene E4 levels are 
correlated with lung function in asthmatic patients [153,154]. 
Apart from this, Apoe−/− mouse studies have shown the signifi-
cant impact of cigarette smoke exposure on lung tissues at the 
level of ceramides, lactosylceramides, galactosylceramides, 
and cholesteryl esters [155,156].
Lipidomics analyses will likely yield useful biomarkers that 
indicate dysfunctionality or toxicity in other organs such as 
the liver, where, for example, the total plasma triacylglycer-
ide levels are elevated in relation to alcoholic liver disease 
in rats [157]. In the brain, specific enzymatic or radical-
induced cholesterol oxidation products such as 27-, 24-, and 
25-hydroxycholesterol have been reported to be associated 
with neuroinflammation and neurodegenerative diseases such 
as Alzheimer’s disease [158–160].
Lipidomics characterization was also at the core of a pre-
vious study that assessed the clinical hepatotoxicity potential 
of 22 drugs belonging to five different therapeutic chemi-
cal classes and possessing various drug-induced liver injury 
effects [161]. Treatment of 3D InSight human liver microtis-
sues with repeated doses of each drug allowed the authors to 
collect lipid fingerprints at five time points. The authors then 
performed multivariate statistical analysis to search for corre-
lations between the lipid fingerprints and hepatotoxic effects. 
They observed an increase in the levels of long-chain and 
mainly unsaturated triacylglycerides, including those con-
taining a docosahexaenoic acid chain, which has been associ-
ated with mitochondrial dysfunction. Additionally, as a final 
example, lipidomics assessment has been used to demonstrate 
the effects of purified biologically active substances derived 
from fungi as potential drug targets, with the findings showing 
unique phospholipid responses to various drugs in HeLa cells 
[162].
7.3.4  Metabolomics
Metabolomics refers to the systematic identification and quan-
tification of the small-molecule metabolic products (the metab-
olome) of a biological system (cell, tissue, organ, biological 
fluid, or organism) at a specific time point. In order to leverage 
the metabolome, it is mandatory to monitor all steps—from 
proper biological sample collection, to adequate quenching 
of the metabolism, and to ensuring proper controls—during 
the whole analytical workflow [163,164]. The Holy Grail is the 
comprehensive quantitative measurement of all metabolites 
present in a defined biological system at a specific time point. 
Unfortunately, the concentration range of the whole metabo-
lome covers a wide dynamic range spanning from millimolar 
concentrations down to sub-nanomolar levels, which presents 
a challenging analytical task. Because the metabolome repre-
sents the final endpoint of the biological phenotype, metabolo-
mics has garnered interest over the last three decades and has 
become fully integrated into the systems biology approach. As 
early as 2003, Zerhouni had included metabolomics as a part 
of the vision of the U.S. NIH (National Institutes of Health) 
roadmap initiative to accelerate medical research [165].
At present, there exist two distinct approaches within 
metabolomics: (1) the biomarker discovery mode, where the

goal is to monitor as many metabolites as possible (untar-
geted analysis) before applying multivariate statistical tools to 
identify key endogenous and exogenous compounds, and (2) 
the biomarker validation mode, where biological hypothesis-
driven analyses enabled by quantitative methods target key 
metabolites and allow their absolute quantification.
7.3.4.1  Untargeted Metabolomics 
(Biomarker Discovery)
By default, most untargeted metabolomics approaches use 
hyphenated high-resolution accurate MS (HR-MS) techniques 
such as liquid (LC/HR-MS), capillary electrophoresis (CE/
HR-MS), or gas chromatographic (GC/HR-MS) separation of 
metabolites before MS detection [166]. It is worth mentioning 
that an extra metabolite separation dimension can be deployed 
by using orthogonal chromatographic techniques such as 
GCxGC/HR-MS [167] or by combining LC/HR-MS with ion 
mobility separation [168,169].
A direct high-throughput metabolomics approach without 
metabolite separation has been put forward as an alternative 
[170,171]. Despite its increased throughput of analyses, this 
shotgun approach cannot differentiate metabolite isomers and, 
therefore, needs to be complemented with an additional chro-
matographic step for identifying key biomarkers.
As a rule of thumb, this biomarker discovery approach relies 
on a well-defined study design with the goal of minimizing 
confounding factors (such as diet, lifestyle, medication, body 
mass index, and sex). On the one hand, the sample size for mea-
surement is crucial for biomarker identification with proper 
statistical power. On the other hand, the analytical methods 
need to accommodate a high level of repeatability to mini-
mize instrumental performance drift during the time course 
of analysis. To cope with these inherent issues, suppliers have 
developed more robust and performant chromatographic MS 
systems, and biological samples are randomized prior to mea-
surement. Moreover, several isotope-labeled internal standards 
and quality control (QC) samples (usually a pooled biological 
sample and/or a certified reference material for monitoring 
batch effects in large studies) are prepared and injected after 
every five to ten study samples to monitor the performance of 
the instrument during the time course of the study; these are 
quite important for applying in any further corrections after-
ward. The various steps needed for untargeted metabolomics 
are depicted in Figure 7.3.
Data Preprocessing: Once the analyses of randomized bio-
logical samples are completed, raw data are either directly pro-
cessed by using the supplier’s software or they are converted, 
mainly to mzXML files, before further processing. Peak pick-
ing, deconvolution, data alignment, and removal of redundant 
information are crucial for generating a final matrix containing 
the mean value pairs (features) of chromatographic information 
(RT or retention index) and m/z values with the peak area (or 
intensity) for all measured features. This step usually generates 
a list of several thousands of features per method and is criti-
cal for proper metabolite identification, especially in the case 
of closely eluting isomers that are pooled into one feature or in 
the case of mass accuracy shift, which leads to a false elemental 
composition being generated for the feature (bias in m/z mean 
value). This process can be made possible by using the instru-
ment manufacturers’ software, such as Progenesis QI (Waters), 
Compound Discoverer (Thermofisher), MetaboScape (Bruker), 
Mass Profiler Professional (Agilent), and ChromaTOF (Leco). 
In parallel, several open-source solutions that combine inter-
connected tools have been introduced and are being widely 
used by the metabolomics research community; these include 
MetaboAnalyst [172], PiMP [173], Workflow4Metabolomics 
(W4M) [174], MZmine2 [175], MetaBox [176], XCMS online 
[177,178], MS-DIAL [179], Galaxy-M for processing direct 
infusion MS data [180], and IP4M [181].

Data Normalization: This process is applied to minimize 
variations between batches as well as systematic errors in the 
data. Numerous techniques, mainly used for targeted quan-
titative metabolomics, have been reported by using isotope-
labeled internal standards [182–185]. Particularly in untargeted 
metabolomics, normalization techniques such as MS total sig-
nal [186,187], MS total useful signal [188,189], median fold 
change normalization [190–193], and quantile normalization 
and QC-based locally estimated scatterplot smoothing are 
frequently used to reduce variations from sample prepara-
tion, instrumental sensitivity, or other unwanted variables 
during analysis [194–196]. Recently, Barupal et al. introduced 
another QC-based systematic error removal technique, which 
uses random forest normalization [197].
Once the data normalization is complete, the missing values 
(for example, in the case of exogenous compounds) need to be 
handled by a gap filling step in order to run statistical process-
ing [198].
Confidence Levels for Metabolite Identification: The 
metabolite annotation step is a crucial link between acquired 
data and meaningful biological interpretation. Therefore, 
confidence in metabolite assignment is essential. In 2007, 
the Chemical Analysis Working Group of the Metabolomics 
Standards Initiative published the first stage of guidelines for 
reporting the minimum metadata related to metabolite iden-
tification and introduced the five confidence levels of identi-
fication [199]. The highest confidence identification (Level 1) 
confirms a structure with a minimum of two independent and 
orthogonal properties from a pure reference standard under 
identical analytical conditions. A lack of reference standard 
acquisition but the availability of additional predictive or 
externally acquired structure evidence—such as MS/MS data 
[200]—exhibiting diagnostic fragments or neutral losses con-
sistent with a specific structure would be considered as puta-
tive identification (Level 2). Preliminary identification (Level 
3) arises when accurate mass and isotopic distribution patterns 
produce tentative structures from database searches. Note that 
a single molecular formula typically gives multiple candidate 
structures. Finally, molecular formula candidates (Level 4) 
and a deconvoluted experimental m/z (Level 5) complete the 
less confident annotation classifications.
The performance of instrumentation and software has been 
improved since the introduction of these five confidence lev-
els for metabolite reporting. Matsuda et al. have highlighted 
the importance of high-accuracy MS instruments, such as 
Fourier transform, in reducing the false discovery rate (FDR) 
of elemental composition to 10% if combined with a small, 
customized compound database [201]. In silico fragmentation 
approaches, such as MetFrag [202,203], CFM-ID [204–206], 
and FingerID [207], have been developed to explore tandem 
MS experiments to reduce the FDR of metabolite annotations 
[208]. Suggestions have been made to clarify the set of report-
ing standards by including a score- or level-based system 
[209]. The future of improved metabolite annotation is likely 
to be influenced also by multiplexed technologies and the ben-
efit of redundant metabolite information originating from dif-
ferent analytical results (e.g., LC–MS, GC–MS, CE–MS, and 
nuclear magnetic resonance). At present, LC and ion mobility 
(IM) have been successfully multiplexed with MS, providing 
RT, collision cross section (CCS), and accurate mass data as 
supporting evidence. Zhou et al. have curated an IM CCS atlas 
with more than 5,000 experimental records before calculating 
CCS values for more than 1.6 million small molecules [210]. 
The authors have shown the wide applicability of searching 
through the CCS atlas (medium relative error, 0.5%–2%) in 
combination with in silico MS/MS spectra for improving the 
accuracy and coverage of both known and unknown metabo-
lite annotations from biological samples.
For metabolite candidates that lack an available reference 
standard, quantitative structure–retention relationship models 
can predict RTs [211–213], and computational calculations can 
estimate CCS values [210,214,215] to be used as evidence for 
metabolite identification.
Biological Interpretation: Data processing and metabo-
lite identification can help researchers achieve a system-level 
understanding of metabolism; but these studies need specific 
bioinformatics tools for building solid scientific interpretation. 
To this end, studies have implemented metabolite- or sample-
based clustering approaches that use hierarchical clustering 
(which forms a dendrogram plot) [216] as well as K-means clus-
tering approaches [217]. Similarly, Goodwin et al. reported the 
structuring of microbial metabolic responses to multiplexed 
stimuli via self-organizing metabolomics maps [218]. Network 
analysis and gene coexpression network analysis have been 
used to discover microbiota-linked biomarkers in patients 
affected by non-small cell lung cancer [219]. Metabolomic 
data have been merged with operational taxonomic units from 
16S RNA-targeted metagenomics and classified by chemo-
metric models. Network analysis has indicated that indole and 
its derivatives, aldehydes, and alcohols could play a signaling 
role in gut microbiome functionality. Similarly, in studying 
heart failure, Zhang et al. [220] have combined the untargeted 
metabolomics results of plasma samples from rats with trans-
verse aortic constriction and from patients at different stages 
of heart failure. They predicted diagnostic biomarkers by com-
paring the variable importance in the projection scores and 
by fold change analysis within and between rats and patients. 
Enrichment pathway analysis and network analysis provided 
an overview of the most disturbed metabolic pathways, and 
those disturbed at different stages and across species were 
confirmed. The significantly changed metabolites and path-
ways in this study revealed the underlying mechanisms of 
heart failure.
Hajjar et al. have used untargeted metabolomics to analyze 
cerebrospinal fluid samples from patients with mild cognitive 
impairment to uncover metabolomic derangements through 
dysregulation in sugar, methionine, and tyrosine pathways by 
combining partial least squares discriminant analysis, metab-
olite annotation, and pathway enrichment analysis [221]. In a 
similar work, Graham et al. indicated metabolite disturbances 
in 22 biochemical pathways and discovered two interlinked 
areas of metabolism (polyamine metabolism and L-arginine 
metabolism) that were differentially disrupted in a well-
defined clinical cohort [222].
Analysis of plasma metabolomic changes in the acute exac-
erbation and stable stages of chronic obstructive pulmonary 
disease (COPD) by Zhou et al. revealed a total of 142 dys-
regulated metabolites and 688 dysregulated lipids in COPD

7.3.4.2  Targeted Quantitative Metabolomics 
(Biomarker Validation Approach)
These hypothesis-driven analyses are performed on the basis 
of either biomarkers that have already been reported in the 
literature or prior statistical data generated from comprehen-
sive untargeted metabolomics analyses. Here, the concentra-
tion levels of key biomarkers are obtained from fully validated 
quantitative methods. These absolute concentration values pro-
vide key information for the validation of biomarkers. Khamis 
et al. have reported the quantitative determination of potential 
urine biomarkers for diagnosing asthma and COPD by moni-

and uracil) by using an LC–MS/MS method [225].
There are growing numbers of biobank/biorepository net-
works and population-based molecular pathological epidemi-

(Big Data to Knowledge), GAME-ON (Genetic Associations 
and Mechanisms in Oncology), and Precision Medicine 
Initiatives of the NIH.
Given the advancements in genomics, epigenomics, pro-
teomics, metabolomics, metagenomics, immunomics, inter-
actomics, and other omics technologies as well as those in 
bioinformatics and computational and systems biology, there 
are numerous opportunities in MPE to study virtually all 
human diseases and contribute to medicine and public health. 
Currently, many disease networks are available only in jour-

the need to deposit network maps in community databases to 
ensure that they can be readily accessed. Few of these findings 
have yet been clinically translated, leaving ample opportunity 
for future translational studies.
7.3.5  High-Content Screening
Molecular changes should be, as often as possible, correlated 
with cellular or tissue-level changes. While histopathology 
is the main approach for gathering such data, phenotypic 
assessment of cells in culture can be performed by using 
high-content screening (HCS) methods [226]. Fluorescence 
microscopy images are, in fact, one of the most fundamental 
resources employed today in research laboratories for inves-
tigating cell function changes. Their value derives from the 
fast-growing list of fluorescence reagents which easily enable 
specific identification and visualization of various molecules 
and organelles.
The turning point in the 1990s, which then enabled a rapid 
escalation of the technology application, was the development 
of tools for automated imaging quantification. This develop-
ment also helped depict the most complex phenotypes that 
were not observable by the human eye. This particular appli-
cation of quantitative microscopy is known as high-content 
imaging (HCI), while quantitative interpretation of the image 
is achieved through the so-called high-content analysis (HCA) 
[227]. The advantages of this technology are its reproducibility 
and high throughput, which together allowed researchers to 
investigate cellular changes on a broader scale. For this rea-
son, this technology was soon employed in drug discovery 
campaigns.
The technique is based on the visual detection of a panel of 
functional biomarkers that provides precise temporal, spatial, 
and contextual information that defines the biological status 
of the cells. The different biomarkers can be captured either 
in fixed cells labeled with multiplexed fluorescence-based 
reagents after stimulation/treatment or directly in living cells 
during the time of exposure. The panel of parameters that 
could be simultaneously measured is broad and could reflect 
changes in the level of expression of cellular products (e.g., 
phosphorylated proteins), changes in distribution (e.g., cyto-
plasm to nucleus translocation), and/or morphology (visual 
appearance) of the cell. Readouts can be adapted to the type 
of cells used in the assay, and they can address key cellular 
mechanisms such as (1) apoptosis and autophagy, (2) cell pro-
liferation, (3) cell viability, (4) DNA damage, (5) mitochon-
drial health, (6) mitotic index, (7) cytotoxicity and oxidative 
stress, (8) nascent protein synthesis, and (9) phospholipidosis 
and steatosis. In cells exposed to stimuli, toxicity is often the 
result of a combination of multiple mechanisms that are more 
likely to be captured easily with a panel of parameters and 
simultaneously through an HCS approach. In addition, these 
mechanistic endpoints can be detected below the point of high 
cytotoxicity, allowing analysis of additional high-throughput 
measurements such as transcriptomics or proteomics profiling 
(Figure 7.2c).
In the context of in vitro toxicology, HCI cellular assays 
quickly became of particular interest for early toxicity screen-
ing of compounds, especially in the process of drug develop-
ment within the pharmaceutical industry. Implementation of 
early toxicity screening during the lead optimization phase in 
an in vitro screening battery based on HTS and/or HCS [228] 
is strategically important for decreasing the risk of drug attri-
tion. One of the first HCI-based approaches developed in this 
context was focused on investigating drug-induced hepatotox-
icity [229]. The assay allowed the evaluation of cell cytotoxic-
ity by combining various readouts such as membrane integrity, 
mitochondrial health, and calcium flux. In recent years, the 
approach has rapidly evolved with the use of more physiologi-
cal cells (HepaRG) and models (spheroids). In addition, the 
development of new fluorescence dyes has enabled the investi-
gation of more functional endpoints. For example, assessment 
of phospholipidosis and steatosis has become a standard pro-
cess in the context of early toxicity drug screening.

2D cell models in recapitulating in vivo biology is, in fact, the 
major driving force for HCI innovation. Most of the recurrent 
challenges in 3D models are linked with the various types of 
support or multiwell plates (microplate) used to generate such 
models and their adaptation to automation and liquid-handling 
tools. The methods used for labeling cells with fluorescent 
probes also had to be reconsidered, together with the type of

dimension. The upcoming paragraph discusses potential solu-

The most commonly used 3D model is probably the so-
called spheroid, which can be a solid cell mass or a hollow-core 
structure. Considering the increased 3D thickness of the tissue 
relative to a traditional 2D culture, it is obvious that the loss of 
excitable light that can be partially reflected or absorbed by the 
tissue is the first challenge encountered during imaging. The 
development and use of clearing regents that allow optimal 
clarity for sharp and bright fluorescence images have enabled 
deeper light penetration into tissues, allowing the cellular 
investigation to be not limited to the first cellular layer [230]. 
Another limitation for obtaining high-quality images is how 
light is focused. Shining light on a specimen could generate 
unnecessary out-of-focus light that is typically scattered and 
convoluted, leading to poor-quality images. To overcome these 
obstacles, today’s advanced and modern high-throughput HCS 
systems are equipped with continuous laser light engines and, 
in some cases, high-powered LED lights that are directed into 
a spinning-disk confocal system, which streamlines targeted 
light through a defined pinhole while removing most of the 
out-of-focus light to achieve high signal-to-noise ratios.
Finally, the most challenging step for HCI-based analysis in 
the context of 3D cell models is certainly image quantification. 
Historically, HCA software solutions were designed to analyze 
images taken in 2D. With the development and introduction of 
cell models extended in a third dimension, image analysis can no 
longer depend on basic 2D image analysis algorithm solutions.
To begin with, 3D image analysis requires much greater 
processing power for computing image stacks, which can 
range from just a few images to several dozen, depending on 
the number of stacks and biomarker probes (multiple fluores-
cent channels). For example, a normal 2D imaging experiment 
in a two-biomarker (two-channel) assay that captures 20 fields 
per well in a 96-well microplate generates 3,840 images per 
microplate. In a comparable approach, in the context of a 3D 
tissue, the number of images would then increase according 
to the number of required z-stacks. For example, if 15 image 
stacks per each field were required, the number of images will 
increase to a total of 57,600. The value could be even greater 
if higher magnification were needed to more precisely image 
specific subcell compartments or organelles. Limitations are 
not only encountered during the acquisition and storage pro-
cesses but are also extended to the method of analysis. To date, 
a few different 3D imaging methods have been developed and 
are summarized in Table 7.3.
The choice of method clearly depends on the biological ques-
tion to be addressed by the assay. It is, therefore, crucial that 
each possibility is accurately evaluated and assessed during 
the assay validation process to determine the optimal method. 
An increasing number of commercially available software for 
3D image reconstruction and analysis are starting to populate 
the market. Similarly, for 3D cell culture models, HCA soft-
ware must evolve to provide more advanced 3D imaging tools 
to improve the overall ease of use for systemically measuring 
3D volumes in the context of HCS campaigns.
Although these aspects have made the whole process more 
time consuming, the use of 3D models in association with HCI 
presents several opportunities for the next generation of cell-
based assays that require a more physiological cellular model. 
The open challenge is on two fronts. On the one hand, how 
do we transition well-established assays in 2D cellular model 
forms into more physiological 3D cell models? On the other 
hand, how do we develop novel assays with improved cell cul-
ture systems to achieve an increased clinical relevance?

7.4  General Considerations for Experimental 
Workflows and Data Transformation
Figure 7.4 shows a typical workflow for a study on measur-
ing omics endpoints in an in vitro or in vivo experiment. The 
statistical design associated with the execution of the experi-
ment is a crucial component for assessing the experimental 
error by handling variation, eliminating any systematic bias, 
and accounting for extraneous confounding variables linked to 
the technology used, the extraction/isolation steps, and other 
variables. It is particularly important to randomize sample 
grouping during the intermediate steps of organ dissection and 
molecular extraction (i.e., RNA/DNA/protein extraction) to 
avoid batch effects that can mask the actual treatment-induced 
signals [232]. Finally, each biological sample must be con-
trolled for quality. Once the experiment has been successfully 
completed and all QCs deemed satisfactory, the generated data 
are ready for input into the next stage of the process to compute 
the systems response profiles (SRP). The SRP expresses the 
degree to which each individual molecular entity is changed 
as a consequence of exposure to the stimulus and is the result 
of rigorous QCs and statistical analysis, as described above.
Both experimental and data processing workflows must be 
standardized to control the quality of raw data and the appro-
priateness of applied transformation methods that lead to 
SRPs.
7.5  Multi-Omics Data Integration
The basic guiding principle of systems toxicology/biology 
is that a “system is more than the sum of its parts”. As out-
lined above, individually, each omics data modality provides 
a wealth of insight into how a biological system responds to a 
perturbation, such as exposure to a toxicant. However, each 
data modality only illuminates a small section of the whole 
picture that constitutes the overall response of the biological 
system. For example, exposure to cigarette smoke permeates 
through the different molecular layers of the lungs: it affects 
lipids [233,234], metabolites [235,236], proteins [237], and 
transcriptional programs [238,239].
Multi-omics data integration jointly analyzes changes 
across the different molecular layers to provide deeper insights 
and more robust conclusions on the investigated toxicological 
responses [240–242]. For example, multi-omics toxicology 
studies have revealed the mechanisms of renal cisplatin toxic-
ity [243], lung nanoparticle toxicity [244], and cigarette smoke 
exposure effects in the lungs [155].
A recent 6-month systems toxicology study in Apoe−/− mice 
illustrates such a multi-omics approach [245]. This study 
aimed to compare the effects of exposure to aerosols from two 
heat-not-burn tobacco products with those of cigarette smoke. 
Lung tissue was analyzed by transcriptomics, proteomics, 
metabolomics, and lipidomics methods to capture the exposure 
responses of approximately 17,500 mRNAs, 5,000 proteins, 
670 metabolites, 400 lipids, and 360 miRNAs. For integra-
tive multi-omics data analysis, the authors took advantage of 
Multi-Omics Factor Analysis, which can be viewed as a gen-
eralization of principle component analysis for multi-omics 
data [246], and multimodality functional network interpreta-
tion. Across all five omics data modalities, cigarette smoke 
exposure was found to induce inflammatory and oxidative 
stress responses and lipid/surfactant alterations, whereas the 
aerosols from the heat-not-burn tobacco products were found 
to be associated with much more limited to absent effects. 
Strikingly, this multi-omics analysis highlighted several mol-
ecules that are involved in the complex immunoregulatory

7.6  Prediction Models and Signatures
Toxicological evaluations are multifaceted. While the primary 
focus of systems toxicology is to understand and assess the 
biological mechanisms underlying toxicological response, 
toxicity prediction on the basis of a few endpoints to enable 
faster and cheaper experiments is also within its scope. Omics 
investigations measure the abundance and properties of a large 
number of molecules; however, only a limited set of these mol-
ecules are required for high prediction accuracy. Selection of 
these predictor molecules and their integration in prediction 
algorithms largely rely on machine learning (ML) approaches, 
which use (learn from) available data to build prediction mod-
els and signatures (e.g., the set of molecules and their impor-
tance). These models can, therefore, be used to evaluate/
predict the toxicity of new substances by computing on the 
abundance/properties of the small set of predictor molecules. 
Apart from qualitative or quantitative toxicity prediction, 
omics-based prediction models can also be used to predict 
exposure status or exposure levels (e.g., cigarette smoke [247], 
biological age [248–250], and inflammation [251]).
Building prediction models typically requires two types 
of variables, dependent (response) and independent (explana-
tory). Dependent variables describe the phenotype being stud-
ied (e.g., genotoxicity), while independent variables (e.g., gene 
expression values) are the measures used to explain the studied 
phenotype. In mathematics, this relationship would be trans-
lated with the following formula: genotoxicity = f (gene expres-
sion) + error term.
A simple design for building a prediction model for a given 
phenotype would consist of two experimental groups (i.e., 
treatment vs. control). The available data must be divided into 
three sets: training, validation, and test sets. The training set 
is used for the model to learn the parameters of the algorithm 
employed; the validation set is used to fine-tune these param-
eters or choose the most optimized ML method, and the test 
set is a set of data that was not used in any step when building 
of the model/classifier, and it is only used to assess the perfor-
mance of the final model [252].
An important aspect in developing such models is coping 
with the high number of predictors available for learning and/
or feature selection. Selection of a subset of predictors is part 
of the training and should not involve the test set. The main 
approaches for reducing the number of predictors include 
filter methods, whereby the data are filtered on the basis of 
an association measure between the predictor and response 
before model learning, and embedded methods, which typi-
cally include constraint or regularization of the values of the 
parameters (e.g., the so-called L1-regularization in the LASSO 
algorithm) [253].
In theory, all omics data can be used as input for ML 
approaches; however, the majority of available prediction 
models are based on transcriptomic and methylome data 
because of the large availability of these two types of data-
sets. For example, gene expression data have been used to 
derive a signature for genotoxicity based on the expression 
of 65 genes [254]. In this study, TK6 cells were exposed to 
a battery of well-characterized genotoxic and non-genotoxic 
chemicals, and transcriptomics data from these two experi-
mental groups were further used in an ML approach (nearest 
shrunken centroids) to select a set of genes that could classify 
chemicals as genotoxic or non-genotoxic. A similar approach 
was used to develop a classifier for predicting sensitizing 
chemicals [255].
The rapid advances in ML approaches and the development 
of user-friendly computational tools, combined with the avail-
ability of a large volume of omics data, are the key pillars to 
developing cheaper predictive toxicological assessment meth-
ods for chemicals.
7.7  A Network-Based Approach for  
Quantifying the Impact of 
Biologically Active Substances
Systems biology approaches yield large molecular datas-
ets that are organized into meaningful biological networks 
which can lend mechanistic insights and an understanding 
of the potentially toxic effects of substances. We have devel-
oped a five-step approach for assessing the biological impact 
of a stimulus that combines systems-wide experimental data 
with a computational process (Figure 7.5). The intensity of 
the biological impact of a stimulus is expressed by a quan-
titative metric termed “biological impact factor” (BIF). The 
main goal of this five-step approach is to adopt a toxicologi-
cal mode-of-action framework to describe how any substance 
may adversely affect human health and to use this knowledge 
to develop complementary theoretical, computational, and 
experimental (in vitro) models that can be used for safety 
assessment [256].
To produce systems-wide data, several well-chosen 
experimental systems are exposed to stimuli in a time- 
and dose-dependent manner. Instead of merely extracting 
lists of differentially expressed genes or proteins, the data 
are analyzed in the context of biological network models. 
Because the network models used for this approach are built 
with cause-and-effect relationships to describe biological 
processes, analysis of these networks can provide a more 
detailed molecular understanding of the observed biological 
perturbations. The approach thus provides the means for a 
mechanism-based quantitative comparison of the biological 
impact of a stimulus between individuals and species and can 
potentially exhibit predictive power through deterministic 
scoring algorithms.

7.7.1  Building Causal Biological Network (CBN) 
Models and Quantifying Their Activation
7.7.1.1  Network Model Basics
While classical toxicogenomics studies use differentially 
regulated gene/protein lists, we use network models to gain 
insight into the effects of a given stimulus. This approach pro-
vides a more detailed molecular understanding of biological 
network perturbations by extracting mechanistic information 
from high-throughput datasets. The networks are built by 
using Biological Expression Language (BEL), a semantic pro-
gramming language that allows representation of biological 
processes in a computable format [257,258]. The BEL frame-
work is an open-source technology for managing, publishing, 
and using structured life science knowledge (http://www.open-
bel.org/).
The nodes in the networks are biological entities such as 
RNA/protein abundances, protein activities (e.g., enzymatic 
activities and transcriptional complex activities), and pro-
cesses (e.g., apoptosis).
7.7.1.2  Model Building
7.7.1.2.1  Knowledge-Based Approach
Building network models is a multistep, iterative process, as 
shown in Figure 7.6a. Construction of network models starts 
with the careful selection of model boundaries, i.e., selection 
of appropriate tissue/cell context and biological processes 
to be included in the model. This is unlike other common 
approaches for building pathway or connectivity maps, where 
connections are often represented out of tissue or disease con-
text. Once the boundaries have been defined, the scientific 
literature is carefully reviewed to extract cause-and-effect 
relationships that comprise the literature model’s nodes and 
edges (causal relationships between the nodes). The network 
model can encompass knowledge from various levels rang-
ing from molecular and cellular events to organs or an entire 
organism level (Figure 7.6b).
7.7.1.2.2  Node Signatures Based on Experimental Data
The biological entities involved in the network (nodes) are typ-
ically protein activities, which are either complex to measure 
experimentally or highly uncertain because of the fast reac-
tions that are occurring (e.g., phosphorylation events). To relate 
these nodes to experimental data, gene expression changes 
caused by the activity of that protein can be used to predict 
the changes in the activity of the node (this approach is ref-
erenced as “reverse causal reasoning” [RCR]). To build these 
signatures, specific studies can be conducted, or public gene 
expression databases can be explored and leveraged.
The application of RCR on transcriptomic data in this way 
implies a critical divergence from the more commonly known 
pathway techniques for model building, such as the KEGG 
(Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes) [259] and IPA 
(Ingenuity Pathway Analysis—www.ingenuity.com). These 
methods identify differentially expressed genes whose pro-
tein product activities act in a pathway of interest, thereby 
relying on the “forward assumption” that gene expression 
changes are only indicative of the abundance of the protein 
or its predicted activity. The caveat remains that changes in 
gene expression do not always correlate with changes in pro-
tein activity. Furthermore, in these cases, it is not possible to 
make any inferences about genes, biological function of which 
is entirely unknown. RCR does not rely on these assumptions 
and can take into account the multiple states of regulation that 
may exist for a protein before action; this is because BEL can

7.7.1.2.3  Model Expansion and Review
Following assembly of the literature-based network model 
scaffold, the signatures stored in the database can be evalu-
ated against molecular profiling data to ensure that the net-
work accurately reflects the biological processes of interest. 
Multiple datasets are used to verify the inclusion/exclusion of 
network nodes, ideally from experiments where the experi-
mental exposure perturbs the biological mechanisms captured 
by the network model under construction. Any predicted 
nodes that are not present in the knowledge-based model are 
investigated in detail by a scientist for relevance and mecha-
nistic connection to the biological processes being modeled. 
This can be achieved, for example, by examining, in the con-
text of the network and/or tissue, whether the inferred node 
is known to be involved or by checking that the direction of 
change matches the current biological knowledge. This results 
in a more comprehensive, integrated model that includes nodes 
derived from existing literature as well as nodes derived from 
experimental data.
In the last steps of the model building, the integrated model 
is carefully reviewed by subject matter experts edge by edge 
and amended to include all relevant biology as well as exclude 
any inaccurate causal relationships and entities from the 
model.

7.7.1.3  Properties of Network Models
Causal network models resulting from the three-step build-
ing process described above provide many advantages over 
differentially expressed gene lists. The networks are highly 
dynamic; they can be modified to specific species and tissue 
contexts by defining appropriate boundaries, and they are eas-
ily updated as new knowledge becomes available. They also 
capture a wide range of biology that exists in a system out-
side of mRNAs, including, but not limited to, genomics, tran-
scriptomics, proteomics, phenotypic or clinical observations, 
chemicals, lipidomics, methylation states or other modifica-
tions (e.g., phosphorylation), and miRNAs. Owing to the pre-
served topology, the causal relationships within the network 
can be easily traced to a measurable entity, thus providing a 
link between the upstream node and the SRPs. By mechanis-
tically interpreting the SRPs, the networks provide the basis 
for the next two steps of the five-step strategy—NPA and BIF 
calculation.
The networks are also amenable to an investigation of inter-
species translatability. Additional analytics have been devel-
oped that can interpret omics data on the basis of a priori 
knowledge to facilitate the comparison of specific biology 
across experimental conditions in different species. One of 
these conditions is the special case where data from an experi-
ment performed in one species can be compared with data 
from another to determine areas of shared and unique biology. 
If a causal relationship between two nodes has been captured 
for one species (e.g., A increases B) and homologs for A and B 
(e.g., A′ and B′) exist in a second species, then this same rela-
tionship is assumed to exist in the second species. One main 
advantage of this property is that an area of biology that may 
be less comprehensively studied in humans, perhaps for tech-
nical or ethical reasons, can still be represented in the network.
We believe that our network approach is well-positioned to 
meet 21st century toxicity testing needs. To progress further, 
pathway definition, pathway annotation, and a central reposi-
tory of the annotated pathways are needed. Moreover, new 
methods for pathway identification need to be established and 
evaluated. Finally, the pathways need to be continually refined 
as new knowledge emerges, and a peer-review process will 
be necessary to enable the scientific community to reliably 
develop new and updated versions of the pathway biology with 
emergent knowledge.
Our network building efforts are a good first step toward 
these goals. By organizing gene lists into a biologically mean-
ingful context, it is more feasible to analyze omics data as well 
as identify potential toxicity pathways. These networks could 
provide a consistent way for grouping stimuli based on their 
MOA and could become the standard in toxicity testing.

7.7.1.4.3  BEL.bio
In addition to CBN and NeuroMMSig, several other resources 
can help scientists to start network model building. BEL.bio 
(https://bel.bio) is the official site of BEL, and it provides an

7.7.1.4.4  BELCommons
Once familiar with BEL and biocuration, scientists can pro-
ceed to assemble their curated BEL documents into CBN 
models by using BELCommons (https://bel-commons.
scai.fraunhofer.de/) [276]. BELCommons is built on top of 
PyBEL, which is a computational framework for BEL devel-
oped by the Fraunhofer Institute for Algorithms and Scientific 
Computing [277]. Its user-friendly interface allows users to 
upload BEL documents for compilation into network models. 
The built-in validation function provides helpful guidance to 
users on how to maintain the required syntax in BEL scripts 
to allow parsing with PyBEL. BELCommons also outputs 
network statistics, provides several other ways for analyzing 
a network model, including visualization, and enables shar-
ing with the scientific community. BELCommons also has 
several functions that help build and explore network models. 
The assembly, seeding, and transformation functions within 
the query builder allow users to select existing networks that 
fit their scientific interests, generate subnetworks based on 
specific molecular interactions, and edit the resulting net-
works, respectively [276]. The network models can be further 
downloaded in multiple file formats, including GraphXMML 
for visualization in Cytoscape.
7.7.1.4.5  BioDati

the research article from which the biological assertion(s)

tion information and article abstract based on its PubMed ID, 
and the BEL autocompletion service then assists in creating 
BEL functions and relationships to build BEL Assertions. 
The curation interface further aids in adding multiple annota-

7.7.1.5  Computing the NPA
Any relevant dataset evaluated against a network model gives a 
qualitative overview of the biological pathways that are affected 
by a given stimulus. To enable quantitative measurements of 
biological network perturbations, we have developed a compu-
tational approach that translates gene fold changes into NPA 
scores [278]. The NPA has two main components (Figure 7.7):
	
1.	A biological network model that describes a biologi-
cal process or mechanism. As described above, net-
work models are a “cause-and-effect” mechanistic 
knowledge representation based on all relevant lit-
erature and published datasets.
	
2.	A gene expression dataset resulting from an experi-
ment, whose controlled perturbations are hypoth-
esized to be involved in the biology related to the 
network.
While RCR has previously been exclusively employed as an 
exploratory tool for identifying relevant perturbed biology 
by drawing qualitative mechanistic inferences on the basis of 
statistical enrichments [279–281], NPA derives a “response 
profile” at the whole network level, which then allows a 
coarse-grained view of the effects encompassed by the gene 
expression change profile. Similar to RCR, NPA not only 
takes advantage of the measured downstream effects to infer 
the activity of every upstream node, but it also extends this 
further by integrating the topology, directionality, and sign 
of the edges of the network in the computation. As the NPA 
is expressed as a linear combination of the measurable data, 
the derived scores are expected to correspond to the resulting

In addition to the confidence intervals that account for experi-
mental error (e.g., biological variation between samples in an 
experimental group), some companion statistics are derived to 
give a sense of the specificity of the NPA score with respect 
to the biology described in the network. To this end, two per-
mutation tests are derived to assess whether the observed sig-
nal is a property inherent to the evidence (genes) or structure 
given by the model. The network is considered to be specifi-
cally perturbed if both p values are low (typically <0.05). The 
NPA results must always be interpreted in light of the three 
companion statistics: the two permutation p values and the 
confidence interval.
NPA scoring is, therefore, an integrated approach that com-
bines omics data with a knowledge-driven network model. 
NPA quantifies the activity changes of a biological/toxico-
logical process relative to the non-perturbed control state of 
the system. By providing a measure of biological network 
perturbation together with its description, NPA allows corre-
lation of molecular events with phenotypes that characterize 
the network at the cell, tissue, or organ level. While an NPA 
score, with its accompanying statistics, provides a quantifiable 
measure of the degree of perturbation of a biological network, 
the backbone differential values describe how a perturbation 
propagates through the network topology to further support 
biological interpretation. This approach differs from gene set 
enrichment analysis, which is based on the enrichment of sets 
of genes in experimental data and which enables qualitative 
investigation of experimental data in light of the statistical 
enrichment of mechanisms represented by each gene set [282].
To summarize, NPA is a mechanistically driven quantitative 
measure of the amplitude of perturbation of a network, which 
can provide a quantitative, system-wide understanding of the 
biological mechanisms leading to a disease or toxic endpoint. 
Besides being a comparative assessment tool, the NPA can 
serve to define biologically based dose–response relationships 
(toxicity thresholds).
Use case. To gain mechanistic insights into the biological 
effects of acute cigarette smoke exposure on bronchial epi-
thelial cells, we exposed lung organotypic in vitro cultures to 
mainstream cigarette smoke for 7, 14, 21, or 28 minutes at the 
ALI and investigated various biological endpoints (e.g., gene 
expression and microRNA profiles and matrix metalloprotein-
ase-1 release) at multiple post-exposure time points (0.5, 2, 4, 
24, and 48 hours). The transcriptomic dataset has been made 
available publicly (E-MTAB-874).
To demonstrate the validity of the NPA approach, we have 
used the aforementioned lung organotypic in vitro culture data-
set (E-MTAB-874) and a public dataset of bronchial brushings 
from smokers (GSE7895) to probe the translatability of the net-
work perturbation by using a well-defined network model. We 
and others have shown that the in vitro airway model closely 
resembles the human situation morphologically as well as at a 
molecular level [14–16,283–287]. A coherence between smok-
ing-induced responses, such as the antioxidant response, has 
been observed between the human smoker bronchial epithe-
lium samples and in vitro organotypic cultures. When scoring 
a network model that captures the signaling involved in xeno-
biotic metabolism, the perturbation amplitude has been found 
to be similar for several nodes (Figure 7.7bi). The xenobiotic 
stress response recapitulated by the network is coherent in both 
systems (Figure 7.7bii), whereas the gene-by-gene contrast does 
not compare in an evident manner (Figure 7.7biii).
7.7.1.6  BIF Calculations
The final step of the strategy is the computation of a BIF. As 
indicated by its name, the BIF aims to quantify the biologi-
cal impact resulting from the exposure of a biological system 
to one or several stimuli; it represents a holistic score that 
describes the systems-wide effect on all processes captured 
in the underlying network models and their associated NPA 
scores. The attributed BIF values for stimuli can then be quan-
titatively compared on the basis of a high-level view of their 
biological effects. Because it aggregates NPA scores that have, 
by themselves, already filtered a large fraction of the noise ini-
tially contained in the SRPs [288], the BIF is expected to pro-
duce results with increased robustness against technical and 
biological sources of variability.
In Figure 7.8, the BIF is represented as a star plot in which 
the multiple axes contain the NPA scores computed for each of 
the considered biological network models. Computation of the 
surface of the polygon formed by the NPA scores obtained for 
a given SRP constitutes an intuitive BIF algorithm. Similarly, 
the fundamental idea behind the BIF is to use the amplitudes of 
the perturbations induced by the exposure in an appropriate set 
of biological networks models as the input for a simple scoring 
scheme that provides a quantitative measure of the global effect 
of these perturbations. From this point of view, the BIF algo-
rithm is first and foremost intended to detect and display trends 
in its input dataset. As a consequence, the a priori selection of 
networks to be included in the BIF calculation—while it must 
be biologically sound—does not constitute its most critical 
aspect, as only the significantly perturbed networks will con-
tribute to the BIF results. Ideally, even if the chosen networks 
do not exhaustively cover the underlying biology, they will still 
capture a significant portion of the systems response.
Once the NPA scores for the selected biological network 
models have been computed, the relative importance of each 
network model must be determined. While the BIF deduced 
from the radar chart in Figure 7.8 weights every axis equally, 
other choices are available. Network preference on the basis 
of a priori qualitative knowledge is not easily translatable into 
objective and reproducible weights. Data-driven weighting 
schemes, such as multivariate dimension reduction methods, 
may be more appropriate for this purpose [289]. The final step 
of BIF calculation involves the aggregation of the weighted 
NPA scores. As illustrated by the surface-based BIF from

Toxicopanomics
the radar chart in Figure 7.8, a simple sum of the weighted 
NPA scores is not necessarily the most meaningful solution. 
Methods based on more advanced geometric considerations

Additional methods are being developed to avoid overweight-

In summary, we have established a well-defined framework 
(Figure 7.8) that enables transparent information agglomera-
tion such that entire SRPs can be mathematically transformed 
into a limited set of numbers (NPA scores and then a BIF 
value). If the validity requirements of the four steps are met, 
the BIF has the potential to provide a simple but scientifically 
sound measure of the biological impact of a stimulus on a sys-
tem, thus serving as a biology-based dose–response model.
7.8  Computational Modeling to Derive 
Internal Exposures for “Omics”
Exposure to toxicants can elicit subtle to sustained pertur-
bations at different stages in cellular processes such as gene 
encoding, protein synthesis, and metabolite production [290]. 
Depending on the exposure paradigm, there could be revers-
ible or permanent alterations in cellular machinery, leading to 
changes in omics profiles. Although recent advances in omics 
measurement techniques have enabled the mapping of cellu-
lar responses, these need to be linked to quantified external 
exposure, dosimetry, and internal exposures for performing a 
toxicological assessment.
Cellular signaling pathways are complex and dynamic, and 
they produce different cellular outcomes depending on the 
duration, intensity, and frequency of exposure [291]. In vitro 
assessments typically involve often short exposures (such as in 
vitro washout experiments) or prolonged exposures with static 
concentrations (Figure 7.9). While recent advances in micro-
physiological systems allow us to create relevant dynamic 
exposure profiles [292], understanding these varied exposure 
profiles on cellular outcomes requires us to estimate internal 
exposures at target sites to translate exposure–response rela-
tionships. Internal exposures are influenced by several factors, 
such as lysosomal ion trapping, protein binding in intracel-
lular cytosol, extracellular media, and type of well plates. 
Mechanistic in vitro computational modeling approaches can 
be applied for predicting internal exposures while consider-
ing all of the influencing factors [293–295]. Modeling inter-
nal exposures in complex 3D in vitro models that mimic the 
structure and physiology of tissues requires one to consider

exposures is complex, as it depends not only on the exposure 
conditions and physicochemical properties but also on the 
mechanical and biochemical processes that lead to mass trans-

membranes, in vivo estimation of internal exposure kinetics 
in the target of interest cannot be solely derived from steady-
state blood levels and external dose (external exposure). 
Extrapolation of in vitro to in vivo exposure–response rela-

to omics responses. Toxicokinetic profiles vary across species 
with different absorption, distribution, metabolism, and elimi-
nation rates. Moreover, in vivo exposure kinetics to chemical 
and biological entities varies across a lifetime according to the 
route of exposure. Exposures can be broadly classified into 
short (30 seconds), intermediate (~10 minutes), and long (more 
than 6 hours) exposures [298]. For example, a single breath of 
inhaled powder takes a few seconds, while the consumption 
of consumer products such as cigarettes lasts for 10 minutes, 
and occupation-related dermal or inhalation exposures could 
last for more than 6 hours per day, as shown in Figure 7.9.

require detailed physiological representation and mechanistic 
modeling for transport in the tissue of interest. For example, 
aerosol dosimetry models with simplified lung morphometry, 
prescribed inhalation patterns, and specified aerosol absorp-

whole-lung modeling approaches [298]. The most widely used 
single-path whole models are the International Commission 
on Radiological Protection inhalation model [299] and the 
freely downloadable multiple-path particle dosimetry models 
[300,301]. These models were developed by considering an 
inhaled aerosol particle’s impaction, sedimentation, and dif-
fusion in rodents and humans. The difference between single-
path and multiple-path models is only in their computational 
implementation—that is, a single-path whole-lung model aver-
ages all branches into a single branch (as representative) and 
calculates the amount of deposited aerosol, which is finally 
multiplied by the number of branches; in contrast, a multiple-
path whole-lung model calculates the deposition in all distinct 
branches and sums up the deposition for each of 24 lung gen-
erations. These inhalation models are limited to predicting the 
deposition of inhaled solid particles. Models for predicting the 
deposition of evolving multispecies liquid aerosol are still in 
development. Hybrid-CFD models [302] have been developed 
by linking CFD-based inhalation models based on Lagrangian 
and Eulerian methods [303] with PBTK models. These hybrid 
models are used to predict the deposition of aerosols in the 
upper respiratory tract and are currently often linked with 
whole-lung models for lower respiratory tract aerosol depo-
sition predictions. The Lagrangian method allows tracking 
of each inhaled particle, treating them separately, while the 
Eulerian method treats inhaled particles as distributions of 
specified types and properties. The computational time and 
costs for these hybrid-CFD models increase with an increase 
in model complexity.
Development and utilization of tools for deriving levels of 
internal exposures while accounting for population variability 
could help reduce the uncertainty in risk assessments for sin-
gle compounds or complex mixtures. Because of their physio-
logically relevant model parameters, these computational tools 
provide a basis for extrapolating in vitro and animal data to 
humans. The internal exposures predicted by advanced dosim-
etry models could, therefore, be linked to quantifiable toxic 
responses for deriving adverse outcome pathway (AOP)-based 
margins of exposure for risk assessment.
7.9  Adverse Outcome Pathways
The AOP concept [304] is a linear construct that encom-
passes a number of essential events that connect mechanistic 
knowledge to apical endpoints in a formalized manner [305]. 
This concept has gained popularity as an effective representa-
tion of the toxicological mechanisms that cover the causal path 
from molecular exposure to individual or population health 
risk. AOPs have become fundamental tools in risk assessment 
and regulatory applications supported by the OECD. In par-
ticular, they play a central role in supporting evidence-based 
decision-making in assessing the toxic effects of chemicals 
on human health [306]. In this section, we will describe the 
role of omics measurements in the context of AOPs and their 
contributions to AOP quantification. To illustrate this topic, 
we will leverage our experience around the AOP entitled 
“EGFR Activation Leading to Decreased Lung Function” 
(short “Decreased Lung Function”) in the context of inhala-
tion toxicology (https://aopwiki.org/aops/148) [307], for which 
several of the techniques mentioned earlier in this chapter can 
be applied under the specific AOP perspective.
7.9.1  AOP Structure
A set of core building principles were proposed for develop-
ing AOPs covering wide human toxicological contexts, rang-
ing from environmental risk assessment to consumer products 
testing [308]:
	
1.	AOPs are chemical-agnostic, i.e., they don’t consider 
“non-toxic” chemical-specific aspects, including 
absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion.
	
2.	AOPs are constituted of a linear sequence of key 
events (KE) that link a molecular initiating event 
(MIE) to the final organism-level adverse outcome 
(AO) (Figure 7.11a). KEs are measurable biological 
perturbations that are essential (but not necessarily 
sufficient) for toxicity, while their directed connec-
tions, termed “hey event relationships” (KER), rep-
resent predictive and/or causal linkages supported by 
biological plausibility and empirical evidence.
	
3.	The KEs of an AOP are expected to cover all levels 
of biological organization: molecules, cells, tissues, 
organs, and individual organisms (Figure 7.11a).
	
4.	The boundaries of an AOP are pragmatically defined 
for development purposes and can be expanded by join-
ing other AOPs that share common KEs and KERs.
	
5.	AOPs may evolve over time as new knowledge 
becomes available.
In comparison to the CBN models mentioned earlier in this 
chapter, AOPs obey much more restrictive KE selection crite-
ria and are, therefore, smaller in size, even though they share 
the causal aspect of their connections.

Lung Function”

function” [307] describes the causes of dysfunction of the lung 
mucosal barrier, which constitutes the natural defense against

7.9.3  AOP Quantification
The original AOP framework consists of purely qualitative 
knowledge assemblies and, as such, is not directly suitable 
for quantitative toxicological risk assessment. Therefore, the 
need for genuinely quantitative AOPs (qAOPs) has emerged 
rapidly, and the first qAOP was published only lately in fish 
ecotoxicology [309]. To be compatible with risk assessment 
requirements, qAOPs must describe dose- and time-depen-
dent perturbations and be able to properly quantify the AO 
[310]. This is usually achieved in two steps, KE quantification 
and KER modeling. The latter is more challenging because 
explicit prediction functions KEn = f(KEn−1, time) need to be 
determined.
Omics measurements present excellent tools for quantifying 
KEs. In the context of the AOP “Decreased Lung Function” 
(Figure 7.11b), a number of omics datasets are currently used 
to build a quantified version of this AOP (under development). 
For example, increased oxidative stress (MIE) is assessed by 
metabolomics analyses, and mucin production (KE6) is mea-
sured by proteomics analyses. In some cases, KEs can be indi-
rectly quantified by using biological network models (e.g., Sp-1 
activation and mucus hypersecretion) or even omics-based 
prediction models. The translatability between test systems 
is also leveraged to perform the measurements under optimal 
conditions (Figure 7.11c).
7.9.4  KER Modeling
Once KEs have been measured, an AOP KER “KEn−1→ KEn” 
can be modeled by a prediction function KEn = f(KEn−1, time), 
which captures the dependency of KEn (value of the down-
stream KE) on KEn−1 (value of the upstream KE) at a particu-
lar time. Even though KER modeling is more technical, some 
of its generic features remain intuitive [310]. For instance, in 
line with the qAOP requirements mentioned above, the pre-
diction function f is expected to display two simple empirical 
behaviors (Figure 7.11d):
	
1.	For a fixed time, KEn exhibits a sigmoid-shaped 
dependency on the upstream KEn−1 value, similar to 
the dose–response curves used in drug development 
and HCS-based toxicity testing [311].
	
2.	For a fixed upstream value KEn−1, the time depen-
dency consists of an initial “activation” increase, 
followed by a maximum and a “relaxation” decrease 
toward a constant value. A non-zero constant value 
after a longer exposure time means that the applied 
upstream perturbation KEn−1 induces an irreversible 
response of KEn, i.e., the system has been brought 
out of homeostasis.
Real cases build on these generic considerations by incorpo-
rating more data and mechanistic knowledge inputs, which 
correspond to Bayesian and systems biology approaches, 
respectively [310]. For KERs at organ or organism levels, the 
systems biology approach can turn into physiological modeling. 
Typically, in the case of the AOP “Decreased Lung Function”, 
the effects of an increased mucus layer in the airways (KE9) 
can be incorporated in a calculation of airflow resistivity by 
using Poiseuille’s law and linked to the AO “decreased lung 
function”, measured by impulse oscillometry [312].
Substantial efforts are currently being spent on the assem-
bly of AOPs and the development of qAOPs. Omics data and 
related systems biology approaches will significantly contrib-
ute to further advances in these areas.
7.10  Trusting Big Data—The Role of 
Transparency and Verification
7.10.1  Transparency
As described above, technology is increasingly enabling the 
collection of “big data” [313,314], including for toxicologi-
cal evaluation of consumer products. However, the ability to 
fully harness the power of big data does not come without 
challenges. Beyond appropriate analytical tools and statisti-
cal methods [315], best practices in data sharing and scien-
tific transparency are critical. Indeed, factors contributing to 
the irreproducibility of biomedical findings that have been 
decried in the last years [316] include (1) inadequate attention 
to experimental design (such as flaws in statistical analysis 
or low statistical power), (2) lack of sharing of material and 
methods, (3) publication bias (e.g., lack of sharing of negative 
results) [317,318], and (4) in only a minority of cases, research 
misconduct [313,314,319], including for toxicological evalua-
tion of consumer products.
Transparency in scientific research implies that scientists 
and physicians share the findings, data, and methods associ-
ated with a study or clinical trial, thus allowing the scientific 
community to verify and build upon their work for valid, evi-
dence-based decision-making [320,321].
Several reporting guidelines have been developed to assist 
researchers in implementing measures for addressing repro-
ducibility concerns, including the Transparency and Openness 
Promotion guidelines [322], Standard Protocol Items: 
Recommendations for Interventional Trials statement for the 
preparation of clinical trial protocols [323], and Animals in 
Research: Reporting In Vivo Experiments guidelines for ani-
mal research [324]. An overview of such guidelines and best 
practices is maintained by the Enhancing the QUAlity and 
Transparency Of health Research network [325].
A conference on reproducibility, replicability, and trust 
in science organized by the Wellcome Genome Campus in 
September 2020 highlighted the following points [326]:
•	 “without good, transparent and open reporting, sci-
ence cannot progress”.
•	 Scientists need to embrace uncertainty: we do not 
know everything and should be open about it.
•	 Strategies to increase the publication of nega-
tive results, including deposits of preprints, regis-
tered reports, post-publication peer review, and the 
Octopus project, a primary research record where 
every step of a research project can be published, are 
gaining ground.

•	 Science is an ecosystem where every stakeholder has 
the responsibility to act to improve the situation: sci-
entists, editors, funders, associations, and the public.
To address reproducibility concerns regarding the evaluation 
of consumer products by their own manufacturers, the online 
platform INTERVALS™ (https://www.intervals.science/) was 
constructed to gather detailed information on the design and 
conduct of relevant studies and the data produced, in accor-
dance with the FAIR (findable, accessible, interoperable, and 
reusable) principles [327,328]. This platform enables easy 
review of methods and results as well as reuse of data and gen-
eration of new hypotheses.
7.10.2  Crowdsourcing Science Verification
With the unprecedented volumes of data that accompany 
various high-throughput technologies, new computational 
approaches are being developed to facilitate robust analysis 
and interpretation of these large datasets. Questions arise as 
to how the uncertainties inherent to the application of systems 
biology for safety testing can be best managed.
The realization of the intellectual power that can be har-
nessed through community collaboration, the so-called “the 
wisdom of the crowd”, has led to the emergence of crowd-
sourcing models such as CASP [329], BioCreative [330,331], 
CAPRI [332], and DREAM [333–336].
With the goal of maintaining scrutiny in data analysis and 
interpretation, we have set up a Systems Biology Verification 
Process and a methodology for verifying the output of research 
processes in industry [337,338]. The IMPROVER (Industrial 
Methodology for Process Verification in Research) method-
ology decomposes a research workflow into building blocks 
that represent small pieces of a complex research program. 
The core idea is that every building block can be verified by a 
challenge—some internally and some by crowdsourcing. The 
approach has many obvious advantages over self-assessment 
of methods. Beyond alleviating any possible bias, it gathers 
and focuses the community around an important and relevant 
scientific problem and allows a comparison of the performance 
of different methods used to solve it. It adds additional value 
through the discovery of complementary methods for solving 
a problem, recognizing that a combination of solutions often 
outperforms the best performing single method [339].
Similar challenges could be built around toxicity testing to 
identify and verify stimulus-specific signatures and MOAs for 
biologically active substances that show a concordant tempo-
ral and dose–response relationship. Developing a community 
of researchers with similar interests in 21st century toxicol-
ogy will foster faster advances in assessing and verifying 
the validity of systems biology-based results (e.g., networks) 
than are possible with disjointed efforts. By using indepen-
dent approaches and methods, it is possible to uncover pro-
cesses where the output (biological plausibility) points toward 
a coherent and complete set of relevant results. This set of 
results can be further used to assess the safety and biological 
impact of a substance or a treatment modality on a particular 
test system. Additionally, because the crowdsourcing approach 
directly addresses the issue of consistency of findings, which is 
important for the acceptance of omics approaches in toxicol-
ogy, success stories will be an important measure of progress 
as well as critical for maintaining engagement and enthusiasm 
within the community.
7.10.3  Independent Peer Review
It is critical that the findings on toxicological assessment of 
consumer products are reviewed by experts. Regulatory agen-
cies, for instance, rely on peer review to ensure that the qual-
ity and scientific integrity of their assessments and decisions 
are maintained [340–342]. The methods that are generally 
used may be qualitative, semi-quantitative, or quantitative; 
but they are typically based on the concept of risk being a 
function of the presence of a hazard and the severity of its 
impact on human health [343]. Manufacturers of consumer 
products may sponsor a review of their own assessment results 
to understand which additional experiments might be needed 
prior to regulatory submission, provide insights into scientific 
plausibility to the external community, and increase confi-
dence in the results. For example, the nonprofit organization 
Toxicology Excellence for Risk Assessment (https://www.tera.
org) provides peer consultation and peer-review services and 
convenes groups of experts to evaluate the scientific basis of 
the work products and its conclusions in accordance with U.S. 
Environmental Protection Agency peer-review procedures.
Along these lines, Philip Morris International commissioned 
a two-tiered scientific peer review of the methods, data, and 
results from studies that have assessed the Tobacco Heating 
System 2.2 (THS), an electrically heated tobacco product [344]. 
Tier 1 panels reviewed the study models and methods, trans-
parency of documentation, quality of data, and interpretation 
of results through online questionnaires with yes/no, multiple-
choice, and open-ended questions. In tier 2, two panels were 
tasked in parallel with reviewing the data package and the out-
come of tier 1 reviews. In both the tiers, the time allotted for 
peer review was not limited, and compensation was paid at an 
hourly rate based on the estimated effort required of the panel.
In total, 65 peer reviewers with expertise levels comparable 
with those of reviewers on the FDA advisory committees con-
tributed to the process. Positive reviews of study models and 
designs in tier 1 were upheld by tier 2 reviews. The peer review-
ers were confident to very confident that state-of-the-art meth-
ods had been used. Overall, in tiers 1 and 2, 65% of reviewers 
found the data that “switching to THS is likely to reduce the 
risk of smoking-related diseases relative to continued cigarette 
smoking” was convincing or very convincing [344].
7.11  Conclusions
While the 21st-century toxicology paradigm shift is in motion, 
toxicologists and regulators need to closely follow the develop-
ment of the new approaches and tools that will be involved in 
the transformation toward the new era in safety assessment. 
However, care should be taken not to evaluate the new meth-
ods against traditional toxicology approaches; rather, the new 
methods should be used to assess how decisions may be sup-
ported by new versus traditional data. Evidence is steadily

Toxicopanomics
accumulating that integrating systems biology data with con-
ventional histopathology and clinical chemistry data can allow 
better and more informed decision-making than traditional 
safety assessment alone. The focus should be not on measuring 
large numbers of endpoints but rather on understanding and 
mining these data. As such, systems biology could be posi-
tioned at the center of these efforts in the future.
As an example of this new way of toxicity assessment, the 
five-step strategy described above tightly integrates experi-
mental and computational approaches in a systems biology 
pipeline that allows tandem analysis of biological network 
perturbations in the context of traditional toxicology data. 
Network- and mechanism-based impact assessment uses 
reverse-engineering principles to infer the activity states of 
signaling mechanisms from observed changes in gene expres-
sion. This allows us to determine common mechanisms that 
are perturbed, even if the underlying differentially regulated 
genes are not the same. This enables causal analysis of the 
components and interactions that are important in a complex 
system and thus increases our understanding of the essential 
features of a complex system. Moreover, the mechanism-
based species translation aspects that the new approaches 
allow us to tackle have immense potential. Indeed, they have 
the potential to increase the predictive value of animal model 
systems and, more importantly, provide a solid foundation 
for development of the 3R principles in toxicity assessment 
[345].
Clearly, in addition to biological network models and algo-
rithms, an intelligent experimental design, state-of-the-art 
technology, and a well-defined workflow are necessary to 
accomplish the various aims of the five-step strategy. In com-
bination, they not only build a robust methodology for iden-
tifying toxicity pathways and MOAs for stimuli that have the 
potential to induce adverse health effects in humans but also 
provide a quantitative mechanistic framework for comparative 
product testing.
