32.1  Introduction
Liver disease is a growing problem in Western cultures. 
Estimates are that almost one-third of adults in the United 
States have some level of liver damage secondary to poor diet, 
obesity, exposure to environmental chemicals and pharmaceu-
ticals, from chronic alcohol consumption or viral hepatitis. 
Liver injury induced by chemicals has been well recognized as 
a toxicological problem. The induction of liver injury follow-
ing chemical exposure depends not only on the agent involved 
but also on the duration of exposure, age of the subject, health 
of the subject and concentration of the chemical agent. The 
vulnerability of the liver to chemically induced damage is 
related to its blood supply from the digestive tract and its role 
in the uptake and biotransformation of xenobiotics. Both phar-
maceutical and non-pharmaceutical chemicals are capable of 
inducing liver toxicity. The effects of chemicals to the liver 
can be classified based on the length of exposure to the toxic 
agent – acute or chronic. Acute toxic effects can result in either 
lethal or sublethal changes in the liver cell resulting in modi-
fied function or cell death. Chronic toxicity is frequently mani-
fested as cirrhosis or neoplasia. Biochemical and molecular 
targets in the cell are modified by toxic chemicals resulting in 
degenerative changes and alterations in function. Some forms 
of liver injury are reversible, whereas others result in a per-
manently deranged organ. The liver possesses multiple func-
tions in maintaining homeostasis, as such a varied response 
to injury is seen. This variability must be considered when 
determining the appropriate testing model and procedure for 
evaluating liver toxicity. This chapter will discuss the major 
methods, approaches and models that are useful in the detec-
tion and evaluation of liver injury.
32.2  Liver Structure and Function
The liver is the largest organ in mammals. The adult liver usu-
ally comprises between 4% and 5% of total body weight of 
the animal. It is located in the right upper quadrant of abdo-
men touching the lower surface of the diaphragm. It consists of 
multiple lobes which are separated by the falciform ligament. 
The blood supply of the liver is very important for its normal 
function as well as the response of the liver following expo-
sure of xenobiotics. Oxygen-poor blood that is rich in mate-
rials absorbed by digestive tract enters the liver through the 
hepatic portal vein. The liver is also supplied by oxygen rich 
blood via the hepatic vein. Blood leaves the liver through the 
hepatic vein which subsequently drains into the inferior vena 
cava (Figure 32.1).
The liver is responsible for multiple physiological and bio-
chemical processes required to maintain the homeostasis of 
the organism. These include metabolism of macronutrients, 
protein synthesis, lipid, glucose and cholesterol homeostasis, 
regulation of growth-signaling pathways and metabolism of 
xenobiotic compounds. The morphological and functional 
integrity of the liver is vital for the health of the animal. 
Most liver functions occur in the parenchymal liver cells or 
hepatocytes.
32.2.1  Structure
The liver consists of liver lobes, which, in turn, are comprised 
microscopically of hepatic lobules [1]. Each lobule is mor-
phologically organized in a hexagonal shape around the cen-
tral vein. At each of the six points of the hexagon are portal 
triads where the branches of portal vein, hepatic artery and 
bile ducts are found. Each lobule is approximately 1 mm in 
diameter. The predominate cells of the liver, the hepatocytes, 
are arranged in the lobule as plates of hepatocytes separated 
by blood sinusoids. The vein is a branch of the hepatic portal 
vein, and the artery is a branch of the proper hepatic artery. 
Together the hepatic artery and the portal vein supply the 
blood to the sinusoids providing a mixture of nutrient rich, 
deoxygenated blood (from the portal vein) and freshly oxy-
genated blood (from the hepatic artery). Blood flows from the 
portal tract to the central vein through sinusoids. In addition, 
bile canaliculi found between hepatocytes display an opposite 
directional flow going to the portal triad and eventually to the 
bile duct (Figure 32.1). It has been increasingly recognized 
that under normal and pathological conditions, many hepa-
tocyte functions are regulated by substances released from 
neighboring nonparenchymal cells. Liver sinusoidal endothe-
lial cells (SEC) perform important filtration function due to 
the presence of small fenestrations blood and the hepatocyte 
surface. Liver blood vessels and sinusoids occupy almost a 
quarter of the mass and volume of the liver and approximately 
12% of the body’s total blood volume. Thus, the liver serves 
as a blood reservoir. Regular blood flow in the liver is around 
1,500–2,000 mL/min comprising a quarter of the total cardiac 
output that allow free diffusion of many substances, but not of 
particles of the size of chylomicrons.
Two models for the description of the liver lobule for mor-
phologic and functional purposes have been used (Figure 32.2) 
[1, 2]. In one, the classic lobule is based on hexagonal structure 
of the liver lobule and describes the regions within the lob-
ule as centrilobular (surrounding the central vein), periportal 
(close to the portal triad) or midzonal (present between the two 
zones). This classic description is founded by the blood flow 
and morphologic appearance of the liver lobule. A second way 
views the lobule as a functional unit, the liver acinus [2]. The 
acinus model consists of the parenchymal hepatocytes, lying 
between two or more hepatic venules with the portal triad as 
the center of the acinus. Since there is no physical separation 
between the acini, the hepatocytes of one acinus are in contact 
with hepatocytes of the adjacent acini. However, despite the 
close contact, the cells within the acinus model are exclusively 
supplied by the blood arising from the centrally located portal 
triad. As with the classic lobule description, the hepatocytes 
within the acinus can be divided into three zones with zone 1 
being the closest to the portal tract, while hepatocytes in zone 
3 are furthest away from the portal tract and zone 2 in the 
middle of the acinus. The importance of the zonal classifica-
tion is important in describing liver pathology as well as help-
ing to define the mechanism for the induction of the pathology. 
In general, the classic lobule is the predominant classification 
used in the description of liver toxicology and pathology.

32.2.2.1  Hepatocytes
The hepatocyte is the dominant structural and functional cell 
of the liver. It is a polygonal cell with a diameter of 25–30 ōm. 
Hepatocytes exhibit a polarity, with the side facing the endo-
thelium displaying microvilli while the opposite side of the 
hepatocyte being in contact with adjacent hepatocytes via 
tight junctions [4]. The hepatocyte polarization is the result of 
molecular differences on the surface membrane. Cell recep-
tors, pumps, transport channels and carrier proteins all form 
three domains of hepatocytes: the basolateral, lateral and 
canalicular domains. The hepatocyte is rich in mitochondria, 
peroxisomes, Golgi, lysosomes, rough and smooth endoplas-
mic reticulum and microtubules/microfilaments. Glycogen 
and lipid (droplets) are also present in the cytoplasm [4]. 
Hepatocytes can differ morphologically and functionally from 
each other based on their location within the liver lobule. 
There are well-established differences in oxidative metabo-
lism, mitochondria number and enzyme activity based on the 
location of the hepatocyte within the lobule. Hepatocytes close 
to the portal tracts receive highly oxygenated blood and con-
tain enzymes involved in oxidative reactions. They make and 
store glycogen and synthesize proteins. Hepatocytes closest 
to the central veins are furthest from the oxygenated blood 
supply, so they do not carry out oxidative activities, instead 
are involved in detoxifying reactions. Hepatocytes in between 
have intermediate metabolic properties. These differences 
play an important role in determining the potential toxic 
effects of xenobiotic chemicals. Hepatocytes are essential in 
the synthesis and regulation of lipid, glucose and proteins in 
the blood. They serve in the regulation of lipid, both synthe-
sis and removal from the blood. The hepatocyte also regulates 
the release of stored glucose into the circulation as the energy 
needs of other organs occur. Circulating proteins including 
albumin, coagulation factors and complement proteins are

32.2.2.2  Kupffer Cells
Hepatic macrophages are an important nonparenchymal com-
ponent of the liver [5]. The liver macrophages can be classified 
into two groups: resident macrophages and those derived from 
bone marrow [6]. The KC is the resident hepatic macrophage. 
They are thought to be formed from the yolk sac during the 
embryonic development and renewed as needed. KCs reside in 
sinusoidal lumen on the endothelial cells, located in the space 
between adjacent endothelial cells and frequently extending 
into the space of Disse. There is a zonal difference in the num-
ber of KCs within the liver lobule with a greater concentration 
seen in the periportal region [5]. A primary role of the KC, 
as with most macrophages, is the uptake and breakdown of 
particulate matter, particularly foreign bodies such as bacteria. 
The greater number of KCs in the periportal region reflects 
this function since the portal blood flow comes from the intes-
tine where bacterial infiltration occurs. The KCs also play an 
important role in the liver homeostasis by regulating inflam-
matory processes, by generating cytokines, eicosanoids and 
reactive oxygen species (ROS) as well as participating in the 
fibrotic response [7, 8].
32.2.2.3  Hepatic Stellate Cells (Ito Cells)
The HSC was first described by Ito using the light micro-
scope. Ito cells are fat-storing cells [9]. These cells are located 
between hepatocytes and endothelial cells in the liver [10]. Ito 
cells comprise approximately 10% of all liver cells [11]. They 
maintain close contact with hepatic cells. Stellate cells pos-
sess different functions depending on the status of the liver. 
Under normal liver function, the Ito cells function for vita-
min A storage, some drug metabolism and detoxification, and 
blood flow regulation [12]. However, in conditions where hepa-
tocyte necrosis and/or excess inflammation occurs, the stellate 
cell functions in angiogenesis, regeneration and may initiate 
liver fibrosis by synthesizing and secreting collagen and other 
extracellular matrix (ECM) proteins [13–17].
32.2.2.4  Cholangiocytes
Cholangiocytes are biliary epithelial cells that line the bile 
ducts both internally and externally in the liver. Cholangiocytes 
form the Canal of Herring where the bile duct connects with 
bile canaliculi in lobule [18]. As seen with other liver cells, 
cholangiocytes are polarized, having different plasma mem-
brane domains within the cell that are involved with differ-
ent transport functions [18]. These cells produce most of the 
bile by volume and function in an immunoregulatory role by 
responding to xenobiotics and foreign antigens within biliary 
tract.
32.2.3  Biliary Tract
The biliary tract is derived from the branches of the bile ducts. 
The biliary tract is the path by which bile secreted by the liver 
is transported to the duodenum [18]. The bile produced in the 
liver is collected in bile canaliculi, between adjacent hepato-
cytes. The canaliculi radiate to the edge of the liver lobule, 
where they merge to form bile ducts. The hepatic ducts exit 
the liver and merge to form the common hepatic duct. The cys-
tic duct from the gallbladder joins with the common hepatic 
duct to form the common bile duct. Bile either drains directly 
into the duodenum via the common bile duct or is temporarily 
stored in the gallbladder via the cystic duct. The common bile 
duct and the pancreatic duct enter the second part of the duo-
denum together at the hepatopancreatic ampulla.
32.2.4  Development
Understanding the embryonic development of the liver is 
important considering the role that the various liver cells 
play in response to toxic and pathologic conditions. The liver 
develops from the third to the eighth week of embryogenesis. 
Arising from the early GI tract in the embryo, hepatic endoder-
mal cells (called hepatoblasts) undergo a morphological tran-
sition from a columnar form to pseudostratified appearance 
resulting in thickening of the early liver bud [19]. The hepato-
blasts migrate into the septum transversum mesenchyme and 
the liver architecture starts to form. These bipotential hepato-
blasts then differentiate into either biliary epithelial cells or 
hepatocytes. The biliary epithelial cells are seen around the 
portal veins forming a bilayer of cuboidal cells. Hepatoblasts 
not adjacent to portal veins differentiate into hepatocytes and 
arrange themselves into cords lined by sinusoidal epithelial 
cells and bile canaliculi. Once the hepatocytes differentiate 
from the hepatoblasts, they take on the functions of a mature 
hepatocyte including the expression of genes involved in drug 
metabolism, carbohydrate metabolism, ammonia detoxifica-
tion and bile production and secretion. The fetal liver also 
participates in blood cell production. Prior to birth, the liver 
releases blood stem cells to the fetal thymus, creating the 
T-cells. After birth, the formation of blood stem cells shifts to 
the red bone marrow.
32.2.5  Liver Function
The liver plays an essential role in carbohydrate, protein, 
amino acid and lipid synthesis and metabolism (Table 32.1) 
[20]. Most of the liver functions are performed by the hepa-
tocytes. The liver performs several roles in carbohydrate 
metabolism. The liver synthesizes and stores glycogen via 
glycogenesis [21]. When required, hepatocytes release glu-
cose into the blood by breaking down this stored glycogen. 
In addition, hepatocytes can synthesize glucose from amino 
acids, lactate or glycerol. The liver is also responsible for the 
breakdown of insulin and other hormones. The liver plays a 
significant role in protein synthesis, metabolism and degrada-
tion. Proteins, including those involved in coagulation and red 
blood cell production, are synthesized in the liver. The liver 
is also the major site of production for thrombopoietin, the

metabolism and detoxification of xenobiotic chemicals as well 
as endogenous hormones. The liver also converts ammonia 
into urea as part of the urea cycle, and the urea is excreted in

32.2.5.1  Major Functions of the Liver
Of the over 20,000 protein-coding genes that are expressed in 
human cells, over 60% are expressed in a normal, adult liver 
[22]. Over 400 genes are specifically expressed in the liver. 
Most of the liver-specific proteins are expressed in hepatocytes 
and secreted into the blood constituting the plasma proteins 
[23]. Other liver-specific proteins are components of bile syn-
thesis such as BAAT and SLC27A5 as well as transporter pro-
teins involved in the metabolism of drugs including ABCB11 
and SLC2A2 [24]. Other liver-specific proteins include apoli-
poprotein A II, coagulation factors F2 and F9, complement-
factor–related proteins and the fibrinogen beta chain protein.

It is critical for mammalian life to maintain proper concentra-
tions of glucose in blood. An important function of the normal 
liver is to maintain normal blood glucose levels over both short 
and long periods of time. Hepatocytes contain the metabolic 
pathways that control the maintenance of proper blood glucose 
levels. Excess blood glucose is rapidly taken up by the liver 
and sequestered in the form of glycogen (a process called gly-
cogenesis). As blood glucose concentrations decline, the liver 
depolymerizes the glycogen to reform the glucose and secrete 
it back into the blood. Hepatocytes can also synthesize glu-
cose from amino acids and non-hexose carbohydrates through 
gluconeogenesis.
Lipid metabolism is predominantly handled by the liver. It 
oxidizes triglycerides to produce energy including the break-
down of fatty acids to acetoacetate. The majority of lipopro-
teins are synthesized in the liver and the liver is the major site 
for converting excess carbohydrates and proteins into fatty 
acids and triglycerides, which are then exported and stored in 
adipose tissue. The liver also synthesizes cholesterol and phos-
pholipids that is packaged with lipoproteins for blood transport 
or excreted in bile as cholesterol and converted to bile acids.
Regarding protein synthesis, the liver in general and hepato-
cytes specifically are responsible for synthesis of most plasma 
proteins. Albumin, the major plasma protein, is synthesized 
almost exclusively by the liver. Also, the liver synthesizes 
many of the clotting factors necessary for blood coagulation. 
Bile production is also a function of the liver. Hepatocytes 
make bile from cholesterol which is converted to bile salts. 
Bile salts emulsify fats and break the fat down into smaller 
units. This increases the surface area of the fat allowing for 
lipases to further digest the fat. The removal of ammonia and 
hemoglobin by products from the body is a major function of 
the liver. Hemoglobin is metabolized to bilirubin in the liver 
and then secreted in bile and removed from the body in feces. 
The liver also participates in the storage of the fat-soluble vita-
mins, A, B12, D, E and K as well as iron and copper. The sum-
mary of the normal liver function is presented in Table 32.1.
32.2.5.3  Drug Metabolism
Metabolism is the process of transformation of chemicals from 
one chemical form to another through enzymatic processes. 
In the liver, the predominant drug-metabolizing enzymes are 
cytochrome P450s (CYP450s) [25]. The primary purpose of 
liver metabolism is to modify and remove endogenous and/or 
exogenous molecules from the body. In general, the metabolism 
process converts lipophilic chemicals to hydrophilic forms to 
facilitate their elimination. While exogenous xenobiotics can 
enter the body through multiple routes, most exogenous chemi-
cals eventually end up in the liver where they are metabolized. 
For example, approximately 70% of an orally administered 
drug is absorbed through gastrointestinal tract and transported 
to liver through hepatic portal vein [26]. The central role played 
by liver in the clearance and transformation of chemicals makes 
it susceptible to xenobiotic and drug-induced injury.
The initial step toward metabolism of chemicals in the liver 
is their uptake from the blood. This uptake is governed by pas-
sive and/or active diffusion across the hepatocyte membrane. 
The diffusion is determined by physicochemical properties of 
the chemical. Passive transport consists of simple diffusion and 
filtration, whereas special transport includes active and facili-
tated transport types. While the charge of a drug can impact 
its ability to permeate the cell membrane via passive diffusion,

Once in the hepatocyte, the chemical can be further metabo-
lized. Xenobiotic metabolism can be divided into two phases: 
Phase 1 and Phase 2 (Table 32.2) [30]. Phase 1 reactions prepare 
the chemical for further metabolism in Phase 2. The Phase 1 
reactions involve oxidation, reduction, hydrolysis, hydration 
and many other rare chemical reactions. These processes tend 
to increase water solubility of the lipophilic chemicals.
Most Phase 1 metabolic enzymes are located in the endo-
plasmic reticulum. This family of Phase 1 enzymes are known 
as CYP450s (Table 32.3) [31]. CYP450s are the terminal oxi-
dase component of an electron transport chain. It is not a single 
enzyme, but rather consists of a closely related family of 50 
isoforms. Diversity of individual P450 gene products is seen. 
The P450 enzymes heterogeneity allows the liver to perform 
oxidation on a vast array of chemicals (including almost all 
drugs) in Phase 1 metabolism. The function of the P450 sys-
tem is influenced by genetic diversity, modulation of enzyme 
activity and inhibition of the P450 enzymes. Each of the P450 
proteins is unique and accounts (to some extent) for the varia-
tion in drug metabolism between individuals. Genetic varia-
tions (polymorphism) in P450 metabolism can be linked to 
observed unusual sensitivity or resistance to xenobiotics [32]. 
In humans, such polymorphism is also responsible for variable 
pharmaceutical response among patients of differing ethnic 
backgrounds. Besides genetics, other substances can influence 
the P450 enzyme activity. Xenobiotic compounds can interact 
with the P450 enzyme family as either inhibitors or induc-
ers of the enzymes. Enzyme inhibitors block the metabolic 
activity of P450 enzymes. For example, grapefruit juice is an 
inhibitor of the CYP450 3A4 enzyme and transporters such 
as P-glycoprotein and organic anion transporting polypep-
tides. This effect usually occurs immediately. This inhibition 
can lead to increases in the bioavailability and serum levels 
of drugs that are detoxified by CYP3A4 such as the benzodi-
azepines. This results in longer sedation. On the other hand, 
inducers increase P450 activity by increasing its synthesis. 
Depending on the inducing drug’s half-life, there is usually 
a delay before enzyme activity increases. There can also be 
competitive inhibition of the P450 enzymes. Other xenobiotics 
may share the same P450 enzyme specificity and in turn com-
petitively inhibit the metabolism of the compound in question 
leading to its accumulation and failure to be metabolized. This 
competitive inhibition would lead to a failure to detoxify the 
compound leading to liver toxicity

Phase 2 metabolism can be induced as a result of activation

For example, the aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR) is linked

expression of the CYP enzyme family, xenobiotic chemicals 
during their metabolism can increase CYP enzymes concen-
tration through increased receptor binding and subsequent

by the liver. Biotransformation of a chemical does not always

membrane permeation via passive diffusion and decrease the 
likelihood of further reabsorption of metabolites in the small 
intestine or kidney.

its metabolite with a glucuronic acid (or sulfonic acid) or zwit-

on drug physicochemical properties and leads to larger changes 
in drug TPSA and its lipophilicity. Common Phase 2 reactions 
include O-methylation, N-acetylation, O-glucuronidation and 
glutathionuria. Phase 2 metabolites that form in the liver are 
effluxes into bile by transporters (MRP1, MRP2, BCRP etc.) or 
into blood with later elimination in urine. There can be a varia-
tion in the elimination routes and ratios between the species 
depending on the transporter expression. Interestingly, acety-
lation and methylation are another conjugation types of Phase 
2 biotransformation, but with a different effect. Methylation 
decreases the TPSA and increases xenobiotic lipophilicity, and 
as a result slows down elimination of a drug.
Transporters also play significant role in eliminating drugs 
that permeate membranes with high rate of passive diffusion; 
however, metabolism is the rate-determining step in drug 
elimination in this case [27, 34]. When a drug reaches liver 
or small intestine, there are two ways of elimination: efflux 
by transporters or biotransformation. CYP450s enzyme family 
plays a major role in metabolizing xenobiotics. Hepatic CYP3A 
enzymes rapidly metabolize drugs that are transported to liver 
by OAPT1B1/3 [35]. Biotransformation of xenobiotics takes 
place in the gut through combined actions of intestinal P-gp 
transporters and intestinal CYP3A. This is true for xenobiot-
ics that are substrates for the efflux transporter P-gp or for the 
hepatic OATP uptake transporters. A number of acidic drugs 
with large molecular weight are metabolized by CYP2C8. 
Acidic drugs with lower molecular weights are substrates for 
CYP2C9 [36].
It is well established that there is interspecies variation in 
chemical absorption, metabolism and elimination. Differences 
in gastric pH among the species are thought to be one of the 
reasons for variation in drug metabolism as it caused evolu-
tionary pressure to select acid-metabolizing CYP enzymes in 
humans [27]. Humans and cynomolgus monkeys are shown to 
absorb acidic drugs by means of passive diffusion more read-
ily than other species due to low gastric pH [37]. In general, 
drugs that enter cells via passive diffusion and they do not 
need efflux transporters in order to enter cells. Phase 2 metab-
olites of these drugs, however, need efflux transporters for 
their elimination. Xenobiotics that permeate membranes with 
a high rate of passive diffusion but are not substrates of Phase 
1 and Phase 2 enzymes are not common. They can be quickly 
absorbed but take a long time to be eliminated from the body, 
which is the case for polyhalogenated aromatic compounds.
32.3  Hepatocyte Toxicity
Every exogenous compound that is ingested and absorbed will 
pass through the liver and become a target of liver metabolism. 
Thus, depending on the dose that the liver is exposed to a cer-
tain compound, it can cause liver toxicity (or hepatotoxicity) 
and cause liver injury [38]. Liver damage caused by chemicals 
has been documented for over a century. In the beginning of the 
20th century, the link between cirrhosis and heavy alcohol con-
sumption in humans and laboratory animals testing for hepatic 
lesions caused by different chemicals such as carbon tetrachlo-
ride and chloroform were discovered. Acetaminophen (APAP) 
is an example of a commonly used, over-the-counter pain 
reliever that, depending on dose, can be toxic to the liver. Its 
misuse is viewed as a classic drug-induced liver injury (DILI) 
[39]. The mechanism of APAP is well studied and linked to the 
depletion of glutathione which detoxifies the APAP. A metabo-
lite of APAP (N-acetyl-p-benzoquinone imine) then increases 
and produces the liver toxicity. The issues of drug-induced 
liver toxicity as well as non-pharmaceutical chemical liver 
injury are recognized as important contributors to liver toxic-
ity [40]. It has been estimated that over 900 pharmaceuticals 
are capable of causing DILI, which results in approximately 
5% of all hospital admissions, with half of those resulting in 
liver failure. A web site, LiverTox® (https://livertox.nlm.nih.
gov/), has been established by the National Institute of Health 
(NIH) as a resource for DILI. LiverTox® provides up-to-date 
information on liver injury that is linked to prescription and 
nonprescription pharmaceuticals and some selected herbal and 
dietary supplements. The LiverTox site is an excellent resource 
for information in idiosyncratic drug-induced hepatotoxicity.
The extent of liver injury produced by a xenobiotic is 
related to concentration of the compound as well as duration 
of exposure [41, 42]. In the case of acute exposure both func-
tional and pathologic changes are observed. These changes 
frequently involve a continuum from sublethal to lethal 
changes in the cells. The sublethal changes can be reversible, 
may be completely repaired or may result in permanently 
impaired function. Lethal changes involve the death of the 
cell(s) (necrosis). Depending on the extent of cell death in 
the liver, the result may be total loss of function and nor-
mal histology resulting in organ failure, or the replacement 
(regeneration) of the liver resulting in return to normal func-
tion and structure. A distinct species and strain difference 
in the effect of toxic agent exposure on the liver has been 
documented, and the differences seen in various species can 
be related to pharmacokinetics and dose. The reason why

32.3.1  Classification of Liver Injury
Following chemically induced liver damage, the morphologi-
cal changes in the liver lobule can be characterized based on 
the location and the type of the lesion produced. Liver damage 
can appear morphologically in a variety of patterns [43, 46, 
47]. Early toxic changes may include accumulation of the lip-
ids in the hepatocytes [48]. Hydropic change in the hepatocyte 
is reversible in a prelude to eventual death of the cell if the 
injury is too severe to repair. Necrosis refers to the process of 
cell death [49]. Various forms of necrosis are seen in the liver 
depending on the extent of the damage and the location of the 
damage [50, 51]. Cells in a hepatic lobule may become necrotic 
as single cells (single cell necrosis) or in tiny clusters (focal 
necrosis), or in all zones (centrilobular or periportal necrosis). 
Zonal necrosis is seen in every lobule while focal necrosis 
is more localized to a few lobules. If the injury is extensive 
enough the liver may undergo massive necrosis resulting in 
liver failure[51]. Lipid accumulation in the hepatocytes can be 
related to uptake of more lipids into the hepatocyte, or the fail-
ure to package the lipids for export. Lipid accumulation is a 
sublethal and reversable change if the damage is not too exten-
sive or prolonged. Besides direct damage to the hepatocytes by 
xenobiotics, cholestatic toxicity, caused by bile flow reduction, 
decrease in bile salt formation and retention of bilirubin are 
retained, is also a common type of liver injury resulting in 
jaundice [52].
During long-term exposure, chemically caused liver dam-
age can affect the entire liver structure and lead to prolif-
erative and degenerative alterations. Neoplastic changes 
including hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC), hepatic adenoma 
and hepatoblastoma are seen especially in rodents following 
chronic exposure to xenobiotics. Human liver cancer is one of 
the most common cancers in the world, with about 500,000 
deaths annually [53]. HCC is the most prevalent form of pri-
mary human liver cancer [54]. While causes of HCC vary, the 
most common etiology is chronic hepatitis B and C, early oral 
contraceptives, Thorotrast imaging material, chronic alcohol 
abuse, aflatoxin exposure, and iron or copper overload.
32.3.2  Liver Injury and Disease
Overexposure to drugs and other chemicals can cause liver 
injury. The types of adverse liver responses include fatty liver, 
cell death, cholestasis, damage to vascular system in the liver, 
fibrosis and cancer. Liver diseases constitute a significant 
global public health issue, with worldwide estimate showing 
that 844 million people have chronic liver diseases with mor-
tality of 2 million deaths per year [55]. Diseases such as liver 
necrosis, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), alcoholic 
fatty liver disease (AFLD) and non-alcoholic steatohepatitis 
(ASH) (NASH) are predominant in Western countries.
32.3.2.1  Necrosis
Death of hepatocytes is classified as liver necrosis [49, 56]. 
Multiple causes of necrosis have been described including 
overdose of pharmaceuticals (such as APAP), chemical expo-
sure (CCL4, CCL3), and virus and bacterial exposure and 
ischemic injury (termination of blood supply to the liver tis-
sue) [49, 51]. The extent of the damage determines whether 
the liver recovers from the necrosis or goes on to liver failure. 
Other factors such as age, nutrition and previous injury also 
contribute to the repair capability of the liver following necro-
sis. The classic form of necrosis in the liver is coagulation 
necrosis where the hepatocytes die but the liver architecture is 
somewhat maintained [57].
Changes that accompany hepatic necrosis include hemor-
rhage, fatty change, cytoplasmic vacuolization, degeneration 
and inflammatory cell infiltration [58]. The pathology term 
degeneration is used to describe damage to cells that is revers-
ible and is often a precursor to necrosis. The classification of 
necrosis is based on type [59]. In the case of xenobiotic-induced 
liver necrosis, the dose and intrinsic factors can determine 
whether hepatocellular necrosis is panlobular or centrilobular,

32.3.2.2  Apoptosis
Hepatocyte necrosis is the typical cell death response follow-
ing chemical exposure, as discussed above [49]. The process of 
apoptosis can also be seen following xenobiotic exposure. In 
necrosis, the dying cell undergoes increased hydropic changes 
and blebbing due to damage to the energy-dependent mem-
brane osmotic pumps [49]. This also results in membrane leak-
age and loss of mitochondria function. The membrane dame to 
the cell provokes an inflammatory response which can dam-
age additional hepatocytes and thus potentiate the number of 
cells dying and the extent of the necrotic lesion. In contrast, 
the apoptotic cell shrinks the cellular organelles under frag-
mentation and does not induce inflammation [49]. Apoptosis 
has been attributed to programed cell death, which is prob-
ably accurate when one is thinking about the process in tis-
sue remodeling during embryogenesis and development. But 
in the case of toxic response, apoptosis represents an effective 
cell death mechanism that is regulated by proapoptotic and 
anti-apoptotic genes and is a more controlled cell death than 
necrosis. Morphologically, apoptosis shows chromatin con-
densation, cell shrinkage and cell fragmentation without pro-
voking an inflammatory reaction [57]. Figure 32.4 is an H- and 
E-stained section of the liver from a F344 rat showing several 
apoptosis cells are various stages of the apoptosis process.
Necrosis, on the other hand, is characterized by cellular and 
nuclear swelling, patchy chromatin condensation, mitochon-
drial swelling, cytoplasmic vacuolization, plasma-membrane 
rupture and the dissolution of DNA (karyolysis) [49, 57, 59, 60, 
65, 66]. In addition, apoptosis only affects a few cells, while 
necrosis affects whole liver lobules. Apoptosis and mitosis 
are complementary in tissue homeostasis, but have opposing 
roles: the first causes cell losses, while the latter leads to tissue 
hyperplasia.
Given this, in the cancer process, it has been shown that sev-
eral nongenotoxic carcinogens prevent active cell death, lead-
ing to an increase in the preneoplastic population that leads 
to cancer progression [43]. For example, sustained activation 
of the AhR by 2,3,7,8-tetrachlorodibenzodioxin (TCDD) 
is believed to lead to the selection and proliferation of pre-
neoplastic hepatocytes by preventing normal apoptosis [41]. 
Similarly, activation of PPAR− by peroxisome proliferator 
compounds has been reported to suppress apoptosis [44, 67]. 
A wide range of toxic substances may trigger hepatocyte apop-
tosis in animals in vivo and in vitro.

32.3.2.3  Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver 
Disease (NAFLD)
Fatty liver disease is the result of accumulation of fat (steato-
sis) in hepatocytes. The diagnosis of steatosis is made when 
fat in the liver exceeds 5–10% by weight. Fatty liver dis-
ease has been classified into two general categories: ASH or 
NASH. In the case of the former, chronic alcohol consump-
tion results in fatty liver due to production of toxic metabo-
lites of alcohol including aldehydes [72]. In NAFLD, multiple 
(non-alcohol) factors including nutritional, drugs and hepatic 
toxicants and metabolic disorders have been identified in the 
initiation and progression of the disease [73]. While different 
in etiology, the pathologic sequelae from the accumulation 
of hepatocellular fat in alcoholic or non-alcoholic steato-
sis are commonly accompanied by a progressive inflamma-
tion of the liver and resulting hepatic fibrosis (steatohepatitis) 
(Figure 32.5). NAFLD consists of a spectrum of liver dam-
age, ranging from simple steatosis (NAFLD) to steatohepatitis 
(NASH), fibrosis and cirrhosis, and HCC [74]. The early stage 
of fat accumulation is reversible and is characterized by sim-
ple steatosis. Continual fat accumulation results in hepatocyte 
damage, inflammation and fibrosis producing the NASH phe-
notype. NAFLD progresses to NASH in approximately 10% 
of patients, and cirrhosis in 2–3% (15–25% of those that have 
NASH) patients usually over a period of 10–20 years. Some 
patients with advanced NASH may eventually develop HCC. 
Multiple factors are involved in the progression from simple 
steatosis to NAFLD to NASH to cirrhosis including diet, 
genetic predisposition and the gut microbiota. These factors 
form the background by which xenobiotics, including drugs 
and environmental agents, may influence the progression 
to steatohepatitis through modulation of multiple pathways 
including oxidative stress, mitochondrial function, fatty acid 
biosynthesis and inflammation.
The accumulation of fat in the liver takes on two forms of ste-
atosis: macrovesicular and microvesicular lipid droplets within 
the hepatocytes. The progression from NAFLD to NASH 
involves an increase in lipid droplet formation, hepatocellular 
injury and inflammation [74]. The lobular inflammation indica-
tive of NASH is predominantly in zone 3; however, with the 
eventual progression to fibrosis, the lesions are not localized in 
zone 3 [75]. The presence of apoptotic cells and necrotic cells are 
also seen with NASH. The progression from NAFLD to NASH 
is not a given. Only a small percentage of people develop NASH, 
fibrosis, cirrhosis and HCC [76]. There have been several rodent 
model developed to induce NAFLD including genetically modi-
fied mice: leptin-deficient (ob/ob), leptin-resistant (db/db), Pten 
knockout, SREBP-1c overexpressed, PPARα knockout mice as 
well as mice given high-fat diets [77].
32.3.2.4  Alcohol Fatty Liver Disease (AFLD)
Excessive alcohol consumption can lead to several liver dis-
eases from steatosis to HCC [78]. Ethanol is metabolized into 
acetaldehyde by alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) (Figure 32.6)

acetaldehyde and reduces NAD+ to NADH in cytosol. Chronic

CYP2E1. Ethanol oxidation by CYP2E1 generates ROS and

carried out by catalase. Resulting acetaldehyde is metabolized

is the main form of this enzyme and is mainly expressed in 
hepatocyte mitochondria. Resulting acetate enters the blood 
circulation and is metabolized into acetyl-CoA by acetyl-CoA 
synthetase. Finally, acetyl-CoA participates in Krebs cycle, 
producing final products of carbon dioxide and water. The dif-

lead to significant differences in enzyme activity when it comes 
to ethanol metabolism. People with heterozygous ALDH22/1 
have 80–90% lower enzyme activity than those with homozy-

one of the first liver responses following acute or chronic alco-

fatty liver and about 35% develop advanced AFLD. As the fat 
continues to accumulate, subsequent changes in the liver follow 
the same progression as seen in NAFLD (Figure 32.5).

genetic factors and cigarette smoking may affect the AFLD.

presence of hormones such as estrogen. Obesity was shown

32.3.2.6  Liver Cancer
HCC is the seventh most common human cancer in the world, 
and it represents 75% of all liver cancer cases [119]. HCC rep-
resents the fourth leading cause of cancer deaths worldwide. 
Only small proportion of HCC tumors are detected at an early 
stage [120]. There is an etiological association between hepati-

disease (ALD) with HCC [121, 122]. Patients who are heavy 
alcohol consumers are at an increased relative risk (1.5- to 
3-fold) of HCC [123]. Additionally, patients with HCV who 
also are heavy alcohol drinkers have a 2-fold increased risk 
compared to heavy drinkers alone due to the apparent syner-
gistic effect of the virus and the alcohol [121]. While most of 
the HCC cases develop in the presence of cirrhosis, there is 
a small percentage that can occur without cirrhosis. In HCC 
cases related to HBV exposure, 10–30% of the cases develop 
in the absence of cirrhosis that seem to be linked to the risk 
factors seen with metabolic syndrome [124, 125]. Lipid

32.3.2.6.2  Adverse Outcome Pathways
The mode of action framework relies to a good extent on in 
vivo data as a primary source to develop the proposed MOA 
with the addition of other information, in vitro data, epi-
demiology results etc. [132]. There was a need to develop 
high-throughput cell-based bioassays to evaluate many more 
compounds than is feasible with animal models [43]. Hence, 
the development of the Tox21 and Toxcast approaches took 
place [135]. In line with the high-throughput approach, the 
adverse outcome pathway approach is being used. This relies 
on the identification of KEs and more specific the MIE(s). The 
overall adverse outcome pathway approach is very similar to 
the mode of action approach in that KEs are identified in a 
toxic pathway [41, 42, 44, 132–134]. One difference is that 
the adverse outcome pathway may be at the individual or the 
population level. An adverse outcome for an individual may 
be impaired development, impaired reproduction, chronic dis-
ease. For population-adverse outcomes, this would result in 
modification of a population structure or even species extinc-
tion [43]. One logistic difference is the reliance on the identifi-
cation of the MIE (Figure 32.10). The MIE begins the toxicity 
process and thus is required for the toxicity endpoint. Several 
examples of the MIE include receptor binding, DNA binding 
and protein oxidation [43].

32.3.2.8  Mitochondrial Function
Mitochondria 
are 
double-membrane–bound 
organelles 
found in most eukaryotic organisms. Mitochondria generate 
most of the cell’s ATP. The involvement of mitochondria in 
hepatic dysfunction is well recognized [142]. Mitochondria 
play a crucial part in fat oxidation and cell energy genera-
tion, with components such as the oxidation and tricarboxylic 
acid cycles, as well as the enzymes cytochrome c oxidase and 
ATP synthase, being essential. It is caused by an interruption 
in the oxidation of fatty acids in microvesicles that occurs 
suddenly [56].
Damage to the mitochondria and loss of ATP production 
are the initial steps in ischemic injury and chemically induce 
necrosis [43]. Once ATP production is decreased, the energy-
dependent plasma membrane pumps are impaired allowing 
for osmotic disruption of the cell. Maintenance of the mito-
chondrial transition pore (MPT) is essential to maintaining a 
functioning mitochondria [46]. Normally, the MPT channel 
in the inner membrane of the mitochondria is closed; how-
ever, when the channel is open, cells undergo apoptosis and 
die. Mitochondria are also the primary source of ROS, which 
can cause oxidative damage to mitochondrial DNA and cell 
death [71, 142–144]. ROS are important cytotoxic and signal-
ing factors in the development of inflammatory liver diseases. 
Exposure to ethanol can cause oxidative stress by increasing 
the generation of ROS. Several drugs, including valproic acid, 
pirprofen, tetracyclines and amiodarone, have been shown to 
specifically target the mitochondria, effecting ATP produc-
tion [46].
32.3.2.9  Oxidative Stress
The production of ROS (superoxide, hydrogen peroxide, 
single oxygen and hydrogen radical) during normal cellular 
metabolism is countered by defense mechanisms in each cell 
to avoid or minimize harm. Oxidative stress is defined as a 
disruption in the balance between prooxidants and antioxi-
dants [71]. Oxidative damage form oxidative stress can result 
in lipid peroxidation, damage to macromolecules (DNA, 
RNA, protein), formation of oxidative DNA adducts and 
modification of gene expression. In a simplified view, three 
general categories of compounds induce oxidative damage 
in the liver. Compounds such as CCl4 and CBrCl3 are metab-
olized to reactive free radicals resulting in lipid peroxidation 
and membrane damage [70, 71]. APAP, which is detoxified 
by glutathione, depletes glutathione when administered at 
high concentrations. Since glutathione is a necessary anti-
oxidant in the cell, its removal results in oxidative stress and 
damage. A third group of chemicals including diquat and 
menadione have redox properties and when metabolized by 
the liver produce ROS [71]. Nonparenchymal cells also are 
involved in the oxidative stress pathways in the liver [145]. 
KCs and neutrophils produce hydrogen peroxide when acti-
vated which in turn can produce hydroxyl and superoxide 
radicals [146].
Figure 32.11 illustrates the sources and effects of oxidative 
stress on the cells. Endogenous sources include intracellular 
organelles (mitochondria being the greatest source of ROS) 
and extracellular sources (predominantly inflammation). 
Exogenous sources include xenobiotics and pathogens. The 
production of ROS is controlled by both enzymatic and nonen-
zymatic antioxidants in other to maintain cellular homeostasis. 
An overage of ROS can, depending on dose, induce functional 
and structural effects on the cell or in the extreme lead to cell 
death. Figure 32.12 illustrates the dose-response effect of ROS 
production. Low levels of ROS may produce modification of 
cell function and structure while high levels are not compatible 
with life and results in cell death.
Measurement of ROS and oxidative damage has been 
studied extensively. Lipid peroxidative is measured by deter-
mining malonaldehyde which is generated by the break-
down of peroxided lipids in thiobarbituric acid [147, 148]. 
Another lipid peroxidation product, the conjugated dienes 
can be determined by measuring F2 isoprostane, which is 
the result of arachidonic acid peroxidation in phospholip-
ids. F2-isoprostane quantification in plasma and liver of rats 
exposed to halothane has been performed and is indicative of 
lipid peroxidation [147, 148].

32.3.2.10  Application of New Approaches 
to Liver Toxicity
The “omics” sciences are a new tool that can help develop a 
more in-depth knowledge for liver diseases without definitive 
diagnostic markers such as NAFLD and drug-induced liver 
injury (DILI). As the name suggests, genomics provides the 
complete genetic overview to better understand the genome 
structure and can include techniques as DNA sequencing and 
gene mapping. Along with the genomics, transcriptomics 
focuses on the genome-produced RNA transcripts. It exclu-
sively concentrates on the modulation of the RNA transcripts 
instigated by environmental factors such as drugs or diet. 
Proteomics, on the other hand, analyzes the protein products 
and any connection between them, while metabolomics evalu-
ates the entire metabolism.
32.3.2.10.1  Genomics
The 1980 Nobel Prize in Chemistry was shared between 
Frederick Sanger and Walter Gilbert for the development of 
sequencing method of DNA [149]. One year following that, 
the first complete genome sequence of a human mitochondrion 
was registered. Since then, genomics grew exponential, and a 
draft of the human genome was completed in 2001. Genomics 
involves three main steps: first, the sequencing of DNA, which 
can be the traditional DNA sequencing performed in sequenc-
ing centers and centralized facilities, or the new generation of 
fast turnaround benchtop sequencing that sequences smaller 
quantities of base pairs than compared to DNA sequencing: 
shotgun and high-throughput (or next-generation) sequencing. 
After sequencing is done, the assembly of that sequence needs 
to be created for a representation of the original chromosome, 
and the annotation and analysis of that representation.
Genomics allows the researchers to identify previously unre-
ported genetic variants associated with a specific outcome. In

32.3.2.10.2  Proteomics
Nowadays, proteomics relies greatly on mass spectrometry 
(MS) for its accuracy and large-scale characterization abilities 
[152]. It can be gel-free, in which samples are directly used in 
the MS, or gel-based, which takes an extra step in the sample 
preparation before being measured [153]. Another peculiar-
ity in the sample preparation in the proteomics approach is 
that the samples of interest can be first separated before being 
individually characterized, or in the case of the “shotgun” pro-
teomics, it is first digested into a mixture of peptides and then 
analyzed [152]. Proteomics is a multidimensional setup to ana-
lyze not only the whole protein expression but also the interac-
tions between proteins. Regardless of the sample preparation, 
the MS downstream workflow included three main steps: 
protein ionization by an ion source, ions separation for mass 
analyzes and ion detection. When done in plasma samples, for 
example for liver disease examination, the plasma proteome 
is becoming more and more attractive due to its minimally 
invasive diagnosis of liver disease, but it is extremely difficult 
to analyze. So far, most of the studies focus on the later stages 
of liver diseases, such as liver cancer [154]. The application 
of proteomics has also significantly contributed to our under-
standing of the phenotype and function of the various cells in 
the liver [155]. MS-based proteomics showed the importance 
of cell-type resolution when studying liver functions as previ-
ously unidentified components of TGF-μ–signaling pathway 
were identified in HSCs [154]. Also, hepatocytes and bile duct 
cells together are equivalent to more than 80% of the liver vol-
ume and can cause not specific results when the whole liver is 
analyzed [154].
When comparing proteomics to transcriptomics, more spe-
cifically RNA sequencing, the data correlation is 0.5–0.6, 
indicating that proteomics modulations cannot rely on the 
transcript level modulation. In fact, there are around 10,000 
proteins for one mRNA [154].
Potential diagnosis, prognosis and progression biomarkers 
have already been proposed by proteomics; however, there 
is still the need for an easier and cheaper MS-based clinical 
tool to be used by hepatologists and medicine practitioners for 
diagnosis and prognosis assessment [154]. Also, expectations 
on proteomics data analysis are expected for better workflows, 
cloud computing and remote accesses to proteomics.
32.3.2.10.3  Metabolomics
Metabolomics is sometimes referred to as “postgenomics” 
as it was developed after the genomics, transcriptomics and 
proteomics approaches. It was first defined by Nicholson in 
1999 [156]. The main purpose of metabolomics is to exam-
ine small endogenous molecule related to metabolism in the 
target organism. Metabolomics is not dependable of the other 
“omics” approach; thus, it can be performed without full 
genome sequencing. Indeed, small modulations in the gene 
and protein level causes an amplified modulation in metab-
olites, and the quantity of metabolites is considerably lower 
than genes and proteins. In addition, metabolites of different 
organisms tend to be similar, causing metabolomics to be a 
very attractive common option. The earliest and most com-
mon technique used for metabolomics is the nuclear magnetic 
resonance. This technique measures the nuclear spin proper-
ties after radiation absorption in a powerful magnetic field. 
Because of that, it can be used in vivo and in situ in a nonin-
vasive and nondestructive way [157]. Other most recent, but 
equally common, techniques combine chromatography with 
MS: liquid chromatography-MS (LC-MS) and gas chromatog-
raphy-MS (GC-MS). The chromatography component works 
on the principal of separating compounds and the mass-spec-
trometry component identifies these components. Both tech-
niques analyze small molecules; however, GC-MS evaluates 
more volatile, gasified and thermally stable substances, and 
LC-MS assesses nonvolatile, nonvaporized and consequently 
more polar compounds. Because most of the biological stud-
ies tend to use samples that are polar, such as blood, tissue 
and urine, the LC-MS is the most used. Since some liver dis-
eases (autoimmune liver diseases, DILI, ALD and NAFLD) 
still lack diagnostic markers, metabolomics provides a new 
possibility on broader prospective biomarkers. For ALD, for 
example, urinary LC-MS metabolomic studies showed a cor-
relation between indole-3-lactic acid and the disease [158], 
opening doors for the possibility using it as a potential diag-
nostic marker for ALD.
32.3.2.10.4  RNA sequencing
RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) is a next-generation sequenc-
ing (NGS) developed more than a decade ago to examine 
RNA quantity and sequences in a specific sample [159, 160]. 
It allows researchers to explore and learn the entire cellular 
content (transcriptome) of the RNA. With this, we can gather 
information on which genes are regulated and expressed in the 
sample, knowing precisely if they are upregulated (activated) 
or downregulated (shut off). Thus, for example, the transcrip-
tome analysis of a specific tissue with a specific condition 
could potentially give us information on a gene of unknown 
function [159]. If a particular gene is expressed, we can then 
have an indication of what its role is. Not only that, but the 
results from RNA-seq allow us to group the genes with the 
help of a software to understand better the pathways related to

[161].
When analyzing the RNA in a more focus and traditional 
approach as the PCR, the researcher could potentially lose 
the opportunity of exploring new genes and pathways do not 
know before. The RNA-seq approach, along with the pathway 
analysis, provides mechanistic insights and potential strate-
gies. Unlike arrays, RNA-seq does not require specific probes 
from species or transcript, thus providing previously unknown 
changes that arrays cannot detect. On top of that, it can quan-

For example, a recent study [163] showed that there are

only that, but the use of RNA-seq also provided insights in 
new pathways and genes in the genetic differences between 
female and male. This subsequently can be translated to dif-

on the sex. In its early ages, RNA-seq, even though innova-

years that RNA-seq became what we know now [161]. To per-
form RNA-seq, there are several steps to be accomplished for 
a successful RNA-seq. The very first step involves isolating 
the RNA from the tissue of interest. Even though basic, this 
step is extremely important to guarantee good RNA quality. 
The second step would be converting the population of RNA 
into cDNA fragments and preparing the cDNA library [164]. 
Only then the cDNA library can be analyzed by NGS. The 
extent to which the library will be sequenced will depend on 
and the sequencing can be done in single-read or paired-end

less costly than the paired-end sequencing. These reads, that 
by the end of the sequencing will be many millions, can be 
then aligned to a genome of reference and gathered to pro-

After this stage, the researcher can focus on analyzing

but also analysis using R code and libraries like DESeq2 or 
limma can help to quantify the expression levels [164, 166]. 
With that, the researcher can explore the pathways through

the scientist to compare it with a particular pathological 
condition. On top of that, it allows the researcher to build 
its own pathway de novo from the DEGs identified as key 
affected elements [166]. RNA-seq, although being a multi-
steps technique, can be a very useful tool in science and 
is becoming more and more popular technique. With that, 
it is also becoming less costly and different genomic and 
bioinformatic centers as rising facilitating the availability 
of this service.
32.3.3  Models and Methods to 
Assess Liver Toxicity
Many laboratory approaches have been used to detect and 
monitor liver diseases. Two major approaches have been and 
continue to be used to assess the potential toxicity of chemical 
agents. These include both in vivo animal studies, predomi-
nantly in rodents (rats and mice) and in vitro studies employ-
ing cells. The overall goal of these toxicological evaluations 
is to provide meaningful, mechanistically data to be used to 
determine the relative risk of exposure to a chemical on human 
health. In evaluating the toxicity, it is important to note that the 
liver, because of its role in metabolism and detoxification, is 
frequently the target of the toxic effect of xenobiotics.
32.3.3.1  In Vivo Studies
For almost a century, animal species particularly rodents have 
been used to evaluate toxicity of chemical compounds [66, 67, 
167–171]. An underlying premise is that the toxic effects seen 
in animal species following short-term and long-term exposure 
will be predictive of the toxic effects of the chemicals in humans 
[43, 168, 170]. This premise is supported by the fact that in vivo 
rodent exposure obeys most of the same physiologic and pharma-
cologic process seen in humans. in vivo studies also allow for the 
utilization of exposure scenarios consistent with potential human 
exposure. For example, orally administered pharmaceuticals can 
be orally administered to a rodent. While the overall process of 
absorption, distribution, metabolism and elimination of chemicals 
in rodents is in general similar to that seen in humans, significant 
differences between rodent and humans metabolism and detox-
icifiation for specific compounds has been demonstrated [67, 171]. 
Although multiple endpoints are evaluated using animals includ-
ing developmental and reproductive effects, DNA altering effects, 
as well as other target tissue, in this discussion, the liver will be the 
primary subject. It is interesting to note that in the in vivo rodent 
treatment studies, the liver is the primary target for toxicity. The 
liver has also been shown to be the primary target for toxicity to 
many pharmaceuticals and non-pharmaceuticals in humans.
The standard toxicity testing for rodents involves the short-
term (7-, 14-, 28- and 90-day) continuous exposure to the agent 
and chronic (2 year) continuous exposure to the compound [56]. 
Usually two species of rodents, mice and rats are employed 
with both genders of each being tested [172]. Multiple con-
centrations of the compound being tested are exposed to the 
mice and rats continuously along with an untreated control 
group for each gender and species [43]. At sacrifice, complete 
necropsies are performed along with clinical pathology and 
histopathology. In addition, additional mechanistic studies can 
be incorporated into the bioassay [56]. For example, the effect 
of the chemical on liver gene expression and cell prolifera-
tion may be also examined. While the overall approach to the 
rodent bioassay has not changed in many years, modifications 
in the strains of rodents used as well as time and duration of 
exposure have been made. Currently the National Toxicology 
Program (part of the NIH) uses Harlan Sprague Dawley rats

and B6C3F1/N hybrid mice as the test subjects [173]. Recently, 
the bioassay has been expanded to incorporate a perinatal 
phase exposure. The perinatal phase includes exposure of rats 
to the testing compound for the duration of pregnancy in utero 
as well as during the lactation period via the mother’s milk. 
Perinatal exposure studies are performed prior to the 13-week 
and 2-year bioassays [174]. This approach was incorporated 
to observe any in utero or post-birth effects of the compounds 
being tested.
32.3.3.1.1  Dose Selection for the In Vivo Toxicity Studies
The selection of doses for the subsequent subchronic and 
chronic bioassays is of utmost importance in obtaining a 
proper understanding of the relative toxicity of the chemical 
being tested [41, 42]. Different regulatory agencies have dif-
ferent guidelines for determining the maximum dose to be 
studied in subsequent subchronic and chronic studies. There 
are typically five options for defining the highest dose. These 
include the maximum tolerated dose (MTD), maximum feasi-
ble dose, a limit dose (maximum of 1,000 mg of drug/kg body 
weight), exposure saturation and dose providing a 50-fold mar-
gin of exposure (from the anticipated clinical treatment dose. 
The most common of these, the MTD, is defined as the highest 
dose of a drug that does not cause unacceptable side effects 
or overt toxicity in a specific period. These side effects can 
range from mild effects such as reduced weight gain, moder-
ate effects such as weight loss up to 20% or substantial other 
effects. The MTD is determined in acute short duration toxic-
ity studies with a small number of animals [41, 42]. The ratio-
nale for using a high dose in the chronic studies is to maximize 
the possibility of detecting any toxic effects in the laboratory 
as a safeguard for human exposure.
32.3.3.2  In Vitro Models
In addition to in vivo models, methods for assessing the hepa-
totoxic properties of chemical agents include hepatic slices, 
isolated liver cells, ex vivo liver and primary hepatocyte 
culture and cell cultures. With the increased emphasis to 
reduce animal testingthere is a recognition to further develop 
alternative non animal models to determine of the toxicity 
of a compound (or mixtures)(175). The latter is particularly 
important in developing scientifically and mechanistically 
based information for potential human risk evaluation [39]. 
In 2008, several federal governmental agencies proposed a 
collaborative research approach to develop and validated in 
vitro cell-based assays using quantitative high-throughput 
screening [176]. This program, Tox21 program, includes three 
charges: (1) identify mechanisms of chemically induced bio-
logical activity, (2) prioritize chemicals for more extensive 
testing and (3) develop more relevant and predictive mod-
els of in vivo toxicological responses (as a continuum of the 
Tox21, in 2019, the USEPA administrator issued a Directive 
to phase out animal testing by 2035) [175]. Thus, in vitro and 
in silico models of predictive toxicity will eventually replace 
the rodent bioassay [177]. There are several benefits of using 
in vitro approaches in comparison to the in vivo bioassays. In 
vitro models are less costly and less time-consuming. Given 
these two benefits, more chemicals and more doses can be 
tested in vitro. In vitro models also allow for the evalua-
tion of mixtures of compounds, which are too expensive to 
be performed in the rodent bioassay [178, 179]. The regula-
tory agencies have focused on the integration of liver toxic-
ity data and the establishment of “toxicity signs” to predict 
the potential toxicity of a compound. Approaches such as 
the USEPA ToxCastTM system have been established to this 
end. Of course, the push to use in vitro models as a replace-
ment for animal in vivo testing has drawbacks. Specifically, 
the properties of a chemical agent with regard to absorption, 
metabolism and detoxification (pharmacokinetics) cannot be 
completely duplicated in vitro. In addition, since the liver is a 
multicellular tissue, maintaining the nonparenchymal cells in 
the culture is important.
Historically the most frequent models for in vitro study in 
the liver have used primary hepatocyte cultures and immortal-
ized cell lines [177, 180, 181]. Primary cultured hepatocytes 
and some liver cell lines have several advantages in that many 
hepatocytes can be secured from one animal, there is the 
opportunity to compare the effects of chemicals on cultured 
human hepatocytes with cultured rodent hepatocytes and the 
CYP P450 enzymes are, at least for several days, maintained at 
near in vivo levels [61, 167, 178, 179, 181, 182]. A problem with 
the cultured hepatocyte model is that only hepatocytes are uti-
lized in the system. In an attempt to maintain the multicellular 
organization of the liver in an in vitro model, three-dimen-
sional cell culture, co-cultures and bio-artificial liver models 
have been developed. In addition, isolated perfused liver mod-
els, while still are dependent of using an animal liver, have 
seen application in measuring compound pharmacokinetics in 
an in intact liver. These multiple in vitro models will be further 
discussed below.
32.3.3.2.1  Isolated Perfused Liver
The isolated perfused liver probably is the closest model to 
studying liver metabolism and function to the in vivo liver 
itself [183]. The liver is removed and supplied artificially via 
perfusion with medium and oxygen, thus preserving the liver’s 
architecture and hemodynamic characteristics. Perfusing iso-
lated livers requires many animals, as each liver is required 
for one data point per experiment. Further descriptions of the 
use and techniques involved in perfused liver preparations are 
summarized in several reviews
32.3.3.2.2  Liver Slices
The culture of liver slices was developed to overcome some 
of the limitations of the isolated perfused liver model and the 
primary culture hepatocytes [184]. Brendel and coworkers 
developed a reproducible method for procurement and cul-
ture of the liver slices [185]. During incubation, their organ-
culture technique exposes the upper and lower sides of the 
cultivated tissues to the gas phase to overcoming the disinte-
gration of the interface into the long-term incubating of tissue 
slices. This precision liver slice technique of Brendel repro-
ducibly produces slices thin enough that allow for the absorp-
tion those compounds as well as nutrients and oxygen from 
the medium into the innermost cells via passive diffusion 
[184, 185]. The use of the precision-cut rat liver slices permits

the measurement of liver damage in a reproducible manner. 
The viability of the liver slices has been reported as being 
a maximum of between 120 and 240 hours. A roller incuba-
tion system has been found to be the most effective way to 
maintain the viability of precision-cut tissue slices in culture. 
Using tissue slices Weldon showed a dose-dependent decrease 
in the metabolism of L-leucine following CCl4 incubation 
with liver slices [186]. Bromobenzene, o-bromobenzonitrile, 
o-bromobenzene and other selective bromo compounds have 
been examined for their relative toxicities in slices from rats, 
guinea pigs and cynomolgus monkey liver. This identifies an 
advantage of the liver slices in that from one liver, multiple 
sample sources and be secured, thus reducing the number of 
animals needed to supply livers. In addition, one can secure 
slices from human liver, and thus, the precision slices allow 
for comparison of metabolism and toxicity between species 
[184–186].
It has been demonstrated that the liver slices in culture are 
capable of both phase I and II of metabolism. In addition, 
peroxisome proliferation, glucose metabolism and DNA syn-
thesis, all important toxicologic endpoints, have been dem-
onstrated in cultured liver slices. Confocal microscopy is 
required, because of the thickness of the slices, to examine the 
slices while in culture. Slices of liver treated to benzo[a]pyrene 
produced DNA adducts similar to that seen in vivo treatment. 
Since Brendel and Smith et al. [185] introduced the precise 
liver slice technique in 1985, the use of the slices for the mea-
surement of hepatic function and toxicity has been successful, 
the tissue slice preserves tissue architecture and the presence 
of all liver cell types having an advantage over cultured indi-
vidual cells.
32.3.3.2.3  Hepatocyte Isolation
Isolated hepatocytes can be easily obtained from an intact 
liver using an established two-stage in situ perfusion method 
[179, 187]. In this procedure, the liver is perfused through the 
portal vein initially with a calcium-free buffer containing the 
calcium-chelating agent (EGTA) (stage 1). This is followed by 
perfusion of a collagenase-containing medium (stage 2). The 
resulting suspension of individual hepatocytes is produced 
with high viability (greater than 90%). The isolated cells can 
be used in suspension for short-term metabolism studies or 
plated onto plastic culture dishes and maintain as primary 
cultures. The two-stage liver isolation has been applied to a 
variety of rodent species including rabbits, mice, rats, guinea 
pigs and hamsters. In addition, hepatocytes have been isolated 
using liver lobes from human liver and non-human primate 
liver successfully and place in culture. Modification to the 
two-stage collagenase perfusion has been used to selectively 
isolate and culture periportal versus centrilobular hepato-
cytes. More recently, cryopreservation methods have been 
successfully developed, which allow for the storage of iso-
lated hepatocytes that can be subsequently placed in culture 
[188, 189]. This is particularly valuable in the human hepa-
tocyte culture since a large number of cells can be obtained 
from the same human subject liver and stored for subsequent 
studies [178].
Isolated hepatocytes have been widely used in studies of 
chemical biotransformation and toxicity. Metabolic enzymes 
in the isolated cells are maintained for several hours, but the 
limited viability of the isolated cells and hence the metabolic 
activity of these isolated hepatocytes decrease relatively rap-
idly and thus have a relative short window for use (approxi-
mately 3 hours or less). Isolated hepatocytes have also been 
used to assess toxicity. The cytotoxicity of newly isolated 
hepatocyte suspensions is a useful method for xenobiotics to 
be screened for hepatotoxic characteristics. Two predominant 
methods have been used to determine hepatocyte viability. One 
method involves the measurement of lactate dehydrogenase 
(LDH) leakage [190]. LDH leakage reflects the breakdown of 
membrane integrity which in turn reflects cell death. A second 
method involves the staining of the cells in suspension with 
trypan blue [191]. Trypan blue is excluded from viable hepa-
tocytes. Thus, measurement and counting of stained cells ver-
sus non-stained cells under a microscope reflects the relative 
viability of the hepatocyte suspension. In addition, other indi-
cators of membrane integrity have been reported including the 
measurement of intracellular potassium, sodium or calcium 
concentrations. Hepatocytes treated with Adriamycin showed 
a 3-hour decline in potassium decline after treatment [192]. 
Isolated hepatocytes exposed to the classic hepatotoxicants, 
bromobenzene and APAP, showed decreases in glycogen and 
protein production. In the examination of heavy metal toxicity, 
Frazier noted that a decrease in protein synthesis in concert 
with increased trypan blue staining proved to be the most sen-
sitive indicators of cadmium, copper and zinc toxicity [193].
Isolated hepatocytes have been evaluated for toxicity fol-
lowing treatment with many of the classic hepatotoxicants 
including APAP, ethanol, methotrexate, fentanyl, bromoben-
zene and chlorinate hydrocarbons [183, 187, 191]. However, 
using these established hepatic toxicants, investigators have 
noted significant differences in the relative toxicity of these 
compound in the isolated hepatocytes versus the in vivo toxic-
ity data. In general, the toxicity observed in the isolated cells 
was less, at comparable doses, to that seen in vivo. Therefore, 
while the use of isolated hepatocytes has some application to 
study short-term metabolism and cytotoxicity of chemical, 
the steady decrease in viability within a short time limits this 
model’s use to well-defined short-term questions.
32.3.3.2.4  Hepatocyte Culture

(Table 32.9). One is the primary culture. These are hepatocytes 
that are taken from the liver of an animal using an in situ two-
stage perfusion method noted above [185, 189]. The cells are 
isolated and place in culture. They have the functional meta-
bolic and molecular properties of the intact liver but a lim-
ited in lifespan to a few days. These cells in culture maintain 
their polygonal appearance, form tight and gap junctions and 
exhibit most of the functions attributed to in vivo liver includ-
ing producing albumin, maintaining functional P450 enzymes 
and maintaining their cellular organelles (Figure 32.13) [194]. 
The technical aspects and methods of isolation and cul-
ture of hepatocytes from a variety of species have been well 
described. The standard approach is to grow the hepatocytes 
in primary culture as a monolayer on plastic culture dishes.

made recently in an attempt to prolong viability and metabolic

32.3.3.2.5  Cell Lines
A second liver culture approach utilizes cell lines derived from 
liver (Table 32.9). The advantages of the cell lines are that they 
are clonal and can be cultured repeatedly, not requiring any in 
vivo liver. A disadvantage is that to date most of the liver cell 
lines are derived from neoplastic livers or from undefined cells 
in the liver. The most used of immortalized human cell lines 
are the HepG2, Hep3B, HBG and the newly created HepaRG 
[194]. While in HepG2 cells expression of genes involved in 
phases I and II metabolism has been shown to fluctuate after 
multiple passages of the cell line. In contrast, the HepaRG cells 
, also an immortalized cell line from a human primary liver 
tumor appears to maintain the expression levels of CYP450s, 
nuclear receptors and Phase II enzymes throughout multiple 
passages [194].
32.3.4  Measuring Liver Toxicity
A variety of tests have been established to detect and evalu-
ate liver function and toxicity. These tests range from indirect 
evaluations, such as measuring liver enzymes in the serum in 
vivo to histopathologic analysis. Multiple reviews and sum-
maries have been reported in the scientific literature on these 
tests, and the reader is referred to these.
32.3.4.1  Serum Enzymes
An effective indirect method for determining hepatic toxic-
ity in the intact animal or human is to measure selective 
hepatic enzymes released into the bloodstream by damaged 
or dead liver cells [198]. The basis for the measurement of 
serum enzymes is due to the fact that as cells are injured or 
die, the cell membrane becomes porous and cytoplasmic com-
ponents (including enzymes) in the cell are released into the 
circulation [198–200]. This includes liver enzymes unique to 
the liver and specific liver cells. Four serum enzymes, ALT, 
AST, alkaline phosphatase (ALP) and ć-glutamyl transferase 
(GGT) have been measured in the serum of animals as well 
as humans to assess the functioning state of the liver in vivo 
[201]. One or more of these enzymes are elevated in liver tox-
icity and diseases. Blood elevations of ALT and AST may indi-
cate the presence of hepatocyte damage and death (necrosis) 
while elevations of ALP and GGT reflect damage to the bile 
ducts and cholestatic function. A list of the commonly mea-
sured biomarkers for liver function and toxicity is presented 
in Table 32.10.
In the liver, both sublethal and necrosis can result in an 
increase in serum ALT [49, 51]. Although in high concentra-
tions in hepatocytes, ALT is also found in the kidneys and 
muscle. In humans, an increase of up to 300 IU/L of ALT may 
not reflect just hepatocyte damage but could be due to the dam-
age in the kidneys or muscles. ALT increases more than 500 
IU/L are more specifically linked to liver damage, although 
the cause of the liver damage will require further investigation 
since infection and toxic injury will present similarly. ALD, 
NAFLD, fat accumulation in liver and steatohepatitis show an 
increase in serum ALT [78, 202]. AST is present in both a 
mitochondrial form and a cytoplasmic form. While like ALT, 
it is also found in other organs including heart, muscle, kidney 
and lungs. It is in high concentrations in the liver, but the wide

As is apparent, increases in serum enzymes may reflect 
liver injury but defining a specific cause of the liver damage, 
whether viral, inflammatory or from chemicals and drugs, 
is difficult. Hy Zimmerman, a renowned hepatologist, after 
observing many cases of drug-induced liver injury concluded 
that chemical- or drug-induced liver damage as a cause could 
be defined if three components were met [207]. One, the drug 
induces hepatocellular injury as seen with increased serum 
ALT or AST 3-fold or greater of normal. Second, the drug also 
causes an increase in serum bilirubin of 2-fold with apparent 
cholestasis (normal serum ALP). And third, there is no other 
apparent cause for the increase in ALT, AST and bilirubin such 
as hepatitis, alcohol abuse or other preexisting liver disease. If 
these three criteria are met, then the cause of the liver injury is 
highly linked to chemical- or drug-induced liver injury.
ALP is found in the cells lining the bile ducts. It is important 
in lipid transposition to the intestine. An increased serum ALP 
is linked to cholestasis from impaired bile formation or bile 
flow obstruction [201]. As with other serum markers, ALP is 
also present in other tissue and is particularly high in the bone. 
Over a half of the normal serum ALP levels can be attrib-
uted to bone as a source. GGT is a microsomal enzyme found 
in hepatocytes, biliary epithelial cells, kidney and pancreas. 
GGT participates in glutathione metabolism by transporting 
glutathione-related peptides into the cell. Increased serum 
GGT as seen with ALP is indicative of bile duct damage and 
cholestasis [201]. Serum GGT is also elevated in a third of 
hepatitis C patients. Serum GGT is also increased 2- to 3-fold 
in non-ALD.
Other serum enzymes, although not utilized as much as the 
four above, have also been studies for the identifying liver 
damage and possible causes of the liver damage. The glyco-
protein 5´ nucleosidase is a membrane glycoprotein found 
in many tissues. It is raised in the serum in jaundice, paren-
chymal liver disease and liver metastases. Ceruloplasmin is a 
carrier of the copper and is increased in liver infections, non-
Wilson liver disease and jaundice [208]. In the case of Wilson 
disease (where excess copper is present), the ceruloplasmin 
levels are decreased resulting in copper accumulation in in 
the liver [209]. Alpha-fetoprotein (AFP) is highly expressed 
in fetal liver with less expression in the normal human liver. 
Significant AFP levels in the serum is seen (400–500 ōg/L) 
with HCC [210]. Oxytocin is produced in liver mitochondria. 
In acute and chronic liver damage, serum levels of oxytocin 
are significantly increased. Drotman reported a 6-fold increase 
in serum OCT after exposure of rats to CCl4 [200]. The level 
of OCT increase correlated with the dose of the CCL4. The 
cytoplasmic enzyme SDH is a liver-specific enzyme that is 
increased in the serum following liver damage. In a study 
looking at the effect of CCL4 on rat liver, SDH was reported 
to be the most sensitive enzyme measure of liver damage.
LDH is found in many body tissues, including the liver. 
Elevated levels of LDH may indicate liver damage [211, 212]. 
Its widespread distribution throughout many tissues requires 
the analysis of selected tissue-specific isoenzymes to identify 
the specific target tissue undergoing injury. Cornish et al. [213] 
investigated the pattern of LDH isoenzymes to identify organ 
injury in rats. They found a difference in the isoenzyme pat-
terns in the serum of rats with liver versus rats with kidney 
injury. Rats with kidney injury had higher levels of LDH-1 
and LDH-2 isoenzymes, whereas rats with liver damage had 
higher levels of LDH-5. In contrast, Grice et al. reported that 
rats treated with CCl4, mercuric chloride, thioacetamide or 
diethanolamine at doses produced tissue damage [148, 213].

32.3.4.2  Histopathological Evaluation
While the measurement of serum enzymes for the presence 
and severity of liver injury is valuable, the evaluation of the 
liver by histopathology is the true indicator of liver damage 
[214, 215]. This is particularly important in rodent in vivo 
studies. The procedure for histopathologic evaluation of the 
liver is well defined and involves euthanizing the rats or mice 
followed by collection of liver tissue [50, 52, 216, 217]. Blood 
samples for serum enzyme analysis are usually also collected 
at the same time to determine the correlation between the 
serum enzyme measurement and the histopathologic finding. 
The liver should be excised from the rodent within a minute 
or two of death, examined grossly for any lesions, weighed 
and sliced into 2–3 mm strips for subsequent histologic and 
biochemical/molecular processing [214, 215, 218–220]. 
Usually, alternate strips are collected with one going into 10% 
neutral-buffered formalin (NBF) for histopathology process-
ing and the other into liquid nitrogen (snap frozen) for fur-
ther molecular biology evaluation [221]. The frozen samples 
are stored at ′ 80 °C. The liver strips for histology are fixed 
overnight (18–24 hours) in NBF, and then routinely processed 
for paraffin embedding. Sections (5 um) are cut and placed 
on slides and stained with hematoxylin and eosin [217, 222]. 
Additional sections may be cut and stained for specific mark-
ers using special stains and/or immunohistochemistry. The 
slides are examined with light microscopy in a blinded fash-
ion. Any pathology is noted as to classification and extend is 
recorded. If liver necrosis is evident, the extent of the necrosis 
and location are noted. The degree of necrosis is scored by 
assigning a value from 0 to 4: with 0 = the absence of necro-
sis, 1+ = minimal necrosis, 2+ = mild necrosis, 3+ = moderate 
necrosis and 4+ = marked necrosis [217, 221, 222]. The pat-
tern, whether it be focal or diffuse and location of the necro-
sis, periportal or centrilobular, is also noted. Histopathologic 
evaluations have been shown to correlate well with other 
markers of hepatic injury and usually exhibit a dose-related 
severity with the increasing dose of the toxic agent [214, 
216–219, 221–223]. The percentage of deteriorated, necrotic 
or abnormal hepatocytes is used to determine the severity 
of the liver lesion (necrotic plus degenerate). Whatever the 
parameters investigated, there is usually a linear relationship 
between the degree of the biochemical change and the histo-
pathologic detected lesion. Most investigators have found that 
elevations in ALT serum activity were the most significantly 
associated with histopathological changes [215, 217, 222]. In 
contrast changes in liver weight were less correlated with the 
severity of liver damage. Electron microscopy has also been 
useful in examining the cellular changes seen in the liver 
following toxic injury. Organelle changes and modifications 
are easily detectable by electron microscopy. The ability to 
correlate ultrastructural changes with functional alterations 
makes electron microscopy a valuable tool in toxicological 
studies [217, 218, 221, 222]. Ultrastructural analysis of liver 
damage can detect intracellular changes before they become 
apparent by light microscopy examination or serum enzyme 
changes [218]. Minor ultrastructural anomalies in the early 
stages of hepatic damage can help determine the origin and 
biochemical processes involved in the liver pathogenesis [214, 
216–219]. More recently immunohistochemistry approaches 
have seen more use in helping to define the mechanisms of 
chemical toxicity to the liver. For example, measurement 
of cell proliferation and apoptosis in the liver is easily per-
formed using immunohistochemical stains. This staining can 
be quantitated with regard to the extent and of pathology and 
location of these cells [56]. Even more exciting is the appli-
cation of molecular techniques to histologic samples. Single 
cells or small groups of cells within a tissue can be visual-
ize by light microscopy and dissect out for traditional in situ 
hybridization or using the latest technology and methodology 
[215]. Advanced RNA-seq and pathway analysis of a single 
cell can also be performed [163, 224].
32.3.5  The Role of miRNAs in Liver 
Disease and Toxicity
miRNAs are a group of noncoding RNAs that are endoge-
nously expressed in almost all eukaryotic cells. They regulate 
gene expression at a post-transcriptional level by binding to the 
untranslated region of target mRNAs. miRNAs were identi-
fied in C. elegans, and so far, 1,800 miRNAs were identified in 
humans. Computational models estimate that approximately 
60% of genes that code proteins are regulated by miRNAs. 
miRNAs have a length of 18–24 nucleotides and are tran-
scribed by RNA polymerase II and III. Primary miRNAs have 
stem-loop shape, which is recognized by DGCR8 protein. The 
DGCR8 protein and primary miRNA form a complex with 
protein DROSHA, followed by cutting the primary miRNA 
and forming smaller precursor miRNA. Precursor miRNA 
then carried from the nucleus into the cytoplasm through the 
nucleopore by Exportin5 transporter protein. The precursor 
miRNA then cleaved into double-stranded miRNAs by RNase 
III endonuclease (Dicer). The argonaut molecule AGO 2 inter-
acts with Dicer and binds to the double-stranded miRNA. The 
miRNA is unwound, and one strand is released. The remain-
ing guide strand interacts with AGO 2 and additional proteins 
to form RNA-induced silencing complex (RISC). RISC can be 
guided to its target and inactivate one or multiple genes. RISC 
binds to a specific location of target gene’s mRNA, and mRNA 
sequence is complementary to the sequence of miRNA. The 
major ways how the mRNA is silenced are cutting the mRNA 
by RISC, RISC inhibiting translation by preventing ribosome 
subunit from binding to mRNA and destabilization of mRNA 
by shortening its poly(A) tail. One of the reasons miRNAs are 
involved in regulation of essential cellular processes is that a 
single miRNA can have several target genes, and a single gene 
can be regulated by several miRNAs. miRNAs were shown to 
be involved in cell proliferation and differentiation. miRNAs 
can be endogenous to a specific cell type or be present in sev-
eral tissues; for example, miR-122 is found only in hepatocytes.

32.3.5.1  miRNAs in Liver Fibrosis and Cirrhosis
As HSCs play the major role in liver fibrosis development by 
producing collagens, proteoglycans, fibronectin and laminin 
into ECM. Studies have shown that when miRNA profiles in 
activated HSCs were compared to those in quiescent HSCs, a 
significant number of miRNAs were dysregulated in former 
cells. For example, miR-143, miR-328, miR-193, miR-501, 
miR-325-5p, miR-349 and miR-29c were upregulated, while 
miR-20b-3p, miR-16, miR-122, miR-146a, miR-92b, miR-375, 
miR-15b and miR-341 were downregulated [61, 220, 225, 226]. 
Among the number of miRNAs that are involved in liver fibro-
sis, miR-29, miR-34 and miR-122 are the most important ones. 
Downregulation of miR-29 (miR-29a, miR-29b, miR-29c) in 
cardiac fibrosis drew the attention to this miRNA as a poten-
tial biomarker for tissue fibrosis. Additionally, miRNA pro-
filing in mice treated with CCl4 led to downregulation of all 
three types of miR-29. miR-29b has the highest expression in 
HSCs compared to hepatocytes, KCs and liver SEC. miRNA 
is functionally linked to several cell-signaling pathways: 
NFΕb pathway, TGF-μ and PI3K/AKT signaling related to 
liver fibrosis progression. The transcription factor AP-1, which 
is involved in miR-29 promotion, was shown to be activated 
during hepatic fibrosis in TGF-μ stimulated HSCs, leading to 
miR-29 decrease. Additionally, miR-29b overexpression led to 
reduced levels of SMAD3 and TGF-μ1, indicating the involve-
ment of miR-29b in TGF-μ1/SMAD3 pathway, thus in progres-
sion of liver fibrosis. Downregulation of miR-29 and decrease 
in ECM synthesis during HSC activation was reported in 
patients diagnosed with HCV-induced fibrosis [227]. Trans-
differentiation of HSC into myofibroblasts leads to reduced 
miR-29 expression, which in turn leads to upregulation of 
PDGF-C and IGF-1 [228]. As miR-29 plays an important role 
in communication between profibrogenic and inflammatory 
signals in HSC, it is considered a good therapeutic candidate 
in fibrosis patients. miR-34a was shown to be upregulated in 
rats with dimethylnitrosamine-induced liver fibrosis [229]. 
miR-34a and miR-34c directly target acyl-CoA synthetase 
long-chain family member 1 and peroxisome proliferator-
activated receptor gamma (PPARć) [229–231]. Both miRNAs 
were shown to negatively correlate with PPARć expression in 
activated HSCs. It was also reported that miR-34a can contrib-
ute to alcoholic liver injury by directly targeting Caspase-2 
and Sirtuin 1. Additionally, alteration in expression of MMPs 
1 and 2 was observed due to miR-34a dysregulation [232]. 
Fibrosis severity was observed to inversely related with hepatic 
miR-122 levels in chronic HBV-infected patients with fibrosis 
[233]. The transcription factor KLF6 has pro-fibrogenic prop-
erties, and being a direct target of miR-122, it was observed to 
be activated in miR-122–deficient mice. miR-122 levels were 
reported to decrease when fibrosis-related genes (−-SMA, 
Col1A1 and FN1) are upregulated in TGF-μ1-activated HSCs 
[234]. Furthermore, miR-122 can be utilized as a biomarker 
for liver fibrosis in NAFLD, as its levels are upregulated in 
mild fibrosis cases, compared to cases with severe liver fibrosis 
[235].
An analysis of miRNA in patients with HCC cases revealed 
that miR-21 contributes to HCC development playing a role 
of an oncogene. Interestingly, miR-21 is highly expressed in 
HCCs and other types of cancer. One of the main roles of miR-
21 in HCC is its inhibition of tumor suppressor gene PTEN. 
PTEN is a direct target of miR-21, and it dephosphorylates 
focal adhesion kinase (FAK). Activation of FAK is correlated 
with aggressive nature of tumor in HCC [236]. For that reason, 
when PTEN is downregulated, it leads to increased FAK phos-
phorylation and aggressive type of tumor in HCC. It has been 
shown that inhibition of miR-21 slows down the HCC cells 
growth and promotes apoptosis [237]. Accordingly, overex-
pression of miR-21 contributes to cell growth and consequent 
migration and invasion of cancerous cells [238]. Expression of 
miR-122 was shown to be downregulated in samples from HCC 
patients and in animal models [239]. Expression of miR-122 
is correlated with increased cell proliferation and decreased 
apoptosis. One of the functions of miR-122 is inhibiting pro-
liferation and migration. It promotes hepatocyte death in HCC 
lines and in mouse models [240, 241]. Deletion of miR-122 is 
correlated with fibrosis, steatohepatitis and in some instances 
with progression of HCC [242, 243]. It has been shown that 
miR-122 regulates proteins that promote HCC, among those 
are A disintegrin and metalloprotease 10, serum response fac-
tor and insulin-like growth factor 1 receptor (review paper). 
Additionally, miR-122 regulates cyclin G1 expression, which 
leads to increased p53 expression [244]. miR-199a was shown 
to suppress tumor growth, to induce cell death and to promote 
cell cycle arrest by regulating MMP-9, frizzled type 7 receptor 
and hypoxia-inducible factor-1− in HCC cell lines [245–247]. 
miR-199a-3p was artificially overexpressed in cell lines and 
was shown to prevent HCC by suppressing cell proliferation 
and migration, and by promoting controlled cell death. It 
was also shown that miR-199a targets tumor-promoting p21 
(RAC1)-activated kinase 4 (PAK4), the mammalian target of 
rapamycin and c-Met [248–250]. miR-199a suppresses tumor 
growth in HCC by regulating pro-angiogenic mediators [251]. 
miRNAs, expression of which is increased in various cancers, 
are known as “oncomiRs”. miR-21 is one of them, as well as

32.3.5.2  The Role of miRNAs in Liver Toxicity
miRNAs are being studied extensively to investigate their 
role as biomarkers of liver toxicity. The study conducted to 
investigate the effect of APAP and CCl4 administration on 
miRNA expression in rats has shown six miRNAs (miR-153, 
miR-302b_AS, miR-337, miR-363, miR-409-5p, miR-542–3p) 
to be upregulated and eight miRNAs (miR-29c_AS, miR-298, 
miR-327, miR-342, miR-370, miR-376c, miR-494, miR-503) to 
be downregulated [253]. The APAP administration was found 
to increase miR-122 and miR-192 in mice sera samples in a 
dose-dependent manner [254]. It must be noted that miRNA 
levels in the mentioned study elevated earlier than serum 
enzyme marker levels. Elevated miR-122 levels are in correla-
tion with AST/ALT levels of I/R mice model [255]. Increased 
levels of miR-122 and miR-192 were also confirmed in serum 
samples of APAP-induced ALF patients [256]. Same results 
were obtained in a study which investigated the sequencing 
data of patients with APAP overdose. Overall, 36 circulat-
ing miRNAs were found to be increased with miR-122, miR-
192, miR-194, miR-483 and miR-210 having increased levels 
in the liver [257]. An early increase in circulating miR-122 
and miR-192 levels demonstrate the ability to perform the 
early liver damage detection, which can improve the survival 
rates in AFL cases. Another miRNA with a potential role of 
acute liver injury biomarker is miR-223 [258]. It is involved 
in regulating hematopoietic linage differentiation and vari-
ous immune cell functions [259, 260]. A study with I/R-
induced liver injury in mice revealed miR-233 upregulation 
in mice liver [261]. Besides having a potential role of serving 
as biomarkers, miRNAs can play a therapeutic role for ALF 
patients. Overexpression of miR-125b-5p has reduced the 
hepatotoxicity of APAP in mice [262]. This effect was linked 
to expression of kelch-like ECH-associated protein1 (Keap1) 
[262, 263]. In a similar fashion, the therapeutic potential of 
miR-155 was investigated in mice with APAP-induced liver 
injury, and upregulation of miR-155 was determined in liver 
and blood samples [264]. miR-155 deficiency led to activation 
of NF-ΕB via the expression of p65 and IKKω, while intro-
duction of miR-155 agomir suppressed APAP-induced liver 
damage, which was shown by decreased AST and ALT levels 
[264]. In conclusion, investigating the deregulation in miRNA 
expression and their target genes can help to close the knowl-
edge gaps in understanding their role in the pathophysiology of 
liver injury and help determine new potential biomarkers for 
prognosis and diagnosis of liver toxicity.
32.4  Overall Conclusions
Although chemically induced liver injury is determined in 
different ways in laboratory animals, no single technique is 
suitable for the detection and quantification of all types of 
injury. Thus, for an accurate assessment of a chemical agent’s 
hepatotoxic potential, a battery of tests combining one or more 
biochemical/functional assays with a histological study of the 
liver are required.
