16.1  Introduction
The topic of safety assessment for cosmetics and personal care 
products (PCPs) is expansive and diverse. Consumer products 
are assessed depending on the sector of use as well as the 
regulatory and legislative requirements of the region where 
the product is sold. Depending on the type of ingredient and 
final application, the approval of safety is determined through 
legally mandated regulations set by government authorities, 
self-regulatory frameworks implemented by industry or a 
combination of both. In all cases, the ultimate responsibility 
is on the manufacturer putting the product on market to ascer-
tain safe use and to ensure there is no toxicological concern to 
either humans or the environment.
As with other chemical sectors, the assessment of cos-
metics and PCPs is based largely on the safety of the indi-
vidual ingredients including their toxicological profile and 
anticipated level of exposure (1). However, when it comes to 
assessing the risk of ingredients used in cosmetics and con-
sumer care products, there are a number of unique factors to 
consider. These include:
•	 Broad population exposure: Since PCPs are widely 
used across the entire population and are often used 
daily, chemicals in these products will reach a larger 
consumer population (so there is a high likelihood 
and frequency of exposure but not necessarily high 
concentration level).
•	 Direct and intended contact: Many consumer prod-
ucts, and consequently the ingredients within, are 
meant to be applied directly to the body.
•	 Multiple applications and uses: It is well under-
stood that the same chemical may be used at differ-
ent levels in different PCPs depending on function.

Fragrance ingredients are a good example of this as 
they are used broadly across different product types 
and are incorporated at different levels depending 
on the olfactory profile sought. Different levels of 
risk may arise depending on the use and the route 
of exposure. There is also a growing understanding 
around the issue of aggregate/co-exposure of chemi-
cals in various consumer products, potentially lead-
ing to combined effects if the individual chemicals 
are present at safe levels (2).
•	 Ethics of animal testing. Traditional animal test-
ing for cosmetics has ethical and legal issues. The 
EU and other regions have banned the testing of 
cosmetic ingredients for safety purposes. This has 
caused new approaches for toxicity testing to be con-
sidered and developed but still, challenges exist for 
some data gaps.
It is important to note that for cosmetics and PCPs, most often 
the final product is composed of more than one substance. As 
such, the final product must be evaluated for safety. While 
much of risk assessment in PCP is done through looking at 
the safety of individual ingredients, how they will behave in 
the final product in aggregate must be considered. For exam-
ple, the finished product may cause eye irritation, although 
individual ingredients are not expected to cause eye irrita-
tion. Additionally, final product formulations may need to be 
assessed for acute toxicity (accidental ingestion), as well as 
skin irritation and skin sensitization.
For the purposes of this chapter, we focus on the human 
safety and risk assessment of cosmetics and PCPs including 
hazard and risk characterization, exposure assessment, and 
dose-response assessment. Hazard identification has been cov-
ered in other chapters and is defined as the process of testing 
a substance to determine if it does or does not cause adverse 
effects. This is an important step because many regulations that 
may affect ingredients used by the cosmetics and PCP indus-
try are based on hazard classification of chemicals. The other 
key step is risk assessment, which assesses whether or not the 
substance is likely to be a problem in real-world usage scenar-
ios given practical issues, including how someone is exposed 
(comes in contact with the substance) and at what amount (dose).
While regulatory bodies have a role in regulating and moni-
toring cosmetics and PCPs, manufacturers are ultimately 
responsible to ensure the safety of their products and ingredi-
ents. As a result, companies often go beyond testing require-
ments (3,4) and design their products with safety in mind, 
meaning they are designed to prevent risk to the consumer, and 
ensure there is a sufficiently large Margin of Safety (MOS). 
The MOS is the ratio of the no-observed-adverse-effect level 
(NOAEL) obtained from toxicology studies to the predicted, 
or estimated human exposure level or dose, for each substance 
in the PCP. The safety of substances in PCPs is established 
in various ways, including using ingredients that have a long 
history of use without evidence of adverse effects, adhering 
to good manufacturing practices, continually evaluating and 
testing ingredients, screening new ingredients early in the 
pipeline for potential hazards (e.g., in silico evaluation), and 
providing consumers with usage instructions to ensure safe 
exposure levels.
16.2  Regulatory Definitions of 
Cosmetics and PCPs
Each region across the globe considers specific definitions for 
cosmetics and PCPs (5–10), as shown in Table 16.1. In general 
terms, cosmetics or PCPs are products that are applied to or 
used on the body to clean, beautify, or change its appearance 
without permanently altering or affecting the physical struc-
ture and/or physiological functions of the body. Although this 
description extends across most legal definitions (e.g., ASEAN 
Cos Dir 2008; FD&C Act, 1938, sec. 321 (i); EU Cosmetics 
Regulation (EC) 1223/2009), the activity of the ingredients 
used in such products and/or claims made by the product can 
determine if the product is actually regulated as a cosmetic 
or if it falls into another category, for example, an over-the-
counter (OTC) or even prescription drugs.
When claims are made for the prevention or treatment of 
disease or for affecting the structure or function of the body, 
some products may meet the definition for both cosmetics and 
drugs, and therefore must comply with the requirements for 
both. For example, in the US, an anti-dandruff shampoo which 
is intended to clean hair and treat dandruff, a toothpaste that 
contains fluoride for a cavity prevention benefit, and antiper-
spirant deodorants are viewed as OTC drugs, whereas in the 
EU, these applications are treated only as cosmetics. While 
regulations on how a product is classified may dictate how a 
product is registered for sale in a country, any safety testing 
that must be conducted, or what data must be kept on file by 
the manufacturer of the product, (the process of substantiating 
the safety of a PCP) largely remains the same regardless of 
classification.

and Self-Regulation Frameworks
16.3.1  Overview of Cosmetics 
Laws and Regulations
The regulatory requirements of cosmetics and PCPs differ 
from one country to another but all are aimed at protecting 
the consumer and ensuring that the products brought to the 
market are themselves safe and contain ingredients at safe 
use levels. In the majority of regions, no government licens-
ing or pre-market approval of products is required; however, 
there are some exceptions (e.g., color additives and sunscreens 
in the US) where responsibility is on the manufacturers to 
ensure the products are safe according to the criteria laid out 
in the respective country’s regulations. Table 16.2 provides the 
various regulations to be aware of, in six key markets, while 
Table 16.3 compares the key requirements under each regu-
lation (11–17). In other regions, cosmetic safety is addressed 
in various ways ranging from complex regulations (e.g., 
Brazil), to self-regulatory processes through trade associations

All regions take precautionary measures and have “red 
lists” which ban or restrict ingredients seen as having not 
acceptable hazard properties. In general, these lists help relay 
information that is easy to understand and are easily acces-
sible to consumers. But without complete information or 
understanding, public perception toward “chemicals of con-
cern” can be greatly impacted. Currently, there are approxi-
mately 1,300 substances that are banned for use in cosmetic 
products in the EU (and similarly in the ASEAN Cosmetic 
Directive), except under specified conditions or applications 
according to Annex III (18). In addition to regulating sun-
screens and color additives, the US FDA has ten regulations 
that prohibit or restrict substances based on identified safety 
concerns (19). Many of these substances are contaminants 
such as polyaromatic hydrocarbons and mercury compounds, 
but the list also includes ingredients that may be hazardous 
but can be used safely at appropriate doses, e.g., preserva-
tives such as chlorhexidine and formaldehyde precursors. 
While there is notably a discrepancy between the regions, 
much of this gap can be attributed to different legal frame-
works and the EU’s precautionary approach, meaning that a 
substance is more likely to be prohibited or restricted if it is 
presumed to be hazardous regardless of potency and that the 
majority of substances banned in the EU from cosmetics use 
have never been used in cosmetics.
Several non-governmental organizations (NGOs), which 
are organizations that operate independently of a government 
body and are typically focused on environmental, social, or 
political issues, have also created lists of ingredients to avoid 
as a consumers’ reference. Substances commonly found on 
these lists include BHA/BHT, formaldehyde, lead, and heavy 
metals, nanoparticles, petroleum, phthalates, silicones, triclo-
san, nitrosamines, and parabens. Again, many of these lists do 
not consider potency or dose and are based on hazard only and 
the precautionary principle. Often these lists contain arbitrary 
scoring of ingredients, inaccurate data, and misinterpreted 
or incomplete assessments. Even if these chemicals are con-
sidered safe when used as intended and, in some cases, have 
supportive assessments for use from authoritative bodies, the 
consumer perception is affected. This often results in manu-
facturers unnecessarily reformulating safe products to avoid 
negative publicity and meet consumer preferences.
16.3.2  Overview of Self-Regulatory Bodies
In addition to regulatory frameworks, expert committees to 
advise on the safety of ingredients, safety programs, and stan-
dards have been established by the cosmetic, personal care, 
and fragrance industries and in some cases governmental 
bodies. These programs are voluntary albeit often required 
for trade association membership. They provide indepen-
dent, risk-based standards designed to go beyond what is 
required by existing regulations. Some key groups involved 
include the Cosmetic Ingredient Review (CIR) Expert Panel, 
The Scientific Committee on Consumer Safety (SCCS), the 
Research Institute for Fragrance Materials (RIFM) with the 
Expert Panel for Fragrance Safety, and the Flavor Extract 
Manufacturers Association (FEMA) Expert Panel. Whereas 
the CIR, RIFM, and FEMA Expert Panels are established via 
industry support, the SCCS is a Committee formed under the 
governance of the European Commission. A key point for all 
these bodies is independent governance and transparency of 
their assessment procedures. A comparison of these safety 
assessment frameworks is provided in Table 16.4.

16.3.2.1  CIR
CIR is an independent Expert Panel established by the 
Personal Care Product Council trade association. The CIR 
Panel is made up of toxicologists, chemists, dermatologists, 
industry representatives, and the US FDA (as a non-voting 
member) who evaluate whether ingredients are safe under 
current uses. The CIR process is described by Boyer et al. 
(20). The CIR Panel evaluates ingredient safety by critically 
evaluating published and unpublished data and literature for 
individual ingredients or groups of chemically similar ingre-
dients. The CIR Panel meetings are open to the public, and all 
comments are considered. The CIR Panel then votes on the 
safety evaluation and publishes a final safety evaluation in a 
peer-reviewed journal. Guidance documents are then created 
for the PCP industry.

16.3.2.3  RIFM
RIFM is an independent international scientific authority for 
the safe use of fragrance ingredients. RIFM conducts safety 
assessments for all fragrance ingredients, based on a risk 
assessment approach that incorporates current use levels, 
product type, and the potential for exposure when products 
are used as intended (3). The safety assessments are regularly 
revised and updated to include new data, scientific advances, 
and risk assessment tools. All of RIFM’s safety assessments 
and research findings are reviewed and approved by the Expert 
Panel for Fragrance Safety, which is an independent Expert 
Panel made up of academic experts that have no ties to the fra-
grance industry (www.fragrancesafetypanel.org). Approved 
safety assessments and research then undergoes peer-reviewed 
publication and is made freely available to the public on the 
Fragrance Material Safety Resource Center (www.fragrance-
materialsafetyresource.elsevier.com). Ingredient assessments 
undertaken by RIFM are translated into Standards by the fra-
grance industry trade association, The International Fragrance 
Association (IFRA). This is a transparent and shared effort 
across the industry as demonstrated in Figure 16.1. IFRA 
develops standards based on the recommendations of maxi-
mum acceptable concentrations (MACs) by the Expert Panel 
for Fragrance Safety following their approval of RIFM safety 
assessments. The IFRA Standards are key to the self-regula-
tory approach of the fragrance industry. They are published on 
the IFRA website (https://ifrafragrance.org/safe-use/library) 
and are shared with their membership. The IFRA standards 
are obligatory for all IFRA members – who produce around 
90% of the global volume of fragrance, and are recognized 
by many government authorities and trade bodies around the 
world (such as in the ingredient restrictions in the Annexes 
of the European Union Cosmetics Regulation (EC Regulation 
1223/2009), the ASEAN Cosmetic Regulation, the Brazilian 
Cosmetics Regulation, and the United Arab Emirates Scheme 
for Perfume Standards).
16.3.2.4  FEMA
FEMA is responsible for the safety of flavors and does not 
evaluate cosmetic product use. However, some flavors may 
also be used in various cosmetic products (e.g., lipsticks, 
toothpastes). The FEMA Expert Panel reviews published and 
unpublished data to assess the safety of candidate flavoring 
substances to determine if the ingredient has sufficient data 
to be granted “Generally Recognized as Safe” (GRAS) status. 
FEMA provides all GRAS determinations and scientific data 
to the FDA, and flavor ingredients that are given GRAS status 
by the FEMA Expert Panel are published in Food Technology. 
Detailed summaries are published in peer-reviewed jour-
nals and by the Joint FAO/WHO Expert Committee on Food 
Additives (JECFA).
16.4  Safety and Risk Assessment of Hazardous 
Substances in Cosmetic and PCPs
Most cosmetics and PCPs are applied to the skin, as a result, 
the most frequent effects observed are local effects including 
irritation, sensitization, and photo-mediated reactions. The 
major routes of systemic exposure to ingredients in PCPs can 
occur through skin penetration, ingestion through the oral 
cavity as well as via inhalation. Considering the potential for 
exposure to these products, there is a need for them to be fully 
evaluated for safety. This means that PCPs need to be safe 
under conditions of normal or foreseeable conditions of use 
and misuse. Also, considering these products can be used over 
a person’s lifetime, it is necessary to consider both acute and 
long-term toxicological endpoints when conducting a safety 
assessment.
16.4.1  Hazard Characterization
Chemical exposure may cause various effects, some are irre-
versible while others are adaptive and reversible. The hazard 
characterization of a chemical is based on its known intrin-
sic properties. To identify potential hazards, it is necessary to 
identify all types of adverse health effects that can result from

16.4.2  Dose-Response Assessment
As discussed in detail in Chapter 3, a dose-response is the rela-
tionship between the dose of an ingredient and a measured 
toxicological response. For endpoints and studies that have a 
threshold effect (e.g., sub-chronic, chronic, and reproductive/
developmental studies), a NOAEL can be determined. The 
NOAEL is then divided by the estimated consumer exposure 
to calculate an MOS considering the estimated consumer 
exposure. In most cases for cosmetic and PCP ingredients, an 
MOS greater than 100 is considered to be a sufficient margin 
providing negligible risk for the consumer. When a NOAEL 
cannot be determined, the lowest dose at which an effect is 
observed (lowest observable adverse effect level (LOAEL)) 
can be used, and an additional safety factor is applied in the 
MOS calculation.
The benchmark dose (BMD) can also be used as a point 
of departure (POD) as an alternative to the NOAEL. A BMD 
is a dose that causes a predetermined change in response, 
referred to as the benchmark response (BMR). The BMR is 
a change in the response rate of an adverse effect, typically 
this is a default of 5% (as specified by European Food Safety 
Authority) or 10% (as does the US EPA). Once the BMR is

16.4.3  Exposure Assessment
Exposure is an essential part of all safety assessment pro-
cesses, including cosmetics and PCPs. The whole concept of 
risk assessment is based on determining if the level of expo-
sure is significantly below the threshold for toxicity. This can 
be a complex process since product use habits can vary widely 
even with a single category of the product since different man-
ufacturers may have different product use instructions or some 
products may have dual uses (e.g., shampoo being used as a 
body wash). Exposure assessment is often a tiered assessment 
depending on need and includes understanding consumer uses 
and habits, identifying potential exposure routes and expo-
sure scenarios, and quantifying the duration and frequency of 
exposure. Exposure data can be deterministic (point estimates) 
or probabilistic (data distributions). Figure 16.2 demonstrates

16.4.3.2  Oral Exposure Model
Oral exposure is calculated in the same way as dermal expo-
sure in Equation 16.1. Since oral exposure to cosmetic and PCP 
ingredients is typically incidental because the products are not 
meant to be ingested, the retention factors for toothpaste and

16.4.3.3  Inhalation Exposure Model
Since consumers can be exposed to cosmetics via inhalation 
from non-volatile substances (e.g., powders) as well as vola-
tiles, exposure estimates of the respiratory tract need to be 
considered. Exposure to volatiles can be estimated based on 
the concentration in the air using models such as ConsExpo 
or the RIFM 2 Box Model Indoor Air Dispersion Model (31). 
For aerosolized non-volatile particles and droplets, deposi-
tion in the respiratory tract can be estimated with models like 
the Multiple-Path Particle Dosimetry model (32). In addition 
to room volume, spray (or application) duration, and ventila-
tion rate, a key parameter in estimating inhalation exposure 
is understanding if a droplet or particle is respirable (<10 µm) 
if the formulation is not respirable no exposure assessment is 
needed.
16.4.3.4  Comparison of Deterministic and 
Probabilistic Approaches
16.4.3.4.1  Deterministic Approach
The deterministic approach generates a point estimate of expo-
sure from simple models using readily available use and habits 
data (21,33–37). This approach assumes that consumers use 
all products in 1 day and that an ingredient is present in each 
product at a maximum use concentration. It also assumes that 
exposure is additive across products and typically considers 
high percentile values of product use (such as 90th or 95th) to 
drive conservative exposure estimates. Such estimates, how-
ever, do not take into account that it is highly unlikely that all 
products used by a consumer on any given day will all contain 
a particular cosmetic ingredient at significant concentrations, 
let alone at the maximum current use levels in every product.
A more accurate estimate of aggregate exposure can be 
obtained by incorporating information on non-use and co-use. 
This was demonstrated in the study by Cowan-Ellsberry and 
Robison (38) which showed exposure to parabens in cosmet-
ics could be lowered by 51%–92% over standard methods by 
incorporating non-use and co-use statistics into the calcula-
tion. The results of this study were supported by limited bio-
monitoring data demonstrating that total systemic exposure 
from all sources and routes of exposure is considerably lower 
than previous deterministic estimates (39).
16.4.3.4.2  Probabilistic Approach
The probabilistic approach is a more complicated modeling 
approach that uses distributions of data and gives a distribution 
of possible exposure estimates, demonstrating the variability

16.4.4  Risk Characterization
Conducting a risk assessment for a cosmetic or PCP ingredient 
is an iterative process that integrates hazard data and estimates 
of consumer exposure. The various frameworks described 
above share many commonalities, including the endpoints 
evaluated and the acceptable data used to make conclusions. 
The RIFM framework (3) is important because fragrance 
ingredients are used in many cosmetics and PCPs across the 
globe and can be used to illustrate a general approach for 
assessing cosmetic ingredients. Figure 16.3 shows the stepwise 
approach of the RIFM framework which is applied for each 
endpoint evaluated. The initial step involves identifying avail-
able data for the ingredient and assessing the quality or robust-
ness of the data (e.g., Klimisch scoring). If there is sufficient 
data available for each endpoint, then a risk assessment can 
be conducted. If not, the next step involves identifying suit-
able analogs based on structure, physical-chemical properties, 
and metabolism. Clustering fragrance ingredients for safety 
assessment evaluation and identifying read-across analogs 
to address data gaps have been explained by Date et al. (47). 
When suitable analogs with sufficient available data are identi-
fied, then a risk assessment can be conducted.
The process moves to step three if no suitable analogs are 
identified. Step three involves the application of the Threshold 
of Toxicological Concern (TTC), a form of exposure-based 
data waiving that relies on setting safe use limits or thresholds 
of no safety concern based on data from groups of structurally

threshold can also be applied to evaluate potential skin sensi-
tizers, by applying the Dermal Sensitization Threshold (DST) 
which is a level below which there is no appreciable risk for 
dermal sensitization to be induced, and is based on protein 
reactivity. A DST of 900 µg/cm2 is used for non-reactive sub-
stances, as a worst-case estimate of the no expected sensitiza-
tion induction level (NESIL; (57,58)) while a lower DST of 64 
µg/cm2 is used reactive substances. Nishijo et al. calculated 
the DST for high-potency chemicals of 1.5 mg/cm2 (59). This 
value presents a useful default approach for unidentified sub-
stances in ingredients considering, as a worst-case scenario, 
that the unidentified compound may be a potent skin sensi-
tizer. The generated NESIL is then used in a dermal sensitiza-
tion quantitative risk assessment (QRA) to set maximum limits 
in products, as described below. If at this stage, the ingredient 
cannot be adequately assessed, in vitro and/or in vivo testing 
may be undertaken as the final step.
16.4.4.1  QRA
QRA is an exposure-based, risk assessment approach that has 
been approved by the Expert Panel for Fragrance Safety and 
has been adopted by RIFM for inclusion in fragrance ingredi-
ent safety assessments for dermal effects, which are the basis 
for the IFRA Standards. QRA is the key strategy for prevent-
ing dermal sensitization by fragrance materials that are identi-
fied as contact allergens. The original method was described 
by Api et al. (60) and then revised as QRA2 (61) to update the 
method to include aggregate exposure and to refine the uncer-
tainty factors. The first step in the QRA is to determine the 
NESIL which is a benchmark that is derived with WoE from 
human, animal, and in vitro data, and is expressed as the dose 
per unit area (µg/cm2). Next, uncertainty factors or sensitiza-
tion assessment factors (SAFs) are applied to extrapolate from 
experimental conditions to actual consumer exposure. A SAF 
is determined by multiplying four factors: inter-individual 
variability, product composition (role of product constituents), 
frequency/duration of product use, and skin site considerations 
(e.g., inflammation). Inter-individual variability is considered 
similarly to general toxicology, and product constituent effects 
consider different product forms with various formulations/
matrices that can affect physical-chemical parameters and 
bioavailability.
The frequency or duration of use considers the use of the 
products over extended periods of time which may result in 
accumulation or a reservoir effect in the skin. The skin site 
considerations include the presence of compromised or 
inflamed skin on a body site, which can increase the amount 
and speed at which an allergen crosses the epidermis. The final 
step of the QRA is to compare the acceptable exposure level 
(AEL) to the CEL.
QRA2 incorporates aggregate exposure from multiple prod-
uct uses as determined for fragrances from the Creme RIFM 
Aggregate Exposure Model. The AEL is determined by divid-
ing the NESIL by the product type SAF and is expressed as 
dose/unit area/day. The Aggregate Consumer Exposure Level 
(CELagg) calculation considers the aggregated consumer daily 
exposure by skin site. The ratio of AEL/CELagg is calculated 
for the fragrance ingredient in each product category, and a 
favorable ratio, meaning the AEL is greater than the CELagg, 
supports the safe use of the potential skin sensitizer.
When considering the MAC for a fragrance ingredient in 
a finished product, other endpoints must also be considered 
(e.g., photoirritation and systemic toxicity). The MACs are 
all reviewed based on aggregate exposure using the Creme 
RIFM Aggregate Exposure Model. The MACs reported in the 
RIFM safety assessments and therefore in the IFRA Standards 
are the lower values derived from the comparison of all the 
endpoints. Usually, this is a comparison of the dermal sensi-
tization and the systemic toxicity endpoints. The MACs for 
a fragrance ingredient, for practical reasons, are based on 
product categories which are the basis of the IFRA Standards. 
Product categorization was introduced in QRA1 (60), and this 
approach is also applied with QRA2. Each category covers a 
range of product types which can be grouped together based 
on risk assessment considerations. The categories themselves 
bring conservatisms to the risk assessment process because the 
product which derives the lowest MAC is the one that drives 
the category. Numerous consumer products are grouped into 
12 general categories based on similar use patterns, critical 
skin sites affected, similar SAFs, and levels of exposure.
Most products are applied to more than one site. As such, 
QRA2 introduces an adjustment factor for aggregate expo-
sure. Thus, a series of iterations starting with the lowest AEL/
CELagg, and adjusting upper concentration levels, provides 
QRA2 aggregate adjustment factors (61). QRA2 aggregate 
adjustment factors are considered by category because the rel-
ative contribution of exposure from each product to the dermal 
aggregate exposure to each application site for all fragrance 
ingredients is the same. Table 16.7 provides all 12 categories

16.5  Next-Generation Risk Assessment (NGRA)
Following public and regulatory pressure, new assessment 
approaches for cosmetic safety and risk are being developed, 
as well as creation of an industry-NGO collaboration to cre-
ate a training program for the implementation of non-animal 
safety assessment for cosmetics (https://www.afsacollabora-
tion.org/cosmetics/). A NGRA is a tiered iterative approach 
which involves forming a hypothesis based on existing data, 
and utilizes in silico, in chemico, and in vitro tools, as well as 
TTC, toxicokinetics, and targeted non-animal testing to dem-
onstrate ingredient safety without the use of animal testing. 
All NGRA assessments need to be clearly documented since 
there is limited regulatory guidance for decision-making with 
many NAMs. The International Cooperation on Cosmetics 
Regulation has defined the basic principles for using NGRA in 
safety evaluations of cosmetics (63).
NGRA is similar to traditional safety assessments in which 
concentrations that do not elicit an adverse biological response 
are compared to the estimated consumer exposure to calculate 
a margin of exposure that protects the consumer from adverse 
effects. However, in a NGRA, bioactivity in non-animal sys-
tems is compared with expected levels of systemic exposure 
through PBPK modeling and not the externally applied dose 
(64). In NGRA, metabolism of the test item is carefully evalu-
ated through PBPK modeling since the in vitro test systems 
generally cannot take into account absorption, distribution, 
metabolism, and excretion (ADME) as a whole organism can. 
To improve predictability, testing of metabolites and predic-
tion of their ADME behavior can be considered, although this 
is considered a limitation of the NGRA approach, due to its 
complexity.
A stepwise workflow for NGRA developed by a joint 
research initiative between the European Commission and 
Cosmetics Europe has been previously described (65) and is 
shown in Figure 16.4. The initial steps involved in a NGRA are 
similar to the RIFM safety assessment framework discussed 
above, with the exception that exposure and read-across steps 
are reversed. The initial steps include understanding the 
exposure scenario and identifying the molecular structure of 
the ingredient, then exiting the workflow by completing the 
risk assessment by applying TTC or read-across, if possible. 
When an assessment cannot be completed, further refinement

16.6  Safety Assessment of Natural 
Complex Substances
While many consumers assume that “natural” ingredients are 
inherently safe, this is not necessarily the case. In fact, various 
reports demonstrate adverse effects from exposure to natural 
ingredients. These effects include irritation, dermal sensitiza-
tion, photosensitization, and allergic reactions (67–69), many 
of these effects can be attributed to a lack of proper risk assess-
ment of the ingredients.
Natural ingredients are typically complex mixtures that 
vary in composition due to natural variation from differing 
growing conditions and geographical locations, different pro-
cessing methods, and extractions from different parts of the 
plant (e.g., leaves, roots, flowers, fruits, and seeds) all have an 
affect the composition of the final product. The safe use of 
natural ingredients is dependent on characterizing the com-
position of its constituents. Various analytical methods are 
available to identify constituents of complex mixtures includ-

be applied to concentrated extracts such as essential oils (72).

TTC, then the safety of the individual constituents is evaluated 
using available data or the TTC. Similarly, as described above, 
non-animal testing approaches may be conducted to fill a data 
gap. This testing can be done on the entire extract or on the 
constituents in order to complete the assessment.
16.7  Consumer Risk Perception
More than ever, consumers are looking for assurance on the 
safety of chemicals in the products they use in their daily lives 
and are seeking information on the potential health impacts 
such products may have. While there is much access to infor-
mation, there is a general confusion or misunderstanding 
around ingredient safety often stemming from beliefs that 
regulations are not sufficient, or that ingredient transparency 
is lacking. This has led to distrust in industry and a higher 
perception of risk compared to how much harm an ingredi-
ent can actually cause. A great deal of misperception around 
the safety of cosmetic ingredients arises from the fact that 
the basic principles of toxicology and risk assessment are not 
understood nor applied. Most lay consumers and NGOs apply 
heuristic approaches when assessing risk and do not consider 
the probability of likelihood nor actual levels of exposure and 
dose. In fact, for the average consumer, risk equates to “some-
thing bad will happen to me”. Thus, people who are fearful 
of cosmetics and chemicals in general, only consider hazard. 
They assume there is similarity between responses observed 
in high-dose animal studies and low-dose product exposure, 
without considering the differences in route, duration, and 
amount of exposure (73). Adding to consumer concerns and 
the misperception of risk is also the way various chemical haz-
ards are discussed. Statements like “endocrine disruptor” or 
“hormone-like substances” are polarizing terms; they sound 
hazardous and can raise concerns and fears in consumers. Yet, 
in the absence of relevant human exposure and potency data, 
these terms are meaningless related to human health risks (74).
Building off public concern, some advocacy groups have 
developed their own safety assessment systems. Unfortunately, 
most of these systems only account for hazards associated with

the same, some possess undesirable properties (e.g., di(2-eth-

others do not (e.g., dimethyl phthalate and dibutyl phthalate

Adding to consumer concerns around cosmetic ingredients is 
also the lack of perceived transparency. This is particularly seen 
with fragrances, whose formulae are considered “trade secrets” 
and are typically not disclosed, only being generally labeled as 
“fragrance” or “parfum”. This lack of disclosure of the details of 
“fragrance” on product labeling, coupled with incomplete pub-
lic understanding of the approach undertaken to assess cosmetic 
and PCP safety assessment, has led to a distrust of both the use 
of fragrances as well as the companies that manufacture them. 
As such, both IFRA and RIFM have put considerable effort 
into risk communication platforms to explain the process the 
fragrance industry uses to assess and regulate fragrance ingre-
dients. This includes regular presentations and discussion fora 
with consumers, regulators, and academic researchers in the 
field, scientific publications, and dedicated dissemination por-
tal for fragrance safety science (Food and Chemical Toxicology 
Fragrance Material Safety Assessment Center: http://fragrance-
materialsafetyresource.elsevier.com).
In order to ensure safe cosmetics and PCPs, it is key that 
consumers seek out factual information on the ingredients 
used and that industry openly support access to data on the 
chemicals they use. Misleading, or “phantom risk”, statements 
are problematic because they can distract attention from actual 
risks (81) and may lead manufacturers to substitute alternatives 
that have not been as well characterized and/or have potential 
data gaps. This is particularly pertinent in light of the move 
toward non-animal testing since not all endpoints are able to 
be adequately covered at this time.
16.8  Summary
The aim of this chapter was not to present a synopsis on the 
safety of cosmetic and PCP products nor discuss the toxic-
ity of the broad classes of ingredients used in these products. 
Much of the data on chemical toxicities can be sought in other 
chapters of this book. Cosmetic ingredients are not unique, 
and the basic tenets of chemical risk assessment are the same. 
However, the fact that cosmetic and PCPs are applied directly 
to the body on a regular basis and may be used over a long 
period of time has resulted in considerable attention and care 
by the industry to appropriately assess, and communicate on, 
their safety. A number of leading efforts have been undertaken 
to ensure that the risk assessment frameworks applied by 
industry and regulators reflect the everyday use and ubiqui-
tous exposure of such products. Here we present an overview 
of the general principles of risk assessment with an emphasis 
on cosmetic and PCP ingredients. Primary reference is given 
to examples from the fragrance industry; however, the overall 
approaches are applicable to all cosmetic and PCP products 
and their component ingredients.
