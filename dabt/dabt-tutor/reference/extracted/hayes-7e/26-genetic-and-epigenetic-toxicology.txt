26.1  Introduction
Genetic and epigenetic toxicology is focused on examining the 
impact of endogenous processes and xenobiotic exposures on 
the genome and epigenome of living organisms. This science 
uses computational predictions, biological assays, state-of-the-
art “-omics” technologies, and quantitative risk assessment 
tools to identify, analyze, and manage exposures to xenobiot-
ics that have the potential to adversely affect the genome and 
epigenome. The genome maintains the genetic code of living 
organisms and the epigenome controls when, where, and how 
genes are expressed. The genome and epigenome are critical 
targets of significant relevance to toxicology. Several human 
diseases are directly or indirectly associated with alteration 
of the genome or compromised regulation of gene expression. 
The primary objective of genetic and epigenetic toxicology is 
to determine and predict potential hazards from xenobiotic 
exposures that directly interact with the cellular genome or 
can affect gene expression by controlling the epigenome.
The term genotoxic describes the property of chemical 
agents to damage the genetic information within a cell, while 
epigenetic toxicity is applied to chemicals that alter epigen-
etic mechanisms that regulate gene expression. However, 
classifying a compound as genotoxic or as one that produces 
epigenetic toxicity is not sufficient to link these events to 
adverse health outcomes [1]. Importantly, genotoxicity is 
not equivalent to mutagenicity; genotoxicity ≠ mutagenicity. 
Mutagenicity results from a genotoxic event and is a cellular 
process that occurs after the initial interaction with the genome 
(genotoxicity), requires viable cell division, and transmission 
of an altered genome to daughter cells of somatic or germ 
cells. Somatic cell mutations are associated with human dis-
eases such as cancers, while germ cell mutations account for a 
significant fraction of human diseases that can be transmitted 
to future generations. The wide-ranging term “genotoxicity” 
includes mutagenicity but also includes DNA damage that can 
be reversed by DNA repair or can result in cell death with-
out transmitting the aberrant structure or information content 
present in the genome. The term genotoxic is therefore used to 
describe the potential of a test compound to induce mutations. 
The term “mutagens” or “mutagenic” is specific to chemicals 
that induce heritable genetic changes, i.e., mutation, most com-
monly through an initial interaction with DNA (genotoxicity). 
The induction of mutation by xenobiotics is emerging as a rel-
evant toxicological endpoint, with the development and analy-
sis of quantitative dose–response mutagenicity data becoming 
integrated with other relevant toxicological and exposure data 
for use in regulatory decision-making [2].
Genotoxicants are characterized by several properties that 
include: (1) interaction with DNA to cause DNA damage 
or covalent binding to DNA resulting in formation of DNA 
adducts, (2) alteration of chromosome structure, (3) chromo-

Genetic toxicology has its origins in early studies con-
ducted in the mid-1900s demonstrating that X-rays, nitrogen 
mustards, and other known toxic exposures in test organ-
isms, such as Drosophila and microbes, caused transmissible 
genetic alterations. Due to concerns regarding the potential of 
radiation from nuclear fallout to affect the human genome, the 
Biology Division at Oak Ridge National Laboratory (ORNL) 
was formed under the direction of William Hollaender, the 
founder of the Environmental Mutagen Society [4]. Early 
objectives of genetic toxicology were focused on the integrity 
of the human reproductive process and the induction of new 
transmissible DNA alterations to offspring. The first mammal-
based mutation assay, referred to as the specific locus test in 
mice, was developed at ORNL. These pioneering studies dem-
onstrated the potential of radiation-induced DNA damage to 
cause heritable DNA alterations and by comparing the effects 
of acute to fractionated radiation demonstrated the critical role 
of DNA repair. Test methods employed initially were largely 
in vivo and oriented toward the detection of alterations to germ 
cells and transmission to offspring. At the end of the 1960s, 
reports from Bruce Ames and other investigators were show-
ing a strong relationship between simple tests for mutagenicity 
in microorganisms and bioassays for rodent carcinogenicity 
[5–7]. More recently, attention has been drawn to a class of 
agents that do not change the primary structure of DNA but 
alter phenotypes through environmentally induced alterations 
in gene expression patterns [8].
Genetic toxicology has a key role in safety evaluation 
used for the identification and risk assessment of genotoxic 
agents capable of altering the integrity of the human genome. 
Assessment of genotoxicity, epigenetic toxicity, and mutagen-
icity are significant components of weight of evidence (WoE) 
to assess genotoxic hazards and potential risk to the human 
genome [9, 10]. A second fundamental role of genetic toxi-
cology sciences is to achieve a better understanding of how 
endogenous DNA damage occurs, how DNA repair systems 
act as a biological defense mechanism against the accumula-
tion of mutations from endogenous and exogenous sources, 
and developing mode-of-action (MoA) data on how xeno-
biotics induce DNA damage, a key event that contributes to 
somatic cell diseases such as cancer.

26.2.1  Gene Structure
DNA is responsible for the hereditary characteristics of all 
living systems, except for some viruses that use RNA. The 
genome is all the genetic material of a cell that is made up of 
DNA. Even those organisms that store their hereditary infor-
mation in RNA go through a DNA intermediary during rep-
lication. The Human Genome Project, which completed the 
DNA sequencing of the first human genome in 2003, spawned 
the development of technologies, computational tools, and 
insights into genome function that has revolutionized the mod-
ern practice of biology and in turn toxicology. The overview 
below is not sufficient to cover 21st century genomics and epi-
genetics; therefore, for details, the reader is referred to a recent 
textbook Lewin’s GENES XI [11] that discusses in detail 
DNA biochemistry, genome sciences, epigenetics, and regula-
tion of gene expression. In addition, the National Center for 
Biotechnology Information webpage (https://www.ncbi.nlm.
nih.gov/) is a rich resource of a basic introduction to genom-
ics, open-source tools for analyzing genomics data, and public 
database repositories of detailed data that provide access to 
biomedical and genomic information.
The basic functional unit in a DNA molecule is termed a 
gene. Most of the early knowledge concerning structure and 
operation of genes was acquired from studies with bacteria 
or bacteriophages. Advances in understanding the molecular 
biology of mammalian cells have resulted in equivalent infor-
mation in eukaryotic cells. The nucleotide composition and 
the mechanisms by which information encoded in a gene is

26.2.2  The Transcriptome: Protein 
Coding and the Epigenome
Although previous studies of gene expression have focused 
primarily on discrete individual genes, it is apparent that the 
genome is pervasively transcribed into the cellular transcrip-
tome. Greater than 75% of the genome is transcribed in one 
cell type or another, and across individual tissues in humans 
greater than 90% of the genome is ultimately transcribed 
[12,  13]. Less than 5% of the transcribed RNAs from the 
genome produce mRNA that code for proteins (Figure 26.2). 
The large fraction of the transcriptome with no protein-coding 
function comprises a broad spectrum of RNA molecules that 
are involved in protein biosynthesis (ribosomal RNA, transfer 
RNA), and ncRNAs that are classified based on their length: 
long non-coding RNAs (lncRNAs) (>200 nucleotides (nt)) and 
short non-coding RNAs (<200 nt), which include microRNAs 
(miRNAs, single-stranded, 19–25 nt), piwi-interacting RNAs 
(piRNAs, single-stranded, 25–30 nt), and endogenous short 
interfering RNAs (esiRNAs, double-stranded, 21–22 nt) [14, 
15]. The RNA transcription process is regulated, in part, by 
epigenetic mechanisms.
In the year 1942, before genetic inheritance through DNA 
was established, Conrad Waddington coined the term epi-
genetics derived from the Greek word “epigenesis”. He 
defined epigenetics as the “casual interactions between genes 
and their products” to explain aspects of development, as the 
determinants of phenotype [16]. The science of epigenetics has 
substantially developed over the years and now is applied to a 
variety of biological processes. Currently, the term epigenetics 
can be defined by the inheritance of gene expression patterns 
without altering the underlying DNA sequence but by remod-
eling chromatin [17].
The epigenome, which is the description of all epigenetic 
modifications that occur across the entire genome, allows 
genetically identical cells to achieve diverse and relatively 
stable phenotypes. Different from the genome, which is vir-
tually identical in all diploid cells from the same individual, 
each tissue and potentially each individual cell or cell type 
may exhibit a unique epigenomic proμle. In addition, the 
epigenomic profile can be inherited by daughter cells during 
cell division, thus enabling the maintenance of tissue identity. 
Thus, although relatively stable, epigenetic alterations occur as 
a natural process essential to development and differentiation 
[18]. Several cellular functions, such as X-chromosome inac-
tivation, tissue-specific gene expression, genomic imprinting, 
and aging involve epigenetic regulation.
The two important features of the normal epigenome are:
	
1.	There are multiple individual epigenomes within an 
organism that are present in the various cell types 
and tissues.
	
2.	The epigenome can be stably maintained, yet it 
changes during an organism’s lifetime in response to 
endogenous and environmental cues.
The detailed characterization of the normal epigenome is far 
from complete, but it is necessary to completely understand if 
epigenetic modifications caused by a toxicant are adaptive or 
adverse effects.

organism. The adult human body is estimated to be made up 
of 1014 somatic cells. The number of germ cells (ova) is fixed 
by sexual maturity in females, while males continuously pro-
duce male germ cells (sperm) from spermatogonial stem cell 
populations. The genome of most somatic cells is found in the

26.2.4  Mutation and Human Disease
The biological consequences of DNA damage are dependent on 
which type of cell (somatic or germ) carries the alteration. The 
mutational basis for many human disorders and anomalies is well 
documented [3]. More recently, de novo mutations in offspring 
early in development have been associated with an increase in 
neurological disorders including autism [19]. The human genome 
contains preexisting mutations referred to as either spontaneous 
or background mutations forming what is called the genetic 
disease burden. Within the human population, the extent of the 
preexisting genetic disease burden is estimated to be as high as 
5% (Table 26.2). Diseases associated with this genetic burden 
contribute significantly to the healthcare costs of most developed 
countries and the high mortality rates in less well-developed 
regions. The precise origin of most preexisting DNA alterations 
found in the human gene pool is uncertain, but Table 26.3 pro-
vides some of the accepted sources for the genetic disease bur-
den. Although human mutagenicity in germ cells has not been 
demonstrated in epidemiology investigations, experimentation in 
animal models amenable to the study of transmissible mutations 
provides convincing evidence that environmental genotoxicants 
would be capable of inducing transmissible mutations in human 
germ cells [20–22].
It is estimated that the human genome consists of approximately 
20,000–25,000 genes controlling all aspects of an organism’s 
biology and behavior. Although knowledge of many gene func-
tions is uncertain, research continues to identify gene functional-
ity at an increasing pace. The Online Mendelian Inheritance in 
Man (OMIM) is a comprehensive compendium of human genes 
and genetic phenotypes that is freely available and updated daily 
(https://omim.org/). At present, the total number of genes with 
phenotype-causing mutations is 4,369. OMIM is authored and 
edited at the McKusick-Nathans Institute of Genetic Medicine, 
Johns Hopkins University School of Medicine, under the direc-
tion of Dr. Ada Hamosh.

Consequently, most genetic damage produced in human germ

impact of dominant mutations on the gene pool (all genes 
contained in each population) is generally limited because 
the affected individuals are aware that they are carrying the 
mutant form of the gene and are aware that they can trans-

severity of the disease or effect, parents can decide, prior to 
reproducing, if the health risk associated with possible trans-
mission to a child is acceptable.
Unlike dominant mutations, recessive disease mutations will 
not be expressed unless both alleles of the gene pair are mutant. 
Two phenotypically normal heterozygous carriers for a reces-
sive mutant allele (on autosomal chromosomes) will theoreti-
cally produce offspring that have a 25% chance of exhibiting a 
recessive disease. Additionally, 50% of their offspring will be 
the same phenotypically normal heterozygous carriers as the 
parents. The frequencies of recessive mutations in the gene pool 
are maintained by the phenotypically normal heterozygous car-
riers. An increase in the incidence of new recessive mutations 
poses the most serious threat to human populations as these 
mutations are usually not expressed immediately after induction 
and can remain silent and passed on to the next generations as 
a “founder effect”. New recessive mutations tend to accumulate 
in the gene pool in the heterozygous configuration. Due to the 
resultant generational latent period, the ultimate expression of 
a new recessive mutation in the population may have no appar-
ent association with the environmental exposure that induced 
it until several generations later. This situation severely limits 
the opportunity to use human epidemiological studies to detect 
human genetic risk [23, 24]. Finally, sex-linked recessive muta-
tions are alterations on the X chromosome which appear as 
dominant mutations when transmitted to male offspring as the 
Y chromosome has no corresponding alleles to offset the effects 
of the X-linked mutation.
Mutations found in somatic cells occur by endogenous 
mechanisms, including exposure to genotoxic agents from 
several sources (Table 26.3). Exposures of human DNA dam-
age to somatic cells are associated with a range of health 
anomalies including inherited and induced cancers [25], ter-
atogenesis [26], reproductive failure [27], and appear to be 
involved in aging [28, 29]. Certain de novo mutations asso-
ciated with neurological disorders are hypothesized to occur 
early in development of the brain by mutations or epigenetic 
alterations during differentiation of neural progenitor cells or 
later stages as the brain continues to develop until maturity 
[19]. Although it is acknowledged that mutations can lead to 
a number of diseases, the primary focus of genetic toxicol-
ogy over the last two decades has been on potential cancer 
risk by conducting initial steps of risk assessment, hazard 
identification, and more recently, quantitative assessment of 
genetic toxicology dose–response data. Traditional toxicology 
risk assessment steps are being adopted in genetic toxicology, 
incorporating dose–response relationships for genotoxic end-
point metrics to determine Benchmark Dose (BMD) and Point 
of Departure (PoD) estimates for germ cell and somatic cell 
risk assessments.
The discipline of genetic toxicology initially relied upon a set 
of in vivo tests focused on the detection of DNA damage to germ 
and somatic cells of rodent species with an intent to extrapo-
late the results to human hazard and risk assessments [1, 4, 30]. 
Early testing approaches employed large rodent assays, to detect 
dominant lethality (implantation failure or early embryonic 
lethality), heritable translocation induction (semisterility), and 
specific locus mutation induction in somatic and germ cells [1]. 
These germ cell mutagenicity tests employed tens of thousands 
of rodents and thus were not feasible to conduct on a routine 
basis. The current regulatory genetic toxicology test battery is 
considered to be able to identify genotoxic and mutagenic com-
pounds irrespective of germ or somatic cell; however, it is clear 
that rapid assessment of germ cell-specific mutagens is a “data 
gap” in genetic toxicology testing(27). The need to expose test 
animals throughout the entire spermatogenesis cycle to properly 
assess germ cell mutagens requires long-term animal studies, 
with a minimum of 6–8 weeks of continuous exposure in mice 
and rats, respectively. Absence of in vitro alternatives that can 
mimic the process of either spermatogenesis or oogenesis lim-
its the assessment of germ cell-specific genotoxic or mutagens 
agents and the reliance on the genetic toxicology test battery 
to predict the genotoxic potential of test articles irrespective of 
germ or somatic cells.
In summary, mutated genes (diseases) are present in the 
human gene pool and occur at predictable rates. The origin of 
this genetic disease burden in humans is not completely cer-
tain, but it is imperative that we, as current caretakers of the 
human gene pool, use all precautions to transmit the gene pool 
to the next generation in no worse shape than it was received.
26.2.5  Mutagen to Carcinogen Relationships
During the late 1960s, a substantial amount of scientific evi-
dence demonstrated that most chemical carcinogens, or their 
metabolites, had electrophilic properties and would directly 
bind to DNA to form DNA adducts [31, 32]. Early studies 
of carcinogen binding used purified DNA extracted from 
exposed cells and did not directly demonstrate a relation-
ship between the binding, mutation formation, and cancer 
initiation. The introduction of an assay to detect mutation 
in a bacterial cell using Salmonella developed by Bruce 
Ames, combined with an in vitro metabolic activation sys-
tem providing cytochrome P450 (CYP450) enzyme metabo-
lism (Ames test), remains a rapid, inexpensive method to 
identify DNA-reactive/DNA-binding chemicals capable of 
inducing mutations and potentially cancer [1, 10]. The Ames 
test was the forerunner of a broad array of in vitro bacterial 
and mammalian cell assays that were later proposed as rapid 
screens for carcinogens and a set of biological assays as the 
first in vitro alternatives to the use of animals to identify 
potential carcinogens [1, 10].
The relevance of genotoxicity assays as predictors of carci-
nogenicity was later supported by studies demonstrating that 
normal, nonmalignant mammalian cells could be transformed

malignant cells [33]. Ultimately, specific genes were isolated 
and identified in the transfected DNA that, when mutated, 
drove cell transformation to a tumor phenotype [34]. These 
cancer genes (oncogenes) are highly conserved genes in most 
eukaryotic organisms. Oncogenes such as ras are activated 
by nucleotide substitution mutations in specific exons [10, 
35, 36]. Mutations in ras produce a permanently activated 
signal-transducing ras oncogene protein, generating a per-
sistent cellular proliferative signal driving cell growth and 
division that can ultimately cause cancer. A genetically mod-
ified (GM) mouse harboring a mutated form of the human ras 
oncogene (rasH2 mouse) results in increased tumor develop-
ment. The rasH2 mouse is an alternative short-term cancer 
bioassay to the 2-year cancer bioassay in mice [37]. Other 
cancer genes called tumor suppressor genes in their wild-
type form regulate cell division, proliferation, and apoptosis. 
For example, the tumor suppressor gene TP53 is a transcrip-
tional activator that responds to diverse cellular stresses to 
regulate expression of response target genes [38]. The TP53 
gene has a key role in the response to DNA damage known as 
the TP53 DNA Damage Response (P53 DDR) and is regarded 
as the “guardian of the genome”. As the guardian of the 
genome in response to DNA damage, the P53 DDR induces 
transcription of a pleiotropic set of genes triggering cycle 
arrest, DNA repair, or if DNA damage is severe, cell death 
via apoptosis. TP53 prevents the accumulation of mutations 
in the genome through its central biological role. Mutation 
of this gene inactivates the P53 DDR, disrupts cell division 
controls, allows a cell to escape apoptosis, and consequently 
is the most common genome change present in a wide range 
of human cancers. Inactivation of the p53 gene in GM mice 
results in increased tumor development and has been used 
as the basis of a short-term model of tumorigenesis that is 
heterozygous (+/′ ) for the p53 gene [37].
Although mutational changes in specific oncogenes and 
tumor suppressor genes have been recognized as key drivers 
that can lead to tumor development, it is now apparent that 
carcinogens can target genes or genomic pathways that are 
biologically aberrant in tumors and are referred to as cancer 
hallmarks. These cancer hallmarks put forth by Hanahan 
and Weinberg are properties of cancers that include genomic 
instability and inflammation, sustained proliferative signaling, 
evading growth suppressors, resisting cell death, enabling rep-
licative immortality, inducing angiogenesis, activating inva-
sion and metastasis, deregulated energy metabolism, evasion 
of destruction by the immune system [38, 39]. Underlying 
these aberrant changes impacting cancer hallmarks are gene 
mutations, genomic instability, and aberrant epigenetic sig-
naling, which drive cancer development. Mutations in genes 
that regulate cancer hallmark pathways are considered can-
cer driver mutations (CDM) [40]. A computational toxicology 
study using data obtained with high-throughput technology 
showed that chemicals that alter the targets or pathways among 
the hallmarks of cancer are likely to be carcinogenic [9].
In order to evaluate and organize evidence of the mecha-
nism of action for the risk assessment of potential genotoxic or 
epigenetic carcinogens, the International Agency for Research 
on Cancer (IARC) has delineated ten key characteristics 
commonly exhibited by human carcinogens: (1) acting as an 
electrophile either directly before or after bioactivation; (2) 
genotoxicity and mutation; (3) altering DNA repair or caus-
ing genomic instability; (4) inducing epigenetic alterations; (5) 
inducing oxidative stress; (6) inducing chronic inflammation; 
(7) being immunosuppressive; (8) modulating receptor-medi-
ated effects; (9) causing immortalization; and (10) altering cell 
proliferation, cell death, or nutrient supply [9, 41]. These char-
acteristics delineate mechanisms by which genotoxic and epi-
genetic carcinogens can produce tumors in rodents and cancer 
in humans.
A striking feature of mutations in DNA from mutagen 
exposure that was observed decades ago is their specificity. 
Mutagenesis induced by chemicals compared to the back-
ground spectrum of mutations is specific both in terms of 
the location of the nucleotide changes and types of base-pair 
changes induced. Furthermore, each mutagen (and “back-
ground” mutagenesis as well) produces a unique distribution 
or “spectrum” of mutations (mutational spectra) in a gene with 
a characteristic pattern of location and type [35, 42]. The spe-
cific spectra of nucleotide substitutions induced within onco-
genes or tumor suppressor genes can be characteristic of a 
particular carcinogen and is referred to as its “mutational sig-
nature”. In vivo experiments with mice documented that nucle-
otide changes in K-ras oncogenes and TP53 mutations isolated 
from tumors induced by some chemical carcinogens are agent-
specific and serve as signatures specific to that chemical [35]. 
Knowledge of the spectrum of mutations induced by chemi-
cal exposures can provide mechanistic insights to carcinogen 
MoA. Mutational spectra can provide the needed data to link 
an exposure to a mutational change in the genome by examin-
ing the mutational signatures induced in experimental systems 
by putative cancer-causing agents compared to that observed 
in tumors. It is apparent that mutational and epigenetic pro-
cesses from different etiologies are active during cancer devel-
opment in experimental animals and humans. The Catalogue 
Of Somatic Mutations In Cancer (COSMIC: https://cancer.
sanger.ac.uk/cosmic) is the world’s largest and most compre-
hensive resource for cataloging somatic mutations in several 
human cancer types. For certain tumor types, such as ciga-
rette smoke-related lung cancer, show mutational signatures 
characteristic of DNA-damage-induced (DDI) mis-replication 
caused by tobacco-related carcinogens, such as benzo(a)pyrene 
(BaP). Other known mutagenic exposures, such as ultraviolet 
(UV) light, aristolochic acid, and aflatoxin, produce character-
istic mutation signatures matching signatures found in human 
tumors related to these exposures. Mutational signatures in 
cancer hallmark pathway genes isolated from human cancers 
are then able to link the human cancer to prior exposures to the 
chemical in question.
An important factor affecting an appreciation for the 
complexity of DNA alterations and accurate carcinogen pre-
dictivity is the need to define epigenetic mechanisms and 
develop tests to detect agents that induce them. Several dis-
eases, including cancer, are associated with aberrant DNA 
methylation [10]. The inclusion of tests for epigenetic effects 
would increase the predictivity of tests assessing genotoxic-
ity and move short-term testing closer to the goal of carcino-
gen identification.

26.3  Mechanisms of Genotoxicity
26.3.1  Background and Spontaneous 
DNA Alterations
DNA synthesis and replication of the 6 × 109 base pairs that 
make up the human genome are not flawless processes, and in 
rare instances, genetic alterations occur spontaneously during 
regular cell division. The frequency of background mutations 
at a single locus in a typical cell culture test system are rare 
events and occur at a frequency of 1–5 × 10∞6. To put this in 
perspective, across the genome, humans differ from each other 
in DNA sequence at 1 in every 1,000 base pairs. Furthermore, 
the spectrum of background or spontaneous mutation with 
respect to site and type of base change is specific, typically 
enabling a clear distinction between background or spontane-
ous mutations and xenobiotic-induced mutations [35]. In addi-
tion, aerobic metabolism produces reactive by-products (e.g., 
hydroxyl radicals, formaldehyde) capable of damaging DNA. 
Peroxisomes compartmentalize oxidative metabolism, lead-
ing to reactive products that would otherwise be detrimental 
to the cell, although under certain conditions, these products 
may escape intracellular compartments and affect the nucleus. 
Most oxidized DNA base lesions are repaired by DNA repair 
systems, but in certain situations of excessive toxicity, unre-
paired endogenous DNA damage is a cause of background or 
spontaneous mutation. Background mutation can also arise 
from exposure to environmental agents such as radiation (e.g., 
solar radiation, X-rays, radon), chemical pollution (e.g., ciga-
rette smoke, fossil fuel combustion), and chemical carcinogens 
present in the human diet (e.g., acrylamide). Disease states such 
as chronic inflammatory diseases that lead to increased endog-
enous sources of DNA-damaging agents, from disease-related 
inflammation (e.g., chronic bowel inflammatory disease), can 
result in increased levels of mutation and chromosome damage.
Although DNA damage due to endogenous processes and 
other natural sources can be minimized, it cannot be elimi-
nated. A comprehensive assessment of DNA adducts across 
species from humans to cows includes a broad spectrum of 
lesions in the genome with apurinic/apyrimidinic sites, a loca-
tion in DNA that has neither a purine nor a pyrimidine base, 
which can occur spontaneously or due to DNA damage; they 
are the most common lesion in DNA, with 10,000 apurinic 
sites and 500 apyrimidinic sites generated in a cell daily [42]. 
Other DNA lesions are derived from endogenous metabolic 
processes producing, for example, acetaldehyde and ethylene 
oxide, with some endogenous DNA adducts identical to DNA 
adducts derived from exogenous exposures to known or sus-
pected human carcinogens.
26.3.2  Classification Scheme for Genotoxic Effects
DNA damage may be classified into several broad categories 
based on the nature (presumed mechanism) of the DNA effects 
(Table 26.4).
26.3.2.1  DNA Binding
DNA binding causes nucleotide damage affecting proper base-
pairing, generally producing point mutations through single 
base-pair substitution, insertion, or deletion. Point or gene 
mutations are generally induced by agents that specifically tar-
get nucleophilic sites on individual bases in nucleic acids. The 
normal DNA base-pairings are shown in Figure 26.3. Adenine 
and thymine form two hydrogen bonds, and guanine and cyto-
sine form three. Hydrogen (H) bonds are weak electrostatic 
forces involving oxygen and nitrogen atoms at specific sites 
on the purine and pyrimidine molecules. When electrophilic 
chemical species covalently bind (adduct) to portions of the 
DNA bases involved in the formation of hydrogen bonds, the 
resultant structures (Figure 26.4) can produce electron shifts 
from the H-bonding sites to areas within the molecules, giving 
rise to opportunities for short-lived mispaired bases (e.g., A:C 
or G:T). However, binding at other sites on DNA, for example, 
at the N7 position of guanine, not involved in base-pairing 
with cytosine, may have little or no genetic consequences.
Detection of DNA binding or even specific adducts is a geno-
toxic event and is not equivalent to the detection of mutation 
[1, 43]. When electrophilic chemicals react with DNA, they 
form both unstable and stable adducts. Unstable DNA adducts 
tend to be removed, forming abasic sites in the DNA that are 
generally repaired. Stable adducts can result in mispairing 
and mutation. The ratio of stable to unstable adducts differs 
by site on specific bases where adducts occur, even among 
closely related structures. Consequently, adduct levels, per 
se, are not an accurate indicator of mutagenicity. However, 
adduct detection in vivo is a sensitive method for demon-
strating exposure to electrophilic agents. Unfortunately, not 
enough is known about adduct processing and repair for an

26.3.2.2  DNA Breakage, Chromosome Aberrations, 
and Abnormal Chromosome Segregation
These are large-scale genetic events affecting millions of 
base pairs of DNA, impacting the structural integrity of 
chromosomes as well as chromosome number. Clastogenic 
effects are visible through cytologic analysis by micro-
scope of metaphase-condensed chromosomes. During 
most of a cell’s existence, DNA is packaged in the nucleus 
of somatic cells as uncondensed chromatin and is not vis-
ible except during mitosis when chromatin is condensed. 
Figure 26.5 illustrates the generalized anatomy of a chro-
mosome during metaphase. The condensed chromosome 
shows distinctive banding when stained using Giemsa. It 
contains a constricted region known as the centromere. 
To either side of the centromere are the chromosome 
arms that terminate in unique sequences called telomeres. 
The darker bands (G+ bands) in the stained chromosome 
represent condensed DNA (heterochromatic) previously 
believed to represent areas of little or no gene expression 
(transcription). The lighter bands (G′  bands) represent 
relaxed DNA sequences previously believed to be active 
genomic areas where gene expression (transcription) is 
occurring. The telomeric regions at the ends of the chro-
mosome structures are important for chromosome stabil-
ity and cell longevity [45]. Telomeres consist of repetitive 
sequences of a small number of nucleotides (TTAGGG) 
that protect the ends of chromosomes from fusing. During 
somatic cell replication, telomeres are not completely rep-
licated. They are gradually shortened, and when the telo-
meres reach a critical threshold, cell division stops in what 
is called replicative senescence [46]. The enzyme telom-
erase (a reverse transcriptase) can replace lost telomeric 
DNA by adding guanine-cytosine base pair–rich regions 
to restore telomere length. Telomerase is almost nonexis-
tent in somatic cells but is found in embryonic stem cells 
and tumor cells.
Chromosomal effects scored microscopically are large-
scale events affecting millions of base pairs and are the 
result of damage to the integrity of the DNA molecule pro-
ducing strand breaks, either single-strand breaks (SSBs) or 
double-strand breaks (DSBs). DNA strand breakage can 
be the result of exposure to reactive oxygen species or from 
cross-linking DNA adducts. Chemicals that interfere with 
DNA synthesis and DNA repair processes may also produce 
increased DNA strand breaks. In addition to DNA-reactive 
agents, chromosome alterations are also produced by second-
ary mechanisms, especially in vitro, by extreme shifts in cel-
lular homeostasis such as high temperature, low pH, osmotic 
changes, or extreme cytotoxicity [47–49]. These secondary 
mechanisms are closely monitored during genetic toxicology 
testing in mammalian cells.
Damage to chromosomal DNA can be subdivided into

chromosome structure (breaks, deletions, rearrangements). A 
wide range of in vitro and in vivo genetic test methods from 
human peripheral blood lymphocytes to cell lines are avail-
able to assess numeric changes and structural breaks. Since 
most chromosome breakage of the types scored in aberration

The tedious nature of scoring chromosomal aberrations by 
microscopy has made the micronucleus (MN) assay emerge as 
a replacement for chromosomal aberration studies in regula-

nucleus (less than 1/3 the size of the cell nucleus) that forms 
whenever an individual chromosome or an acentric fragment 
of a chromosome is not incorporated into one of the nuclei in 
daughter cells post cell division. Early cytogeneticists develop-
ing assays for chromosomal aberration knew from experience 
and theory that no agent could produce heritable chromosomal 
aberrations without also producing acentric fragments, and 
therefore, MN [50]. The MN assay is now the predominant 
genotoxicity test for the detection of chromosomal damage in 
vivo and in vitro due to ease of scoring by microscopy or by 
flow cytometry. MN can originate from chromosome fragments 
resulting from chromosome breakage (clastogenesis) or whole 
chromosomes that fail to migrate properly during cell division 
(aneuploidy) [51]. Therefore, the MN assay detects clastogenic 
and aneugenic chemicals in cells that have undergone cell divi-
sion during or after exposure to the test substance. MN can be 
scored quickly and analysis can be automated by flow cytom-
etry, enabling the scoring of thousands instead of hundreds of 
cells per treatment, thus increasing the power of the assay [50].
26.3.2.3  Genotoxicity to Produce Mutagenicity
The conversion of an initial DNA-damaging event, genotoxic-
ity, to produce a mutation in daughter cells is a cellular process. 
Stable DNA adducts formed at H-bonding sites between G:C or 
A:T base pairs can give rise to opportunities for mispaired bases 
(e.g., A:C or G:T). If this aberrant pairing is stable and occurs 
before or during a DNA replication cycle, the wrong base may be 
substituted in the opposite strand, producing a mismatched het-
eroduplex (Figure 26.6). If not repaired, a second round of DNA 
replication produces a mutated cell, converting a G:C base pair to 
an A:T base pair at that site. Therefore, two cycles of DNA repli-
cation are needed to fix the initial genotoxic damage in parental 
cell DNA to create base-pair substitution mutations. This process 
is referred to as the fixation period in mutation assay protocols 
and can take 3–8 days depending upon the test system. Base-pair 
addition and deletion mutations, also called frameshift mutations, 
result from the addition or deletion of one or a few nucleotide 
pairs from the nucleotide sequence in an exon or gene. Because 
the codon sequence reads in one direction and is not punctuated, 
the loss or gain of a single base-pair changes the reading frame 
of the gene – hence, frameshift mutation. The deletion of a base-
pair shifts the reading frame to the right, and the addition of a 
base-pair shifts it to the left. This type of mutagenic mechanism is 
illustrated in Figure 26.7. Both frameshift and base-pair substitu-
tion gene mutations result in alterations in translation of mRNA 
into the proper amino acid sequence in the gene products, produc-
ing a mutant cell or organism. Base-pair substitution and frame-
shift mutations occur in human genetic diseases and are referred 
to as CDM when they occur in cancer hallmark genes, and these 
specific mutational types are measured in bacterial mutagenicity 
tests used in the regulatory test battery.

26.3.3.1  Repair of DNA Damage
The fidelity and integrity of genetic information in organisms 
are maintained by several types of enzymatic DNA repair. The 
characteristic of self-repair is unique to DNA and emphasizes 
the importance of the integrity of this molecule to the survival 
of an organism. Most components of DNA repair operate con-
stitutively in organisms, although a few repair processes are 
adaptive (inducible) following cellular exposure to genotoxi-
cants [52, 53]. The significance of DNA repair was noted in the

2015 Nobel Prize awarded to Tomas Lindahl, Paul Modrich,

Perhaps, the largest source of human DNA damage is 
derived from endogenous sources. There are more than 20 dif-
ferent oxidative DNA base lesions involved in the estimated 
10,000 oxidative hits which occur per cell per day in the mam-
malian genome [42]. Constitutive cellular repair capacities are 
generally adequate to compensate for the typical endogenous 
background damage; however, recurrent exposures to high 
concentrations of exogenous genotoxic agents may saturate the 
DNA repair capacity, leading to mutation, thereby accelerating 
aging and associated chronic diseases such as cancer.
The common feature of repair processes is their ability to 
detect, remove, and replace damaged segments of DNA [52, 
53]. If a DNA lesion (i.e., DNA strand break or adduct) can 
be repaired prior to mutation fixation, the net effect of the 
DNA damage to an organism may be negligible. This is espe-
cially true following intermittent, low-level exposures to geno-
toxicants where repair enzymes are not fully saturated. DNA 
adducts are not all recognized or repaired equally by excision 
repair mechanisms [55].
Test systems measuring some parameters of the DNA repair 
process have been used as screens for detecting primary DNA 
damage. Virtually, all organisms from bacteria to mammalian 
cells can induce a battery of DNA repair systems to repair a 
broad spectrum of DNA lesions following chemical insult. Thus, 
stimulation or induction of repair activity following chemical 
treatment at sublethal concentrations is a good indicator that the 
target organism has experienced DNA-directed toxicity. DNA 
repair induction (i.e., induction of unscheduled DNA synthesis 
[UDS]) has been used previously as a screen for genotoxicity, 
however, the lack of sensitivity of the rat liver UDS assay has 
removed this test from regulatory genetic toxicology testing as a 
follow-up to in vitro test positive responses [54].
Studies of DNA repair kinetics indicate that once pre-muta-
tional lesions have been induced in the DNA, both error-prone 
and error-free repair processes are activated. Error-prone sys-
tems attempt to maintain DNA continuity and, in the process, 
may actually generate nucleotide mismatches (e.g., A:C or 
G:T) that result in mutations. Error-free repair replaces the 
damaged DNA site with a correct nucleotide sequence. The 
fidelity of repair depends on the degree to which the two dif-
ferent processes are involved. Factors that determine whether 
error-prone or error-free pathways predominate include (1) 
the target organism species, (2) the cell type involved, (3) the 
chemical mutagen, and (4) the specific DNA lesion induced. 
Some data suggest that the error-free repair pathways predom-
inate at low exposure levels, and error-prone pathways come 
into play only following saturation of the error-free enzymes.
Some repair processes directly reverse the damage in a 
single step. Thymidine dimers (covalently linked, adjacent 
thymidine bases) induced by UV light exposure are reversed 
by a photolyase enzyme. Alkylating agents form adducts at 
the guanine-O6 position and can be reversed by alkylguanine 
transferase. This enzyme removes the alkyl adduct from DNA 
transferring it to a cysteine residue in a single step [56].
The remaining DNA repair processes involve multiple 
steps, with recruitment of multiple proteins to the repair sites 
as a repair complex, to catalyze the various steps involved in 
removing DNA damaged bases and restoring genome fidelity. 
Various proteins (i.e., TP53, CHEK1, CHEK2) regulate the 
cell cycle and preventing DNA damaged cells from entering 
S-phase, enabling repair processes time to remove the damage 
prior to DNA synthesis using a damaged template [52, 53].
Base excision repair (BER) and nucleotide excision repair 
(NER) are the primary repair mechanisms for chemical dam-
age. The general processes involved in BER and NER are 
shown in Figure 26.8. BER corrects damaged bases or abasic 
sites in DNA. In BER, glycosylases also involved in mismatch 
repair (MMR) catalyze the release of the inappropriate or dam-
aged base, followed by replacement and ligation [52, 53, 57]. 
The enzyme complex responsible for NER of bulky adducts 
requires more than 20 proteins and consists of several steps. 
An endonuclease cleaves the DNA at the site of the damage, 
an exonuclease cuts out the damaged region including nucleo-
tides to either side (i.e., 25–30 nucleotides), and new bases are 
replaced by a DNA polymerase using an editing function to

incorporate incorrect bases leading to mismatched pairings 
that do not properly hydrogen bond and escape proofread-

general genome-wide increases in gene mutation. A specific 
repair process identified as MMR uses proteins that recognize 
aberrant DNA pairing caused by replication errors and tar-

of the DNA duplex is excised and filled by the repair poly-
merase. This is a redundant repair process that occurs after 
BER and NER and improves the accuracy of those processes. 
Deficiencies in MMR proteins are associated with increased 
cancer risk.
Several less-studied repair systems have been identified, one 
of which is specific for sealing (ligase) SSBs after remodeling 
of the opened ends by poly (adenosine diphosphate [ADP]-
ribose) polymerase and another that repairs interstrand DNA 
cross-links that block DNA synthesis at the replication fork. 
The repair enzymes break the DNA at the site of the cross-link 
followed by restoration of the replication fork, which removes 
lesions from the template DNA strands of actively transcribed

Theoretical assumptions and data from studies of repair 
in many species support the belief that at background or low 
exposure levels, an error-free removal of adducts from DNA 
can be virtually 100% effective, which supports the arguments 
for thresholds in cancer initiation [60]. Thus, one observes sur-
vival shoulders and non-linear kinetics for mutation induction 
in repair-proficient cells and the loss of apparent no-effect 
regions in repair-deficient cells.
26.3.3.2  Xenobiotic Biotransformation 
in Genetic Toxicology
A major deficiency of the current in vitro assays used in genetic 
toxicology testing and most high-throughput assays (HTA) is 
the absence of active Phase I (e.g., CYP450 family) and Phase 
II (glucuronyltransferases, Glutathione (GSH)-transferases, 
sulfotransferases) drug-metabolizing enzyme systems respon-

metabolizing enzyme systems are localized in the cellular 
endoplasmic reticulum and cytoplasm, are inducible, and this 
endogenous system, a critical determinant of drug toxicity, 
is absent in all cell lines used for regulatory genetic toxicol-

26.3.3.3  Role of Microbiome in 
Genetic Toxicology
While research efforts to understand the role of microbiota 
and microbiome (microorganisms that colonize the human 
body and their combined genetic material, respectively) in 
human health have begun relatively recently [63], it is evi-
dent that this factor should be taken into consideration when 
assessing chemical toxicity [64, 65]. Some of the effects can be 
caused by the metabolism of xenobiotics by bacterial enzymes 
and systemic absorption through enterohepatic recirculation; 
others are related to dysbiosis due to death of microflora after 
exposure to certain chemical agents. Moreover, some species 
of intestinal microbiota were shown to produce mutations 
directly as a result of DNA damage (alkylation) caused by 
enzymes, such as colibactin produced by E. coli [66–68].
26.4  Regulatory Genetic Toxicology Testing
Over the last decades, hundreds of tests have been used to iden-
tify agents with genotoxic properties. After retrospective eval-
uation of the literature, database assessments, and numerous 
workshops on specific genetic toxicology bioassays and testing 
strategies used in regulatory toxicology, the initial set of genetic 
toxicology regulatory tests developed by the Organization for 
Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) in 1997, 
adopted by the International Council for Harmonization of 
Technical Requirements for Pharmaceuticals for Human 
Use (ICH), US Food and Drug Administration (FDA), and 
US Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) were revised in 
2016. Fundamentally, the testing strategy to assess genotoxic 
hazards relies on the in vitro assessment of “small” changes 
in the genome by gene mutation assays (base-pair substitu-
tions, frameshift mutations, small deletions) and large-scale 
chromosomal changes (chromosomal breaks and aneuploidy) 
as detected by the MN assay, to detect rodent carcinogens and 
in vivo genotoxins [69]. The in vitro MN (MNvit) assay is fol-
lowed up by in vivo tests in rodents: an MN assay to assess 
chromosomal damage, along with a comet assay to assess DNA 
damage, which is usually performed using liver tissue [70, 
71]. Although the test battery outlined below is directed to the 
pharmaceutical industry, the OECD test guidelines that support 
these specific genetic toxicology tests are relevant across all 
industries and can be considered a “core” genetic toxicology 
test battery of significance to toxicologists.

26.4.1.1  Bacterial Reverse Mutation Assay
A bacterial mutagenicity assay (sometimes called the Ames 
assay), as developed by Bruce Ames and colleagues in 
Salmonella test strains and by others in E. coli, remains a key 
component of genotoxicity testing batteries [73, 78, 79]. The 
most recent revision of the ICH Guidance on Genotoxicity 
Testing (ICH, 2012) [80] retains the requirement for a test for 
gene mutation in bacteria. It is estimated that over 2,000 labo-
ratories worldwide use this test system as a predictive tool for 
detecting biologically hazardous agents. The assay is a simple, 
sensitive, and cost-effective test for detecting chemical muta-
gens, a cancer key event, and is recognized as an established 
carcinogen prescreen by regulatory agencies worldwide: see 
OECD 471 2020 [72].
Tester strains used in the bacterial reverse mutagenicity test 
are derived from Salmonella typhimurium or E. coli which 
are defective in excision repair and contain specific muta-
tions in the histidine (S. typhimurium) or tryptophan (E. coli) 
operons that render these organisms unable to synthesize the 
essential amino acid. The commonly used S. typhimurium 
mutant strains contain either a base-pair substitution mutation 
(hisG46-TA1535, TA100) or one of the frameshift mutations 
(hisD6610-TA97, TA97a; hisC3076-TA1537; or hisD3052-
TA1538, TA98) and an ochre mutation (hisG428-TA102) and 
trpE – for E. coli WP2 uvrA pKM101. These tester strains show 
a specificity to revert to a prototrophic condition by mutagens 
that cause base-pair substitutions or frameshift mutations [72]. 
The addition of further genetic mutations that increase the tes-
ter strain sensitivity to mutagens (deletion of excision repair 
genes), and the use of metabolic activation systems such as 
induced rat liver S9, have allowed detection of a wide variety 
of chemical mutagens. Since each tester strain is considered to 
be an independent test system, a positive response, based upon 
a greater than 2–3-fold increase, a dose–response exceeding 
the 95% confidence interval of historical controls, by a test 
article in any strains is considered relevant. Depending on the 
strain specificity of a positive response, it is possible to gain 
insight into the mutagenic mechanism(s) involved.
26.4.1.2  In vitro MN Assay
The MNvit assay detects chromosomal damage as MN in the 
cytoplasm of interphase cells (OECD 487) [74]. MN may origi-
nate from chromosome fragments resulting from chromosome 
breakage (clastogenesis) or whole chromosomes that fail to 
migrate properly during cell division (aneuploidy) after expo-
sure to the test substance [51]. MN can be scored quickly, and 
analysis can be automated by flow cytometry, enabling the scor-
ing of thousands instead of hundreds of cells per treatment, thus 
increasing the power of the assay. The MNvit assay is robust and 
effective in a variety of human and rodent cell types.
26.4.1.3  In vitro Mouse Lymphoma (MOLY) 
TK Gene Mutation Assay
The in vitro mammalian cell gene mutation test can be used to 
detect gene mutations induced by chemical substances (OECD 
490) [75]. The MOLY TK gene mutation assay is conducted 
using the cell line L5178Y Tk+/-3.7.2C. This cell line is het-

using the TK locus include both gene mutations and chromo-

exposed to a test substance, both with and without metabolic 
activation, then subcultured for 3 days to allow phenotypic 
expression prior to mutant selection. Cytotoxicity is usually 
determined by measuring the relative total growth of the 
cultures after the treatment period. The treated cultures are 
maintained in a growth medium for a sufficient period to allow 
near-optimal phenotypic expression of induced mutations. 
Mutant frequency is determined by seeding known numbers 
of cells in the medium containing the selective agent to detect 
mutant cells, and in the medium without a selective agent to 
determine the cloning efficiency (viability) at selection. After

26.4.1.4  In vivo MN Assay
The rodent erythrocyte MN test (OECD 474, 2016) [76] is the 
primary in vivo test to assess chromosomal alterations [70, 76, 
81]. Since MN are formed either from acentric chromosome 
fragments or from lagging chromosomes that fail to migrate 
to the cellular poles during anaphase, the determination of 
MN frequencies is a reliable method for evaluating the poten-
tial of a chemical to induce structural or numeric chromo-
somal alterations or both. The in vivo target cell population 
for the MN assay is the rapidly proliferating bone marrow 
red blood cell lineage, which works by detecting and quan-
tifying damage induced by test articles to the chromosomes 
or the mitotic apparatus of erythroblasts. The MN assay 
evaluates micronuclei present in reticulocytes (referred to 
as polychromatic erythrocytes) or erythrocytes (referred to 
as normochromatic erythrocytes) sampled either in the bone 
marrow or peripheral blood cells of rodents. The decades of 
experience with the MN assay, the solid genetic basis of MN 
as biomarkers of chromosomal damage, and the development 
of automated flow cytometry-based assay methods across 
multiple species, including humans, have established the MN 
assay as a cornerstone of in vivo genotoxicity assessment. 
Quantitative and automated flow cytometry-based methods 
have largely replaced microscopy due to several advantages 
over traditional manual, slide-based scoring methods [50]. 
Scoring cells by flow cytometry eliminates bias and allows for 
the evaluation of a much greater number of cells per animal, 
thereby greatly reducing sampling variability and increasing 
the statistical power of the assay. Using flow cytometry-based 
methods, data from an entire experiment can be obtained in 
a single day, rather than several days to weeks, using minute 
quantities of blood. Furthermore, flow cytometry can simulta-
neously provide information about the quantity of DNA pres-
ent in the observed MN, thereby providing an indication of 
the mechanism by which MN are induced. The procedure can 
be applied to blood and bone marrow from mice and rats with 
minor procedural adjustments.
26.4.1.5  In vivo Comet Assay
The comet assay (OECD 489, 2016) [77, 80] detects DNA 
repair and a broad spectrum of DNA damage in individual 
cells, including strand breaks, apurinic sites, alkali-labile DNA 
adducts, and a spectrum of reactive oxygen and lipid peroxida-
tion species-induced DNA lesions [71, 77]. The comet assay is 
used in genetic toxicology to detect DNA damage in a broad 
spectrum of tissues because the comet assay requires only a 
small number of cells and does not require cell division for 
evaluation of DNA damage, making any tissue type in rodents 
amenable to investigation. The comet assay can be conducted 
in freshly isolated tissue at necropsy or tissue can be cryopre-
served for analysis later [82]. The assay has demonstrated good 
concordance with in vivo mutation assays in GM rodents [83]. 
The in vivo comet assay is included as a second tissue bioassay 
in ICH S2(R1) to evaluate genotoxicity in rodents [80].
26.4.2  Genetic Toxicology Testing 
Positive and Negative Criteria
As part of the revisions to the OECD test guidelines, a set 
of criteria were put forth to determine a positive or negative 
response in each individual assay conducted as part of the 
genetic toxicology test battery. These criteria are listed below.
A test article is considered clearly positive if, in any of the 
experimental conditions examined:
	
1.	at least one of the test concentrations exhibits a sta-
tistically significant increase compared with the con-
current negative control
	
2.	the increase is dose-related in at least one experimen-
tal condition
	
3.	any of the results are outside the distribution of the 
historical negative control data (e.g., 95% confidence 
interval)
When all three criteria are met, the test article is considered 
positive.
When none of the criteria are met, the test article is clearly 
negative.
If 1–2 of these criteria are met, the test article is equivocal.
Very significantly, for in vivo genotoxicity assays that pro-
duce a negative response is the requirement to demonstrate 
that the test article is systemically available.
26.4.3  Testing Strategies, Data 
Evaluation, and Application
A genotoxic compound may be defined as an agent that 
produces a positive response in a bioassay measuring any 
genetic endpoint (e.g., mutation, DNA breaks, clastogenic-
ity). Although this definition considers virtually all forms of 
damage to DNA to classify an agent as genotoxic, that clas-
sification should not be interpreted a priori as an indication of 
hazard or risk. The genetic toxicology test battery is part of 
hazard identification, and a WoE from the entire test battery 
should be considered prior to determining the genetic hazard 
and risk to somatic or germ cells. Chromosomal aberrations, 
MN, or DNA damage assays (comet assay) do not determine 
the transmission of these events to daughter cells; they are 
measures of genotoxicity, not mutagenicity. The permanent, 
hereditary changes, mutations, contribute to the mutational 
load of an organism affecting somatic cells passing these 
genetic anomalies to daughter cells, or germ cells passed on to 
future generations [1, 2]. The multiplicity of mechanisms that 
could potentially be involved in genetic toxicity necessitates 
the use of a battery of tests that will detect primary genotoxic 
mechanisms and outcomes of the initial interaction of a xeno-
biotic with DNA. Virtually all regulatory strategies include

1426
Hayes’ Principles and Methods of Toxicology
tests for gene mutation and clastogenicity and require at least 
one in vivo assay. The general guidance to assess the genetic 
toxicology test battery results developed by most regulatory 
agencies considers the overall strengths and limitations of the 
tests recommended, to minimize missing genotoxicants with 
high intrinsic risk.
Compared to conducting the tests, data assessment and 
interpretation can often be a more complex task. There is min-
imal guidance regarding the interpretation of heterogeneous 
outcomes from a test battery. The general recommendation 
for the interpretation of mixed results (either all positive or all 
negative) is to conduct additional testing using in vivo or MoA 
studies until a WoE assessment of the intrinsic properties of 
the agent is clearly defined [21].
A valuable application of genetic toxicology results to cancer 
risk estimation has been the use of genotoxicity data by regula-
tory agencies as a key component in determining the MoA for 
carcinogens [1, 2, 9, 10, 21]. Carcinogens with positive effects 
in genetic tests are considered to have a genotoxic or muta-
genic MoA through direct effects on DNA. Carcinogens that 
test negative for genotoxicity are presumed to produce tumors 
through one of several epigenetic MoAs. The EPA has devel-
oped a decision tree approach, which uses genetic test results 
to aid selection of the most appropriate data extrapolation 
model for cancer risk assessment [21]. A genotoxic mechanism 
has historically implied a default assumption of a no-threshold 
mechanism and linear extrapolation for risk, whereas a thresh-
old may exist for epigenetic MoAs. The default assumption of 
no-threshold for genotoxic compounds is being challenged by 
numerous quantitative non-linear dose–response data using 
sensitive tools with prototypic and well-known genotoxic 
compounds [60]. Genotoxicity data are amenable to traditional 
BMD calculations useful for risk assessments.
26.4.4  Genetic Toxicology Screening – In 
Silico and In Vitro Testing
Several higher-throughput screening approaches, in contrast to 
the tests outlined above, can be used to prioritize test agents 
prior to the conduct of a GLP-compliant regulatory genetic tox-
icology test battery. These include computational Quantitative 
Structure Activity Relationships (QSAR) tools, miniaturized 
MNvit assays conducted as part of the genetic toxicology test 
battery, high-content assays that can assess genotoxicity and 
predict MoA with respect to clastogen or aneugen, toxicoge-
nomics (TgX)-based DDI response biomarker (TgX DDI), and 
a spectrum of genotoxicity tests that use transfected reporter 
genes as indicators of genotoxicity. At present, some of these 
methods can be used early in product development, and per-
haps more importantly, in the qualification of drug impurities 
as per ICH M7 (R1) [84, 85]. These same tools and alternatives 
to animal testing are being used across several areas, in safety 
assessment of food additives, fragrances, cosmetics, crop pro-
tectants, and chemical industries.
QSAR computational tools rely upon a combination of 
mathematical algorithms and qualitative experience rules to 
define the biological relationships between molecular struc-
ture and genotoxicity [86]. Commonly used QSAR models 
(e.g., MultiCASE, Derek, Sarah) are primarily used as tools 
to suggest the potential for genotoxic hazards and needs for 
follow-up testing. These tools are relied upon to predict the 
outcome for bacterial mutation assays based upon a statistical 
ruling and an expert ruling with respect to potential genotoxic 
structural alerts. The presence of structural alerts in a drug 
impurity can lead to further testing, beginning with the con-
duct of a bacterial mutagenicity test or screening assay. These 
QSAR models require the use of a statistical and expert ruling 
to identify structural alerts in a test article.
There are several laboratory-based assays to assess genotoxic-
ity that can be used in screening for genotoxic potential. Although 
some of these are higher-throughput versions of bacterial muta-
tion and MN assays used in the regulatory genetic toxicology 
test battery, others are focused on MoA with respect to chromo-
somal aberrations due to chromosome breaks, clastogenicity, or 
aneuploidy, an important distinction regarding assessment of 
genotoxic risk. Although these assays are predictive to varying 
degrees to the outcomes observed in the regulatory genetic toxi-
cology test battery, it is important to note that these assays are not 
GLP-compliant and do not replace the assays outlined in the test 
battery for regulatory purposes.
Screening assays for the bacterial mutagenicity tests include:
MiniAmes – a scaled-down version of the bacterial

ToxTracker – a genotoxicity assay that uses a panel

It is of significance that none of the screening assays out-

reporter assays depend upon DNA damage signaling and do 
not measure direct DNA or chromosomal damage or muta-

events (DNA damage, gene mutation, chromosomal damage) 
in cell culture and in animals, is the cornerstone of genotoxic-
ity assessments and is required for the safety assessment of 
new chemicals, food additives, and pharmaceuticals. Although 
several genotoxic screens have been developed with high-
throughput screening technology as part of the EPA ToxCast 
and National Toxicology Program (NTP) Tox21 programs that 
depend upon indirect measures of DNA damage detection 
(e.g., ćH2AX, p53 phosphorylation), the prediction of cancer 
risk by these assays has been reported by some authors as no 
better than chance [90].
Several genotoxicity assays that are referred to as New 
Alternative Methodologies (NAM) are under development to 
reduce the reliance on genotoxicity assessments in animals. 
Human hepatocyte models, with intact Phase I and Phase II 
biotransformation and cellular compartmentalization, are 
being developed to replace the use of highly induced rat liver 
S9 fraction to provide bioactivation. Assays developed using 
other species, e.g., chicken and turkey eggs, maintain intact 
Phase I and Phase II biotransformation and increase the 
genomic landscape to assess DNA damage, are rapid, and can 
distinguish genotoxic from non-genotoxic carcinogens.
26.4.5  Assessment of Chromosomal Aberrations 
– Classification of Clastogen or Aneugen
Despite the importance of determining whether a compound 
showing genotoxic activity in mammalian cells is a clastogen 
or aneugen, very limited and insensitive methods have been 
used to make this distinction and none are automated. Since 
a clastogen is regarded as a higher genotoxic risk than an 
aneugen, this can become significant information in the WoE 
approach to assessing genotoxicity. A high-content multi-
plexed platform that classifies test compounds as nongenotoxi-
cants, aneugens, or clastogens known as the MultiFlow® assay 
(Litron Laboratories, Rochester NY), simultaneously deter-
mines the response of four endpoints, ćH2AX, TP53, phos-
pho-histone H3, and polyploidy. A computational assessment 
of those data is used to make the classification with respect 
to non-genotoxic, clastogen, or aneugen. The assay has been 
validated across several laboratories and used to assay many 
chemicals to create a varied chemical space to which the assay 
responds [87]. To assess clastogen versus aneugen MoA in vivo 
remains more technically difficult, requiring immunochemi-
cal approaches for the presence of a kinetochore with the MN 
itself, or by assessing DNA content of individual MN. MN 
with high DNA content are presumed to arise from aneuploidy 
MoA when compared to micronuclei with lower DNA content, 
indicating micronuclei harboring DNA fragments.

The most problematic data evaluation situation is a unique, 
unexpected positive response among a battery of otherwise 
negative results. The positive response may be an important 
piece of information defining potential hazard or it may be a 
false-positive result. Distinguishing between the two possibili-
ties is not always a simple task. When the positive response 
is produced by an in vitro test that can be followed up by an 
in vivo study measuring the same endpoint, a decision can be 
derived from the results of the in vivo test. A well-conducted 
negative in vivo study often overrules the in vitro positive. 
This is especially true if the in vitro positive is associated 
with certain testing features linked to the generation of false 
responses (e.g., excess cytotoxicity). Important lessons have 
been learned that can be applied when testing in vitro that may 
avoid spurious results and unnecessary additional testing [72, 
90]. Most decisions made for regulatory purposes are based on 
the response profile from a battery of tests specified by regula-
tions or guidelines and use a WoE approach that includes test 
data and expert judgment.
Genotoxicity assays have different weights for assessing 
potential risk. Genotoxicity tests measuring mutations and 
chromosome damage have greater weight than “indicator” 
assays that measure DNA damage. For example, for human 
pharmaceuticals, ICH S2 (R1) (ICH 2012) [80] states that 
“fixation of damage to DNA in the form of gene mutations, 
larger-scale chromosomal damage or recombination is gen-
erally considered to be essential for heritable effects and in 
the multi-step process of malignancy”. The OECD Expert 
Working Group for Genetic Toxicology that revised the test 
guidelines from 2014 to 2016 published an “Overview of 
the Set of OECD Genetic Toxicology Test Guidelines and 
Updates Performed in 2014–2015” (OECD 2015) [62]. This 
document states, “There are tests that detect primary DNA 
damage or genotoxicity (i.e., the first in the chain of events 
leading to a mutation), but not the consequences of this 
genetic damage. The endpoint measured in these tests does 
not always lead to a mutation, a change that can be passed 
on to subsequent generations (of cells or organisms). The 
DNA damage measured in the comet assay may be a result 
of DNA repair enzymes cutting DNA to initiate DNA repair, 
returning the DNA either to its original state or DNA dam-
age that could lead to cell death, or unrepaired DNA damage 
that can result in mutation”. At the time of analysis for the 
comet assay, it is uncertain which of these three events is 
ongoing. When evaluating the genotoxic potential of a test 
article, more weight should be given to the measurement of 
permanent DNA changes (i.e., mutations) rather than DNA or 
DNA repair events that are reversible.
Other criteria for the WoE approach to assess genotoxic 
hazards include studies conducted according to OECD guide-
lines and compliance with GLP regulations: GLP studies have 
greater weight than non-GLP studies. The number of pieces 
of evidence within a specific category – genotoxicity ver-
sus mutagenicity – influences the WoE. Divergent responses 
(positive or negative) within most studies exhibiting concor-
dant findings are insufficient to alter the WoE. WoE is an 
aggregate of the weights of all the pieces of evidence within 
a single test category (e.g., tests for gene mutation). Data from 
in vivo genotoxicity or mutagenicity are weighted more heav-
ily than evidence developed from tests conducted in vitro or 
in non-mammalian models. Finally, in vivo test responses in 
rodents are able to predict potential hazards more accurately in 
humans since traditional measures of risk assessment, calcula-
tion of BMD, and identification of PoD as used in traditional 
toxicology risk assessment are being applied to in vitro and in 
vivo genetic toxicology measures.

26.4.8  Regulatory Guidance for Environmental Agents
Recommendations outlining methods for routine genetic 
toxicology assays that basically mirror that outlined above 
for ICH S2(R1) have been published by the EPA [95], OECD 
[96], Canadian Health and Welfare [97], and the European 
Economic Community [98]. Genetic toxicology regulatory 
testing requirements worldwide are harmonized, as are the 
protocols used to conduct the tests through the OECD pro-
cess. As stated previously, the overall structure of genotoxicity 
testing strategies is designed to capture small DNA base 
changes (gene mutation) and large-scale chromosomal aber-
rations (Table 26.7). Level 3 and 4 tests outlined below have 
been seldom used over the last decade since many germ cell 
genotoxicants are genotoxic in either in vitro or in vivo test-
ing using somatic cell systems or are identified as reproduc-
tive toxicants using standard in vivo toxicology testing such 
as the Extended One Generation Reproductive toxicology test. 
Secondly, the genetic toxicology test to assess germ cell muta-
gens are animal-intensive, and at present, there is a mandate to 
reduce reliance on animal testing in toxicology as part of the 
3Rs to reduce, refine, and replace animal testing. How success-
ful these new approaches will be in identifying assessing germ 
cell genotoxicants is uncertain.
Genetic toxicology practice is moving from the assumption 
of linear, no-threshold dose–response default assumptions to 
traditional toxicology dose–response modeling for risk assess-
ment. Gene mutation and certain genotoxic endpoints are 
considered human-relevant toxicological endpoints that can 
be analyzed like any other toxicology endpoint [2, 99]. Dose–
response data from the genetic toxicology test battery and 
genomic DDR response network can be used to develop BMD 
and an estimated PoD. The PoD from genetic toxicology end-
points is used with the exposure information to develop risk 
estimations (e.g., using reference dose and margin of exposure 
[MoE] approaches) in a risk characterization and presented to 
risk managers for informing decision-making.
Recent advances in quantifying genomic cell response net-
works to xenobiotic exposures, the DDR, and alteration in the 
epigenome response, integrated with apical measures of cyto-
toxicity, genotoxicity, and mutations, add MoA information 
used in WoE to identify potential risk to humans. At present, 
it is uncertain how regulatory bodies will recognize this para-
digm shift from hazard identification to quantitative analyses 
identifying BMD and PoD to assess genotoxic risk to humans. 
However, data supporting the application of “traditional” risk 
assessment metrics to genetic toxicology and genomic profil-
ing endpoints are showing that genetic toxicology- and genom-
ics profiling-derived BMD and PoD are not significantly 
different than traditional cancer-endpoint-derived BMD and 
PoD metrics.

26.4.9  Limitations of Current Testing Strategies
Over the last decade, numerous workshops, literature reviews, 
and expert panels culminated in the publication of revised genetic 
toxicology testing strategies and OECD guidelines in 2016 for 
genetic toxicology test methods used in regulatory toxicology 
testing. These workshops recognized and addressed many of 
the limitations observed with years of genetic toxicology testing 
and implemented these within each guideline to reduce potential 
false positives or false negative responses. The genetic constitu-
tion of established somatic cell lines used in safety assessment 
and high-throughput screenings is known to be compromised in 
genetic pathways and circuitry responsible for how cells respond 
to toxic stressors and compromise DDR. However, it is of impor-
tance to recognize some of these limitations when developing a 
WoE approach in the assessment of genotoxic risk for a specific 
test material. Some of these include:
	
1.	The source of biotransformation enzymes used in 
genetic toxicology testing is derived from highly 
induced rat liver supporting only CYP450 metabo-
lism and “bioactivation” without Phase II enzyme 
detoxication. This does not reflect in vivo biotrans-
formation of xenobiotics where Phase I and Phase II 
metabolism are linked, to convert lipid-soluble xeno-
biotics to water-soluble excretory products.
	
2.	The bacterial organisms used in bacterial mutation 
assays are defective in DNA excision repair. Nucleotide 
excision DNA repair in model organisms can prevent 
up to 99% of point mutations, almost uniformly across 
the mutation spectrum [100]. Mammalian cells have 
evolved specialized DDR networks that orchestrate 
multilayer signaling cascades for specific lesions to 
ensure that genomic integrity is preserved over time.
	
3.	The in vivo comet assay that detects a broad spec-
trum of lesions can be susceptible to false-positive 
responses from excessive toxicity using maximum 
tolerated dose (MTD) regimens outlined in OECD 
test guidelines. MTD dosing can cause tissue toxic-
ity, cell death, or apoptosis at the target tissue site 
[77]. When conducting in vivo comet tests, it is 
prudent to include supplemental evaluations of tar-
get tissue histopathology to preclude false-positive 
responses due to secondary toxicity.
	
4.	Negative responses from in vivo genotoxicity assess-
ments need to include an indication of tissue exposure. 
In the case of MN assay, assessment of bone marrow 
toxicity by determining the reticulocyte to red blood 
cell ratio is an indicator of bone marrow toxicity and 
therefore exposure. However, most commonly, this is 
accomplished using bioanalytical methods for the par-
ent compound or its metabolites in target tissues.
26.5  Assessing Genetic Hazard and Risk
26.5.1  Germ Cell Risk in Human Populations
Observations of new dominant mutations arising in human 
populations and extensive experimental data demonstrating 
chemically induced mutations in somatic cells as well as 
in germ lines of mice, document the presumption that all 
mammalian species, including humans, are susceptible to 
endogenous and environmentally generated mutation [42]. 
The ability to demonstrate genetic toxicity to human popula-
tions through epidemiological methods has been limited by 
the following:
	
1.	The rarity of induced mutations in a population. The 
sample size required to detect even a large increase 
in mutation is prohibitive.
	
2.	The small number of dominant sentinel genes (i.e., 
express a visible phenotype) that are uniquely identi-
fied with specific genetic diseases.
	
3.	The difficulty in identifying reproductively active 
populations exposed to biologically significant levels 
of suspected mutagenic agents.
	
4.	Variable expression of newly induced recessive 
mutations in the F1 generation post-exposure.
In 1992, the United Nations Environment Program reviewed 
the status of and methods available for genetic risk assessment 
[99]. A more recent classification scheme was proposed by the 
US EPA [101]. The system provides a qualitative risk classifica-
tion for an agent that is derived from a four-level testing scheme 
that starts with in vitro methods and progresses through in vivo 
somatic tests in Level 2 and finally into germ cell assays in 
Levels 3 and 4 (see Table 26.7). Based on the compilation of test 
results, chemicals will fall into one of the classes ranging from 
not mutagenic to a human mutagen as shown in Table 26.8. Some 
regulatory agencies have modified the four-level strategy shown 
in Table 26.7 by combining levels (e.g., some combine Levels 
3 and 4); however, the scheme shown in Table 26.7 provides a 
suitable road map for most situations. The complete genetic risk 
assessment process involves several steps:
	
1.	Hazard identification – The qualitative assessment of 
the intrinsic toxicity of an agent. This is based on 
the existing database and addresses the question of 
whether there is potential for human genotoxicity.
	
2.	Dose–response assessment – The relationship 
between the dose of an agent and the induction of an 
adverse (genotoxic) effect is determined.
	
3.	Exposure assessment – A determination of the extent 
of human exposure to the agent.
	
4.	Risk characterization – A description of the nature 
and potential for genotoxicity risk to humans using 
the information from the exposure assessment and 
the dose–response data.

studies in humans of genotoxic damage have involved the

26.6  Toxicogenomics and Emerging 
Technologies in Genetic Toxicology
The Human Genome Project launched a broad range of biological 
and computational tools enabling the study of various macromol-
ecules or subcellular components in response to exposures (i.e., 
environmental, chemical) and therapeutic treatments. These tools 
have impacted all the biological sciences including toxicology. 
These tools referred to as “-omics” technologies have impacted 
basic research toxicology, regulatory toxicology by providing 
MoA data as part of WoE, and toxicology testing by develop-
ing biomarkers that can link exposures to early responses and 
ultimately to disease outcomes. These genome tools combined 
with bioinformatics have fostered the expansion of toxicologi-
cal testing to include a new area – toxicogenomics – that cuts 
across the various disciplines within toxicological sciences. In 
genetic toxicology, toxicogenomic-based biomarkers have played 
an important role in interrogating how cells, tissues, and organs 
respond to DNA damage. By developing an understanding of 
how these evolutionarily conserved biological processes main-
tain genomic integrity, predictive MoA biomarkers have been 
developed.
There is an ongoing significant shift in toxicity and risk 
assessment regarding environmental chemicals and new drug 
candidates to reduce, refine, and replace certain traditional 
toxicity testing in animals and implement higher-throughput in 
vitro gene expression- and pathway-based screening tools. The 
proposed changes have occurred in parallel with increasing 
emphasis on the use of MoA through toxicogenomics in safety 
assessment. The use of toxicogenomics in safety assessment 
has been endorsed by the National Research Council, the EPA, 
and the National Institute of Environmental Health Sciences 
(NIEHS) NTP Tox21 [91, 102, 107–109]. Toxicogenomics is 
also being used by the pharmaceutical industry in drug discov-
ery, aiming to reduce off-target effects and to identify poten-
tial liabilities prior to conducting costly and time-consuming 
GLP-compliant studies with drug candidates [103].
Significant advances in gene expression profiling technol-
ogy by Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS) have enabled 
global transcriptional profiling (referred to as RNA-Seq) of 
the cellular genome output (transcriptomics) across species at 
a level never possible before [108]. NGS, initially referred to 
as massive parallel sequencing, has revolutionized the ability 
of scientists to quantify and count the individual RNA outputs

between genotoxic or non-genotoxic MoAs (TgX DDI) and 
certain gene sets are proposed as predictors for tumor out-

TgX DDI – The cellular p53 DDR detects DNA lesions, sig-

in turn, induces the transcription of several genes that regu-
late cellular responses to DNA damage, including cell cycle 
arrest, activation of DNA repair systems, induction of apop-
tosis, and modification of the cellular epigenome [112–114]. 
Toxicogenomic gene signature-based mRNA biomarkers have 
been described for distinguishing various MoAs as genotoxic 
or non-genotoxic using the TgX DDI biomarker developed 
in collaborative studies with several laboratories, sponsored 
by International Life Sciences Institute and the Health and 
Environmental Sciences Institute and studies conducted at 
Health Canada, Georgetown University Integrated Laboratory 
Systems [112–114]. The TgX DDI biomarker is a 65-gene 
classifier developed in human TK6 cells that includes vari-
ous genes associated with a DDR in which the P53-signaling 
pathway is significantly overrepresented and can differentiate 
genotoxic from non-genotoxic compounds with high accuracy 
(Figure 26.10) [112]. This biomarker can be used in conjunc-
tion with current computational tools and genotoxicity testing 
batteries to differentiate compounds that may produce irrel-
evant false-positive findings from indirect DNA damage due 
to excessive cytotoxicity from direct DNA-damaging test com-
pounds [115].
Toxicogenomics and BMD – Due to the evolutionary con-
servation of the genome, toxicogenomics may also provide 
the needed data to better inform cross-species extrapolation. 
Particularly, whether the effects of chemicals in test animals 
can be used to predict human responses or not. Integration of 
toxicogenomics with investigative toxicology holds significant 
promise in the development of biomarkers useful for in vitro 
to in vivo extrapolations, in cross-species extrapolation needed 
for risk assessment, and in clinical trials to provide early warn-
ing signs of potential adverse effects. Toxicogenomics data 
have been used to derive “traditional” BMD to identify points 
of departure for risk assessments [116]. This has been enabled 
using a software package, BMDExpress, developed by EPA 
researchers [117]. BMDExpress, currently under the steward-
ship of the NTP, can conduct transcriptomic dose–response 
analysis and identify points of departure needed for risk 
assessments [118].
Data from toxicogenomic studies generate biological infor-
mation that support efforts in predictive and mechanistic toxi-
cology, biomarker discovery, risk assessment, identification of 
disease mechanisms, and drug development. Expression profil-
ing in toxicology is used to: (1) evaluate toxicant MoA based on 
patterns of gene expression compared to unexposed controls; 
(2) integrate gene expression dose–response profiling with 
phenotypic anchoring; (3) study the toxicological effects of 
chemical mixtures. There are a number of websites that accept 
toxicogenomics-based gene expression data (Table 26.10) and 
are publicly available for researchers to interrogate.
Duplex Sequencing and Analytical Determination of 
Mutation and Mutation Spectra – The field of genetic toxi-

the potential of chemicals to induce alterations in DNA. The 
ability to clone and sequence DNA permitted the determina-

genotoxic agent at the molecular level. The changes in base

a permanent, unequivocal record of the outcome of the muta-

induce a mutational spectrum that is typically distinct from 
the background spectrum of mutations [36, 119]. Mutagenesis 
induced by chemicals compared to the background spectrum

nucleotide changes and types of base-pair changes induced. 
This striking feature of DNA mutations from mutagen expo-

or humans.
Until recently, using current tools to determine the spec-

Duplex sequencing is an NGS and bioinformatics approach 
that reduces errors in DNA sequencing to below one artifac-

fold increase in accuracy compared to standard sequencing.

compounds without conflicting with ethical aspects or regula-

26.6.2  High-Throughput Testing Programs 
Safety Assessment for 21st Century
An estimated 10,000 animals are used in toxicology safety 
assessments to meet all the regulatory requirements to approve 
a new pharmaceutical or to register a single pesticide “active 
ingredient”. With thousands of new industrial chemicals 
introduced each year and the need to assure the safety of new 
pharmaceuticals, there is a critical need to reduce reliance 
on animal testing and to develop highly predictive human-
based biological models for safety assessments. In response 
to this need, a significant shift is beginning to occur in the 
safety assessment of new drug candidates and environmental 
chemicals that can revolutionize the practice of regulatory 
toxicology. This includes a significant reduction or in some 
cases elimination, of traditional toxicity testing in animals 
with the potential implementation of higher-throughput testing 
schemes using human cell systems. These efforts have gained 
significant momentum recently through mandates requiring 
the EPA to reduce and replace animal testing where scientifi-
cally feasible. In 2013, the EU completed the ban on the sale 
of animal-tested cosmetics, applying to both products and 
ingredients, regardless of whether there were alternative test-
ing methods. The replacement of traditional animal toxicity 
tests for toxicology safety assessments with alternative tests 
encounters significant regulatory hurdles, with validation – the 
proof that a test can accurately predict the responses known to 
occur both in traditional regulatory testing and, more impor-
tantly, in humans – as the biggest challenge for acceptance of 
an alternative method by regulatory agencies.
Government research agencies such as the NIEHS are very 
active in characterizing toxicogenomic applications in toxi-
cological experiments, evaluating toxicant-specific patterns 
of gene expression as related to dose–response relationships, 
molecular mechanisms, and biomarkers of human exposure. 
A systems biology approach integrating gene expression pro-
filing with toxicological effects of individual chemicals and 
chemical mixtures is now mainstream in the toxicological sci-
ences, and is now more frequently employed to address MoA, 
human relevance, and dose responses.
26.6.2.1  TOX21
In 2008, the EPA, NTP NIEHS, National Center for 
Advancing Translational Sciences, and the FDA formed the 
Tox21 Consortium with the goal of developing HTA for hazard 
identification and to providing MoA insights [126]. After more 
than a decade, the programs have matured, initial assays used 
have been refined to evaluate the safety of commercial chemi-
cals, pesticides, food additives and contaminants, and medical 
products rapidly and efficiently. Tox21 has developed a new 
strategic and operational plan that expands the focus of its 
research activities to include, (1) identification of mechanisms 
of chemically induced biological activity; (2) prioritization of 
chemicals for more extensive testing; and (3) development of 
more relevant and predictive models of in vivo toxicological 
responses to reduce, refine, and replace certain animal models.
The Tox21 new areas of focus expand the development of 
human-relevant alternative test systems that are predictive of 
human toxicity, address technical limitations HTA test sys-
tems, curate animal (in vivo) toxicity testing data to serve as 
a reference for new test systems and endpoints, develop confi-
dence for the use in vitro test systems in toxicology decision-
making, and refine alternative methods for characterizing 
pharmacokinetics and disposition in in vitro assays which are 
critical determinants of in vivo toxicity.

26.6.2.2  TOX 21 – Adverse Outcome 
Pathways (AOP) and Threshold of 
Toxicological Concern (TTC)
Evaluation of genotoxic potential and biological testing are 
critical components of chemical risk assessments, since dam-
age to the genetic material leading to mutations, chromosome 
damage, or genetic instability can result in a broad spectrum 
of hereditary diseases and cancer. Therefore, genotoxicity, 
mutations, and specific epigenetic changes are being recog-
nized as key toxicological endpoints and regarded as adverse 
key events. Thousands of new industrial chemicals, drug dis-
covery drug candidates or impurities, and novel food addi-
tives introduced each year have limited toxicology safety 
assessment data. The need to assure safe levels for thousands 
of data-poor compounds requires more efficient and effective 
ways to evaluate the large backlog, including the use of HTA, 
novel NAM, and computational tools to prioritize compounds 
for further testing and identifying levels of exposure where 
potential genotoxicity in humans is negligible [109, 122]. As 
indicated above, although traditional genetic toxicology end-
points and toxicogenomics data are used to identify BMD and 
PoD for use in risk assessments; these are too cumbersome for 
high-throughput assessments of the thousands of test articles. 
Computational tools have been developed to construct frame-
work MoA linkages of initiating events to adverse outcomes, 
calculations of levels of exposure where human risk is negli-
gible, and online predictive tools of potential carcinogenicity 
to assist in decision-making.
In 2012, the OECD initiated the development of AOP as a 
knowledge base of models used to describe a sequential chain 
of causally linked molecular and cellular events that lead to an 
adverse health outcome (see https://www.oecd.org/chemical-
safety/testing/adverse-outcome-pathways-molecular-screen-
ing-and-toxicogenomics.htm). AOPs are becoming a central 
organizing element of a framework built to support chemical 
risk assessment based on current mechanistic understandings. 
The framework for the construction of an AOP for genetic tox-
icology links the identification of molecular-initiating events 
(e.g., DNA adducts) to key events (e.g., misrepair, DNA strand 
breaks) and to an adverse outcome (chromosomal aberration or 
mutation) [127]. Causally linked events require dose–response 
assessment to identify the shape of dose–response curves in 
biological assays, BMD, and PoD calculations. An AOP enti-
tled “alkylation of DNA in male premeiotic germ cells leading 
to heritable mutations” has been developed [128]. AOPs are 
living documents that will evolve over time as new knowledge 
emerges, using the AOP wiki (https://aopwiki.org/), allowing 
the scientific community to contribute to each AOP.
The TTC is a level of human intake or exposure that is of 
negligible risk. TTC was originally developed for food con-
tact-related materials with little or no toxicological data and 
is being used for an initial assessment and prioritization tool 
to decide whether a comprehensive risk assessment or addi-
tional toxicology testing is required [21, 84, 129, 130]. Based 
upon literature reviews, examination of extensive toxicology 
and cancer bioassay databases, a TTC level of 1.5 ōg/day 
for non-genotoxic compounds was established, and for com-
pounds demonstrating structural alerts for genotoxicity by 
QSAR or that are genotoxic at TTC levels tenfold lower, 0.15 
ōg/day has been established. A classification scheme, Cramer 
Classification, has been developed to initiate the estimation of 
a TTC and several in silico tools are now available to assist 
toxicologists in estimating a TTC for use by a toxicologist to 
calculate a MoE for use in safety assessments [130].
Drug synthesis uses reactive intermediates, solvents, and cata-
lysts that can end up in the final drug product as drug impurities. 
Certain intermediates that exceed levels of >1.5% with little or 
no toxicological data require qualification prior to marketing. 
The ICH M7 (R1) guidance M7(R1) Assessment and Control of 
DNA-Reactive (Mutagenic) Impurities in Pharmaceuticals To 
Limit Potential Carcinogenic Risk outlines the steps in qualify-
ing a drug impurity by acquiring and evaluating safety assess-
ment data and calculating a TTC [85]. Impurities with little or 
no genotoxicity data are initially assessed for genotoxic potential 
using QSAR to identify any potential genotoxic structural alerts. 
This document outlines a testing scheme using computational 
tools, in vitro and in vivo follow-ups for genotoxicity assessments 
of drug impurities, and outlines the steps using case studies for 
the calculation of a TTC.
26.7  Epigenetic Mechanisms
Epigenetic mechanisms can regulate gene expression by control-
ling the dynamic structure of the chromatin, either by triggering 
its condensation to transcriptionally inactive heterochromatin 
or by its decondensation to active euchromatin. The most stud-
ied epigenetic mechanisms are methylation of cytosine bases of 
DNA, histone PTMs, and ncRNAs. These epigenetic marks do 
not act alone; they are multilayered, interconnected, and may 
occur concurrently in the same genomic region [131]. The epi-
genetic modifications can be permanent or transient.
26.7.1  DNA Methylation
In mammalian cells, the process of DNA methylation adds a 
methyl group to the 5-carbon position of cytosine that precedes 
guanine, a dinucleotide sequence called a CpG site, resulting 
in the formation of 5-methylcytosine (5-meC). The distribu-
tion of CpG sites across the human genome is not even, as they 
tend to cluster in the regions of large repetitive sequences and 
in CpG islands, stretches of DNA which are typically located 
within the promoter region of genes. While 60–80% of CpG 
sites are methylated, the majority of promoter-associated 
CpG islands tend to remain unmethylated during embryonic 
development and in differentiated tissues. In contrast, other 
DNA regions rich in CpG sites, such as repetitive genomic 
sequences and intergenic regions that are distributed through-
out the genome, are heavily methylated [132].
DNA methylation plays a crucial role in many processes, 
including transcriptional regulation of gene expression as well 
as genomic imprinting, an epigenetic process that results in 
monoallelic expression of genes in offspring depending on the 
parental origin. Effects of DNA methylation on gene expression 
may differ, depending on the genomic region where it occurs. 
Thus, DNA methylation at gene promoters, enhancers, or both, 
usually exhibits silencing effects on gene transcription. In this

case, DNA methylation can either prevent the recruitment of 
regulatory proteins to DNA, such as transcription factors or 
attract other proteins, such as methyl-binding domain proteins, 
that mediate gene repression [133]. It is important to recognize 
that cytosine methylation in CpG islands frequently occurs 
close to gene transcription start sites or at proximal upstream 
or downstream positions. In contrast, DNA methylation within 
the bodies of genes has either no correlation to transcription 
status or can increase gene expression, possibly through facili-
tation of the transcriptional elongation process [134].
DNA methylation is mediated by a family of DNA meth-
yltransferases (DNMTs) that transfer a methyl group from 
S-adenyl methionine to DNA. This group of enzymes includes 
two de novo DNMTs, DNMT3A and DNMT3B, that have 
equal preference for unmethylated and hemimethylated DNA, 
and maintenance DNMT, DNMT1, which commonly binds to 
hemimethylated DNA and assures that the DNA methylation 
pattern is maintained and inherited after cell replication [135]. 
Demethylation occurs passively via methylcytosine dilution 
during replication or actively via oxidation catalyzed by ten-
eleven translocation (TET) 1–3 enzymes followed by base 
excision DNA repair [135].
26.7.2  Histone PTMs
Histones are proteins that are rich in positively charged basic 
amino acids and thus have a high affinity for the negative charge 
of the DNA. In eukaryotes, genomic DNA is wrapped around a 
histone octamer to form a nucleosome, a basic structural unit 
of chromatin, which in turn condenses to form more compact 
structures. Each nucleosome contains dimers of the core his-
tones 2A (H2A), 2B (H2B), along with dimers of histone 3 (H3) 
and histone 4 (H4). Histones contain unstructured N-terminal 
tails which protrude out from the core of nucleosomes and are 
subjected to various PTMs. Histone PTMs, such as methylation, 
acetylation, ubiquitylation, sumoylation, and phosphorylation, 
arise from covalent enzymatic addition of respective groups 
(i.e., methyl, acetyl, ubiquitin, small ubiquitin-like modifier, and 
phosphate groups) to residues on target histones and can control 
the local accessibility of chromatin structure and regulate gene 
transcription, DNA repair, and replication.
Histone PTMs can lead to either activation or repression of 
gene transcription, depending upon which amino acid residues 
are modified and the type of modifications present. For exam-
ple, histone acetylation correlates with transcriptional activa-
tion. This modification alters the charge of the lysine residues 
and has the potential to weaken the interactions between his-
tones and DNA, leading to a locally relaxed state of the chro-
matin which may contribute to activation of gene transcription 
(Figure 26.12). This epigenetic mechanism is catalyzed by 
enzymes known as histone acetyltransferases (HAT) and his-
tone deacetylases (HDACs). In contrast, histone methylation 
does not influence the protein charge; instead, it either inter-
feres with the recruitment of binding proteins or constitutes a 
docking site for other epigenetic factors that can contribute to 
either gene activation or repression depending on which amino 
acid is methylated and the degree of methylation [136]. For 
example, nucleosomes that flank active, unmethylated gene 
promoters, are marked by trimethylation on histone h3 lysine 
4 residue (H3K4me3) and histone acetylation that facilitate 
transcriptional initiation. In contrast, the histone h3 lysine 27 
trimethylation (H3K27me3) is associated with condensed, inac-
tive chromatin and repression of gene expression [137] (Figure 
26.12). This histone mark, when associated with Polycomb 
Repressive Complexes, can extend over many nucleosomes and 
is indicative of heterochromatin and repressed genes [138]. The 
methylation of histones is catalyzed by a group of proteins with 
methyltransferase or demethylase activity.
26.7.3  Non-coding RNAs
Once regarded as “transcriptional noise”, it is now apparent 
that ncRNAs molecules also contribute to the regulation of 
gene expression [139]. For example, lncRNAs show strong 
tissue specificity that can impact genome regulation from 
epigenetic modifications and chromatin remodeling to tran-
scriptional and post-transcriptional control of gene expression 
[140]. To date, few lncRNAs have been functionally character-
ized (especially in rodents), but those that have been charac-
terized are emerging as regulators of key cellular processes 
known to be important in the cellular response to xenobiotic 
exposures and include p53-mediated DDR, cell cycle regula-
tion, and cell proliferation, apoptosis, maintenance of DNA 
methylation/demethylation, and stem cell pluripotency and 
differentiation [141]. Generally, lncRNAs are not capable of 
encoding proteins or peptides.
miRNAs provide a critical link for the rapid responses of 
cells to environmental stimuli. They have particularly impor-
tant roles in regulating stem cell “self-renewal” and in many 
key biological processes (e.g., cell growth, cell proliferation, 
and apoptosis) [142]. miRNAs repress gene expression by 
base-pairing to the 3’ untranslated portion of a target mRNA 
or multiple target mRNAs (Figure 26.12). Since one miRNA 
can bind to multiple discrete sequences, miRNAs can control 
networks of genes and pathways that produce an integrated 
phenotypic response to environmental stimuli. In addition, 
miRNAs can target enzymes responsible for DNA methyla-
tion and histone modifications. piRNAs and esiRNAs bind to 
complementary transposable elements and induce their degra-
dation and are important for genome stability [143].
Like normal genes, the expression of ncRNAs can be regulated 
by epigenetic mechanisms, such as DNA methylation and histone 
PTMs. The interaction among the epigenetic marks re-empha-
sizes the integrated nature of epigenetic mechanisms involved in 
the maintenance of global gene expression patterns [144].
26.7.4  Enzymatic Control of Epigenetic Mechanisms
The epigenome function is controlled by three categories of 
proteins, epigenetic “writers”, “readers”, and “erasers” [145].
26.7.4.1  Epigenetic Writers
A group of enzymes capable of modifying nucleotide bases 
and specific amino acid residues on histones, by introducing 
epigenetic marks in response to the environment, e.g., DNMTs, 
HATs, and histone lysine methyltransferases, which introduce 
a covalent chemical modification to DNA or histones.

26.7.4.2  Epigenetic Readers
Diverse range of proteins that possess specialized domains 
capable of recognizing and interpreting specific epigenetic 
marks in the DNA and histones. For example, methyl-CpG

CpGs and bind to them to trigger transcriptional repression,

26.8  Epigenetics and Toxicology
Epigenetic control of gene expression is emerging as an impor-
tant mechanism of relevance to toxicology, due to the fact that 
epigenetic aberrations may occur in response to exposures to 
environmental factors, and are associated with human dis-
eases such as cancer, neurodevelopmental disorders, cardio-
vascular diseases, type-2 diabetes, obesity, and reproductive 
toxicity [146–150].
The association of the epigenome with adverse effects hap-
pens in two ways: (1) environmental factors can induce epi-
genetic alterations that will lead, drive, and maintain adverse 
effects; and/or (2) the individual’s epigenome can influence the 
response to environmental factors and alter the predisposition 
to adverse effects and diseases.
Diet, alcohol intake, smoking, chemicals, and stress are 
examples of environmental factors that are capable of induc-
ing adverse effects by altering the epigenome [151, 152]. 
Environmental factors may alter the epigenome not only dur-
ing the period of exposure or during pregnancy, but throughout 
life from fetal development to aging [153, 154].
Epigenetic regulation is one of the key tenets of developmen-
tal origins of health and disease (DOHaD), a hypothesis that 
early life exposures can have long-lasting health impacts into 
adulthood [155, 156]. For example, during a certain life period, 
from early embryogenesis to early life, significant alterations 
in DNA methylation patterns occur. Environmental exposures 
during this period can potentially reprogram the epigenome 
and alter susceptibility to diseases [157].
Environmental epigenetics is the study of how environmen-
tal exposures, with particular interest in environmental toxic 
chemicals, may interact with genetic and epigenetic mecha-
nisms [158]. Due to the significance of epigenetics in human 
health and disease, the National Institutes of Health (NIH) 
Roadmap Epigenomics Program and a number of worldwide 
initiatives such as the Human Epigenome Project (HEP) and 
the International Human Epigenome Consortium (IHEC), are 
focused on identifying, cataloging, and interpreting genome-
wide DNA methylation patterns of all human genes in all major 
tissues (http://www.roadmapepigenomics.org/overview).
26.9  Epigenetics and Cancer
The concept of mutagenesis as the principal mechanism of car-
cinogenesis has been modified by the knowledge that epigene-
tic mechanisms may persist through multiple cell divisions and 
offer an alternative path to carcinogenesis [159]. Currently, it 
is known that the occurrence of cancer is dependent on the 
interplay between the genome and the epigenome. In fact, epi-
genetic alterations have been identified in all stages of carcino-
genesis and may be much more frequent than genetic changes 
in human cancer cells [160]. As such, epigenetic alterations 
constitute an exciting area in basic cancer biology with great 
potential for clinical translation [146].
The epigenome of cancer cells is characterized by changes 
in chromatin composition and organization caused by abnor-
mal patterns of DNA methylation and histone PTMs, altered 
expression profiles of chromatin-modifying enzymes, such as 
epigenetic readers, writers, and erasers, and dysregulation of 
ncRNAs expression [146]. The misregulation of these epigen-
etic mechanisms results in altered gene expression profiles, 
mainly the up-regulation of oncogenes and down-regulation of 
tumor suppressor genes.
Aberrant changes in DNA methylation patterns, such as 
global genomic DNA hypomethylation and gene-specific 
hypermethylation of critical genes, are the best-characterized 
epigenetic modifications observed in many cancers. Genomic 
DNA hypomethylation frequently occurs in repetitive ele-
ments, such as long interspersed nucleotide elements (LINE)-1 
and short interspersed nucleotide elements (SINE), retroviral 
intracesternal A particles (IAP), and transposable Alu ele-
ments located at centromeric, pericentromeric, and subtelo-
meric chromosomal regions. As a consequence, transposable 
elements may be reactivated and integrated at random sites in 
the genome, leading to mutagenesis and genomic instability. 
Global DNA hypomethylation may also cause significant ele-
vation in mutation rates [161], activation of normally silenced 
oncogenes [162], and loss of imprinting [163]. Aberrant hyper-
methylation of CpG islands associated with silencing of tumor 
suppressor genes is another significant epigenetic pathway that 
contributes to carcinogenesis. Approximately 60% of all gene 
promoters contain CpG islands, most of which, as previously 
mentioned, are not methylated at any time in normal develop-
ment to maintain an open chromatin state that is ready to be 
transcribed [134, 146, 160]. However, in tumors, hundreds of 
genes can be silenced by promoter hypermethylation [134] and 
loss of their function is recognized as a “driver function” for 
carcinogenesis [146]. Such critical genes include tumor sup-
pressor genes that are involved in the control of cell prolifera-
tion, such as p16 [164] and adenomatous polyposis coli (APC) 
[165], apoptosis, such as caspase-8 (CASP8) [166], or DNA 
repair, such as breast cancer gene 1 (BRCA1) [167].
Dysregulation of histone PTMs have the potential to directly 
influence nucleosome dynamics and stability and may shift the 
balance of gene expression leading to alterations in critical cel-
lular processes such as transcription, proliferation, apoptosis, 
and DNA repair, ultimately contributing not only to tumor 
progression but also to tumor initiation [136, 168]. The pat-
tern of histone PTMs is disturbed in cancer, both at the global 
level across the genome [169, 170] and at specific gene loci. 
For example, global absence of histone H4K16 acetylation and 
H4K20 trimethylation is a common distinguishing character-
istic of human cancer [171]. At specific gene loci, hypermeth-
ylation of promoter-associated CpG islands in cancer cells is 
associated with a particular pattern of histone marks, includ-
ing deacetylation of histones H3 and H4, loss of histone H3 
lysine trimethylation, and gaining of H3K9 methylation and 
H3K27 trimethylation [172].
Changes in the expression of ncRNAs, such as lncRNAs 
and miRNAs, are also detected in many human cancers and 
are often associated with exposure to environmental chemi-
cals and carcinogens. LncRNAs impact biological processes 
that are considered cancer hallmarks (e.g., apoptosis, cell 
proliferation) [173] and have been revealed to play an impor-
tant role in tumor growth and metastasis [174]. Inhibition of 
tumor suppressor genes by aberrant expression of miRNAs

has been associated with most aspects of tumor biology, 
including tumor progression, invasiveness, metastasis, and 
malignant cells acquiring resistance to various chemothera-
peutic agents [175].
Another important feature of the cancer epigenome is its 
direct interaction with the genome. It has been shown that 
mutations in genes that encode for components of the epigenetic 
machinery may contribute to abnormalities in the epigenome, 
which affect gene expression patterns and induce genomic 
stability [134, 146, 176]. For example, mutations in enzymes 
that control DNA methylation or histone PTMs are frequently 
mutated in cancers [134, 177]. Conversely, epigenetic silencing 
or activation of genes may predispose cells to further muta-
tions [146]. For example, 5-meC is a pro-mutagenic site, since 
methylation of the 5 position of the cytosine ring increases the 
rate of hydrolytic deamination of the base in double-stranded 
DNA. The deamination product of 5-meC, however, is thy-
mine rather than uracil, as is the case for unmethylated cyto-
sine. DNA repair mechanisms are subsequently less efficient 
at repairing deamination-induced mismatches in DNA [146].
26.9.1  Translational Advances
The characterization of cancer epigenome is important for the 
development of new tools for early cancer detection, diagnosis, 
prevention, and therapy. The recognition that epigenetic changes 
may occur early in carcinogenesis has inspired the development 
of epigenetic cancer biomarkers. For example, several commer-
cial tests for methylation-based biomarkers are approved for 
clinical use [178]. These DNA methylation biomarkers can be 
used as specific markers of cancer risk, molecular prognosis, or 
as predictors of response to chemotherapy [146].
The established function of writer, reader, and eraser 
enzymes indicates the reversibility of epigenetic mechanisms 
and created an opportunity for developing epigenetic drugs 
that reprogram neoplastic cells toward a normal state [179]. 
Many epigenetic readers, writers, and erasers are recognized 
as druggable targets and several inhibitors of DNA demeth-
ylation and histone deacetylation have been approved for 
clinical use, particularly for the treatment of hematological 
cancers [180]. As the field of application of these epigenetic 
drugs increases, it is also necessary to improve the knowledge 
related to their safety.
Another promising area in development is the applica-
tion of epigenetics to regulatory science for chemical safety 
assessments. It is important to note that alterations in epi-
genetic mechanisms alone do not predict adverse effects, 
but disruption of the epigenetic balance could be used for 
screening assessments to prioritize chemicals for addi-
tional testing [181] and for determining the toxicant MoA. 
Accumulating evidence suggests that regardless of their 
MoA, both genotoxic and non-genotoxic carcinogens may 
produce prominent epigenetic abnormalities, and this infor-
mation can be useful for more informed risk assessment and 
better characterization and classification of carcinogens. 
Epigenetic endpoints are being increasingly used in cancer 
hazard assessments; for example, “epigenetic alterations” 
have recently been included as one of ten key characteristics 
of human carcinogens [182], and the IARC Monographs 
Program incorporates a formal search for studies on epigen-
etic effects in all evaluations [183].
26.10  Methods for Analysis of 
Epigenetic Modifications
Multiple techniques are currently employed for quantita-
tive and qualitative assessment of epigenetic modifications. 
They utilize various methodology, including array- and 
sequencing-based platforms, enzymatic cleavage or chemi-
cal treatment, immune-based assays, high-performance 
separation techniques, and imaging tools. The selection of 
the most appropriate method should be based on the par-
ticular research question being addressed and the available 
resources. Various bioinformatics techniques and computa-
tional pipelines which can be applied to data analyses are 
well described in available literature [184–191]. As the field 
of epigenetics is rapidly progressing, approaches that inves-
tigate the epigenetic landscape and its role in biological 
functions are evolving at an exponential rate.
26.10.1  DNA Methylation Assessment
Several experimental techniques described below can be 
applied to analyze the occurrence and location of DNA meth-
ylation throughout the whole genome or in specific loci. Some 
utilize bisulfite conversion and others are based on affinity 
enrichment or differential enzymatic cleavage with restriction 
enzymes. The combination of these techniques can signifi-
cantly improve sensitivity and specificity of the assays. While 
methods described below are widely used for the evaluation of 
nuclear DNA, many of these techniques can be also applied 
to the analyses of mitochondrial DNA [192] or cell-free DNA 
obtained from liquid biopsy samples [193].
26.10.1.1  Restriction Enzyme Cleavage-based Methods
Enzyme cleavage methods utilize differential digestion prop-
erties of various methylation-sensitive restriction enzymes 
(MSRE) derived from bacteria, for example, HpaII (from 
Haemophilus parain uenzae ), SmaI (from Serratia marces-
cens), and their respective isoschizomers (endonucleases that 
recognize the same sequence but have different sensitivity to 
methylated cytosine), MspI (from Moraxella sp.), and XmaI 
(from Xanthomonas malvacearum) [194]. These enzymes can 
recognize and cleave specific DNA sequences, allowing for 
enrichment with hypo- or hypermethylated DNA segments 
that can then be evaluated using different approaches, includ-
ing MSRE and multiplexed polymerase chain reaction (PCR) 
with gene-specific primers (MSRE-PCR), NGS, or conven-
tional electrophoresis (Southern Blot) [195–197].
26.10.1.2  Affinity Enrichment-based Methods
Among commonly used affinity enrichment methods are 
assays that are based on the use of either antibodies against

5-meC (e.g., methyl-DNA immunoprecipitation [MeDIP] 
assay) or proteins with methyl-CpG binding domain (MBD) 
to enrich methylated DNA regions [198]. In the MeDIP assay, 
immunoprecipitated enriched DNA fractions can be evaluated 
using array hybridization (MeDIP-chip) or high-throughput 
NGS (MeDIP-seq). While such methods are cost-efficient and 
do not require a high concentration of starting DNA, they have 
relatively low resolution (100–300 base pairs) and are biased 
toward the detection of CpG-rich fragments, which are more 
likely to be enriched compared to CpG-poor regions [199, 
200]. Methyl-capture sequencing (MethylCap-seq), which 
utilizes MBD proteins, offers better coverage of differen-
tially methylated regions compared to MeDIP-seq [201]. The 
Methyl-CpG island recovery assay (MIRA), which also uses 
MBD proteins (MBD2b/MBD3L1), has high sensitivity and 
can detect low-density methylated DNA at a single-nucleotide 
resolution [202].
26.10.1.3  Bisulfite Conversion-based Methods
One of the most widely utilized techniques for the analysis 
of DNA methylome that detects methylcytosine at a single-
nucleotide resolution is bisulfite sequencing [203]. This 
technique is based on the principle of deamination of non-
methylated cytosine by sodium bisulfite treatment, which 
results in conversion of cytosine to uracil and, following PCR 
amplification, to thymine (Figure 26.13). In contrast, under 
these conditions, methylated and hydroxymethylated cytosine 
residues (5-meC and 5-hmeC) remain unmodified and con-
sequently amplify as cytosine (Figure 26.13). Various “non-
selective” and “selective” downstream analyses can be applied 
to bisulfite-converted PCR-amplified fragments to detect and 
quantify individual methylation sites and methylation patterns 
in a particular genomic region (gene-specific methylation) or 
genome-wide (global DNA methylation).
26.10.1.4  Gene-Specific Methylation Profiling
Post-bisulfite conversion techniques, which focus on locus- or 
gene-specific methylation, include direct PCR, Droplet Digital 
PCR (ddPCR), and pyrosequencing analyses [203, 204]. Such 
non-selective methods can be enhanced by specific primers 
that recognize methylated and unmethylated DNA templates. 
This approach is used in many techniques, including meth-
ylation-specific PCR (MSP) and quantitative real-time PCR 
(qMS-PCR), and methylation-enrichment pyrosequencing 
(MEP) [203, 205]. In fact, MSP is one of the most frequently 
used approaches for the analyses of gene-specific methylation 
status [206]. A valuable technique for the detection of low-fre-
quency methylated DNA regions is a fluorescence-based real-
time PCR method, MethyLight, which has higher specificity 
compared to MSP [207].
A highly promising quantitative method for analyses of 
gene-specific methylation is methylation-sensitive single-
nucleotide primer extension (SNuPE), which can be conducted 
in combination with high-performance liquid chromatogra-
phy (SNuPE-IP RP HPLC), ion-paired reverse phase HPLC 
(SIRPH), or mass spectrometry (SNuPE/MS) [208, 209]. The 
Combined Bisulfite Restriction Analysis (COBRA) incorpo-
rates bisulfite sequencing with subsequent digestion of DNA 
with methylation-specific restriction enzymes. The percentage 
of the digested fragments can be assessed with PCR or elec-
trophoresis [210, 211].

26.10.1.5  Global DNA Methylation Profiling
Currently, the most comprehensive high-throughput assess-
ment of global DNA methylation can be achieved with the 
whole-genome bisulfite sequencing (WGNS), which can eval-
uate the methylation status of either CpG-rich or low-density 
CpG regions [212]. While data produced by WGNS is complex 
and requires sophisticated bioinformatics analyses, this tool is 
a standard method used by major epigenome consortia, such 
as NIH Roadmap Epigenomics Mapping and Encyclopedia 
of DNA Elements (ENCODE) Consortium [213, 214]. The 
reduced-representation bisulfite sequencing (RRBS) offers a 
more targeted approach. RRBS combines bisulfite conversion, 
Msp1 restriction enzyme digestion, and NGS methodology 
to assess the methylation status of the majority of CpG-rich 
sites, which are mainly present in the promoter regions [215]. 
Reduction in the number of sequences allows profiling larger-
scale samples, as in the case with epigenome-wide associa-
tion studies (EWAS) [216]. Another common approach used 
in EWAS studies is hybridization-based methylation arrays, 
such as Illumina Methylation450 (450K) [217]. This assay uses 
predesigned probes to examine over 450,000 methylation sites 
covering most CpG islands and surrounding sequences of the 
promoter regions and less dense CpG regions [218]. A more 
advanced version of this array is MethylationEPIC BeadChip 
array which covers over 400,000 additional CpG sites (over 
850,000 sites in total), significantly improving the coverage of 
regulatory regions [219].
Some of the commercially available kits for assessment of 
global DNA methylation (e.g., Epigentek, Abcam) are based 
on enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) principle 
using labeled antibodies that target methylated cytosines (anti-
5meC or anti-5-hmeC). The fraction of DNA methylation is 
then assessed colorimetrically with a spectrophotometer.
26.10.1.6  Single-Cell Methylome Analysis
Single-cell sequencing was developed to assess heterogene-
ity of methylation patterns across different individual cells 
[220]. This method modified adapter ligation until after bisul-
fite treatment (post-bisulfite adapter tagging, PBAT) which 
reduces the loss of sequencing templates, allowing for a much 
smaller input of genomic material. Thus, such analysis does 
not require a large amount of input material. In combination 
with other “-omics” approaches, this tool can provide impor-
tant input into the biological functions and link between epi-
genetic modifications and other processes on a single-cell 
level. Both WGNS and RRBS methods have been adopted for 
single-cell bisulfite sequencing [220–222].
26.10.1.7  Detection of Oxidized 5-meC
While traditional bisulfite conversion does not distinguish 5-meC 
and 5-hmeC, it can be enhanced either by chemical oxidation 
or by the addition of TET proteins, which catalyze oxidation of 
5-meC to 5-hmeC. Such an approach was adopted by oxidative 
(OxBS-seq) and Tet-assisted (TAB-Seq) bisulfite sequencing 
methodologies. Both methods allow for targeted detection of 
5-hmeC on gene-, loci-specific, or genome-wide levels [223, 224].
26.10.1.8  Additional Methods for Assessment 
of DNA Methylation
Several other methods are available for assessing DNA 
methylation status. Separation-based methods (e.g., high-
performance capillary electrophoresis [HPCE] or HPLC) in 
combination with chemical hydrolysis or enzymatic digestion 
were among the earliest techniques applied for quantifica-
tion of 5-emC or 5-hmeC content [171, 225]. The sensitiv-
ity of HPLC and HPCE can be further improved with mass 
spectrometry [226]. Enzymatic incorporation of radioactively 
labeled methyl groups, termed radiolabeling, in combination 
with two-dimensional thin-layer chromatography (TLC) was 
also among pioneering methods for quantitative measurement 
of DNA methylation [227]. This method was enhanced by 
the addition of MSREs and adopted by restriction landmark 
genomic scanning (RLGS) [228]. Radiolabeling can be also 
used in the indirect approach based on the incorporation of 
radiolabeled methyl groups from S-[3H-methyl] adenosyl-
methionine by unmethylated DNA regions with the help of 
bacterial SssI methyltransferase from Spiroplasma sp. strain 
MQ1 (methyl-acceptance assay) [229]. In this assay, the level 
of DNA methylation in the sample is inversely proportional to 
the detected radioactivity levels.
In addition to analyzing DNA methylation status directly, 
some assays also focus on the assessment of activity of 
enzymes, DNA methyltransferases (DNMT1, DNMT3A, and 
DNMT3B), which catalyze DNA methylation. Such assays are 
based on isotope labeling, fluorescent, electrochemical, colori-
metric, and chemiluminescent techniques [230].
26.10.1.9  Emerging Approaches for 
Assessment of DNA Methylation
The advancement in sequencing technologies and the emer-
gence of third-generation single-molecule sequencing allow 
for direct detection of DNA methylation without the need 
for bisulfite conversion. While such tools, including sin-
gle-molecule real-time (SMRT) sequencing from Pacific 
Biosciences and nanopore sequencing from Oxford Nanopore 
Technologies, have certain limitations, such as availability, 
cost, and lower throughput compared to the second-generation 
sequencing methods, they can certainly contribute to the dis-
covery of novel epigenetic markers and enhance understand-
ing the role of these markers in regulating the genome [231].
26.10.2  Assessment of Histone Modifications
Post-translational histone modifications can be analyzed using 
various techniques. Among the most widely used methods are 
mass spectrometry-based and chromatin immunoprecipitation 
(ChIP) assays [232, 233].
26.10.2.1  Mass Spectrometry-based Methods
Mass spectrometry is currently a technique of choice for 
assessing global levels of histone modifications. The ability of 
high-resolution mass spectrometry methods (e.g., tandem mass 
spectrometry (MS/MS) to measure up to 200 modifications

in a single analysis gives this technique a big advantage 
considering that many histones can undergo multiple modi-
fications simultaneously [234]. Three proteomic approaches, 
Bottom-Up, Middle-Down, and Top-Down, can be adopted 
for mass spectrometry analysis of histone modifications [235, 
236]. The Bottom-Up and Middle-Down approaches involve 
proteolytic digestion of proteins prior to analysis by mass spec-
trometry to small- or medium-size fragment peptides, respec-
tively. The Top-Down approach does not utilize proteases and 
therefore allows for the characterization of an intact protein.
26.10.2.2  Chromatin Immunoprecipitation Assays
ChiP-based assays can recognize locus-specific histone modi-
fications, thus allowing for evaluation of nucleosome structure 
and interaction between histone modifications and specific 
genomic regions. Conventionally, ChiP involves chemical cross-
linking of proteins to DNA (e.g., using formaldehyde treatment); 
however, since histones are tightly bound to DNA, this step can 
sometimes be avoided. The next steps include lysis of chro-
matin by mechanical shearing (sonication) or treatment with 
nuclease, and immunoprecipitation of histone–DNA complexes 
using specific antibodies to histone molecules or transcription 
factors of interest. Analyses of enriched complexes can be con-
ducted with quantitative PCR (qPCR) for known target loci, 
microarray hybridization (ChIP-on-chip), or NGS (ChIP-seq) 
for genome-wide mapping of histone modifications [237, 238]. 
The ENCODE and modENCODE consortia have developed a 
number of valuable guidelines for ChIP-seq methodology and 
data analyses [239]. Several improvements to the ChIP protocol 
have been developed over the years. For example, to minimize 
non-specific binding, ChIP can be combined with lambda exo-
nuclease digestion (ChIP-exo) [240]. Sequential ChIP (re-ChIP) 
offers an evaluation of multiple chromatin modifications, as it 
incorporates multiple antibodies or ChIP reactions conducted 
back-to-back that can simultaneously identify several modifica-
tions on a single DNA sequence [241].
Integrative analysis of DNA methylation status at genomic 
regions with histone modification ChIP-bisulfite methylation 
sequencing (ChIP-BMS, BisChIP-seq) combines ChIP meth-
odology with bisulfite sequencing to evaluate dynamic inter-
action between chromatin and DNA methylation [242, 243]. 
This technique allows for assessing DNA methylation status 
of immunoprecipitated histone–DNA complexes at locus-spe-
cific (ChIP-BMS) or global (BisChIP-seq) levels.
26.10.2.3  Additional Methods for Assessment 
of Histone Modifications
Early methods for the analysis of histone modification sta-
tus were based on the incorporation of radiolabeled precur-
sors (e.g., methionine-methyl-C14) and protein digests [244]. 
Later, a panoply of monoclonal antibodies was developed for 
the assessment of specific histone modifications using Western 
blot, immunohistochemistry, and ELISA-based methods [245]. 
Antibody-based methods not only provide information regard-
ing the presence of certain histone modifications that are being 
investigated but also allow for semi-quantitative assessment 
of data. Such methods are very sensitive but are limited with 
respect to the number of modifications that can be assessed 
at once. In addition, the possibility of cross-reactivity of anti-
bodies with other modifications should be taken into account 
[246]. Modifications of histones can be also evaluated with the 
help of fluorometric or colorimetric assays. In addition, assays 
that measure the activity of enzymes (writers and erasers) that 
are responsible for the addition or removal of histone modifica-
tions can also provide valuable data [247].
Some of the novel techniques include Epigenetic land-
scape profiling using cytometry by Time-Of-Flight (EpiTOF), 
which utilizes multiplexed mass cytometry for analyses of his-
tone modification landscapes at a single-cell resolution [248]. 
Another recent advancement in the field was the development 
of an immunoprecipitation-free method, Chromatin Integration 
Labeling (ChIL) for the investigation of genome-wide epigenetic 
profiles from small numbers of cells. This method utilizes two 
antibodies: (1) a primary antibody targeting areas of interest and 
(2) a secondary antibody (or ChIL probe) that recognizes and 
conjugates with the primary antibody and allows for carrying out 
a tagmentation-like library preparation reaction [249].
26.10.3  Methods for Chromatin Accessibility 
and Structure Assessment
26.10.3.1  Methods for Chromatin 
Accessibility Profiling
Methods that assess structural modification of chromatin focus 
on the physical accessibility of chromatin using enzymatic or 
chemical means for the enrichment of either accessible or inac-
cessible genomic regions followed by NGS [188]. Most of these 
techniques incorporate digestion with nucleases to generate 
DNA fragments from nuclease-accessible genomic regions, since 
tightly packaged chromatin will be inaccessible to nucleases, 
and thus resistant to digestion. Such principles were adopted by 
several methods for genome-wide mapping of nucleosome posi-
tions and occupancies, including micrococcal nuclease digestion 
with deep sequencing (MNase-seq), DNase I hypersensitive sites 
sequencing (DNase-seq), and single-cell micrococcal nuclease 
sequencing (scMNase-seq) [140, 250, 251]. In addition, restric-
tion enzymes that digest DNA at specific sequences can also be 
used, as in the restriction endonuclease digestion of chromatin 
coupled to deep sequencing (RED-seq) method [252].
Formaldehyde-Assisted Isolation of Regulatory Elements 
(FAIRE-seq) and Assay for Transposase Accessible Chromatin 
(ATAC-seq) are also widely used methods for the assess-
ment of chromatin profiles [253]. FAIRE-seq does not utilize 
enzymes and antibodies, but rather uses phenol-chloroform 
extraction of cross-linked chromatin, followed by immediate 
assessment of the enrichment of regulatory regions in the aque-
ous phase. The ATAC-seq uses bacterial transposase, which 
inserts sequencing adapters that act as tags, into accessible 
(open) chromatin. ATAC-seq does not require a high quantity 
of input material and therefore can be used for smaller cell 
populations. A novel tagmentation-based alternative to ATAC-
seq is the cleavage under targets and tagmentation (CUT&Tag) 
method, which can also identify nucleosome-depleted regions 
at regulatory elements that are contiguous with transcription-
associated histone modifications [254].

Another major scientific breakthrough was the development of 
the “clusters of regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats”

26.10.3.2  Methods for Assessment of Three-
Dimensional (3D) Chromatin Structure
Recent technological advances resulted in the development of 
microscopy techniques and conformation capture methodol-
ogy to investigate the 3D organization of chromatin. Super-
resolution microscopy, transmission electron microscopy 
(TEM), cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM), atomic force 
microscopy (AFM), and 3D-structured illumination micros-
copy provide molecular recognition and live-cell imaging for 
mapping 3D genome structure [256–258]. One of the most 
widely used imaging techniques for visualizing genomic loci 
is the fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) method. This 
method is based on the use of fluorescently labeled probes that 
hybridize to specific genomic regions [259]. Over the years, a 
conventional FISH methodology was modified by the use of 
custom-synthesized oligonucleotide probes, which in combi-
nation with super-resolution microscopy, has led to the devel-
opment of a high-precision technique, COMBinatorial Oligo 
FISH (COMBO-FISH) [260]. Furthermore, FISH can be per-
formed on preserved nuclei (achieved by fixation with formal-
dehyde) using 3D imaging (3D-FISH), thus characterizing 3D 
spatial configuration of chromatin [261].
Chromosome conformation capture (3C) assays and 3C-based 
methods (3C sequencing, or 3C-seq), circular chromosome con-
formation capture (4C), and chromosome conformation capture 
carbon copy (5C) involve formaldehyde‐mediated cross-linking, 
digestion with a restriction enzyme, and re-ligation of digested 
fragments for simultaneous detection of physical proximity 
between multiple genomic loci [262]. Ligation products are then 
analyzed with PCR, microarrays, or sequencing. These meth-
ods examine chromatin domains (e.g., topologically associating 
domains (TADs) and chromatin loops). Advances in C-based 
technologies resulted in the development of a high-throughput, 
genome-wide chromosome conformation capture assay (Hi-C), 
which involves the incorporation of biotin-linked nucleotides 
prior to the re-ligation step to enrich for ligation junctions and 
to eliminate non-ligated DNA fragments, thus generating high-
resolution chromatin contact maps [263]. Substitution of restric-
tion enzymes with other nucleases might improve the resolution 
of Hi-C. Several novel assays have been developed to simulta-
neously capture chromatin architecture and DNA methylation 
status at a single-cell level. Among these techniques are single-
nucleus methyl-3C sequencing (sn-m3C-seq) and Methyl-Hi-C, 
which combine 3C or Hi-C with bisulfite conversion (WGBS) 
[264, 265]. Another widely used assay is chromatin interaction 
analysis by paired-end tag (ChIA-PET), which combines 3C 
and ChIP enrichment of DNA–protein complexes, which allows 
evaluating chromatin interactions [266].
The evolution of methods for mapping of the 3D genome 
architecture has led to the establishment of 4D Nucleosome 
Project, which aims to develop methodology for comprehen-
sively evaluating the organization and dynamics of the genome 
and to gain a better mechanistic understanding of the biological 
significance of these features [267]. The limitation of the prox-
imity-ligation 3C-based assays can be overcome by the use of 
NGS-based methods that do not use ligation, such as genome 
architecture mapping (GAM), split-pool recognition of interac-
tions by tag extension (SPRITE), and chromatin interaction anal-
ysis drop (ChIA-Drop) [268, 269]. These techniques can evaluate 
additional spatial parameters of chromosomal organization, such 
as radial distributions and chromatin compaction of a locus and 
long-range interactions not attainable by 3C methods.
As mentioned above, the development of CRISPR-based 
technologies can significantly contribute to our understanding 
of epigenetic processes. For example, CRISPR system can be 
used to deliver sensors to specific loci of DNA or on histones 
for fluorescent imaging or to induce site-specific methylation 
for functional studies of epigenetic modifications [270–272].
26.10.4  Methods for Assessment of 
Non-coding RNAs
26.10.4.1  Methods for ncRNAs 
Expression Profiling
The majority of the techniques currently used for profiling of 
short and long non-coding RNAs (ncRNAs) expressions are 
based on q-RT-PCR, hybridization-based platforms, and high-
throughput RNA sequencing [273]. High-throughput sequenc-
ing analysis of gene expression (SAGE) and its successor, Cap 
analysis of gene expression (CAGE), are methods for analyses 
of the transcriptome, which allow for identification and char-
acterization of transcripts, including ncRNAs, and in the case 
of CAGE, simultaneous mapping of 5’-capped RNAs [274, 
275]. In addition, a variety of computational bioinformatics and 
in silico tools were developed to assist with the prediction of 
ncRNAs and evaluating their structure, function, and network 
interactions [150, 191]. Novel single-cell transcript sequencing 
methods do not require a large sample size to detect low-abun-
dance transcripts. For example, optimized Switching Mechanism 
at 5’ end of RNA Template sequencing (SMART-seq2) relies on 
template-switching, which is based on the addition of extra nucleo-
tides to the 5’ end of RNA template by reverse transcriptase and 
synthesis of cDNA strand using complementary template-switch-
ing oligonucleotides that carry 3 riboguanosines at the 3’ end, thus 
providing more coverage compared to methods that are based on 
poly(A)-tailing (addition of poly(A) tail that only contains adenine 
bases to the 3’ end of an RNA transcript) [276]. Other examples 
include the ligation-based method Small-seq, Designated Primer-
based RNA sequencing (DP-seq) which uses heptamer primers, 
and the Quartz-seq method which utilizes specifically designed 
suppression primers to minimize synthesis of by-product [277–
279]. Additionally, third-generation single-molecule sequencing 
techniques, such as nanopore platforms, are emerging as reli-
able methods for detecting and mapping ncRNAs; currently, this 
sequencing approach is the only method that does not require 
reverse transcription of RNA into DNA. Thus, this methodology

avoids any bias or distortion of RNA structure after PCR ampli-

that does not require PCR amplification is Nanostring nCounter 
Analysis [281]. This multiplex methodology relies on the attach-
ment of color-coded molecular barcodes to target transcripts and 
can detect up to 800 targets in a single reaction.
Traditional methods, such as northern blotting or nuclease 
protection assays (NPA), remain reliable tools for validation of 
ncRNAs expression [282, 283]. In addition, in situ hybridiza-
tion (ISH) and FISH methods can be used for the detection 
and visualization of ncRNAs, providing information regarding 
their expression and subcellular localization even at a single-
cell resolution [284, 285].
26.10.4.1.1  Considerations for Small ncRNAs Profiling
The short size of small ncRNAs and lack of common sequences, 
such as poly(A) tails, can pose difficulties for methods based on 
reverse transcription or hybridization. Thus, certain consider-
ations should be taken into account when profiling small ncRNAs, 
beginning with sample processing and RNA isolation procedures, 
which should be modified to retain small RNA fragments [286]. 
These limitations can be overcome by using modified probes and 
primers. For example, stem-loop reverse transcription primers 
or polyadenylation of miRNAs with polymerase can lengthen 
target cDNA for qRT-PCR assay [287, 288]. For ISH and FISH 
detection, the use of hapten-labeled Locked Nucleic Acid (LNA) 
oligonucleotide probes showed to be the most advantageous for 
the detection of miRNA [289]. Commercially available microar-
ray platforms for miRNA detection also commonly incorporate 
LNA-modified capture probes [290].
26.10.4.2  Methods for Analysis of 
ncRNA Interactions
26.10.4.2.1  RNA–Protein Interactions
The majority of methods that analyze intra-molecular interac-
tions of ncRNAs with proteins are based on immunoprecipitation 
techniques. Thus, the most frequently used methods to investi-
gate RNA–protein interactions are RNA immunoprecipitation-
based arrays or sequencing (RIP-chip and RIP-seq, respectively) 
[291, 292]. RIP techniques often do not require cross-linking, 
but the addition of DNase I or RNase H digestion is warranted 
to eliminate indirect RNA–protein binding through DNA. 
High-throughput sequencing of RNA isolated by cross-linking 
immunoprecipitation (HITS-CLIP and CLIP-seq) is a modifica-
tion of RIP methods, in which UV radiation is used to induce 
RNA–protein cross-links to improve the mapping of binding 
sites between protein-bound ncRNAs and their mRNA targets 
[293]. RIP and CLIP-based methods utilize antibodies against 
a specific protein, which limits the scope of detection. To over-
come this limitation, novel approaches, such as Targets of RNA-
binding proteins Identified By Editing (TRIBE), were developed 
[294]. TRIBE uses fusion proteins that contain the RNA editing 
enzyme adenosine deaminase acting on RNA (ADAR) to mark 
RNA transcripts near the RNA–protein binding sites, followed 
by sequencing to identify these marks. Affinity-based techniques, 
including Comprehensive identification of RNA-binding pro-
teins by mass spectrometry (ChIRP-MS) show great sensitivity 
and specificity in identifying RNA–protein interactions. In this 
method, cross-linked RNA–protein complexes are labeled with 
biotinylated oligonucleotides, eluted, and identified by MS [295].
26.10.4.2.2  RNA–Chromatin Interactions
Several approaches can be used to map locations of ncRNAs and 
investigate RNA-binding sites on chromatin on a genome-wide 
scale. These include Chromatin isolation by RNA Purification 
(ChIRP), domain-specific ChIRP (dChiRP), Capture hybridiza-
tion Analysis of RNA Targets (CHART), and RNA-antisense 
purification (RAP) [296–298]. All of these methods involve fixa-
tion of RNA–chromatin complexes by cross-linking, followed by 
hybrid capture with biotinylated (biotin-labeled) antisense oligo-
nucleotides with high affinity for ncRNA (as in RAP) or to pre-
dicted ncRNAs open binding sites (as in CHART).
26.10.4.2.3  RNA–RNA Interactions
Certain modifications to cross-linking methods allow the 
investigation of intermolecular RNA–RNA interactions. For 
example, the RAP technique can be modified by the use of 
4-aminomethyltrioxalen, which generates cross-links between 
uridine bases, or by a commonly used cross-linker such as 
formaldehyde, with or without disuccinimidyl glutarate [299]. 
In order to avoid chemical-induced formation of protein–pro-
tein cross-links, UV radiation can be also used as a cross-
linker, as in cross-linking, ligation, and sequencing hybrid 
(CLASH) or hybrid and individual-nucleotide resolution ultra-
violet cross-linking and immunoprecipitation (HiCLIP RNA) 
methods [300, 301]. Transcriptome-wide mapping of various 
types of RNA–RNA interactions can be achieved with the help 
of sequencing-based methods, including ligation of interacting 
RNA flowed by sequencing (LIGR-seq) or psoralen analysis of 
RNA interactions and structures (PARIS) [302, 303].
26.10.4.2.4  Additional Methods for Assessment of ncRNA
Since lncRNA may serve as a template for miRNA upon 
degradation, some techniques, including genome-wide map-
ping of uncapped and cleaved transcripts (GMUCT), parallel 
analysis of RNA ends (PARE), and degradome sequencing 
(degradome-seq) focus on mapping uncapped and cleaved 
transcripts [304–306]. Several methods, such as Global 
run-on sequencing (Gro-seq) and 5’-bromo-uridine immu-
noprecipitation chase-deep sequencing analysis (BRIC-seq) 
are designed to measure half-lives and assess the decay rate 
of mRNA or ncRNA [307, 308]. Analysis of the secondary 
structure of RNAs was made possible with the develop-
ment of selective 2’-hydroxyl acylation analyzed by primer 
extension (SHAPE) and parallel analysis of RNA structure 
(PARS) [309, 310].
