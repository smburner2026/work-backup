13.1  Scope of Toxicology and the Framework 
of Its Information Resources
Informally defined as “the science of poisons,” toxicology 
is further defined by the US Society of Toxicology (SOT) in 
2005 as:
Toxicology is the study of the adverse effects of 
chemical, physical or biological agents on living 
organisms and the ecosystem, including the preven-
tion and amelioration of such adverse effects.
This definition was elaborated by a consensus discussion:
Toxicity is the adverse end product of a series of events 
that is initiated by exposure to chemical, physical or 
biological agents. Toxicity can manifest itself in a wide 
array of forms, from mild biochemical malfunctions 
to serious organ damage and death. These events, any 
of which may be reversible or irreversible, include 
absorption, transport, metabolism to more or less toxic 
metabolites, excretion, interaction with cellular macro-
molecules and other modes of toxic action. Toxicology 
integrates the study of all of these events, at all levels 
of biological organization, from molecules to complex 
ecosystems. The broad scope of toxicology, from the 
study of fundamental mechanisms to the measurement 
of exposure, including toxicity testing and risk analy-
sis, requires an extensively interdisciplinary approach. 
This approach utilizes the principles and methods of 
other disciplines, including molecular biology, chem-
istry (analytical, organic, inorganic and biochemistry), 
physiology, medicine (veterinary and human), com-
puter science and informatics.
To expand further upon the definition, toxicology can be con-
sidered from additional perspectives. One option is by type of 
agent – chemical, biological, or physical. Chemicals, singly as 
well as in mixtures, in varying scales from nanoscale on up, 
and as used in drugs, food, industrial applications, consumer 
products, etc., or that show up as waste, can all be investi-
gated from a toxicological perspective. Biological agents such 
as animal venoms and plant toxins also fall within the wide 
scope of toxicology. Finally, one can explore the toxic poten-

Another way to look at the subject would be via the effects 
on target sites, such as liver, kidney, skin, nervous system, or 
type of effect such as cancer, mutation, or inflammation. For 
instances involving humans and other animals, the subject’s 
susceptibilities due to gender, age, genetics, and general state 
of health are criteria that should be considered. Furthermore, 
one needs to know the extent to which acute, sub-chronic, or 
chronic exposures or effects are involved.
Toxicology is multidisciplinary, encompassing research 
studies conducted, in various settings, on laboratory animals, 
in vivo in humans (much less often, within ethically controlled 
settings), in vitro (i.e., tissue or cell culture), and in silico (i.e., 
using computer programs to estimate the toxicity of chemi-
cals). Epidemiological investigations also play an important 
role in toxicology. Despite an emphasis on laboratory research, 
clinical toxicology is an important component and aligns with 
the prevention and amelioration part of the SOT definition. 
Clinical toxicology focuses on the diagnosis, management, 
and prevention of poisoning due to toxic agents as does medi-
cal toxicology, with the latter recognized as a medical sub-
specialty by the American Board of Medical Specialties and 
practiced by physicians. Translational science, that seeks for 
ways to translate bench science or, increasingly, computer sci-
ence to bedside clinical practice, is helping to bridge the gap in 
toxicology between research and patient care. Poison Control 
Centers take calls from physicians, pharmacists, and other 
medical professionals with specific training in toxicology.
Sometimes the setting in which one is exposed dictates the type 
of toxicologist called upon to explore a problem. Occupational 
toxicology attempts to assess the hazards and risks to health 
posed by chemicals found in the workplace. Workers may be 
exposed to chemicals at a significantly higher level than the gen-
eral public and to chemicals not typically found in the general 
ambient environment. Regulatory toxicology, which cuts across 
the workplace and other settings, utilizes scientific knowledge 
to develop regulations to reduce and control exposure to danger-
ous chemicals and plays an important role in health and envi-
ronmental safety. Yet another branch of toxicology, sometimes 
intersecting clinical, regulatory, and workplace toxicology, is 
forensic toxicology, which is concerned with determining the 
presence or absence of toxic substances and their metabolites 
in biological fluids or tissues to solve a medico-legal problem.
Environmental toxicology considers the effects of toxic sub-
stances intentionally or unintentionally released into the environ-
ment. Ecotoxicology, a somewhat narrower discipline, depending 
upon who is doing the defining, is devoted to the study of the 
adverse effects of chemicals on ecosystems structure, functions, 
and biodiversity. Pollutants altering elements of our natural envi-
ronment, including plants, animals, and climate, can also have a 
short- or long-term impact on human health.
Risk assessment integrates numerous disciplines of toxicol-
ogy. Consisting of qualitative and quantitative components, its 
four steps have been traditionally defined as: hazard identifica-
tion, dose-response assessment, exposure assessment, and risk 
characterization. Risk assessments are performed to estimate the 
nature and probability of adverse effects and are critical in help-
ing understand the perils potentially posed by a variety of agents.
While animal experimentation continues to be widespread, 
it is expensive, labor and time intensive, and raises a host of 
ethical concerns. New approach methodologies are increas-
ingly being employed as alternatives to animal testing, and this 
seems to be the future of toxicology testing.
Many of the more recent advances in toxicological sciences 
stem from the Human Genome Project and DNA mapping 
efforts as well as the pharmaceutical industry’s projects to screen 
for chemicals possessing specific biological functionality while 
maintaining safety. Toxicology has embraced the -omics revolu-
tion. Microarrays and next-generation RNA sequencing are just 
two of the many newer technologies now within a toxicologist’s 
arsenal. Big data, public access, and data sharing have all had 
roles in moving toxicology forward and made it increasingly 
interdisciplinary. Understanding how to map the molecular path-
ways of toxicity in cells will play a major role in enhancing our 
understanding of what makes a substance toxic. Bioinformatics 
and computational toxicology, as well, are offering new ways to 
not only evaluate and assess, but also predict chemical toxicity.
As should be clear from the above, the breadth of toxicology as 
a subject ranges far and wide. Toxicology information resources 
are equally wide-ranging and cover foundational concepts, best 
practices in experimental design to generate evidence, journals 
and databases that present results of toxicological and epidemio-
logical studies, approaches to conduct literature-based assess-
ments, and conclusions from assessments conducted by Federal, 
state, and international agencies. Resources that span these 
concepts are inventoried below along with information on key 
societies and organizations dedicated to the field of toxicology. 
This chapter, though, does not attempt to comprehensively point 
to information resources in every aspect of the field but instead 
serves as a companion to the sub-disciplines discussed in the 
book’s other chapters, with the focus on (non-pharmaceutical) 
chemicals which pose a risk to health. Thus, it does not high-
light areas such as drugs, foods, analytical and clinical toxicol-
ogy, industrial health, and ecotoxicology/environmental health, 
all of which would be relevant to the subject in an encyclopedic 
treatise but more than can be encompassed here. This is also in 
keeping in line with the range of chapters covered in this book.
The remaining sections of this chapter are intended to high-
light useful resources for toxicology information: books, com-
prehensive reviews and assessments, journals, chemical data and 
information resources, and organizations. A brief section guides 
the reader on where and how to search for toxicology literature. 
The final section spotlights recent trends that impact both the 
generation and dissemination of toxicology information.
13.2  Selective Information Resources: 
A Starting Point
13.2.1  Monographs
Books, used here synonymously with monographs, describe, 
summarize, and/or discuss information drawn from primary 
research data and literature. Although books may have out-
dated information, they are often a useful starting point for 
learning the basics, obtaining an overview of a subject, and 
identifying primary sources.

Readers can readily locate books by searching Google 
Books, any of the large book suppliers such as Amazon, 
or directly on publisher websites (e.g., Elsevier’s 
ScienceDirect at sciencedirect.com). These suppliers usu-
ally offer the ability to preview the table of contents and 
may allow “search within this book” to view excerpted con-
tent before purchasing. Major public library catalogs, both 
technical and general, are also sources for locating books. 
Examples are the National Library of Medicine (NLM) 
(https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nlmcatalog/), the New York 
Public Library’s Shared Collection Catalog (https://www.
nypl.org/research/collections/about/shared-collection-cata-
log), and the Library of Congress (https://catalog.loc.gov/). 
Also, consider searching the library catalog of your local 
university. Most universities allow the public to purchase 
an annual library card to borrow books in their collections. 
Most ­publishers now make books available online as well 
as in print.
One book not fitting neatly into the following headings but 
worth highlighting for readers interested in a more compre-
hensive framework of toxicology information is Wexler P., ed. 
et al. Information Resources in Toxicology, 5th edition (2020). 
Academic Press. In two volumes, it is a comprehensive portal 
to the information framework of toxicology and related fields, 
and serves as a global, annotated bibliography and directory of 
its major resources.
The following compilation of books is not intended to be 
comprehensive, nor even extensive, but rather to highlight 
recently published and classic texts in general and special-
ized areas of toxicology. Coverage includes standard books, 
dictionaries, handbooks, textbooks, and encyclopedias that 
address the key concepts covered elsewhere in this Hayes’ edi-
tion. The selected titles appeal to a range of audiences, from 
members of the public and toxicology students to public health 
specialists, regulators, and practicing researchers. The books 
are categorized by the section headings used here in Hayes’.

13.2.2  Journals
Commercial publishers, government organizations, and profes-
sional societies currently publish over 120 toxicology journals, 
based on the Toxicology category listed at Scimago Journal 
Rankings (scimagojr.com). Many of these journals emerged as a 
result of the growth of sub-disciplines within the field of toxicology. 
This number of journals does not even reflect toxicology research 
published in other discipline-related literature journals such as 
Biomarkers, Carcinogenesis, and Metabolism, or in data journals 
such as Data in Brief, Genomics Data, or Scientific Data.
Journals now use a standard electronic format which allows 
for the delivery of information not only via personal computer 
but also via mobile devices. The online availability of content 
enables interactive graphics and the easy distribution of data in 
reusable data formats. It also makes it easier for readers to stay 
current by subscribing to alerting services that provide regu-
lar updates on recently published research. Almost all journal 
publishers as well as bibliographic resources; e.g., PubMed, 
Scopus, and Google Scholar provide research alerts.
Since 2003, with the Bethesda Statement on Open 
Access 
Publishing 
(https://www.researchgate.net/publica-
tion/48547523_The_Bethesda_Statement_on_Open-Access_
Publishing), there has been an increasing shift to make online 
published peer-reviewed literature free, immediate, and per-
manently open access. With toxicology literature no longer 
sitting behind a paywall, research results are now being dis-
seminated to and accessed by a significantly wider audience.
In support of this movement, many publishers currently offer 
the ability for authors to pay fees to make their research open 
access. Some publishers, such as Biomed Central and PLoS, 
are open access only, and authors must pay a fee to publish 
in their journals, whereas other publishers, e.g., Nature and 
Springer, offer hybrid journals where authors have a choice 
whether to pay the fees to make the article open access. The 
government-sponsored Environmental Health Perspectives is 
one of a few journals that do not impose author fees. Finally, 
policies such as the National Institutes of Health (NIH) Public 
Access Policy (https://publicaccess.nih.gov/) mandate that 
NIH-funded researchers submit their peer-reviewed accepted 
manuscript to PubMed Central (PMC). The Directory of Open 
Access Journals (https://doaj.org/) and Free Medical Journals 
(http://www.freemedicaljournals.com/) are useful resources 
for readers to locate free and open access research.

13.2.3  Comprehensive Reviews and Assessments
Comprehensive reviews typically involve evaluating the qual-
ity of, and summarizing the key research regarding, the harm-
ful effects of a chemical on people and/or the environment. 
Assessments tend to extend that review by making conclusions 
based on the evidence regarding the chemical’s hazard, the 
dose-response and/or level of harmful exposure, and conclud-
ing with a characterization of the known risk for that substance.
Such comprehensive reviews and assessments usually evalu-
ate and integrate information from peer-reviewed epidemio-
logical, experimental model, and mechanistic studies. In some 
cases, the publications may identify knowledge gaps and pro-
pose areas for further study.

13.2.5  Chemical Data and Information Sources
An abundance of websites containing rich collections of toxi-
cological data has exploded in recent years and growth con-
tinues unabated. Much of these data are open access, mostly 
driven by the establishment of the FAIR Guiding Principles 
for scientific data management and stewardship, which pro-
vides guidelines to improve the findability, accessibility, 
interoperability, and reuse of digital assets (Wilkinson et al. 
2016).
The principles specifically emphasize how computer sys-
tems find, access, and reuse data, and interoperate. These are 
helpful, if not necessary, because of the increasing reliance 
on computers to deal with data and the enormous increases in 
volume, complexity, and rate at which data is produced. Data 
can now be discovered, downloaded, and repurposed with just 
a few clicks, thereby allowing scientists to aggregate data for 
further investigation, for modeling, and for inclusion into their 
research projects.
The availability of public data allows for the conversion 
of data into forms that can be fed into derivative informat-
ics systems. For example, Luechtefeld et al. (2016) report on 
the transformation of ECHA data into a structured, machine 
readable, and searchable database. The European Registration, 
Evaluation, Authorisation and Restriction of Chemicals 
(REACH) data has also been made available via the AMBIT 
cheminformatics data system (http://cefic-lri.org/toolbox/
ambit/).
A significant number of projects, especially in Europe, 
have released toxicological data to the community. Various 
industries including cosmetics and pharmaceutical companies 
are interested in such toxicological data as they can utilize 
it within their own informatics systems and models to sup-
port their research. Member companies participate directly in 
projects such as SEURAT-1 (Gocht et al. 2015), EUToxRisk 
(Daneshian et al. 2016), and Innovative Medicines Initiative 
projects focused on drug toxicology, e.g., eTOX (Sanz et al.

690
Hayes’ Principles and Methods of Toxicology
2017) and OPEN PHACTS (Williams et al. 2012). Other 
organizations benefit as well from the availability of data 
to reuse and repurpose data for their own tools and models. 
This includes government agencies such as ECHA, EPA, and 
Food and Drug Administration (FDA), communities such as 
Edelweiss Connect (https://www.edelweissconnect.com/), and 
commercial entities such as IdeaConsult Ltd (https://www.
ideaconsult.net/).
In addition to accessing data via a myriad of websites, a num-
ber of which are mentioned below, there has been an increasing 
effort to provide access to data analysis tools and download-
able software tools that are either free-to-use or open-source 
and available to the community to extend and enhance with 
their own contributions. The field of cheminformatics coupled 
with advances in Internet-based technologies has had a sig-
nificant impact in enabling many of these online tools espe-
cially in regard to providing access to QSAR (Quantitative 
Structure-Activity Relationship) and Quantitative Structure-
Property Relationship prediction models. There are thus many 
beneficiaries to the release of toxicological data and not just 
the individual users of online applications. If data are made 
available according to FAIR principles, then derivative appli-
cations and models can be developed and delivered to the 
community expanding on the availability of tools available to 
support both research and regulatory applications.

13.3  Conducting a Literature Search 
and Bibliographic Resources
A comprehensive literature review is typically considered nec-
essary at the beginning of an assessment, research project, or 
thesis. One should begin the literature review with an effective 
literature search approach (Taylor 2002). A librarian can pro-
vide useful assistance in guiding one through the steps. The 
first step is to define a guiding concept (e.g., the research objec-
tive, problem, issue, or thesis topic). After selecting the topic, 
one must determine the appropriate keywords and databases to 
use in the search (see the bibliographic resources and chemical 
information resources sections below). The keywords should

696
Hayes’ Principles and Methods of Toxicology
be a diverse set of related terms. The search results should be 
reviewed to determine if the search was broad enough to cap-
ture all the relevant material but still narrow enough to exclude 
most irrelevant material, and the number of results is appro-
priate for the topic. If there is a problem with any of these 
areas (i.e., too little data for a common topic, too much data 
for an obscure topic), then the keywords for the search should 
be revised and the search conducted again until the results are 
satisfactory.
Once the literature search results are considered adequate, 
they should be reviewed to identify relevant literature. All 
relevant literature should be retrieved, read, and analyzed; 
additional relevant literature may be identified through the 
citations of reviewed literature, and then organizing the litera-
ture into sections that represent themes or trends rather than 
list a summary of each study. By organizing the literature, one 
can critically analyze the data to synthesize and evaluate it 
in relation to the guiding concept (e.g., the research objective, 
problem, issue, or thesis topic). Increasingly, systematic review 
methods are being used to conduct toxicological assessments.

treatment breakthroughs, drug interactions, and toxicology, as 
well as many subject areas. Proquest Central is available by 
subscription or by transactional access fees.
PubMed® 
https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/
PubMed is from the NCBI at the US NLM (NCBI/NLM) and 
comprises more than 30 million citations for biomedical litera-
ture from MEDLINE, life science journals, and online books. 
PubMed includes links to many sites providing full-text arti-
cles and other related resources. PubMed can be searched using 
medical subject heading terms, author names, title words, text 
words or phrases, journal names, or any combination of these. 
PubMed offers a feature which displays related articles for any 
citation and allows search results to be exported into reference 
management software.
Since PubMed offers an application programming interface 
and Web services, both PubChem and the CompTox Chemicals 
Dashboard integrate literature data at a substance level, with 
the Dashboard offering its Abstract Sifter module as both an 
integrated software module and Excel Add-in (Baker et al. 
2017). Abstract Sifter performs real-time searches against 
PubMed using the CAS RN and Preferred Name associated 
with a chemical, together with a set of predefined queries, to 
perform the search. The Sifter returns the number of occur-
rences of each term found in the title and abstract combined 
and the Web-based spreadsheet’s citation counts can be sorted 
and viewed in an occurrence heat-map type of visualiza-
tion. The module allows for further filtering with the option 
to create or enter more granular keyword search terms. The 
Excel add-in allows the user to work with batches of chemi-
cals rather than be limited to the single chemicals view of the 
Dashboard.
PubMed Central and Europe PubMed Central 
http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc and https://europepmc.org/
PMC, launched in 2000, is a free full-text archive of biomedi-
cal and life sciences journal literature from NIH’s NLM. In 
keeping with NLM’s legislative mandate to collect and pre-
serve the biomedical literature, PMC serves as a digital coun-
terpart to NLM’s extensive print journal collection. As of 
2020, almost 10,000 journals contributed more 6.5 million 
articles to PMC.
Europe PMC offers alternative access to PubMed and PMC 
content, but is expanded to include agricultural literature from 
Agricola, biological patents, Chinese biological research, 
National Health Service guidelines, books, and preprints from 
various preprint servers and open research platforms (e.g., 
bioRxiv and ChemRxiv).
Science.gov
https://www.science.gov/
At the time of writing, Science.gov searches over 60 databases 
and over 2,200 scientific websites to provide users with access 
to more than 200 million pages of authoritative federal science 
information including research and development results from 
14 federal science agencies including but not limited to the 
Departments of Defense, Energy, Health and Human Services, 
the EPA, the National Aeronautics and Space Administration, 
and the National Science Foundation. Science.gov allows 
clustering or grouping of results by topic, year, or author, and 
includes Wikipedia topics and EurekAlert items related to the 
search. Search results can be imported into reference manage-
ment software or e-mailed to colleagues.
SciFinder
http://scifinder.cas.org
SciFinder, produced by CAS, contains a wide variety of con-
tent from journal articles to information on chemical struc-
tures, properties, and reactions. There are three separate 
search sections in SciFinder: references, substances, and 
reactions. The bibliographic records that are retrieved from 
a reference search come from the CAplus and MEDLINE 
databases. At the time of writing, it contains over 53 million 
records from more than 180 countries and indexes more than 
50,000 journals, 1,500 of which are core chemistry journals. 
Coverage also includes patents from worldwide patent offices. 
Records in SciFinder’s substance search section come from 
the CAS REGISTRY, which contains more than 160 million 
chemical substances dating back to the early 1800s and is 
updated daily.

https://www.webofscience.com

through subscribing libraries or institutions.
13.4  Social Media
In the late 1990s, a new concept of information exchange was 
introduced, push technology. Push technology is a style of 
Internet-based communication in which the consumer initiates 
a request for information that is set to be delivered directly 
as the information becomes available. The first application of 
this was with the Blackberry and the ability to access e-mail 
directly on a handheld device. Other sources of push technol-
ogy followed with such applications as listservs, e-subscrip-
tions to newsletters or journals. More recently, the term push 
technology has been merged with social media. This merger 
has created a vast array of technologies such as RSS feeds, 
Facebook, Twitter, Wikipedia, podcasts, webcasts, blogs, wid-
gets, and mobile device applications (Apps). It is now possible 
to receive information anywhere in almost any format desired, 
and the underlying technologies are the catalysts to the efforts 
of scientists to engage in online networking, data sharing, 
and research activity distribution (Williams et al. 2017). The 
majority of the newer technologies utilized in social media 
have been viewed as personal resources, but many organiza-
tions have utilized these technologies to better communicate 
with others outside their organization whether it be colleagues 
or the general public. Social allows for the bidirectional flow 
of information among potentially millions of people in real 
time. Until recently, scientific information was generally pro-
vided by a few acknowledged experts in the field and was uni-
directional in nature and relatively static.
Today, many professional societies, such as SRA and SOT, 
are utilizing social media. The SRA recently added a social 
media subcommittee to manage all of its social media outlets, 
including Twitter and LinkedIn. They are utilizing these ven-
ues to communicate news to its members. The SOT utilizes 
various e-newsletters and an RSS feed for communicating the 
latest society news and research.

When utilizing information from social media venues, it 
must be evaluated to determine the quality of the informa-
tion provided. Fortunately, for most science-based informa-
tion utilizing data from well-known organizations, such as 
US government agencies or well-known reputable private 
organizations. Certain social media venues, for example, 
those of US government agencies, such as the CDC and 
EPA, are closely vetted for the accuracy of information 
presented. Others, such as Wikipedia, are self-regulated to 
some extent, inasmuch as contributors serve as peer review-
ers, limiting the amount of erroneous information that is 
posted. However, most social media outlets do not monitor 
their sites to ensure that the information presented is accu-
rate or complete. Therefore, it is important for those seek-
ing toxicology and other science-based information to have 
the tools to evaluate each resource as regards the quality of 
information content.

13.5  Conclusions
Information and data generated by advances in the toxicologi-
cal sciences, in line with that of other scientific disciplines, 
continue to grow at a phenomenal rate. The increasing com-
plexity derives from the interdisciplinary nature of toxicol-
ogy and the societal manifestations of science. Fortunately, 
computers and the development and expansion of the Web 
have allowed unprecedented access to and utilization of the 
vast toxicological information base as it continues to expand. 
Moreover, innovation in technology and software applications 
have greatly expanded and diversified the ways in which toxi-
cological information is presented, distributed, and shared.
