1.1. General Introduction 
 
1. 
A skin sensitiser refers to a substance that will lead to an allergic response 
following repeated skin contact as defined by the United Nations Globally 
Harmonized System of Classification and Labelling of Chemicals (UN GHS) (1). 
There is general agreement on the key biological events underlying skin 
sensitisation. The current knowledge of the chemical and biological mechanisms 
associated with skin sensitisation initiated by covalent binding to proteins has been 
summarised as an Adverse Outcome Pathway (AOP) (2) that begins with a 
molecular initiating event, leading to intermediate key events (KEs), and 
terminating with the adverse effect, allergic contact dermatitis. 
2. 
The skin sensitisation AOP focuses on chemicals that react with amino acid 
residues (i.e. cysteine or lysine) such as organic chemicals. In this instance, the 
molecular initiating event (i.e. the first KE), is the covalent binding of electrophilic 
substances to nucleophilic centres in skin proteins. The second KE in this AOP 
takes place in the keratinocytes and includes inflammatory responses as well as 
changes in gene expression associated with specific cell signalling pathways such 
as the antioxidant/electrophile response element (ARE)-dependent pathways. The 
third KE is the activation of dendritic cells, typically assessed by expression of 
specific cell surface markers, chemokines and cytokines. The fourth key event is 
T-cell proliferation, and the adverse outcome is presentation of allergic contact 
dermatitis. 
3. 
The assessment of skin sensitisation has typically involved the use of 
laboratory animals. The classical methods that use guinea pigs, the Guinea Pig 
Maximisation Test (GPMT) and the Buehler Test (OECD TG 406) (3) assess both 
the induction and elicitation phases of skin sensitisation. The murine tests, such as 
the local lymph node assay (LLNA) (OECD TG 429) (4) and its three non-
radioactive modifications — LLNA: DA (OECD TG 442A) (5), LLNA: BrdU-ELISA, 
and BrdU-FCM (OECD TG 442B) (6) — all assess the induction phase exclusively 
and have gained acceptance, since they provide an advantage over the guinea pig 
tests in terms of animal welfare together with an objective measurement of the 
induction phase of skin sensitisation. 
4. 
Mechanistically-based in chemico and in vitro test methods (OECD TG 
442C, 442D, 442E) (7, 8, 9) addressing the first three KEs of the skin sensitisation 
AOP can be used to evaluate the skin sensitisation hazard potential of chemicals. 
With the exception of the kinetic Direct Peptide Reactivity Assay (k-DPRA) 
(TG442C), none of these test methods are considered sufficient stand-alone 
replacements of animal data to conclude on the skin sensitisation potential of 
chemicals or to provide information for potency sub-categorisation according to the

UN GHS (sub-categories 1A and 1B). However, data generated with these in 
chemico and in vitro methods addressing multiple KEs of the skin sensitisation 
AOP can be used together, as well as with information sources such as in silico 
and read-across predictions from chemical analogues, within integrated 
approaches to testing and assessment (IATA) or defined approaches (DAs). 
Results from the individual information sources can only be used in DAs if the 
substances fall within the applicability domains of the methods, which are described 
in the “Initial Considerations, Applicability and Limitations” sections of the 
respective methods (TG 442C, Appendices I-II; TG 442D, Appendices IA-IC; TG 
442E Annexes 1-4) (7, 8, 9), and in Appendices I-V of this guideline for the in silico 
information sources. 
5. 
Results from multiple information sources can be used together in DAs to 
achieve an equivalent or better predictive capacity than that of the animal tests to 
predict responses in humans. A DA consists of a fixed data interpretation 
procedure (DIP) (e.g. a mathematical model, a rule-based approach) applied to 
data (e.g. in silico predictions, in chemico, in vitro data) generated with a defined 
set of information sources to derive a prediction without the need for expert 
judgment. Individual DAs for skin sensitisation and their respective information 
sources were originally described in Guidance Document 256, Annex I/II (10) and 
a preliminary assessment was published in Kleinstreuer et al (11). The DAs use 
method combinations intended to overcome some of the limitations of the individual 
methods to provide increased confidence in the overall result obtained. The 
ultimate goal of DAs is to provide information that is equivalent to that provided by 
animal studies, i.e. information that can be used for hazard identification, potency 
(sub)-categorisation, and/or a quantitative measure of sensitisation potency, used 
to derive a point of departure (PoD). 
6. 
Testing laboratories should consider all relevant available information on 
the test chemical prior to conducting the studies as directed by a DA. All available 
information could include, for example, the identity and chemical structure of the 
test chemical and its physicochemical properties. Such information should be 
considered in order to determine whether the individual OECD test guideline 
methods and any in silico information source used under a specific DA are 
applicable for the test chemical.

7. 
When performing a hazard evaluation, potency (sub)-categorisation, and/or 
PoD prediction based on the output from an in vivo (e.g. LLNA) test, an in chemico 
test, an in vitro test, an in silico approach, a DA, or any combination thereof, the 
same principles always apply. That is, all available information relevant to the 
chemical in question as well as toxicological data on structurally related test 
chemicals, if available, should be taken into consideration.

9. 
Multiple rule-based DAs are included in this Guideline and are described in 
the context of their primary intended regulatory purpose: hazard identification, i.e. 
discrimination between skin sensitisers and non-sensitisers (Part I), potency sub-
categorisation (Part II), or derivation of a point of departure (Part III)1. The DA 
included in Part II is also suitable for hazard identification (Part I). The DA(s) 
included in Part III cannot be used in Part I or Part II unless mentioned specifically 
for their use. The evaluation and review of the DAs are described in detail in the 
Supporting document to the Guideline (GL) on Defined Approaches (DAs) for Skin 
Sensitisation (12). 
10. 
A comprehensive dataset of 196 chemicals with DA predictions, data on 
individual information sources, highly curated LLNA and Human Patch Predictive 
Test (HPPT) data, and physicochemical properties, was compiled and is attached 
as Annex 2 to the Supporting document to the Guideline (GL) on Defined 
Approaches (DAs) for Skin Sensitisation (12). Information on the curation of this 
data is detailed in Annexes 3 and 4 of the Supporting document to the Guideline 
(GL) on Defined Approaches (DAs) for Skin Sensitisation (12). Out of the 196 
chemicals, 168 chemicals have classifications based on LLNA data and 66 
chemicals have classifications based on human data, which were all agreed upon 
by the EG DASS and used to evaluate the performance of the DAs when using the 
initially included (see paragraph 11) test methods and in silico models. Due to the 
availability of data, this dataset contains mainly cosmetic ingredients but also other 
types of chemicals that are used across sectors such as preservatives, dyes, or 
food ingredients. The dataset is chemically diverse as shown by the range of 
physicochemical properties covered by these chemicals: it contains small and large 
molecules (molecular weight ranges from 30 to 512 g/mol), hydrophilic and 
hydrophobic substances (Log P ranges from -3.9 to 9.4), liquids and solids (melting 
point ranges from -122 to 253 ºC), volatile and non-volatile substances (boiling point 
ranges from -19 to 445 ºC). Further details on the chemical space 
characterization of the reference database are available in Section 4 of the 
Supporting document to the Guideline (GL) on Defined Approaches (DAs) for Skin 
Sensitisation 
(12).

17. 
All DAs described in Parts I and II of this guideline can be used to address 
countries' requirements for discriminating sensitisers (i.e. UN GHS Category 1) and 
non- sensitisers (UN GHS Not Classified (NC)), though they do so with different

Defined Approaches (DAs) for Skin Sensitisation) (12)). 
18. 
The ITS DA can also be used to discriminate chemicals into three UN GHS 
potency categories (Category 1A = strong sensitisers; Category 1B = other 
sensitisers), and NC. 
19. 
The known limitations and applicability domains of the individual information 
sources were used to design workflows for assigning confidence to each of the 
predictions produced by the DAs described in this guideline. In order to have a high 
confidence prediction, the underlying data must meet criteria in the respective in 
chemico/in vitro TGs (see TG 442C, Appendices I-II; TG 442D, Appendices IA-IC; 
TG 442E Annexes 1-4 (7, 8, 9)); for the ITS DA, in silico predictions must fall within 
the applicability domain of the model used (see Appendices I-II). DA predictions 
with high confidence for hazard identification and/or potency categorisation are 
considered conclusive. DA predictions with low confidence are considered 
inconclusive for hazard identification and/or potency categorisation (see Sections 
2.1.4 and 3.1.4 for further information).These inconclusive predictions may 
nevertheless be considered in a weight-of-evidence approach and/or within the 
context of an IATA together with other information sources (e.g. demonstration of 
exposure to the test system, existing in vivo data, clinical data, read-across, other 
in chemico/in vitro/in silico data, etc.).

the overall correct classification rate for all UN GHS sub-categories (Category 1A = strong sensitisers;

potency estimate (MSPE) and is reported as a weight-of-evidence potency metric 
calculated from human data. SARA-ICE PoD estimates were computed for the 196 
chemicals in the defined approaches for skin sensitisation (DASS) reference data

Gerberick, 2022). A DSA1+ MSPE value was defined for 54 / 66 chemicals with

ICE PoDs are within 2-fold of the reference value. SARA-ICE PoDs, calculated in 
the same way as above, were also compared to 154 chemicals with reference 
LLNA MLLPs sourced from OECD DASS reference data (12). A positive correlation 
exists (Pearson correlation coefficient is 0.71) and 122 / 154 (79%) of SARA-ICE 
PoDs are within 10-fold of the reference value. Again, roughly one third, 49 / 154

applicability domain is the response and chemical structure space in which the 
model makes predictions with a given reliability and the applicability domain should 
therefore consider the parametric, structural, mechanistic, metabolic and response 
space of the model. Where a DA for skin sensitisation includes an in silico tool, 
users should refer to the limitations and applicability domain of the individual in silico 
tool as detailed in Appendices I-II of this Guideline. Depending on the DIP, 
chemicals outside the in silico model’s applicability domain may result in DA 
predictions of low confidence that are considered inconclusive.

2.1 “2 out of 3” Defined Approach 
2.1.1. 
Summary 
39.

sensitisation hazard of a chemical (i.e. UN GHS Cat. 1 vs. UN GHS NC) without

designed to provide information on the potency of a skin sensitiser. 
40. 
The combination of test methods included in the 2o3 DA covers at least two 
of the first three KEs of the AOP leading to skin sensitisation, i.e. KE1: protein binding 
(OECD TG 442C) (2); KE2: keratinocyte activation (OECD TG 442D) (3); and KE3: 
dendritic cell activation (OECD TG 442E) (4).

addressing at least two of the first three KEs of the skin sensitisation AOP 
determine the final classification. For the classic 2o3 DA was evaluated against a 
curated dataset of up to168 chemicals with LLNA-based reference data and up to 
66 chemicals with human-based reference data agreed upon by the EG DASS 
(Annex 2 of the Supporting Document to the Guideline (GL) on Defined 
Approaches (DAs) for Skin Sensitisation (1). The performance of the DAs using 
the original (i.e., classic DAs) and alternative information sources has been

2.1.2. 
Data interpretation procedure 
42. 
The DIP in the 2o3 DA is a transparent, rule-based approach requiring no 
expert judgment (5, 6, 7). The approach predicts skin sensitisation hazard by 
sequential testing, in an undefined order, in up to three of the internationally 
accepted non-animal assays mapping to KE1-3. In other words, the assays are run 
for two KEs, and if these assays provide consistent results, then the chemical is 
predicted accordingly as skin sensitiser or non-sensitiser. If the first two assays 
provide discordant results, the assay for the remaining KE is run. The overall result 
is based on the two concordant findings considering the confidence of the obtained 
predictions as described in Section 2.1.4. 
43. 
The performance of the 2o3 DA was found to be impacted by the 
consideration of BRs for each of the methods, as described below in Section 2.1.4, 
and further detailed in Section 3.3 and Annex 7 of the Supporting Document to 
the Guideline (GL) on Defined Approaches (DAs) for Skin Sensitisation (1). A 
decision tree is provided in Figure 2.1 of Section 2.1.4 to derive predictions for the 
2o3 DA, with no modification of the 2o3 DA DIP.

2.1.4.

47.

the three KE of the 2o3 DA, (see Annex 1 of this document, and Section 3.3and

3.1.1. Summary 
63. 
This defined approach was constructed as an Integrated Testing Strategy 
(ITS) for prediction of the skin sensitisation hazard potential and potency sub-
categorisation according to the UN GHS (sub-categories 1A and 1B) classification 
system. 
64.

QSAR Toolbox (5).

66.

4.1 “SARA-ICE” Defined Approach for derivation of a point of departure 
4.1.1. Summary 
86. 
The Skin Allergy Risk Assessment-Integrated Chemical Environment 
(SARA-ICE) DA is a Bayesian statistical model which estimates an ED01, which is 
provided as a probability distribution. The ED01 is an estimate of a dermal dose (µg 
cm-2) at which there is a 1% chance of inducing sensitization from a human 
predictive patch test (HPPT). The SARA-ICE DA uses a set of defined input data 
and specifies a single point, computed as the geometric mean of the ED01 
distribution conditional on it being less than 60,000 µg cm-2, which provides a point 
of departure (PoD) that can be applied within a quantitative risk assessment for 
skin sensitisation.  
 
87. 
The SARA-ICE DA is based on data from test methods from KEs 1-4 of the 
adverse outcome in the AOP.  The SARA-ICE DA is based on a previously 
published SARA model which initially included 81 chemicals in the database and 
was subsequently expanded to 434 chemicals, leveraging chemicals contained 
within the Integrated Chemical Environment (ICE, https://ice.ntp.niehs.nih.gov/, 
(2,3)). Further description of the database on which SARA-ICE is trained is 
provided in Annex 10 of Supporting document to the Guideline (GL) on Defined 
Approaches (DAs) for Skin Sensitisation (1). Evaluation of SARA-ICE is described 
in paragraph 100 and performance is described in section 4.1.4. Test methods 
which can be used as input information sources include: the Direct Peptide 
Reactivity Assay (DPRA; OECD TG 442C ), kinetic DPRA (kDPRA; OECD TG 
442C), KeratinoSens (OECD TG 442D), the human cell line activation test (h-
CLAT; OECD TG 442E), and U-SENS (OECD TG 442E), as well as in vivo 
methods such as the local lymph node assay (LLNA; OECD TG 429, OECD TG 
442A, OECD TG 442B) and the human predictive patch test (HPPT) methods i.e.,

for Derivation of a Quantitative Point of-

the ED01. Note that the SARA-ICE model may accept existing LLNA data and HPPT

The SARA-ICE DA was evaluated first using posterior predictive checks, 
which are recommended for any Bayesian statistical model. Checks included a 
comparison of prior marginal distributions against posterior marginals to ensure 
model parameters are identifiable from the data. The SARA-ICE DA uses latent 
variables to represent average quantities for each input type. Checks that latent 
variables are both correlated with the ED01 and their respective input data in the 
SARA-ICE database were performed. Generating ED01 estimates for chemicals not 
within the core SARA-ICE database requires the joint posterior for the full database 
be approximated analytically. This approximation is then used as a prior within a 
SARA-ICE production DA to generate ED01 estimates for new input data. Further 
checks were performed to ensure the production version returns the correct 
estimates. Details of these analyses can be found in Annex 11 of Supporting 
document to the Guideline (GL) on Defined Approaches (DAs) for Skin 
Sensitisation (1). The database on which the model was evaluated is static 
(finalized December 2022) and in case of subsequent version changes, it will be 
evaluated per OECD procedures. 
 
4.1.2. Data Interpretation Procedure 
89. 
The SARA-ICE DA uses a Bayesian statistical model to estimate the ED01. 
A PoD is computed as the geometric mean of the ED01 distribution conditional on 
it being less than 60,000 µg cm-2. Technically, the model can be run with no 
chemical-specific inputs, in which case the resulting ED01 estimates will be taken 
directly from the prior distribution. Using chemical-specific data to inform estimates 
will, for any typical input, return an ED01 estimate whose variance (uncertainty) is 
reduced relative to the prior. The magnitude of reduction in uncertainty is a function 
of several factors including input type(s), input numbers, and input concordance. 
To ensure an appreciable level of uncertainty reduction is achieved, a minimum 
of 2 test methods per compound, covering 2 separate KEs from within OECD 
TG 442C, 442D, or 442E, should be entered into the input template used to run the 
model (LINK TO SARA-ICE APP16). Admissible data types are indicated in Table 
4.1 and Paragraph 87. There is no restriction to the maximum number of stated 
data inputs, in type or experimental repeats, the model can include. Any available 
in vivo data may also be included, but new in vivo data should not be produced to 
be used in this DA. Figure 4.1 demonstrates the workflow for use of SARA-ICE, 
and further details are provided in Appendix III of this document.

4.1.3. Description and limitations of the predictive model and its information sources 
91. 
The individual in chemico, in vitro, and in vivo information is generated using 
existing OECD test guidelines (OECD TG 429, 442A, 442B, 442C, 442D, 442E (4-
10)) and the protocols detailed therein. In the case of the kDPRA and KeratinoSens 
assays, commonly reported units are expressed as molar concentrations. If these 
are provided as inputs, the model also requires the specification of the chemical’s 
molecular weight to enable conversion to mass concentrations. Note that the 
SARA-ICE model may accept existing LLNA data and HPPT data as inputs; 
however, new in vivo data should not be generated.  
 
92. 
The following assays have been characterized and included as potential 
input 
information 
sources 
in 
the 
SARA-ICE 
DA: 
 
• 
Direct Peptide Reactivity Assay (DPRA; OECD TG 442C; KE1 [7]): Skin 
sensitisers are generally electrophilic and react with the nucleophilic 
moieties of proteins. The DPRA measures depletion of two peptides 
containing either cysteine or lysine residues due to covalent binding. A test 
chemical that induces mean peptide depletion of cysteine- and lysine-
containing peptide above 6.38% (or in the case of co-elution, cysteine-only 
depletion above 13.89%) is considered to be positive. The SARA-ICE DA 
does not make use of this classification rule. Instead, the input into SARA-
ICE is the percentage depletion of both peptides. The SARA-ICE DA then 
determines the maximum depletion of either of the peptides. Depletions less 
than 1% are interpreted as < 1%, whilst depletions greater than 99% are 
interpreted as > 99%. It is noted that while the DPRA gravimetric method 
has not been specifically evaluated for use in the SARA-ICE DA, given that 
the DPRA gravimetric evaluation that was conducted under OECD TG 442C 
found that the approaches are equivalent, it is expected to provide data 
within an acceptable range for use as an input into SARA-ICE.  
 
• 
Kinetic Direct Peptide Reactivity Assay (kDPRA; OECD TG 442C; KE1 [7]): 
The kDPRA is intended to quantify the reactivity of test chemicals in a time 
and concentration dependent manner. The rate of depletion of the cysteine 
peptide (used within the DPRA) is determined. Kinetic rate constants are 
calculated and reported as the log Kmax value with Kmax having units M-1 
s-1. The kDPRA is used in the discrimination of UN GHS subcategory 1A 
from those not categorized as sub-category 1A. For SARA-ICE, the log 
Kmax is converted to units g L-1 s-1 and multiplied by -1 (such that higher 
potency chemicals are associated with lower values). This is handled by the 
model, assuming the molecular weight of the chemical has been specified. 
If the reactivity rate is too slow to measure, then the input should be < X 
where X is the limit of detection of the test. For training purposes, this has

© OECD (2025)

reactivity in the kDPRA, the input should be <-3.5.

KeratinoSens: (In vitro Skin Sensitisation: ARE-Nrf2 Luciferase Test 
Method; OECD TG 442D; KE2 [6]): Keratinocytes harbouring a reporter 
gene construct react to possible sensitisers via the Nrf2-Keap1 pathway. A 
test chemical that causes > 1.5 fold luciferase induction, at viabilities > 70% 
when compared to the vehicle control, is considered to be positive. The 
SARA-ICE input is the EC1.5 (µM) and the IC50 (µM). These are converted 
by the template to units of µg mL-1, assuming molecular weight has been 
provided. The model also accepts inputs as > X or < X where X is a test 
concentration within the assay. This is required, for example, to input a 
negative test into the model (for which no EC1.5 is defined). If the test is 
negative, then no luciferase induction occurs at any test concentration and 
the input into the model should > X where X is the maximum tested 
concentration. Similarly, if > 1.5 fold induction is observed at all test 
concentrations (i.e., the test is positive at all test concentrations), the input 
into SARA-ICE should be provided as < X where X is the minimum test 
concentration.

Human Cell Line Activation Test (h-CLAT; OECD TG442E; KE3 [4]): 
Activation of antigen presenting cells is characterized by the up-regulation 
of CD86 and/or CD54. The h-CLAT is considered to be positive if CD86 
induction exceeds 1.5 fold and/or CD54 exceeds 2 fold at viabilities > 50% 
when compared to the vehicle control. The SARA-ICE input is both the 
CD54 EC200 and the CD86 EC150 (µg mL-1) and the CV75 (µg mL-1). If 
either value is not reported because no change in the biomarker was 
observed, the input is > X where X is the maximum concentration tested. In 
the case that induction exceeds thresholds at all test concentrations for 
either biomarker (i.e., the test is positive for that biomarker at all test 
concentrations), the model may also accept < X where X is the minimum 
test 
concentration.

U-SENS (OECD TG 442E; KE3 [4]): Activation of antigen presenting cells 
is characterised by the up-regulation of CD86. The U-SENS is considered 
positive if the CD86 induction exceeds 1.5-fold at viabilities > 70% 
compared to vehicle control. The SARA-ICE input is the CD86 EC150 (µg 
mL-1) and CV70 (µg mL-1). If the test is negative, the input to the model is > 
X where X is the maximum concentration should be used. In the event that 
CD86 induction exceeds the 1.5-fold at all test concentrations, the input 
should be < X where X is the minimum test concentration.

Local Lymph Node Assay (LLNA; OECD TG429, 442A, 442B; KE4 [5,8,9]): 
The induction phase of skin sensitization (KE4) is characterized by 
lymphocyte proliferation in the lymph nodes draining the site of the exposure 
and is proportional to the dose and potency of the substance. The LLNA 
measures proliferation relative to the vehicle control treated group for mice 
exposed on their ear to a test substance of interest. The ratio of the mean 
proliferation for each treatment group compared to the vehicle control is the 
Stimulation Index (SI). An SI ≥ 3 is considered to be indicative of skin 
sensitization. The input for SARA-ICE template is the EC3 expressed as a

percent. These are converted to µg cm-2 by the model. The model may also

tested concentration. Alternatively, if all test concentrations result in an SI

following exposure to a (potential) allergen at some chosen dermal dose. 
The HMT incorporates a pre-treatment with an irritant and typically has a 
smaller test pool than HRIPT. Required HPPT inputs into the SARA-ICE DA 
are the dermal dose expressed as µg cm-2, the total number of test subjects 
and the total number of subjects sensitized following the challenge.  
 
93.

to solubility, are described in the respective test guidelines and the validation 
studies cited therein. No filtering of assay data was performed prior to training the 
SARA-ICE DA, therefore uncertainty measures provided by the DA should reflect 
such limitations. However, caution is advised when selecting inputs since biases 
may nevertheless occur (e.g., when using a DPRA input for a chemical whose 
sensitization potential is enhanced via metabolic activation). Only valid assay 
results, i.e. results within the applicability domain of each assay used as input, 
should be used to run the DA for regulatory purposes. Evaluation of data quality 
should be made prior to use in the DA, this includes use of human data for which 
Strickland et al., 2023 [10] can be used for reference.  
4.1.4. Confidence in the SARA-ICE DA predictions 
94. 
The SARA-ICE DA is built upon a Bayesian statistical model and as such 
provides potency estimates with a numerical measure of precision. The provided 
distribution represents the range of ED01 values that are both consistent with user-
specified input data and the model – where the model is a characterization of the 
association between ED01 estimates and assay data for a training set of 434 
chemicals. Further information on the SARA-ICE database can be found in the 
Technical Specification in Appendix 2. A decision on whether to use specific source 
data is dictated by the limitations of the different input methods found within the 
Annex of the requisite test method (TG 429, TG 442A, TG 442B, TG 442C, TG 
442D, TG 442E). As indicated, for a higher confidence estimate, at least 2 methods

4.1.6. Predictive Capacity of the SARA-ICE DA 
97. 
SARA-ICE derives a point of departure for risk assessment. The model 
estimates the dose at which skin sensitisation is likely to occur in 1% of an HPPT-
eligible human population (ED01), reported as a distribution with associated 
confidence intervals. For PoD estimation, an upper bound is imposed on the ED01 
distribution at a threshold of 60,000 µg cm-2. This reflects the bound for a GHS 
classification of NC and encodes an implicit assumption that the chemical under 
consideration for PoD based risk assessment is a sensitiser. The PoD is therefore 
defined as the geometric mean of the distribution of the ED01 conditional on the 
ED01 being less than 60,000 µg cm-2. 
 
98. 
PoDs were computed for all 196 chemicals in the DASS evaluation set 
(Annex 2) using a) in chemico and in vitro data also reported in the evaluation set 
(which consists of a single input for DPRA, KeratinoSens and h-CLAT) and b) a 
single kDPRA sourced from Natsch et al., 2022 [12], if available). It should be noted 
that 187 / 196 (95%) of chemicals in the DASS evaluation set are presented in the 
SARA-ICE database. However, the data used for training versus evaluation differs 
for most chemicals. For training purposes, a chemical may have both in vivo and 
in vitro data with multiple studies per data type. For evaluation, inputs are restricted 
to a single DPRA, single  KeratinoSens, single h-CLAT and, if available, a single 
kDPRA study. It follows that ED01 estimates obtained during training may be 
considerably different from those obtained in the evaluation.

Introduction 
 
102. 
As described in Section 3.1, the ITS defined approach (DA) are based on 
three information sources: an in chemico assay (DPRA, ADRA, TG 442C) (1), an 
in vitro assay (h-CLAT, U-SENS™, GARD®Skin, TG 442E) (2), and one in silico 
method (Derek Nexus or OECD Toolbox) prediction from the models listed in 
Appendices I-II of this guideline (referred to hereafter as in silico)) (3-4). For each 
information source a score is given depending on the outcome of the individual assay 
and/or prediction, that is then summed to obtain the DA prediction (5). 
 
Applicability domain of the individual information sources 
In chemico/in vitro information source  
103. 
A test chemical is considered to be within the in chemico/in vitro information 
source domain (i.e. applicable) if it can be tested according to the individual 
protocols, taking into account the technical and chemical type limitations of each 
assay (as defined in the respective TGs (442C and 442E (1, 2)). The in chemico/in 
vitro results are considered applicable, in case there are no technical or chemical 
space specific limitations and no reason why the results obtained from the assay 
cannot be considered. 
 
In silico information source 
104.

structures. These in silico sources rely on molecular representation of the 
chemicals: input usually by drawing the chemical structure, or by entering the 
Simplified Molecular-Input Line-Entry System (SMILES) or the IUPAC International 
Chemical Identifier (InChi). A test chemical is considered to be within the in silico 
information source domain if it is a single chemical (i.e. mixtures  and UVCBs are 
outside the applicability domain) and a prediction can be made, taking into account

Appendix I: Description, applicability domain, and protocol for Derek Nexus predictions 
 
Description of Derek Nexus  
 
111. 
Derek is an expert, knowledge-based software tool comprising alerts on 
several toxicity endpoints, including skin sensitisation. Derek fires alerts based on 
structural features, i.e. whether a hapten has potential for electrophilic binding to 
skin proteins either directly or following metabolism/auto-oxidation (1).  Additional 
information on this model can be found in the QSAR Model Reporting Format 
(QMRF) 
(2). 
 
Applicability domain of Derek Nexus  
 
112. 
To each skin sensitisation alert, a likelihood level is associated. Chemicals 
firing an alert with a likelihood of certain, probable, plausible, or equivocal are 
considered to be positive. Chemicals with a negative prediction of ‘non-sensitiser 
with no misclassified or unclassified features’ are considered to be negative.   
 
113. 
Skin sensitisation predictions from Derek Nexus v6.1.0  are used in the ITS 
DA. All positive predictions (likelihood = certain, probable, plausible or equivocal) 
are considered to be inside the applicability domain. Negative predictions 
(likelihood = doubted, improbable, impossible or non-sensitiser) are also 
considered to be in the applicability domain unless they contain misclassified 
and/or unclassified features. A prediction of non-sensitiser with misclassified 
features indicates the presence of a fragment that has been observed exclusively

Description of OECD QSAR Toolbox  
 
115. 
The OECD QSAR Toolbox is a freely available software application that 
supports reproducible and transparent chemical hazard assessment. It offers 
functionalities for retrieving experimental data, simulating metabolism and profiling 
properties of chemicals. These information and tools can be used to find 
structurally and mechanistically defined analogues and chemical categories, which 
can serve as sources for read-across and trend analysis for data gap filling 
(1)].  Additional information on this model can be found in the QSAR Model 
Reporting Format (QMRF) (2).

Explanation and rationale for the use of different domain layers:  
 
1. Positive predictions (both by read-across and profiling): the presence of an 
alert (which is the requirement for positive Toolbox prediction to be 
considered within in the mechanistic domain) is sufficient to consider the 
prediction to be within the Toolbox domain. Substances triggering an alert 
are considered as in domain because they contain the toxicophore that has 
been observed experimentally in skin sensitisers. No further checks are 
needed in this context to consider the prediction within the Toolbox in silico 
domain.  
2. Negative predictions by read-across: the structural and parametric domains 
are not taken into account because the Toolbox has already ensured some 
level of similarity with other substances in its training set that met the 
requirements to be selected as suitable analogues for read-across (these 
requirements are explained in detail in the DASS AW description).  
3. Negative prediction by profiling predictions: all domain layers are taken into 
account to ensure the highest possible reliability level for the Toolbox 
prediction. Stricter requirements are needed mainly for two reasons: 1. lack 
of alerts is not equal to proof of lack of sensitisation potential and 2. to apply 
a cautious approach since acceptance of negative predictions may lower 
the human health protection level risk in case of a false negative 
predictions.

Running the model involves drawing samples from the posterior distribution using the No-U-
Turn sampler implemented within the probabilistic programming language Stan. By default, a 
sample of 10,000 draws of the ED01 is returned to the user. Summary statistics such as 
quantile estimates of the distribution are calculated empirically on this sample.  
The No-U-Turn sampler produces a Markov Chain whose stationary distribution is the 
posterior distribution of the model. A successful model run is achieved when the Markov Chain 
converges to the target distribution. The Stan software provides several measures of 
convergence to assess whether convergence may be assumed.
